# Chapter 24: The Bait

The keeper stops setting meat at my line. He goes up the green and takes the one warm thing that ever filled the far mouth, and lays *that* out bleeding instead.

---

I read it the way I read every turn of this place, in the gut and then the head, by the green doing the thing it does not do.

There is a sound in the high country that does not belong to the high country. I am down in the drowned roots over the bone-spit when it comes, two lows after I last ate the keeper's open hand and walked back up clear with the dull-water sliding off me, and the body has me up out of the mud before the head has the shape of it. A sound from the high stones. From my high stones. The squared dead and the kept fire and the great gray bulk of the Kala folded among the still ones — up there, where no spit reaches and no hooked iron has ever crouched at a bled line, where the tribe lays its dead faced to the sun and the small ones run between the legs of the big — up there a sound is going up that I know in the deep buried place the way I know the gap behind my teeth.

The iron-on-stone sound. The net sound. The owning sound.

They have gone up.

I have read this whole island for the slavers low after low and I read it wrong, and the wrongness lands behind my breastbone like a stone dropped in the still water of the rot-pool, the rings going out and out. I thought the spit was the line. I thought the green was the wall — that the killing island that drops every other body, the fever-air and the venom and the bad water that I alone walk clear through, was a wall the owning kind could not climb. That they would lean and lean at the edge of my green with the open hand and the dull-water and never get past the drowning death of it to the high stones. That up there, at least, the family was safe behind the same green that has tried to kill me my whole life and could not.

But the green does not kill them fast. It kills them slow. And while it was killing them slow they walked up it, low after low, the way the patient one always walks toward a thing — and now the owning sound is going up off the high stones, and the green did not stop them, and the family was never behind the wall at all. The family was only further up the road.

I am moving before I finish the thought. Up out of the roots, up the bare gray, up my own green by the runs my own feet wore into it, and the near mouth is gone and the far mouth is gone and there is only the third thing under both of them, the thing that has no mouth, the thing that runs.

---

I come up over the last shelf into the high stones and I crouch in the lean of the great trees against the rock where the shadow is, and I read it whole in one long breath, the way the body takes the whole shape of a thing before it goes down into it.

The kept fire is scattered. Kicked through, the red coals raked out across the gray and going to ash, the held light that the tribe made and tended and never let go out across all the lows I counted it from the drowning green — out. The squared dead are pulled from their faced rows. The small wrong things the tribe lays among them, the gifts set squared and facing, are kicked loose and scattered down the stone. And there are owning shapes up here on the high country, six of them, eight, more coming up the road behind, with the hooked iron and the long net and the lashing-rope coiled on the shoulder, standing squared and weight-even on the ground the tribe bled into being, taking up the room as if the high stones owe them the standing.

And the Kala is down.

She is down on the cleanest high shelf, the one where the sun strikes first, where her small one is laid faced among the dead — she is down on her side across the very stone she keeps, and the net is over her, the great gray-silver bulk of her tangled in it, the long arm I have lain under fighting the mesh and the mesh holding. Two of the hooked irons are in her. I see them from the shadow, the barbed iron driven into the great shoulder and the haunch, the turned barb gone in to stay, to drag, to keep a live thing on the line, and the dark blood of her welling slow and thick down the gray hide and pooling in the seams of the stone. She is making a sound. Low. Not the gentling sound, not the keeping one she laid over me in the fever. The other one, the one with the tear in it, the one she made over the dead small thing — but turned outward now, up, a thing dragged out of the great chest and thrown at the owning shapes that stand over her, and it goes nowhere, and the high stones swallow it.

They have not killed her. That is the thing I read last and it is the worst thing, and the body has it before the head can shape why. Two irons in her and she is bleeding into the stone and they have not finished her. A thing they wanted dead, they would have finished — the great heart is right there under the gray hide and the iron knows where the heart is. They put the iron in her shoulder. They put it in her haunch. They put it where it holds and drags and keeps and does not kill, and they stand over her with the net holding and they do not close, and they are not looking at her at all.

They are looking at the green.

The squared shapes stand over the downed Kala with their backs half-turned to her and their faces turned out, down the road, down the bare gray to the high drowned roots, to the dark — to me. To the place the shape lives that wears the keeper's mark and does not run and does not break, the thing the owned girl walked twice down the killing spit to stand at the wall of. They are not looking at the thing in the net.

They are looking at the thing the net is set to catch.

---

It is bait.

I know it is bait the way I know the rot-pool from the drink, the way I knew the steaming meat at my line was bait, in the gut a long breath before the head lays it plain — and the head lays it plain, hard and flat and whole, and there is no part of the law I do not already have. I made myself a king of the wild things on this law. The staked thing that screams to bring the bigger thing. The kill left open on the trail to draw the dispute up out of the rot-thickets. Bait is a thing of theirs set out as a thing of yours, set where the wanting will reach for it — and the keeper has set out the one thing on this whole green that the wanting will reach for through any net, any iron, any law.

He read me right. He has been reading me right low after low and I knew it and I leaned anyway. He set the warm meat at my line and the dull-water in the bright gourd and the open hand, and the body slid all of it, the dull-thing pouring off me clear, the hook smelled out and named whole — and he watched the bark go clean each low and learned the thing under the body that does not slide. He learned that the shape in the green has a hunger he can feed. And a man who reads that a thing has a hunger you can feed reads, after, *what* the hunger is for. He read the road between me and the owned girl and cut it. And he read the road up the green to the high stones, to the great gray thing that the small wrong man-shape came down off its crown to guard, and he did not cut that road.

He walked up it. And took the warm thing off the end of it. And laid it bleeding on the stone where the wanting cannot help but reach.

The keeper has stopped baiting the belly. He stopped baiting it lows ago. He stopped baiting it the low he learned the dull-water slides. He is baiting the far mouth now in the only road that ever reaches it — and the far mouth is the throat the whole net is set for, and the Kala is the hook he has run down it, and there is no smelling this one out and walking back up clear. The hook is not in the meat. The hook is in the thing I love. And I file it on the shelf low and hard, the worst thing I have ever filed, and I do not turn it over, because there is no turning it, because the body has no head for what to do with it, only the shape of its teeth.

---

I count the owning shapes from the shadow because counting is the thing I do with a thing I cannot hold.

Eight up on the high stones now and four more come up the road, twelve, and the road behind them is not empty. There are more in the green below, I can hear the iron of them, the net-men set wide the way they set wide at my line, holding the gaps, no shape drifting, no road left open that they have not stood a body across. The patient one is among them. I cannot see him but I know his hand in the laying of this the way I know his crouch in the black — the slow sure weighing-on, the net set not to drop in a panic when the dark comes but to close slow and sure around a thing that has already walked into it. He has not set this net to catch a thing that runs. He has set it to catch a thing that comes.

He knows I will come. That is the whole of the bait. He does not need to drive me or crowd me or push me to the cliff. He has read that there is one thing in the green that the wanting reaches for through any wall, and he has laid that thing bleeding in the open with the net wide around it, and he has gone still, and he is waiting for the reach. The way I went still over the staked thing and waited for the dispute to come up out of the rot-thickets. The way the green goes still and sets the screaming thing out and waits behind it with teeth.

I am the dispute now. I am the bigger thing the screaming brings. He has staked the Kala out under the open sky on the cleanest high shelf, and he is the green, and I am the thing that cannot leave the screaming alone.

---

I could go.

The thought comes up the way it always comes, the old free thought, the high-circling thought, the one that wears the wings of the wide-sailing carrion-birds that ride the heat off the canopy and answer to no ground at all. I let it come because I have learned that letting a thing come is how I read it true. I could go. Down off the high stones, back into my green, into the deep drowned country where no road runs and the fever-air lies thickest and the bad water stands in the still pools breathing up the death that takes them and does not take me. The owning kind cannot follow me there. They walk up the green slow, paying a low of fever and a low of rot for every length, and the deep green would lay them down the way it lays everything down — everything but me. I could go into the killing heart of my own island, the one country on this whole green that is mine and only mine because nothing else can live in it, and they could close their net on the high stones and find a bleeding monster and an empty road where the man-shape was, and never once lay the hook in me. No hand on the back of my neck ever again. Free the way the high-sailing bird is free, riding the heat with no ground under it and nothing it owes.

And the Kala is on the stone with two irons in her, making the torn sound at the sky.

I crouch in the shadow at the edge of my own high country and I look at the great gray bulk of her tangled in the net, the long arm fighting the mesh, the dark blood going thick down the gray hide into the seams of the stone, and I do not move toward the deep green.

Because she came down off the high shelf in the fever-dark and folded the great arm over the burning man-shape the green tried to kill and could not, and made the gentling sound over the top of my head, low, up and down, no edges, *the keeping one,* over and over, when every fast part of me screamed *hands, hold, the weight on the neck* and she was not the hand, she was never the hand. Because the far mouth that no kill has ever filled and no water has ever filled, the hole under the breastbone that ate me from the inside while the green ate me from the out, went still against her warm — the only time in all my life I can reach that it went still — and lay down quiet under my ribs and was full. Because there is a place in her shaped like a thing taken, and a place in me shaped like a thing taken, and we lay down in each other's hole and neither of us ever had a word for it and neither of us ever needed one.

The keeper does not know any of that. He cannot read the body and he cannot read the warm that knows it holds me. But he read the *want.* He saw the dark move up the green to guard her and not move to guard itself, and he laid the want bleeding on the stone, and he is right. The far mouth does not weigh the net. The far mouth does not count the irons or read the patient one's hand in the spacing or care that the whole of it is a thing of theirs set out as a thing of mine. The far mouth knows only that the one warm in all the world that ever closed it is down on the stone with the hooks in it, and it reaches.

---

I have it whole, then, crouched in the shadow with the scattered fire going to ash and the owning shapes turned out to the dark waiting for my reach. I have it the way I have the spit and the seep and the loose rocks, the whole shape of the thing, every tooth of it, and the having does not change one thing the body means to do.

This is the law and I will not lie to the shelf: *the thing you reach for is the thing with the hook in it.* The oldest law on the shelf, the one I made a king on. And folded inside it, deeper than the shelf, older than any word I ever stole off the tribe's tongues to set against it, is the thing the law was never able to hold — that some things you reach for anyway. Some hooks you take in your own teeth with your eyes wide open, knowing the barb is turned, knowing the patient one is still in the green with the net wide, knowing the hand is already half-closed on the back of your neck — because the thing on the end of the hook is the one thing the world has ever set warm against the hole under your breastbone, and a thing that has filled that hole once is a thing you do not leave on the stone, not for the deep green, not for the high bird's freedom, not for never feeling the hand again. The keeper baited the one hunger no green and no crown and no slid poison and no wall could ever fill. He found the hook that no smelling-out can pull. And I crouch in the shadow of my own high stones with the whole trap drawn clear in the gut and the head both, and I do the thing the bait was set for, the thing he staked her out to make me do, the thing I would do again.

I start down toward her. Down off the one high edge where no hand could reach me, down toward the net and the hooked iron and the patient one waiting in the green, toward the great gray warm that knows it holds me, bleeding on the stone — by my own feet, by my own choosing, with my eyes wide open.

And that is the reach that lays the only hook in me that was ever made to hold.
