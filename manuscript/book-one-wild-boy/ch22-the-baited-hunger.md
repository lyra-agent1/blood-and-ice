# Chapter 22: The Baited Hunger

The keeper stops hunting the dark. He starts feeding it.

---

I read it the low after the watcher, the way I read every turn here, by the camp doing the thing it did not do before.

I have lain four lows now in the high drowned roots over the bone-spit, and I have learned the leaning of the camp the way I learned the spit and the seep. The first low after the owned girl drew my shape at the owning-fire, the camp leaned up the spit at the dark with the weighing-look on it, the iron-and-net look, and I read it for what it was: a thing that means to cut the wrongness out of the green. So I lay flat and gave it nothing. I did not move a frond. I laid the want of the second mouth down under the beast and dug my pads in the mud and waited for the iron.

The iron does not come.

This is the new thing, and the body reads it long before the head can shape why. The keeper does not send the net up the spit. He does not loose the owned line into my green with hooks to drag the dark. A man made of the slow sure coming-on, the one who walks toward a thing the way the green walks toward the Hesh — and he does not come. He does the other thing. He turns the camp around. Where it leaned up the spit with the cutting-look, now it leans up the spit with an open hand.

I do not trust the open hand. I have only ever seen one close.

They set a thing at my line.

I watch them do it at the bare of the low, when the steam comes up thick off the cooling mud and the gray goes soft. Two of the owned carry it down the slick black spit, bent under it the way they go bent under everything, and an owning walks behind them with the hands on the hips, and they set it on the dry mud just short of the wet, just short of the first of my mangrove — a thing on a flat of bark. Meat. Cooked meat, the grease-smell of it coming up the down-wind into the roots where I lie, and beside it the bright water in a gourd that is not the rot-water, the kept-water, the water they drink and the owned do not. They set it at my line the way a hand sets a thing down in the open palm. And they go back up the spit. They do not stay to watch from the stumps. They leave it, and they go, and the green is left alone with the warm meat steaming at its edge.

I lie in the roots and I do not move for it.

---

It is bait. I know it is bait the way I know the rot-pool from the drink, in the gut before the head.

I have set bait my whole life in the green. The noose under the leaf-fall, the staked thing that screams to bring the bigger thing, the kill left open on the trail to draw the dispute up out of the rot-thickets where the Skell wait. Bait is a thing of yours set out as a thing of theirs, set where the wanting will reach for it, and the law of bait is the oldest law on the shelf: *the thing you reach for is the thing with the hook in it.* I made myself a king of the wild things on that law. I read it on the owned girl the low the watcher walked her down — the soft live thing set out on the line to draw the dark up where the iron can cut it. I read it now on the steaming bark at my line. The keeper has stopped trying to cut me out of the green. He is trying to draw me out of it. He has set the meat where the wanting will reach, and gone back up the spit to wait for the reach.

So I do not reach. I lie above it through the dark and I do not go down, and the near mouth turns over slow at the grease-smell, the belly-want, the easy fat I did not have to run down and break in the mud — and the near mouth is not the mouth the bait is set for, and I know it, and I hold. The bait is set for the other one. The far one. The keeper does not know my belly. He has read my chest. He knows there is a thing in the green that wears his mark, a thing that does not run, a thing the owned girl walked twice down the killing spit to stand at the wall of — and a man who reads that does not bait the belly. He baits the wanting under the breastbone. He is not setting out meat. He is setting out *welcome*, in the only road the camp has to lay it, and the welcome is the hook, and the second mouth is the throat he means it for.

I file it low and hard and I do not turn it over. And I lie above the meat and I do not go down, and the grease goes up the down-wind into the dark, and the far mouth pulls tight, and I hold.

---

The owned girl does not carry the next one.

This is the thing the keeper has read, and it is a hard thing to read, and I lay it on him as a thing to fear: he has seen that the dark moves for her and does not move for him, and so he keeps her out of it. He has read the road between us, the road of the careful falling sound and the hand against the wet mud, and a man who reads a road takes it for himself or shuts it. He shuts it. The next low the meat is set at my line by two of the owned with an owning behind them, and she is not among them, and she is not at the wall of the camp when they come, and I read her up the spit at the owning-fire kept close with the big one's shape between her and the dark.

He has cut the road. He sets the welcome out himself, in the camp's own hand, and keeps the one who could carry it back behind his fires. He does not want the dark drawn to *her*. He wants it drawn to *him*. The bait is the same, the meat and the bright water, but the hand that sets it is the keeper's now, and the keeper's hand is the one that closes.

And the far mouth does a thing it should not do, and I file it as the worst thing on the shelf, under the four and the five and the mark I cannot read: it leans toward the meat anyway. Not the belly-lean. The other lean. Because no thing has ever set warm meat at the edge of my green and walked away and left it for me. The green does not do it. The green sets the thing that screams and waits behind it with teeth. The tribe — the tribe gave me the high stones and the meaning-sound and the Kala's vast slow arm, and the tribe never once set a kill at my feet in the open palm and stepped back. The high water of the family was the bold heart pressed to my chest and the seer's gray finger and the home with no between in it — and even in the home, the meat was earned, run down and broken and dragged in, the place bled into being a low at a time, borrowed back off the killing island and poured away the low the hand opens. Belonging in the green is a thing you bleed for and hold by force. It is never *given*. Nothing has ever opened its hand at me and set a warm thing down and gone away and left it mine.

The keeper does. He sets the warm thing at my line and opens the hand and goes. And the second mouth, the far one, the one that no meat or water answers — it does not know bait from gift. It knows only the open hand. And it leans.

---

I go down to the meat on the fifth low.

I tell the head it is to read the bait. That is the head's road, and the head lays it plain: a thing the keeper sets out is a thing the keeper is reading, and to read the reader I must touch the thing he set, learn the trap by walking its edge the way I walked the seep and the spit. So I go down in the full dark when the fires are heavy and the watch is thin, poured into the mud the way the green pours, and I come to my own line where the dry goes to wet, and the meat is there on the bark, fresh-set, steaming faint in the warm wet dark.

I do not eat it. I crouch over it and I read it. I read the bark for the cut of their tools and the meat for the beast it came off and the bright water for the smell of the keeper's hand on the gourd — and there, low under the grease, is the thing I came down to find, the thing the head needed and the gut already knew. A second smell. Faint. Laid into the bright water and into the meat both. Not the rot-poison the bad water carries, not the venom-smell, not the spoiled under-smell of a kill gone over. A made smell. A thing they put in. The dull-smell. I have the shape of it from the lashed Mure in its cage, the way the tide-thing went heavy and slow and stopped fighting the rope — they feed it the dull-thing in its water, and it goes quiet, and its eyes go flat. The keeper has laid the dull-smell into the welcome. The warm thing at my line is the open hand, and folded inside the open hand is the closing one. Eat, and go heavy. Drink, and go slow. Lean into the welcome and let it lay you down in the mud where the net is easy.

I should leave it. The law says it, the legs say it, the oldest thing on the shelf says it: *the thing you reach for is the thing with the hook in it,* and now I have the hook in my hand, smelled it out, named its shape — leave it, go up to the high stones, never lay my throat where the dull-water waits.

I take the meat.

I take it and I eat it, there at my own line in the dark, the warm fat and the dull-smell folded through it, and I drink the bright kept-water down with the made thing in it, and the body does the thing the body does, the wrong thing, the thing off the shelf I do not turn over. The dull-thing comes up in me slow, a heaviness reaching for the back of my eyes, a hand laid soft on the back of the head the way the keeper means it — and it does not land. It slides. The way the fever slid, the way the rot-water slid, the way the venom went out of me fast and left no mark while every other body knotted and dropped. The dull-thing goes through me and out of me and leaves me clear, my eyes wide in the dark, my pads steady under me, the meat in my belly warm and only warm. The keeper has fed me the thing that lays the Mure down flat in its cage, and I crouch at my line in the full dark, clear-headed, light, the heaviness pouring off me as fast as it comes, and I read the truth of it whole.

His hook does not hold me. He does not know that yet. He thinks he is laying me down a low at a time, drawing me heavy and slow toward the open palm. And I am eating his bait out of his open hand and walking back up into the green untouched, clear, the dull-water sliding off me like rain off a leaf — and only I know it.

---

But the far mouth is not the belly, and the dull-thing does not slide off the far mouth.

This is the thing I lie awake on in the high roots after, the meat warm in me and the made thing gone, and it is the thing I cannot eat my way clear of. The keeper has read me right. Not the body — he cannot read the body, no man has ever read the body, it is the one book that takes no eye but mine. He has read the want. He set warm meat at my line and opened his hand and went away and left it mine, low after low, and the body slid the hook every time — but the second mouth took the open hand in and held it, and the open hand does not slide off. It lodges. It is the only open hand the world has shown me since the Kala's arm, and the second mouth does not know the dull-smell is folded inside it. The second mouth only knows that a thing set warm food at the edge of my green and stepped back and called it mine, in the road of the upright kind, my own kind, the kind I came down off my crown to lean at — and the far hunger lies down around that the way it lay down around the girl's warning, and it will not get up off it.

I have the hook in my teeth and I have smelled it out and named it whole — the dull-water, the cut road, the keeper drawing the dark toward his own closing hand — and the head knows all of it, and the head is not the part the bait was set for. The far mouth wants the open hand. The far mouth wants the meat set down and left, the welcome the tribe never gave because the green does not give, the warm thing put in the palm and the palm not closing. And the far mouth does not care that the palm is closing. It has wanted the open hand so long that it will lean into the closing one to feel the open one a breath longer.

That is the bait. Not the meat. Not the dull-water the body throws off. The bait is the welcome itself, the open hand of my own kind, and there is no part of me that throws *that* off, and the keeper found it without ever reading the body at all. He baited the one hunger no green and no crown and no slid poison could ever fill, and I am leaning into it with the hook smelled out and the trap drawn whole on the shelf and my eyes wide open in the dark.

---

I go down again the next low. And the next.

I tell the head the head's reasons and the head lays them plain and true — to read the keeper, to learn the trap, to know whose mark is mine and why it lives in me, to be near the man-shaped road I lost — and the reasons are true, and they are not why my legs carry me down the killing spit a low at a time toward the open hand. I know they are not. I lay it down plain in the gut where I lay everything and I will not lie to the shelf: I go down because the welcome is set out warm at my line, and the far mouth fills a little at the open hand, and the second mouth has been empty so long that a little is a flood.

I see the keeper learn it. I read him reading the bark gone clean each low, the meat taken, the gourd drained, the dark come down to his open hand and gone back up — and the slow sure weighing-look settles deeper into him, the look of a man whose bait has been taken, who knows now that the thing in the green has a hunger he can feed, and a thing with a hunger you can feed is a thing already half in the hold. He does not know the dull-water slides. He thinks I am going heavy. He thinks the welcome is laying me down low by low toward the palm, and he is wrong about the body and right about the want, and the want is the door, and the want is the door he is walking the brand through low after low with warm meat in its open hand.

And the law comes up plain and hard behind my teeth the way it always comes, fact turned to survival-law in the gut: *the green never gives; only the trap gives.* The warm thing set down and left in the open palm is the shape of the snare, every time, in every wood — a thing of theirs laid out as a thing of yours where the wanting will reach. The tribe never gave because the tribe was true. The keeper gives because the keeper is a snare. And I know it, I have it whole on the shelf, the open hand and the closing one folded inside it and the dull-water sliding off the body and the want it does not slide off — I know it, and the far mouth leans into the open hand anyway, low after low, because the want is older than the law and deeper than the shelf, and it was here, hollow under the breastbone, before I ever had a single word to set against it.

I do not eat the bait because I am fooled. I eat it because I am hungry in the place no meat answers, and the keeper found that place, and there is warm welcome set out at the edge of my green in the open palm of my own kind, and I have wanted the open palm my whole life.

The shape I named to no one has a hunter now. The hunter has stopped chasing the dark. He has started feeding it. And the body throws off the dull-water, and the body throws off the venom and the fever and the rot, the body throws off every poison the green ever put in me — and there is one poison it will not throw, and the keeper poured it, and it is the open hand. I have it whole on the shelf and it does not matter that I have it. *The poison you can name is not always the poison you can spit.* The far mouth is reaching back, low after low, down the killing spit, into the open hand, toward the place where the palm closes — and I am letting it, with my eyes wide open in the dark.
