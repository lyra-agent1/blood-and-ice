# Chapter 27: The Hold

I know this dark before they put me in it.

It went down to the bottom of the world with the first ship and waited there for me. Now they have built it again on the water and carried it back to me, the dark that took my first word out of my mouth lows ago — and the body is in it before the head can say the word, screaming the one thing it has always known: *here. I have been here.*

---

They drag me down off my green a low and a low and a low, and I count every one.

The hand stays on the back of my neck the whole road down. They trade it man to man so the closing never lifts — when one arm tires another spreads its fingers over the same place, the heel of the palm to the same knob of bone, so that the weight is one weight that does not change, only the meat under it changing. They learned that out wide on the high ground too. They learned that the shape in the green slides the dull-water and eats the open hand and walks back clear, so they do not trust the iron alone; they keep the hand on me to read me, to feel the surge come up before it comes, the way I once laid a hand flat to the green at dusk and read the night coming up warm through the soil. They read me with the hand. I read them back with the neck. It is a low trade and I am the lower in it, folded down, walking where the hand walks me, but it is a trade, and I keep my side of it open behind my teeth where no hand reaches.

The green takes its length out of them on the way down, the way it took its length on the way up. I am folded under the nets on the drag-pole, but the ears are mine still, and I hear the wet rattle start in two of them by the second low, the shaking-cough of the fever-air going into a body the air does not slide off. I smell the rot wake in the leader's furrow — the long open I tore from his eye to his jaw, that on me would already be drawing shut clean and on him goes hot and tight and stinks sweet by the third low. They paid a length of fever and a length of rot for every step up. They pay it again going down, dying slow, to carry the one thing on the island the dying does not touch. I file it where the still place is, low under the red: *the green is killing them even as they carry me. It is on my side and does not know it. Neither do they.*

The high stones go small behind me and then go to haze and then go to nothing, and the great gray bulk of her with them, left where the iron left her, and the squared dead, and the scattered fire. I do not look back past the place where looking back stops giving me anything. That is a thing the soles taught me long before the hand: you do not spend the eye on ground that is already gone. You spend it on the ground the foot is in. The foot is in the down road. I count the down road.

---

The water-edge stinks of them before I see it.

Tar. The black seam-stink, the hold-tar, the smell that lived in the dark of the first ship low under low under the piss and the bodies and came up screaming behind my gone teeth when I bit the net. It is here, laid on the warm wet air, and the body knows it the way the body knew the hand — before the head, in the gut and then the teeth. They have drawn a boat up the black mud where the green drowns into the channel, and it sits low and dark and wrong on the brown water, a made thing, the only straight lines in all the drowning green, and the smell of it is the smell of the inside of my whole life before the shore.

They take me down to it across the mud the way they took everything, together, one animal with many backs. The Mure would be in this water — I read it even now, even folded, the warm shallows at the channel-mouth where the pale wet tide-things rise at the low water to ambush what wades — and I feel the slavers feel it too, the way they bunch and hurry and put their backs to the green and their eyes to the brown. The green is full of teeth for them every length of the way. It is not full of teeth for me. That is the last gift my green gives me as they haul me off it: I am the only thing on this whole shore that the shore will not eat, and they are carrying me away in iron because of it.

They get me to the boat and there are others in it already.

---

There is a cage in the boat, low under the thwarts, bars of split hard-cane lashed with the slaver-rope, and there are things in it. A young Skell, the lean wrong-made carrion-kin, its jackal-coded muzzle pushed to the bars, its eyes gone flat and far the way an eye goes when the body has given the fight to something lower down. Two of the small Mure, taken at some low water, their wet pale skin gone dull and cracking in the open air, web-fingers slack. The green that the slavers came across the water to harvest, walking into the death they could not survive — here is the catch of it, low in the boat, breathing the tar.

And there is the girl.

She is not in the cage. She is lashed at the wrist to a ring in the thwart, sat upright where she can be reached, the way they sat her at the fires to name the world to the dark — the owned girl, Yara, the one who laid me the road one stone at a low under the watcher's eye, who drew the Eye-and-Thorn on the wet air and went still and looked at me wrong, *you are something.* The rope at her wrist is the rope at my wrist. The ring she is lashed to is the ring I will be lashed to. She is the use that talks; I am the use that bleeds and walks back clean. We are the same catch in two skins, and both of us are theirs.

She looks at me when they bring me down. I see her read it — the nets, the rope at my throat, the hand on my neck, the blood drying where the rock split my lip and the lip already shut under it — and her face does the thing it did when she named the relic. Not the beast-fear. The other thing. The face of a thing finding a piece of its own gone world in the mouth of the green that eats it. She knows what the hand on the neck is. She has worn it. The face is not for the monster they think they netted. It is for the boy. *They got you too.*

I hold her eyes the one moment the hand allows before it turns my head down. I have no sound to give her — the green ones torn out, the human ones never mine, only the road she laid and never finished. So I give her the only thing the body has. I do not break. I let her see me not break. That is a thing you can hand another caught thing across a boat with no words in it: *I am still in here.* Her eyes go down to the rope at her own wrist, and her jaw sets, and that is its own answer. Two of us in the boat are not broken. That is a count too.

---

They put me down into the hold.

There is a hatch, and a dark under it, and they lower me into the dark on the drag-pole and then haul the pole out after and drop the hatch, and the dark closes over me the way the rot-dark closed over me the first night in the den-crack — except the rot-dark held me and this does not hold me, this swallows me, and there is a difference the body knows and screams at. The den was a hole I chose. This is a hole that has me.

I am here before. The body is screaming it. The wet wood under me that moves, the always-tilting, the world with no down in it. The reek of tar and too many bodies. Bars near my face in the black. A floor that breathes when the ship breathes. The dark of the hold, low under low, where the hand let go and the water came in warm and black across the moving floor and climbed and the bodies all moved at once. It is the dream. They have built me the inside of the dream and lowered me into it. Every low of it comes up the throat at once the way the hand brought every low of the hold up at once on the high stones, and the scream gathers behind my teeth, the buried one, the one that has no words because the words went out of me here the first time —

And I do not let it out.

That is the thing I cannot account for and do not reach for a name for. On the high stones, when the hand first closed, the scream tore out of me whole and I could not hold it and did not try. Here, in the worse place, the truer place, the place the scream was born — I hold it. I take it down. I press it flat behind my teeth beside the name, in the gap where the three went, where no hand reached then and no hand reaches now, and I keep it there. Not because the fear is less. The fear is more. The fear is the whole black hold full to the hatch. I hold it because of a thing the still place learned on the stones when her eye went out: that the scream is theirs the moment it leaves me. They listen for it. They lowered me into the dream to draw it out. The quiet is mine. So I keep the quiet, and I keep the scream behind it, banked, the way I keep the brand behind the name — kept, not gone, kept against the one low I can hand it back.

---

I lie in the dark and I find the count again, and I build it.

The body knows this work — it is the same work as the green, only the green was teeth and water and this is plank and iron: read the place with the soles and the fingers and the teeth and the smell, and take it apart slow into things you can hold. I cannot stand the dark whole. The dark whole is the dream and the dream takes me. So I do not take it whole. I take it the way the green taught me to take a thing too big to take: a stone at a time, a low at a time, borrowed back from the dark.

The plank under my cheek. I learn it. The grain of it, the wet of it, the give of it where the worm has been. The roll of the floor — I find its count, the long lift and the long fall, the small catch at the top where the water lets go, and I lay my own breath under it until the roll and the breath go together and the floor is no longer doing a thing to me but a thing with me. The smell I take apart into its threads: tar under brine under the green-rot of the caged things under the high sweet rot of the leader's furrow somewhere above. The sounds: water on the hull, the slow groan of the made thing taking the channel, the wet cough of the two dying ones up in the light, the scrape of the Skell's claw on its cane bars, and — far, thin, through the plank — a low human run of sound that is the girl's voice, naming something to the dark she thinks cannot answer. Still naming. I lay that thread along the others and it holds a little of the dark off, the way her road always held a little of the hole off, and I file it: *she is up there, and she has not stopped.*

I count the caught. The Skell. The two Mure. The girl. Myself. I count the slavers by their feet on the deck above, the weights and the gaits, the one that drags now from the rot in its leg. I count the dying — two, by the cough, and the leader's furrow makes a third that does not know yet it is dying. I count the things behind my teeth: the name my mother gave me, woken and whole, *Rook,* pressed flat like a coin in the gap. The brand, ground small, kept to hand back. The road the girl laid, unfinished, the gathering one. And under all of them, lower than the red, lower than the second mouth, in the still place that has no name — her. The great dark eye going out turned to me with the gentling unbroken, holding nothing. The price. I set the count of all of it down in the still place like a man laying squared stones, faced and even, the way the tribe laid its dead and its gifts on the high stones, and the laying of them is the thing that holds the dark off the longest of all.

The far mouth is open. It is torn wider than the green ever tore it, the hole under the breastbone where her warm was, howling silent into the black hold. Nothing reaches it. No water reached it on the shore and no kill reached it through all the green and the only thing that ever reached it was the warm she laid over me in the fever-dark, and that is gone, iron-killed on the stone, and the hole is open all the way down. I do not try to fill it. I have learned the thing the whole book of the body was written to teach me: that the far mouth is the engine and the engine does not run on being fed. It runs on the want. And the want, here in the dark, with her face set in the still place and the name woken behind my teeth, does not fold into grief and lie down. It does the other thing. It turns. The grief is in it and the grief does not weaken it; the grief is the weight on the line that draws it tight. I lie in the dark that has me and I feel the far mouth go from a hole that eats me to a thing that pulls, low and even and sure, toward the one low, the far low, the low I cannot see yet but count toward anyway — when the hand opens. The hand always opens. It opened once on this same kind of floor and dropped me into the warm black water, and the water carried me to the shore, and the shore made me. The hand will open again. I do not know how and I do not know when and I do not need to. I only need to be the thing that is still counting when it does.

---

They lift the hatch once, in the dark, and a slaver comes down into it with a low light.

It is to look at me. I read it before the light finds my face — the careful step, the held breath of a man going down to a thing he has paid too much for. He crouches and turns my chin up in his hard fingers, up into the small flame, and looks at the gap behind my teeth where the three went, and I let him. I do not surge. The surge is a boy's strength and it ran out on the stones, and I will not spend on him the little that has come back. And his face does the thing the leader's did, the girl's did, the seer's did — the moves-in-it that is not the hunting-want, the *you are something,* the lip already shut, the body that should be fevered in this tar-dark and is clear. He has no word for it. He has only what he reads: *this should be dead and is not, and a thing that should be dead and is not is worth the long water and the iron.*

Then he makes the sound. Low, behind his teeth, the felt-shape of the closing hand turned to noise. The brand. The hold-word. *Mine. Be still. Come. You are a thing.* He lays it on me the way they lay the hand, sure it wraps and holds, here in the dark where it was born, where it held me once whole and small when I had no name to put against it.

It does not wrap. It falls off the face that will not take it and lands behind my breastbone where I file the things to hand back, and it lies there beside the leader's brand, two of them now, kept small and hard against the one low. He does not see it not work. He sees the quiet and takes the quiet for breaking, the way they all take the quiet for breaking, the small loosening going through him, the breath let go — *the animal is done.* He takes the light back up the dark and drops the hatch and the black closes over me again, and he thinks he has left a broken thing in the hold.

He has not. He has left a counting thing in the hold.

---

The floor lifts and falls and the made thing takes the channel out toward the water-crossing, toward the iron, toward the pits across the brown and the brine, and I lie in the tar-dark that is the inside of the worst dream I own, and I am not in the dream. I am under it, in the still place, laying squared stones.

They have made me property again and they are wrong about all of it. They are wrong about the brand, that falls off and holds nothing. They are wrong about the quiet, that is mine and not theirs. They are wrong about the broken thing in the hold, that is not broken but banked. They think they are carrying an animal across the water — a wrong-blooded animal that does not die, worth the iron, but an animal, a thing the hold-word wraps and the hand makes still. They are carrying a boy with his mother's name woken whole behind his gone teeth, and a dead warmth set faced in the still place that will not pour away, and a count that does not stop, and a far mouth that has turned from a hole into a pull. They are carrying the thing the green could not eat. They walked into the death I survive to take me, and the death is in them now, the fever and the rot, riding their own bodies down the water while my body lies clear in their dark.

The green wore my name to an animal sound and the Kala woke it back, and the slavers took the body that carries it and cannot reach the name. The hold took my first word lows ago and the tribe gave me a voice, and the slavers can take the voice but they put me in the one place that taught me how to keep a thing where no hand reaches. They are giving me back, plank by plank and roll by roll, the exact dark that made me. I do not think they know what they are doing. I am beginning, low under the red, where the still place is, to.

The hatch is shut. The hand is somewhere above on the deck, resting, sure of its work. The water goes by warm and black against the hull the way it went by the first time, before the shore.

I count the rolls of the floor, and I lay the squared stones, and I wait for the hand to open.

It always opens.
