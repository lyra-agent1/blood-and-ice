# Chapter 15: The Long Hunt

You do not hunt the thing that eats you with the last of yourself. You hunt it with the second-to-last, and you keep the last back for the dying you do not mean to do.

That is the first law I lay down, and it is not a brave law. The brave ones go up against the Hesh strength to strength and are rolled and held and not given back. I am the smallest thing in the green. I do not go up against the Hesh. I go up against the *ground* the Hesh stands on, and I keep the last of myself banked low under the ribs the whole way, for the dying I will spend it on if the reading is wrong.

The shore gives once, and then it makes you go further. The high stones made the further a thing with teeth set for the long killing and high eyes that do not weigh. So I go further. I go down off the high clean shelf in the thick fever-light before the sun, into the steaming dark under the drowned giants, into the rot-thicket where the long body drags its certain weight, and I begin the hunt that has no blow in it, the reading-hunt, the only one the weak thing has.

---

For three lows I do not throw a thing or sharpen a thing. I only learn the Hesh, because I will not go up against the apex of my green knowing nothing and bleed for it the way I have bled for everything else.

I lie still in the warm soup at the channel-edge, downwind, a stone that does not move, the make-yourself-nothing the hands beat into me in the dark turned now to the watching. The Hesh does not find me. The high eyes ride the flat skull set to watch the water and the bank both at once, and they pass over the place where the nothing is, because a thing that has never met the thing it had to hurry from has never learned to look for the still small thing in the rot. That not-looking is my whole standing against it. I lie in the not-looking and I read.

I read where it lies up in the heat of the low, sunk to the high eyes in the still rot-pool. I read the firmer mat it drags its weight along, because even a thing that fears nothing chooses the ground that holds. And I read the one thing the body fixes on and will not let go: the Hesh is built for the water and the soft edge. It does not climb. It stopped where the firm ground began and lifted its head after the old male and did not follow, because the long heavy weight that makes it the strongest thing in the green is the one thing the high stone has no use for. The strength is the death of it on the rock, the way the old male's strength was the death of him in the rot.

A thing has a way it must go. Every strong thing in the green is roped to the one ground its strength can stand on, and the strength that ropes it there is the thing I will pull.

On the third low I find the place. There is a run the Hesh drags itself along between the rot-pool and the deep mouth of the thicket, a firm root-mat threading the drowned green, the one line of held ground through a soup that would take a heavy thing down. The Hesh goes the firm way because the firm way is the only way the long weight can go. And a body's-length off the run, the deep rot keeps its still hidden mouth — the dark vein of soup under the root and rot-leaf that took the old male to the chest, that reads weight and thrashing and pulls and does not give back.

The Hesh keeps off the mouth. It reads the ground the way I read the ground; it is the apex because nothing has ever taught it not to be. It will not set the long weight down where the ground is a lie.

So I will make the lie hold where the truth was, and the truth where the lie was, and move the line a foot-width over, the way the shore moves the law a notch further out and does not give back.

---

I do not have a word for the thing I am building. I have all the weather of it, and the weather is the noose.

The loop is for the neck of a thing that runs, and the Hesh does not run. But a noose is only the small shape of a larger law I learned on the shore with stones too heavy to lift: a weight over a high seat, a long pull, and the thing too heavy to lift comes up. I made the green the arm when I brought the old male out of the rot's mouth. I will make it the arm again — only this low the green will not pull the strong out of the killing ground. It will pull the killing ground out from under the strong.

I work it three lows more, in the heat when the Hesh lies sunk in its pool and the high eyes are down. I am out on the firm run alone, downwind, crawling beside the thing that names the bank its own, and the near mouth is silent in me the whole while — not with wanting, with the knowing of meat that has met the thing that eats it. I keep the name behind the gap where the three teeth are gone, biting down on it, the one un-stolen thing, the stone I bite when the body wants to bolt and the bolting would wake the high eyes.

I cut the long woody runners thick as my arm off the leaning giants and weave them across the deep mouth, a mat of vine laced root to root the way the rot-leaf laces it, and cover it with the held root I peel off the firm run, laid over the lie so the lie reads firm. And along the true firm run, the line the Hesh trusts, I work the held root loose at its under-veins where the soup waits below, leaving the green skin whole on top so the truth reads like the lie. I move the line a foot-width over. Where the ground held, I make it a mouth. Where the mouth was, I lay a green floor that will take a long weight one breath and then let it through.

It is slow work and wet work and it costs me. The leeches find me in the warm soup and fix and feed, and I peel them off, and the small wells they leave draw shut before I have crawled a body-length on, fast, too fast, closing in their own short time. There is a sting-thing in the rot-leaf I lay over the weave, a spined leaf that drives its fire up under the skin of my forearm, and the arm swells and goes hot and the fire climbs to the shoulder, and I lie still in the soup and wait for it to drop me the way it would drop the old male, and it does not drop me. The swelling goes down under my own eye. The poison goes out the way the bad water goes out, the way the rot in a cut goes out, fast and leaving no mark, and I am clear-headed in the fever-thick air that should bring the shaking-sickness and does not.

I do not look at the knowing straight. I put it on the back shelf with the rest — the wounds that draw shut in their own short time, the swarm that rings off my skin, the fever-fog I sleep in and wake whole from, the venom that should knot and kill me and slides off instead. The shelf goes everywhere I go. Tonight there is a new wrong thing on it, the sting that did not drop me, set up beside the old four. I do not take it down and turn it over. It does not come apart in the hands. I keep my eyes on the work.

And the whole while, low under the ribs, the second mouth is open. The far one does not forget. It was up on the high stone when the small one pressed into my chest to be put between it and the thing in the dark, and I was the keeping-place and had nothing to put. It is the engine of the work. I am not building a trap because I am strong enough to. I am building it because the far mouth was given a thing to keep and shown the thing it cannot keep it from, and there is a thing in me the green has not broken and the hold did not break and the hands did not find behind the gap where the three teeth are gone, and it will not lie still on the high stone and let the long certain weight take from the edges of the only home I have ever had.

---

A trap is only the half of it. The other half is the bringing. A mouth in the ground takes nothing that does not walk into it. I must make the Hesh come the line I have moved, and come it fast, in the rush of its own certain strength — because a thing reads the ground slow and certain, and forgets to read it in the rush.

So I must be the thing it rushes. The bait. The moving thing that wakes the high eyes set to watch the bank. There is a way that is not strength, and the weak thing has it.

The body does not love the deciding. It has lain in the soup struck silent under those high eyes and knows what the long body does to the thing it reaches. But the body has the deciding before the head can say it, older than fear and lower down: I will stand on the green floor I laid over the moved line, the moving warm small thing the apex has named meat, and make it rush — and at the last breath I will be light where the Hesh is heavy, off the floor before the floor knows a foot was there, a thing too light to pull. The Hesh, heavy, certain, in the rush, will set the long weight down where I stood, and the green skin will hold it one breath and let it through. I will make the apex's own strength the death of it, the way the rot made the old male's strength his death.

---

I do it in the thick light before the sun of the next low, when the Hesh comes off its pool to take, the high eyes up, the long body sliding its certain weight along the firm run toward the deep mouth of the thicket.

I show myself. I stand up out of the not-looking on the green floor over the mouth, the small warm moving thing where the high eyes can find it at last — and they find it. The broad flat head comes round, the high eyes fix, and the long body that has never once had cause to hurry hurries, because here is meat that moved on the bank the Hesh has named its own. It comes off the firm run and onto the line I moved, fast, the certain weight rushing, the rot-water sheeting off the ridged hide, the rows of teeth already wide — and I am light.

I go off the green floor the breath before the long weight comes down on it, foot finding the true held root at the floor's edge and gone off it before the root can know a foot was there. The Hesh cannot do this. Its weight is its whole strength, and on the lie I laid its whole strength is the thing that kills it. It sets the long heavy body down where I stood, in the rush, and the green skin holds it one breath — one — and then the deep rot reads the weight and the thrashing the way it read the old male, and pulls, and the firm ground that was a lie opens under the long body and lets it through into the still soup that does not give back what it swallows.

It goes down fighting, the way the strong always fight, the way that makes the wet take them deeper. The great long body beats the soup to froth, the rows of teeth snapping on the rot and on nothing, and the more the strength fights the more the rot reads it and pulls — a law strength makes worse. I take my turn of the laced vine to the leaning giant and run it over the high stub of the broken limb, and I do not pull it to lift the Hesh out. I pull it the other way, the long pull that holds the green skin closed hard over the mouth, so the soup keeps what it has read, so the long body cannot find the firm root to drag itself up, because every line of firm ground around the mouth I have worked to lie. I am the eye. The green is the arm. The rot is the mouth. I have made the place where the three meet, and stood the apex on it.

It is a long killing. The Hesh made the long killing its law, the teeth set for the take and the hold and the roll that does not give back, and the green gives it its own law back across the soup. I hold the weave, and the line does not break. The near mouth comes back at last out of its struck silence as the froth slows and the high eyes still and the long body goes to the deep stillness in the soup, the stillness of a thing that has weighed the stronger thing that holds it and stopped its own body. I know that stillness. I have been it, on a deck, in a hold, with the hand closed on the back of the neck. I feel it come off the apex of my green into me, and the second mouth stands all the way up over the long body going under, fed nothing, leaning at the strength gone still — and I make myself stop the leaning short, because I do not know what I become if I feed it.

---

I do not carry the whole of it. It is longer than the old male and me laid end to end, and the soup keeps the most of it, the way the rot keeps everything it swallows. But I take the broad flat head. I cut it free in the soup with the dead expedition's knife, the rows of teeth and the high eyes that never learned to weigh, and I sling it in the laced vine, and I begin the dragging-home, which costs more than the hunt the way the dragging always costs more than the going.

The climb up to the high clean shelf is the long killing run backward, the head dragging the vine raw across my shoulder, the splits the rock opens on my forearms welling warm and bright and drawing shut too fast as I go. I come up over the broken lip in the dark, shaking, the rot drying stiff and pale on me, and the tribe is crowded frightened on the high stone, the gladness gone out of it, the small ones pressed in close against the thing in the dark.

And I lay the head of the Hesh down on the clean stone in the middle of them.

The whole tribe reads it. The broad flat head, the rows of teeth, the high eyes that named the bank its own — laid down dead by the smallest, softest, wrongest thing among them, the one the green had the easiest claim on of all of them. The pressed-down fear that moved down the shelf low after low breaks open. The old male with the torn ear comes and stands over the dead head and over me and makes the sound a strong thing makes to the thing that stood between it and the squared dead, low, slow, the great head bowed. The half-grown ones circle the head and the small scarred thing that carried it. The bold small one rides my shoulder at last, the quick heart gone easy, the keeping-place become the thing that kept.

The Kala folds down beside me, the great gray-silver bulk, and lays the keeping arm over me, and makes the still-sound whole this low — *be still, you are held, nothing comes* — and the *nothing comes* does not die half-made in the great chest the way it died when the Hesh had come, because the thing that came has gone into the soup the small wrong thing she chose out of the fever-fog laid the green down on. I make the sound back, low and torn, scraping over the gap where the three teeth were gone, looking up.

Because the king is on his broken lip. Gull-Marr stands turned in now, not out into the dark where there is no longer a thing to turn toward — turned to the dead head of the apex, to the small scarred thing the tribe circles. The flat eyes do not warm. They go narrow, the weighing look, no letting-stand, the *could* and not the *is*. The strength-king met the apex and found his strength too short, and turned away, and called it the law. The weak thing went up into the dark and made the green the arm and brought the apex down. Every eye on the high stone has read which one of them did it.

---

I lie in the warm in the dark with the near and the far, the small one's quick heart and the Kala's slow vast tide, the dead head cooling on the clean stone, and I lay the new law down behind my teeth, in the gap where the name lies, beside the brand I will give back someday across the water, the hardest the high stones have taught me yet:

*The green has an apex, and the apex does not weigh and does not let stand and does not ask — and a thing too strong to be fought is not too strong to be read, and the law of the green will bend to the thing that reads the ground the strength stands on.*

The island's own law bends to me this low. Not because I am strong. Because I am light, and I read the ground, and I made the killing ground itself the thing that kills. The high stones are easy in the dark for the first time since the green went quiet, the fear gone off them, the tribe whole.

The shore gives once, and then it makes you go further. I have gone as far as the apex of my green and brought its head back up the rock, and there is no further left to go but the one. The high stone has only the one thing on it now stronger than the thing I killed, and it stands on the broken lip with the whole law of the strong behind it, the narrow weighing eyes turned in on me.

The seer comes across the crowded rock before the light, the ash-pale head slow, and folds down at the lip where I lie, and the pale finger touches the Eye-and-Thorn bound at my chest and makes the long meaning-sound, the *means* — and then lifts and turns, not out into the empty dark this low, but up, toward the broken lip where the king stands, and makes the sound again. *This one. And that one. And the two are one telling, and the telling is not done.*

I do not sleep. I lie in the warm with the second mouth full and the near one easy and the dead head cooling and the king weighing, and over all of it I lay the next going-up, the one the seer pointed at, and the body keeps it, the way the shore taught it to keep a thing either way it goes.
