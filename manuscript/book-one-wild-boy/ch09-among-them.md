# Chapter 9: Among Them

I learn the high stones the way I learned my green. With the soles first, and the fingers, and the teeth. The head comes after, the way it always comes after, dragging behind the body.

---

The first thing I learn is where I may put my weight.

There is a country up here that is not the country I see. The bare sun-bleached shelf looks all one thing to the eye, all heat and stone, but it is not one thing to the tribe. There is the high clean ledge where the sun strikes first, for the still ones, the laid-out dead, squared and faced, and no living foot goes up onto it but the keeper's. There is the wide lower shelf where the kept fire holds, banked and fed and never let die. And there is the broken lip where the king sits, and a clear bare stone all around him that the others leave bare, the way the swarm leaves a clean hand's-width bare around me. I learn that bare stone with the body before the head — I go to cross it on a mending low, and a great arm comes down across my chest, not hard, only sure, and turns me. I learn the bare stone is the king's the way I learned the rot-pool was death. By the body being stopped at the edge of it.

The king is Gull-Marr. I do not have the sound yet on that first low. I have only the shape of him sitting highest, heavy past all the others, the old silver come into the dark of his hide along the great ridged shoulders, and the eyes that go over me and do not warm and do not tip — the one head on the whole shelf that does not tip toward me the way the rest have started to. He looks at me the way I look at a thing on the shelf of wrong things. A thing that should not be, and is, and has not yet been turned over to see what is under it.

I file it. *The king has not chosen me.* The keeper has chosen me, and the old silver one has bowed to the mark at my chest — but the king has only looked, and looked away, and a thing the king has not chosen is a thing on borrowed stone. I have lived my whole life on borrowed stone. I know its feel under me. The ground you stand on that pours away the moment the hand that lent it opens.

---

The second thing I learn is that they have sounds for everything, and the sounds are a country too.

The keeper makes the gentling sound at me, low, up and down, no edges, and I had thought it was one sound. It is not one sound. I lie against her warm through the mending lows with my ear to the great slow heart, and I learn that the gentling has many shapes, and each shape is a different thing. There is the one she lays on me when the firelight is low — soft, falling, the tail of it trailing off, that means *be still, you are held, nothing comes.* There is a shorter one, two beats, hard at the front, that she throws when a small one wanders toward the lip of the rock, and it snaps the small one back the way a jerked line snaps a hooked thing, and that one means *no, back, the edge.* And there is one that rises at the tail, asking, that the big ones throw to each other across the shelf and answer, *where, here, where, here,* the line of sound that runs between them and says *you are still ours.*

I take them all in. I hold them behind the gone teeth, in the gap where three were torn, the warm hollow where I keep things. They stack up in the dark of me, and something turns over. Slow. The way the high water finally gets under a stone too big for the small tides and rolls it, and the wet underside comes up to the air pale and strange and crawling with the small blind things that lived in the dark of it. I had sounds once. There was a mouth that made sounds at me, before the black, low and soft and falling, and I made them back, and the making-back meant we were two and not alone. I lost it. But I have theirs now. I am stealing them one low at a time, off the keeper's tongue and the young one's and the small ones', building back a thing I lost the shape of, with another body's names.

---

The small ones teach me fastest, because the small ones are not afraid.

The big ones move around me still with the half-step of a thing drawing back from a thing it half-understands. But the small ones have no shelf of wrong things yet. They have not learned what should not be, so nothing about me is wrong to them. They climb me the way they climb the big ones, and make their sounds in my face, close, and wait — and I learn that this is how a sound is given. Close. In the face. The mouth shaping it slow so the eye reads the shape while the ear takes the sound.

One small one, dark and quick, the boldest, the one that looked at me with no fear at all on the first low, takes to me the way a thing takes to warm. It rides my chest in the firelight and points its small dark finger at a thing and makes a sound and waits, the way a thing waits when it is giving the world its names. The kept fire — a sound low in the throat, round at the back. The water caught in the rock-hollow — another, wet at the front. The high green, the bare stone, the dark coming down, each with its sound, the small one laying them on me one at a time, waiting to see if I will take them.

I never answer. The beast keeps my throat shut and I let it keep it; a torn sound out of me would turn the small face the wrong way and set the half-step to the full retreat. So I stay shut. But I am not empty. I am full to the back of the teeth with their sounds. And the small one does not need me to answer. It only needs me to take, and I take, and it points again.

This is a wrongness I cannot file with the others, because it is not a wrongness of the body. The body's wrong things sit easy on the shelf — the fever that walks through me and leaves me clear-headed, the swarm that spares me, the wound that draws shut clean and fast where every other body's goes to rot in the wet. Those I do not turn over. But this — a small dark thing laying the world's names on me, free, asking nothing back — this has no shelf. The second mouth takes it instead. The far one, the one no meat answers, the one that went still at last against the keeper's warm. The names go down into it and do not fall straight through. Not enough. Never enough. But the empty closes a little around them, the way a hand closes on a thing it means to keep and not crush, and I lie under the small one's pointing finger and feel it be new.

---

The keeper has a sound I learn before all the others, because it is the one the tribe lays on her.

It comes out of the big ones' mouths when they call to her across the shelf, when a small one needs the keeping. Two falls. Soft, then softer, the second lower than the first, dropping away under it. *Ka-la.* They throw it up at her on the high clean ledge where she tends the still ones, and she lifts the great head and answers, and comes. I hear it thrown low after low until the shape of it is worn into me, and I learn what it points at: her. The one who keeps. The one who carries the small things against the killing ground and scoops them up off the rock and folds them in.

The Kala.

I take it the way I take her warm. It is not a name the way the name behind my teeth was a name — given by one mouth in the dark, mine and no one's else. This is a name the many made and the many throw. But it is hers. It points at her and at nothing else in all the high stones, and when I have it whole behind my teeth, lying down in the warm hollow beside the brand, I find I have stopped taking the keeper as *the warm.* I have started taking her as the Kala. The sound has gone into the second mouth and come out the far side as a thing that means *her,* and I did not decide it, the way I did not decide to be held.

---

There is one low the king comes down off his bare stone.

He does not come to me. He comes to the fire, the way he comes most darks, heavy and slow, the clear bare stone moving with him like the swarm's clean line moves with me. But this low his eyes are on me as he comes, and do not leave me, and the gentling goes out of the shelf — the small ones feel it before I do, the bold one slipping off my chest and away between the big legs, and the keeper goes still beside me, her great arm tightening over me, not hard, only sure.

Gull-Marr stops at the edge of his bare stone and looks at me across it, and he makes a sound.

It is not the gentling. It is not any sound the small ones have given me. It is low and flat, and it has a thing in it I know in the body before the head can find it — careful, laid down with teeth in it. It means *what is this. Why is this on my stone.* And the keeper answers. She lifts her head off me and throws a sound back across the bare stone at the king — soft, and long, and with the keeping in it, the shape she lays on the small ones, laid now on me in front of him. *Mine. This is a kept thing. I keep it.* And the old silver one, the seer, the one who bowed to the mark, comes slow into the firelight on the king's far side and makes its own long sound, and lifts one finger and points it, not at me, at the disc bound at my chest — the eye, ringed, looking, and the thorn driven through the looking — and the king's flat eyes go to the mark, and hold there.

I do not have the words. I have all of the weather. The king does not want me on his stone. The Kala says I am hers to keep. The seer says I am marked — the eye-and-thorn means a thing the old blood carries, I am not only a wrong thing but a *known* one. And the king looks from the mark to my face and back, and he does not choose me, and he does not unmake me. He lets the borrowed stone stay lent. Kept by one. Marked to another. Borne by the king on sufferance.

The far mouth widens at it anyway — because three of them are speaking *about me,* and a thing spoken about is a thing that *is,* and I have not been a thing in any tribe's mouth since the hold made me a thing in the worst one. I file the danger of the king on the shelf where his bare stone is already filed. And I let the far mouth have the rest.

---

The word comes on a dark when I am not reaching for it.

That is the thing about it, after, when I turn it over and cannot wear it smooth. Every low I went in behind the teeth, into the gap where three were torn, reaching to find a thing to send back into the warm — and every low the hollow was warm and empty, my own sound gone down into the deep with everything from before, only the brand left in it, the ugly one, the hold-word that closed like a hand. I reached and reached and found nothing of mine. And so this dark I do not reach. I only lie against the Kala in the low firelight with her slow vast heart going through my ear and the bold small one come back to ride my chest, and the keeper makes her still-sound over me, soft, falling, *be still, you are held* — and the small one on my chest makes a sound back at her, a clumsy shape of the still-sound, learning it the way I am learning it, throwing it up at the warm —

and I make it too.

I do not decide it. There is no place in me where the deciding happens. The thing rises the way the heat rises off the firestones into the wet dark, up through the chest, up to the gate of the throat where the beast keeps its hand — and the beast grabs for it the way it grabs to throw me flat, and the beast misses. It closes on nothing. The sound goes past the place where the throat shuts under a hand. It gets out.

I make the still-sound. Low, up and down, no edges. Back at the Kala.

It is hoarse. Cracked, half a shape, scraping over the gap where three teeth are torn — a sound that has not been a sound in this mouth since the hold closed on my neck, since before the black, since before the deck and the closing hand. The throat does not know how to do it anymore. It has been a throat for breathing and for the low warning-rasp and nothing else so long that the sound tears coming out, costs skin, drags raw over the place the silence has worked. But it is the right shape, near enough — the keeper's still-sound, the soft falling one — and it lands on her.

I feel it land. The great head comes up off the stone. The slow heart against my ear does not change, but the breath does, the vast tide of it catching once, and the eyes that went over me on the first low and did not warm come down to my face, close, and read me slow, taking the whole shape of the thing before going down into it. I have made her sound back at her. I have laid the gentling on the one who gentles, lifted it off the rock and handed it back to her in a torn and living voice, and there is no part of her left that takes me only as the burning man-shape carried up to die.

She makes the still-sound again. Soft, then softer. And waits.

And I make it back. Better the second time, the throat learning it on the way out, the tear not so deep. And I understand, in the making, what I have done. I have not found my own sound. The hollow is still empty of the one that is mine. But I have found that the throat still works — that a sound can still go out of me toward a warm and land on it and not close. That I am not only the place where a sound was torn out. I am still a place a sound can be made.

---

My breath goes out with the sound, thick with the fire-smoke and the rot-damp of the high stones, and it carries the torn shape of the gentling out into the firelight and shows nothing else. No wrongness in it. There is only the wrong that is already known — the fever shrugged off, the swarm's clean line, the wound that closes fast — and the Kala has read all of those already, carried me through the worst of them, peeled the rot off me with her arm. She does not draw back from the new sound the way the small ones draw back from a thing that should not be. She takes it the way she took me. She makes room for it against her warm.

The seer hears it.

It is across the fire, low and folded into itself, the milk-pale eyes half shut — and at the torn sound out of me they open, and come around, and find me. And the old silver one makes its own long sound, the one with the thing in it turned toward, turned up, the one it made over the mark at my chest. It looks from my mouth to the disc and back. A thing that should not make a sound is making a sound. A marked thing, a known wrong thing, the eye-and-thorn carried on it — and now a voice where no voice was. The seer reads it the way it read the mark, and bows the great head, slow, the firelight running red along the silver. I do not know what it reads. I file it — the mark, the bow, the long turned-up sound — on the shelf where the seer already is, and I do not turn it over, because the body has no head for it, only the deep slow shock of *known.*

---

I make their sounds back, after that, low after low. Not many. Not well. The throat is slow to remember a thing it forgot so deep, and the words come torn and half-shaped and cost skin each time. But they come. I make the still-sound at the Kala. I make the edge-sound at a small one wandering toward the lip, and it snaps back at the torn shape of my voice the way it snaps back from hers — and the doing of it, the throwing of a sound that *moves a thing,* that runs down the country and lands and is answered, opens a place in me I did not know was shut.

The bold small one learns my torn voice the way it learns the big ones'. It throws the rising asking-sound at me across the firelight, *where, where,* and I throw the answer back, hoarse, cracked, *here* — and it comes quick and crooked across the stone and climbs my chest, because the line of sound ran out from me and it followed the line home. I threw a sound, and a warm thing came up the line of it to me. Nothing in all my green ever came up a line of sound to me. The green never answered. The black water took the mouth that answered, and I have lived in a country that does not throw a sound back since.

The far mouth does not only widen now when a thing comes up the line to me. It holds. The way it held against the Kala's warm. I am making sounds, and the sounds are landing, and the landing is bringing things back up the line to me, and the second mouth holds, and holds, and for the first low in all my counting it is not the lights between I count.

---

I do not have my own sound yet. The gap where three were torn is still warm and still empty of the one that is mine, my mother's gift, the soft falling thing that meant *me* and not the thing a hand closes on. I reach for it once, this dark, with the still-sound fresh in my throat, sure for half a breath that the making of their sounds will have shaken mine loose — and the hollow is warm, and empty, and there is only the brand in it, the ugly one, the one that did not leave. I will not send that. I would lay nothing into the firelight before I laid the shape of a hand closing on a tribe that holds me without closing.

But their sounds are working at the warm place where mine went silent. I feel it the way I feel weather coming, bone-sure, before the sky shows it. One low — through this same warm, in these same torn mouthfuls of the tribe's own sounds — the empty hollow will give my own sound back, and I will open the gap where three teeth are torn and let it out into the high stones, and they will have it.

The Kala makes the still-sound over me. I make it back, low and torn, soft-then-softer, two falls. And the small one on my chest, half asleep now in the red light, makes it after us both — clumsy, the wrong shape, learning — and the three of us lie in the kept warm above the drowning green and throw the one soft sound back and forth between us, around and around, *be still, you are held, nothing comes,* and no part of it closes like a hand, and the far mouth holds it whole.
