# Chapter 21: The Sigil

The shape I carried out of the dead men's mud has a hunter now, and the hunter wears its brother on his chest.

---

She did not keep it. That is the first thing I learn, and I learn it the way I learn a turn in the weather, by the camp doing what it did not do before. The owned girl drew the Eye-and-Thorn on the wet air at my line and laid her careful sound on it, and she did not keep it under her own teeth the way I keep things. She carried it back up the spit in her bent mouth the way she carries every sound, across from the road of the owned to the road of the owning, and she laid it down at the owning-fire, and now the fire knows.

I read the knowing off the camp from above, three lows lying in the high drowned roots over the bone-spit, reading the upright pack the way I read a herd that has caught a scent. Before, the camp bunched at its work — the hauling, the cutting, the endless setting-down and taking-up of things, the cages dragged and lashed and the green hacked back off the old high-water flat. Now a thing runs through it that did not run before. It goes from mouth to mouth, low, the way fear goes through a herd before the herd knows what it fears, a turning of heads, a leaning-in, a sound passed and a sound passed back. They have a thing to chew that is not meat. I do not have the sounds of it. But I have the shape of it in the way they move, and the shape is this: there is a thing in the green that wears the keeper's mark, and the keeper wants to know.

The far mouth pulls tight and hard under my breastbone, and beside it the man-shaped hole, the one I learned new at these fires, leans down toward the warm — and I lie still in the roots between the two of them and I read the camp leaning back. Up the spit at me. The thing they chew is mine.

---

I should have killed her, or left her. The law said it and I knew the law and I did the third thing, the thing that was not the law, and now the third thing has a face turning up the spit toward my green.

But the body will not lay it that way. The body lays it the way it lays everything, plain, in the gut first: I let her see the green move, and she did not run, and she gave me back the shape torn out of me, and I would do the same again. The far mouth would make me do it again. I file the knowing low on the shelf — that the want of the second mouth has opened a door in my own wall and walked the brand a step closer through it — and I do not turn it over. I keep my eye on the fire.

---

The captain reads the mark himself. I watch him do it.

She is brought to him. Not roped — they do not rope her; she is the one they keep close, owned and worth keeping — but two of the owning walk her to the owning-fire with their hands near her the way a hand stays near a thing that might bolt, and she goes bent, shoulders up around her ears, the fold a body makes around a hurt to keep it from spilling. And the captain stands and the camp makes a path for him the way the green makes a path for the Hesh, and he comes to her slow, with the still on him, the coming-on of a thing that does not have to hurry to what it means to take. I have stood under that still on a deck. The back of my neck crawls under it now from three lows off, the old crawl, the skin trying to get away from a hand that is not on it.

He says a thing to her. The edge of the sound comes up the spit to me on the down-wind, flat and low, sounds set against sounds — and she takes it and turns it in her mouth and lays it back changed, and her hand comes up off her bent side, slow, and draws on the air between them.

She draws the shape. The eye, and the thorn crossing it. The same shape she drew at my line in the rain. I see her draw it for him the way she drew it for the dark, and the far mouth twists, because the shape that was a thing between two of us alone at the two ends of the tide-line is a thing now between her and the keeper, given over, gone up the road of the owning.

And the captain goes still in a new way.

I know that stillness. It is the stillness the seer went into with its old ash-dark finger laid to the blood in my throat, the stillness of a thing turning toward a thing it did not look for and now half-sees. It is the stillness the Kala went into the low she half-saw the thinking in me. The captain hears the shape of his own mark drawn on the air by his owned girl, drawn off a thing she found in the dark of the green that eats his men, and the slow certain weighing-look comes up in him and fixes. Not on her. Past her. Out, up the spit, into the wall of the dark where the green begins.

Into the place where I am.

His eyes go over the high drowned roots where I lie poured into the mud, and slide off me the way every eye slides off me — but they go slow now, where before they passed quick. He is reading the green the way I read the green. Looking for the wrongness in the still, the thing that moves when nothing should move it. He does not find me. The wind holds true and gives him nothing and I do not move a frond, not this low, not for him. But I have learned the shape of him whole the way I learned the spit and the seep: the mark on his iron has a brother out in the dark, and the brother is mine, and the keeper has just learned that a thing he does not own is wearing the thing that says *owned* — and a man made of the slow sure coming-on does not let that lie. He files it the way I file, low, sure, and keeps it.

We are reading each other across the spit now, the keeper and the kept that got away, and only I know we are both doing it.

---

She comes back at the bare of the low.

I did not think she would. The owners kept her in after the relic-low, the big one's hand lifting over her bent neck and not falling, and I thought the camp would close her up now that she had carried the shape to the fire and the fire had caught. But she slips the thornwood at the same hour as ever, when the owning-fire is heavy with grease and the owned line is let down off the bundles, and she comes down the slick black mud, slow, picking her way, toward the dark wall of mine. And she is wrong. She comes wrong. The shape of her on the spit is not the easy talking-to-the-dark shape. It is the strung shape, the hunted-going-still shape, the whole rope of her pulled tight — and she keeps looking back over her bent shoulder at the camp, twice, three times, the way a thing looks back that knows it is watched.

She is watched. I read it before she is halfway down. There is a shape come out past the thornwood behind her, low, in the gray, not coming down the spit but standing at the wall of the camp where the cut stumps are, still, with the captain's still on it — a smaller one, sent, the way a captain sends a hand to do the watching his own eyes will not do. They have let her come. They have let her come to see where she goes, and what she goes to, and whether the thing in the green will move for her the way the shape drawn on the air says it might.

She is the bait now. She does not know it, or she knows it and comes anyway, bent and strung and looking back — the owned girl walked down the killing spit a second time, but this low she is the hook and not the hand on it, the soft live thing set out on the line to draw the dark up where the keeper's watcher can cut it out of the green.

I lie in the roots and the beast lies flat in me and reads the watcher at the stumps, and the law comes up plain and hard behind my teeth: *do not move the green.* Do not lean. Do not give the watcher the wrongness in the still. The far mouth wants her, wants the careful falling sound that does not close, wants the shape she gave back — and I hold the want down by force, my pads dug into the mud, my throat shut by the beast, because the want is the door the brand walks through and the brand has a watcher at the stumps this low with the captain's eye in it.

---

She stops at my line.

She does not draw on the air. She does not name the weed or the snail or the going-out of the water. She crouches in the streaming gray at the edge of where the dry mud goes to wet, two of my body-lengths off, near enough that the down-wind brings me the rain off her skin and the thin sour under-smell of a body that has been hungry — and she makes a new sound. Low. Quick. Aimed at the dark where the frond moved before, where she thinks the thing lives that cannot answer.

It is not the talking-easy sound. It is not the careful naming-sound with the teeth in it. It falls fast and it does not close — it is hers, the open-ended one, the one that lands in my chest and does not grab — but it is shaped wrong, shaped like the camp-sounds, the sharp careful ones she lays down like stones across bad mud, and it comes fast, too fast, a thing pushed out under a held breath. And she does the thing then that turns the whole low over. While she makes the fast low sound at the dark, her carrying-hand, the one with the rope-mark worn above it, stays down low against the mud where the watcher at the stumps cannot see it — and the fingers move. Small. They draw, low against the wet, where only the dark can see: the eye, and the thorn crossing it. And then they break. They scatter, the fingers, a thing flying apart. And then they point — quick, low, back over her bent shoulder, at the camp, at the watcher, at the fires — and close. A hand closing.

She is telling the dark a thing.

I do not have her sounds. I have stolen the weed and the snail and the two leaning water-sounds and the careful camp-sounds, and they are not enough to take the fast low push of this. But the hand I read. The hand is the body's road, the road under the easy road, the one I have read all my life when the mouth-road was shut to me — and the hand says the shape, and the hand says the shape flies apart, and the hand says the camp, the closing. She is bent in the rain telling the dark that the mark has set the camp moving. That the keeper wants the thing that wears it. That the closing hand is coming for the shape she drew, and she drew it, and she has carried that knowing down the killing spit under a watcher's eye to lay it at the wall of the green like a thing she could not keep behind her own teeth.

She does not know what she warns. She thinks she warns a beast, a haunt, the green itself. But the far mouth knows. It takes the hand-road in and reads it whole, the eye and the flying-apart and the closing, and under the warning the second mouth fills a little more, the empty closing around it the way a hand closes on a thing it means to keep — because no thing has ever come down onto the killing ground under a watcher's eye to tell me the hand was coming. Not since the Kala. The owned girl is not the home; she is the mirror, the owned thing on the killing ground; I lay it down again and I will not lie. But the mirror has just turned its bent face to the dark and warned me of the keeper, and the far mouth does not know mirror from home, it only knows the warning, and it lies down around it and holds it.

---

The watcher calls her in.

A sound comes from the stumps, short and edged, the closing-sound, the brand-shape laid on the air — *come* — and her head comes up and her body folds and she goes. She does not look at the dark again. She rises out of the crouch and turns up the spit and walks back into the camp bent, owned, the warning given and carried and spent, and the watcher falls in behind her the way a hand falls in behind a thing it drives, and the thornwood takes them both.

And I lie in the streaming roots with the brand-shape ringing in my ear, *come*, the low ugly sound that means *mine, be still, you are a thing*, and behind my teeth I hold the three things I held before and a fourth one new. The name that is mine and answers nothing. The brand that is theirs and means *you are a thing*. The road she lays me one stone at a low. And now the fourth: the eye flying apart, the camp, the closing hand. The warning. I lay it down beside the rest in the warm hollow where the lost name lies, and the warm comes up slow around all of it together, and I understand, the body first and the head long after, what the low has made plain.

The shape I carried out of the dead men's mud means *owned* to the men. The seer read it on my blood as kin-omen, a thing of the kept, a thing that comes from before — and the keeper reads it on my chest as a thing of his, a stray gone loose in the green, wearing his mark and not owned by him. The same shape, two readings, and both of them reach for me. The seer reached with an old ash-dark finger laid light to the blood and bowed its head and named me *kept*. The keeper reaches with the still face and the slow weighing-look and the hooked iron, and what he will name me is *cargo*. A thing worth the taking. A thing worth carrying off across the water in the dark of a hold, the way I was carried once, the way the lashed Mure goes dull in its cage this low.

I do not have the head for why the mark is mine and his both. I set it on the shelf, the deepest thing on it now, under the four and the five and the fever I do not feel and the gap where my name went — and I do not turn it over. But I have the body of it, and the body of it is this: I have spent my whole life a thing the world keeps reaching for, the hand closing on the back of the neck, the brand, the hold — and I went into the green and the green could not eat me and I made myself a king of the wild things, and I thought the highest stone was the safe stone. And it is not. The mark on my chest is a hand reaching up out of the before, out of the dead men's mud, out of the captain's iron, and it has found me again at the top of my green, and it is closing.

---

I lie above the camp until the gray dies and the fires come up red on the cut mud.

I watch the captain stand among them with the Eye-and-Thorn on his breast, still, fed, the keeper of the warm in the killing green. I watch the owned girl at the owning-fire, kept close, fed from the owning-pot, the shape she drew gone up into the keeper's reading now and out of her hands. I watch the watcher set by the thornwood where the spit comes in, new this low — a body posted to read the dark for the thing that wears the mark. They are looking out for me now. The camp leans up the spit at the green, where before it leaned only at its work. I drew its eye without meaning to, the way the highest stone draws the next taking, and the drawing is done and will not come undone.

I should take the tribe and go deep, up into the high broken stone and the fever-fog where men die and I do not, where no boat reaches and no brand burns, and never lay my shape where the keeper's weighing-look can cut it out of the green. The near part of me says it, the legs say it, the law of the green says it: a thing the keeper wants is a thing that is already half in the hold.

I do not go. I have not gone yet and I will not go yet, and the body knows why before the head can shape it. Because the man-shaped hole is down there at the fires leaning, full of the easy road I lost. Because the mark is mine and his both and I have to know whose it was and why it lives in me. And because there is one down there at the owning-fire who came down the killing spit under a watcher's eye to tell the dark the hand was coming — the owned girl, the mirror, the one who knows the shape and does not know who wears it — and the far mouth has lain down around her warning and will not get up off it.

I go back into my green in the full dark, up toward the high stones, toward the Kala and the bold heart and the home with no between in it. And the whole way up the rot I carry the new knowing behind my teeth, laid against the name and the brand and the road, in the warm where the lost things lie: that the mark I cannot read has made me a thing worth the taking, and the keeper has read it, and the camp has caught it, and the hand is coming up the spit out of the before — and that the one who set it moving and the one who warned me of it are the same bent owned girl.

The shore gives once, and then it makes you go further. I went all the way up. And the taking has read my mark off the air at last, and it knows there is something in the green worth the iron, and it does not yet know what — and neither do I — and the not-knowing on both sides is the only thing between me and the hold.

I do not know yet that the keeper will not wait to learn it. That a thing he cannot read but knows is worth the taking is a thing he takes first and reads after. I only know, lying down at last in the warm of the Kala's vast slow arm with the eye looking out from my chest into the dark, that I have been seen. Not the way the seer saw, with the bowed head and the meaning-sound. The other way. The weighing way. The way a hand reads a neck before it closes.

I have been seen, and I am wearing the mark, and the mark says *mine*.
