# Chapter 28: The Pit

The water-crossing ends the way the down road ended: with the hand on the back of my neck, and a hole opening to take me, and the body knowing the hole before the head can put a shape to it.

I count the crossing in rolls, not in lows, because there is no light in the hold to cut the dark into days. The floor lifts and falls and I lay my breath under it and I keep the count, and somewhere in the long count the water under the hull changes its voice — goes from the warm channel-brown to a deeper grind, the open brine, the long swells that take the made thing and shake it the way a brute shakes a small thing in its jaws. Two of the dying ones go quiet up in the light. I hear the quiet come. The cough that rattled them low after low stops between one roll and the next, and the slavers' feet change their weights above me, the dragging gait carrying something over the side, and the splash, and then fewer feet. The green is still in them. It rode their bodies onto the water and it does not stop killing them just because they have left the shore that grew it. I file it where the still place is. *The death is in them and it does not let go.*

Then the brine-voice shallows again. Another channel. Another drowned green, smaller, sourer, the tar-stink of more made things laid close on warm wet air, and over it, riding the planks into the hold, a sound I have not heard since before the shore — the sound of many human throats together. Not naming. Not the girl's slow careful laying of word on the dark. A massed sound, low and constant, the way the surf is constant, the way a swarm is constant: men, more men than I have ever smelled or heard at once, packed close on a far shore, living their lives in the open the way no man lives long on my green.

We have come to where the humans keep their own dark.

---

They take me up out of the hold and the light comes down on me like a blow.

I have been in the tar-dark so long that the day goes into the eyes like a spear, white and total, and I hang in the nets blind while the body does what the body does — reads the place through the other doors while the eye is shut. The smell first. Smoke, but not green-smoke; the flat reek of many small fires fed on cut wood and dung and the grease of cooked meat. Iron, everywhere, the flat metal tang of it laid over everything the way the rot-stink laid over my green. Dung and piss and the sweet-sick of bodies kept too close too long without the rain to wash them. And under it, faint, threading through — the high green rot of caged things. Brutes. Tide-kin. Carrion-kin. The catch of other boats. The green of my whole island, harvested and hauled across the water and held here in the human dark, dying slow in iron the way the dying ones died in the hold.

The eye comes back and I wish it had not.

It is a place of pits. That is the first true thing the eye gives me. The ground is cut open in rings, deep, the walls of them sheer and slick, and around each cut the men stand massed and shouting, and down in the cuts things move that I cannot see from the nets but can read off the men's mouths — the same mouth-shape on all of them, the wide hungry stretch of throats that have come to watch a thing be killed and want it slow. I knew that mouth on the leader's men out wide on the high ground. Here it is on every face, low after low of faces, a whole shore of men whose far mouths have learned to feed on the bottom of a pit.

They have built my whole life out of holes that have me. The hold was a hole. The green was a hole I learned to call mine and could not keep. Now this. They take what they own and they put it in a cut in the ground and they stand around the rim and shout while it dies. The body knows it before the head: I am for the pit. The wrong-blooded thing that does not die of the green — they have carried it across the water to find out, in the one place built to find out, how it dies of men.

---

There is a stir at the rim, and the leader comes.

I know his gait before I see the furrow. The drag is worse — the rot I smelled wake in his torn face has gone down into him, the long open from eye to jaw gone tight and shining and sweet, the green riding him too, even him, the patient one. He is dying of my island and he does not stop. He comes to the edge of the nets and crouches and turns my chin up into the white light with his hard fingers, the last time, and he looks at the gap behind my teeth where the three went, and his face does the thing all their faces do — the moves-in-it, the *you are something*, the reading of the wrong-blood he cannot name. He has carried me across the water on that face. He has paid a furrow full of rot for it. And now he is handing me to other men, men with more iron and deeper pits, and I read in the set of him that he is selling the reading along with the body. *This one does not die. Watch. Watch it not die.*

He makes the sound. Low, behind his ruined teeth, the felt-shape of the closing hand turned to noise. The brand. The hold-word. He lays it on me one last time, and it falls off the face that will not take it the way it always falls, and lands behind my breastbone where I file the things to hand back, and lies there small and hard, a third one now, three brands ground down and kept against the one low.

Then they cut the nets off me, and the hands come, many hands, and they walk me to the rim of a pit on legs that are mine but folded, and the white light goes all the way down the cut to a floor I cannot see, and the massed throats around the rim open wider, and they put the hand on the back of my neck.

And they let go.

---

The fall is not long. The body counts it anyway.

I come down the way the soles taught me to come down off a stone, loose, rolling the blow out through the shoulder and the hip, and the floor of the pit is packed earth gone dark and slick, and the smell that comes up off it is the smell that lived under everything else in this place: old blood. Low after low of it, soaked down into the dirt, gone black and sweet and deep, the floor of a hole where things are brought to be killed and have been killed past counting. I am on my hands in it. I am breathing it. My split lip, the one that drew shut clean in the hold, opens again against the packed earth where the fall splits it, and the salt-iron of my own floods my mouth and mixes with the old black blood of the floor and I cannot tell, for one roll of the world, which is mine and which is the pit's.

Above me the throats roar. The rim is a ring of dark heads against the white sky, fists going up, the want pouring down the slick walls the way the rot-vapor poured down off the high stones at dusk. They have come to watch. I am the watched thing on the floor of the hole, the small wrong-blooded thing, and they want to see the way it goes.

There is something else in the pit with me.

I read it before I see it — the shift of weight at the far wall, the low cut-breath of a big thing kept hungry, the green-rot of it gone wild and close. They have put a thing in the pit to do to me what the green could not. A brute, maybe, taken off some shore, starved down to nothing but the want. I do not turn to it yet. I am still on my hands in the old blood. I am still finding the count. And it is here, on the floor of the deepest hole, with my own blood and the pit's blood mixed in my mouth and a hungry thing rising at the far wall, that the wrong thing happens.

---

It is the heat.

It starts behind the breastbone where the far mouth is, where the second mouth has howled silent and empty since the iron went into the Kala on the stone. But it is not the far mouth. The far mouth is a pull, a want, a hole. This is the other thing. This is warm, and it is rising, and it is rising out of the place the blood is.

The body has always kept its small wrong secret. The neck warm under the hand where it should run other than warm. The lip drawing shut while the slavers' wounds went to rot. The fever-air sliding off me, the venom going out fast and leaving no mark, the swarms ringing me and not landing — all of it filed on the shelf of wrong things, set there and not turned over, never named. This is on the shelf too. But it will not stay set. The blood in my mouth, mine and the pit's together, lights something, the way a coal banked under ash lights when the air gets to it, and the warmth comes up out of the breastbone and goes down the arms and into the hands pressed in the old black blood of the floor, and it is not the red rage. I know the red rage. The red rage is teeth and want and the boy gone thin. This is lower than the rage. This is under the rage the way the still place is under the second mouth. It is warm, and it is sure, and it is mine, and it has been waiting.

A pulse comes up that is not my heart.

I feel it the way I once felt the green give a thing back to my soles in the dark — the warm of a rot-hollow rising into me where every other body went to fever, the heat the killing air laid on me that should have laid me down and never did — except there is no green now, there is no hollow, there is only me, and the heat is coming out of me into the cut earth and the spilled blood and coming back changed, answered, the way a sound comes back off a near wall. Something in the blood on the floor and the blood in my mouth has reached down into the place I never turn over and found a thing there that is awake. It does not speak. It has no words, the way I had no words, the way the far mouth has no words. It is older than words. It is a feeling like recognition, like a hand laid over the back of a young thing's head — *kala*, the keeping sound, the one that woke my name — except no one lays it. It rises from inside, out of the blood, out of me, *for* me. The first thing in all the lows since the iron took her that has reached the far mouth from the inside.

I kneel in the old blood with the warmth going down my arms, and the hungry thing comes off the far wall at me, and I am not afraid of it.

I do not have a name for what this is. I file it on the shelf with the rest, the deepest one yet, the one I will not turn over: that down here, at the bottom of the hole, in the blood, something woke that did not wake on the shore or in the green or under the Kala's hand. The pit reached for the thing that should be dead and is not, and the thing that should be dead and is not reached back.

---

I rise.

The warmth is in the hands now, in the teeth, behind the eyes, low and even and sure, and the hungry thing checks its rush a half-stride because some animal in it reads what some animal in me has become, the way a beast reads another beast across the dull water and slows. I do not slow. The boy's strength ran out on the stones, ran to mud, and a boy's strength does not come back in a night — but this is not the boy's strength. This came up out of the blood and it does not run to mud. It pours into the empty places the boy left and fills them warm.

I take the thing the way I took the apex on my own ground, lows ago, when I learned I was not the strongest thing on the island and won anyway. Not with strength. With the still place, with the count, with the wrong-blood that does not break when it should. It comes and I am already moving low under it, into the dirt and the old blood, reading its weight, and my hands close, and the warmth goes down them into the work, and above me the massed throats change their sound. The roar that wanted me dead turns. It pitches up, ragged, the way a sound pitches when men see the thing they came to watch do the thing they did not come to see. The wrong-blooded thing on the floor of the hole is not dying the way they paid to watch it die.

I do not look up at them. You do not spend the eye on ground that is already gone, and the rim is gone to me; I will not climb it this night. I spend the eye on the floor the foot is in. The floor the foot is in is dark with old blood, soaked deep with every caught thing they ever brought here to kill, and I am standing in it, and it is in my mouth, and the warmth in my blood has found the warmth in theirs and made of it a thing I do not understand and do not let go.

---

They will haul me out when it is done. I read that already, the way I read the trap on the high ground: they will not waste the wrong-blooded thing on one pit. They have a use that bleeds and walks back clean, the same use the leader read, the same use the girl read, the same use the seer read off my blood with the old signs. They will keep me. They will put me down in a cage of split hard-cane or worse and they will bring me back to the rim again and again to find out the thing they carried me across the water to find out, and every time they will lay the hand on the back of my neck, and every time they will make the sound, sure it wraps and holds.

It does not wrap. It never wrapped. They are wrong about the brand, that falls off and lands behind my breastbone with the other three, kept against the one low. They are wrong about the quiet, that is mine and not theirs. They are wrong about the broken thing they think they own.

They are wrong, most of all, about what woke in the pit.

I do not know what it is. I will not turn it over. I set it on the shelf with the warm neck and the shut wound and the fever sliding off, the deepest wrong thing, the one that reached back. But I know what it is *for*, the way the body knows a thing before the head. I kneel and rise in the old black blood with the count going and the warmth going and the far mouth pulling low and even toward the low I cannot see yet, and I lay the squared stones in the still place, faced and even, the way the tribe laid its dead on the high stones. I lay them down.

The name my mother gave me, woken and whole behind the gap where the three went, where no hand reaches. *Rook.* Mine.

The three brands, ground small, kept to hand back across the water to the mouths they belong in.

The road the girl laid, unfinished, the gathering one, still being named in some dark above me by a throat they have not stopped.

And her. The great dark eye going out turned to me with the gentling unbroken, the price, set faced in the still place where it will not pour away. The Kala. The one who carried me. The one who woke the name. The warm that knew it held me.

I do not make it a grief and lie down under it. I make it the weight on the line that draws the want tight. The far mouth turned from a hole into a pull in the hold, and here, in the pit, with the new warmth rising out of the blood to meet it, the pull goes from a thing I feel to a thing I aim.

They took the family and they will pay it back.

They took the crown I bled into being out of the killing green a low at a time, and they will pay it back.

They put the hand on the back of the neck and made me a thing, twice, and one low — I do not know how and I do not know when and I do not need to — the hand will open. The hand always opens. It opened once on the moving floor and dropped me into the warm black water and the water carried me to the shore and the shore made me. It will open again. And the thing it opens on will not be the small wrong boy it closed on.

I rise in the blood of the pit, and the warmth comes up to meet the want, and the massed throats roar and do not know what they are roaring at. I count the rim. I count the caught. I count the squared stones in the still place. And under all of it, lower than the red, lower than the second mouth, in the place that has no name and woke tonight, the new warmth pulses once, slow and sure, like a thing turning over in its long sleep and beginning, at last, to wake.

Let the hand stay closed a while yet.

I am still counting.

And now I am not the only thing in here counting back.
