# Chapter 18: Smoke on the Water

There are things on the water that sit it wrong.

---

I am up on the seaward stack in the green half-light of the low, where my green runs out of green and goes to bare wet rock and the channel opens past the last of the mangrove into the wide flat water and the haze. I come up here before a hunt to read the tide — the set of the swell, the dark roads under it where the fat river-pig runs the shallows, the mud-banks the low bares at their hour. The water lies past the pinch the way it always lies, hot and pale and old under the standing haze. And far out on it, where the far reach stands low and drowned-green in the steam, there are shapes.

Long dark shapes that hold their line against the swell.

I have read this water more lows than I have counted. I know how a thing sits it. The tide-beast rolls with the swell, gives to it, is the swell, a dark back rising and going under and the water none the worse. A drift-log does not roll but it has no will in it, it goes where the brown flood-water shoves it, dumb, a thing pushed by things. These do neither. They sit up on the water with their will in them and they cut it. The swell lifts them and they hold through the lift and come out of it still pointed where they were pointed. A thing that holds its line against the sea has a head, and a head chooses.

The word comes up out of me. Up from far down, up from under all the green I have packed over everything that was before the green, and it comes up whole.

*Boats.*

The whole skin of me draws tight at once, the small hairs lifting along the arm, the gut clenching to a fist. The body knows the word before the head can turn it. The body wore the word.

On the boats — small dark uprights. They move. Not the dumb sway of a thing the sea moves. They bend and pull and bend, all together, the same shape doing the same work down both sides at once. I have watched the channel pull a thing without the name for what pulled it. Now I have the name. Hands at oars. Many of them, bending and hauling the dark boats across the pale flat water toward — no. Not toward. The long low reach where the dead ones died. Toward the spit of drowned-green where, lows past counting ago, I found the broken ship in the mud and the bones the fevers laid down and the relic with the Eye-and-Thorn bound on it that hangs at my chest this low.

The iron floods my mouth though I have bitten nothing. My own blood, the taste of it, sudden and bright on the back of the tongue without a wound to give it — the body opening some old seam in me to lay the taste of danger where I will not miss it. Blood without a cut. Fear-blood. I have not tasted it since the hold.

---

I am off the stack before the head knows the body chose to move.

Low. Belly to the wet rock. I go down off the high stone into the broken country I know in the dark of me, the rot-gullies and the root-cages and the mud that takes a foot to the knee if the foot does not know the seat of it. I know the seats. I go fast and silent through the place that is only mine, *my green*, and I do not stop at the crack worn to the shape of my back, and I do not go up to the high stones where the tribe sleeps easy under the new crown. I go to the lookout over the dead-ones' spit, the bone-spit, the place I have not walked since I dragged the relics off it, because the far mouth is already turned that way and I cannot not look.

My heart goes against the slab like a hunted thing in a fist. And it is not the boats my heart runs from. It is what comes up behind the word for them.

The brand. The dark of the hold, the wet wood sweating, the close stink of too many bodies packed where they could not stand. The big hand closing on the back of my neck from behind, slow, sure, owning the whole of me in the one grip — and the low ugly sound made behind the teeth that went with the hand every time, the sound that meant *come,* that meant *be still,* that meant *you are a thing and the thing is mine.* The hand and the sound were one. I feel it now as a weight on the back of the neck, the felt-shape of the grip, and the skin there crawls and tries to get away from a hand that is far out on the water and lows past counting gone into the deep.

That is what the shapes mean. That is what hands mean. That is what *many-of-them, together, pointed* means.

I lie flat on the bone-spit lookout in the steam and I watch the boats come in.

---

They come the way a thing comes into ground it does not read — slow, the oars feeling the channel, the dark uprights leaning out over the bows to read the brown water for the bank that will take the keel. They do not know the bank. I know the bank. I know the mud that breathes and the still warm shallows where the Mure rise at low water, pale and wet-skinned, web-fingered, and take a thing down into the brackish before it has the sound out. The boats come in over the Mure's own water and do not know it, the way the dead ones came in and did not know it, the way I did not know it the first low the green spat me up half-drowned on the mud.

The shore gives once. Then it makes you go further. These have not learned even the once.

They put the boats up on the spit. The dark uprights come over the sides into the shin-deep warm and haul the hulls up the mud, and there are more of them than I have fingers and toes to lay against, more than the tribe is. They go on two legs, all of them. They carry. They set things down and take things up and set them down again, the endless handling of things that only the upright pack does. Some are big. Some are smaller. None are the very small ones that dart and stumble between the legs; these are a pack come without its young, the way a pack comes when it comes to take and not to live.

And I lie in the steam and I read them the way I read a herd before the hunt — counting, learning where it bunches thick, where it thins and goes soft and a thing could come in along the blind side — and the far mouth opens under my breastbone so wide it hurts worse than the belly ever hurt.

Because they are mine. Not the tribe-mine. The other mine. The shape of the thing I had before the hold turns over far down in me, under the green, under all the lows. They go on two legs the way I go. They have the hands, the working hands, the dark scarred clever hands that turn a rock to see what folds under it. They make the sounds at each other, the high clear sounds with edges, sounds set against sounds, words thrown mouth to mouth and caught and thrown back, the whole easy road of it that I lost behind the gap where three teeth are torn. They are the shape of the hole in me that the tribe filled with everything but its own exact shape — and the hole knows its own shape when it sees it walk up out of a boat on two legs, and it leans.

I have a crown this low. I have the Kala's arm over me in the easy sleep and the bold heart warm on my shoulder and a home with no between in it, the first I have ever had. The far mouth was full. I felt it go full on the king's seat, full all the way down. And it leans anyway. It leans toward the pale flat water and the two-legged pack on the bone-spit, toward a warmth shaped exactly like the warmth that was torn out of me before the green, and it will not be told it is already fed.

That is the new wrongness. Not on the shelf of wrong things, the body-wrong, the blood-wrong. This is a head-wrong, a want-wrong: that a thing can be full and lean anyway, that the second mouth fed full to the brim by the tribe can open a new seam under the full one the way the body opens a seam to taste fear, and let in the old far hunger, the human-shaped one, that no Vorth was ever cut to fill.

---

I should go. Every fast part of me says go, go up to the high stones, go to the crown and the green and the Kala and put the whole broken country between me and the hands. A pack this many will sweep the green the way the rains sweep the gullies. I am king of the wild things and the king is still one thing, and I have seen what the many do to the one. I wore it on the neck. I wore the hand and the ugly sound and the count of lows they cut out of me and the three teeth they tore from the front of my mouth trying for everything else I would not give. The many are how the one is unmade.

But I do not go.

I lie on the wet rock and I watch them set up on the bone-spit where every other human died, and the body reads the thing the head is slow to hold:

They are going to die.

I know it the way I know a tide before the sky shows it. The green will eat them. Not the teeth first — the green does not need its teeth for the upright pack. It has the fever-air that drops every other body and leaves me clear-headed. It has the bad water that knots a gut and the still rot-pools that look like drink, the clear-glass fish in the warm shallows, the snake and the spine and the sting that laid the dead ones down in the mud with their charts and their knives and their relic. It has the swarms — already I can see the dark of them lifting off the rot as the boats settle, the cloud of biting flies and the crawling things that blanket every body that is not mine. It has the Mure under the warm shallows they beached the boats across. It has the killing heat that will not let a cut close, that will turn every scrape these hands take to rot-green and fly-blown in a few lows while mine draw shut clean. The green eats humans. That is the one law of this place I learned without learning it, because I am the one human it has never been able to eat, and I never knew until I had no others to measure against that this was a strange thing to be.

I watch the dark uprights build their fire on the spit. Standing smoke, straight up in the still steam, the grease and char of fed fire, fire a thing makes on purpose and tends and does not let go out. Smoke on the water. And I lie above it, the thing the green could not kill, and I count, low by low, the deaths the green has already laid out for them and they cannot read, and the far mouth leans toward them all the same, leans toward the dying pack, because they are the shape of the hole, and a thing will lean toward the shape of its hole even over a grave.

---

There is one comes apart from the rest.

The light is dying and the fed fire is up red on the bone-spit, and one of the dark uprights walks down to the tide-line alone where the boats are drawn up, and stands, and looks out at the channel. Out toward my green. It is big, and it stands the way the strong stand, the way Gull-Marr crossed the bare stone slow because the slow is its own telling — *I do not have to hurry to the thing I am going to take.* It puts its hands on its hips and it turns its head along my shore, reading it, the dark green wall of it, the way a thing reads ground it means to walk into. It does not know what reads it back.

And it makes a sound. Across the water, worn down by the distance to almost nothing, the way a smell comes from very far and is only the ghost of itself — a sound thrown back up the spit to the others, a word, an order, an edge laid on the air, and up by the fire a shape lifts a hand and answers it. The easy road. From down in the head where the wanting lives, up the throat, out through the mouth, laid clean on the air, caught, thrown back. A thing it does not even know it is doing, easy to it as breath.

Something turns over in me so hard the body makes a sound on its own — and the sound that comes out is the rasp, the cough with the growl riding its back, the thing the throat makes now where words used to be made. I clap my hand over my mouth in the steam to keep the rest of it in, to keep my shape from giving itself up across the water.

And under the hand I go in behind the teeth, into the gap where three are torn out, the dark hollow where the one true thing has always lived — the name my mother set on me before the ship, before the hands, the name they never knew to reach for and so never took. *Rook.* I go in to find it the way I have gone in a thousand lows, to hold it under the tongue where no one can take it, to set it against the brand that big slow upright would lay on me if it ever closed a hand on my neck. The name and the brand. The one true thing and the ugly behind-the-teeth thing that means *mine, be still, come, you are a thing.* I hold the name down hard in the gap, and the warm comes up slow and lies down in the hollow around it the way it always comes, and the name is there, and it is mine, and it is the one un-stolen thing.

But I cannot throw it.

That big slow thing on the tide-line throws a word across the water and gets one back, and I have a name held under my tongue I have never once said out loud in all the lows of the green, a name that is mine and answers nothing because there is no one on this whole drowning island that I would say it to, no one shaped to catch it, no one I could say it to who would not also be the hand. The tribe gave me back a voice — the rasp, the falls, the still-sound I make to the bold heart, the two-falls I make to the Kala — but the tribe could not give me back the road from the head to the mouth that lays a name clean on the air for another head to catch. Only the upright pack has that. And the upright pack is the hand.

I take my hand off my mouth. There is no sound to keep in. I have a name and no one to throw it to, and the ones shaped to catch it are the ones who tore the throwing of it out of me in the first place.

---

I lie on the bone-spit lookout until the light is gone and the fed fire is the only thing red on the black water, and I do not go up to the crown.

I should go up. The Kala will wake to the empty place at her side and make the still-sound into the dark, *be still, you are held, nothing comes,* and the *nothing comes* will hang half-made in the great chest because the king is not at the side where the king sleeps. I should go up and lay the warning into the high stones the only way I have, the body-way, the sound and the set of the jaw and the turn toward the water that says *a thing is on the green that I have no good sound for.* The seer would read it. The seer reads everything, reads the blood under the Eye-and-Thorn, reads the wrong things on the shelf the body did and did not agree to. The seer would lay the wondering *means* over it and know, the way the seer knows, that the thing the water carried in is the thing it has been waiting for since the pale finger first came up.

I do not go up. Not yet.

I lie above the dying pack with my front turned to their fire, the way I stood up into the open over the king's seat and let myself be seen, and I hold the two things the water gave me this low and lay them down behind my teeth together.

The first is the law. The green eats humans. I am the one it could not eat, and these are come to walk into the death I survived, and I know it before they take their first bad drink, and there is a thing in me — old, low, under the crown — that wants to lie here and count them down into the mud the way the dead ones went, fever and rot and venom and the Mure under the warm, until the green has eaten the shape of the hole and the far mouth can stop leaning at a thing that is only a grave.

The second is the leaning. That the far mouth, fed full by the tribe, leans at the dying pack anyway, because they are the shape of it, because they have the hands and the words and the two legs and the warmth that was torn out of me before the green, and a thing fed on everything but its own exact shape will crawl across a grave toward the shape of its hole. The crown did not close it. The Kala did not close it. Nothing on this whole drowning island closes it but the thing the green is going to eat.

I keep the law behind my teeth. I keep the leaning behind my teeth, beside the name no one can catch and the brand I will give back someday across the water. And the fed fire burns low and red on the bone-spit where every human before me died, and the swarms lift dark off the rot and settle on every body but the one body lying still above them in the dark, and far out past the channels and the drowned giants the wide pale water stands up into the steam the way it has always stood, and somewhere out on it there are more shapes that hold their line, because boats come in packs, and the smoke I am watching is only the first thread of it.

The crown is the highest thing I have ever stood on. And the smoke on the water is the thing the next taking came in on, and I knew it would come, and it has a fire now, and the far mouth in me has already started counting the lows between.
