# Chapter 19: The Slavers' Camp

They have built where the dead built.

I know it before I am close enough to read a single body. The green here is wrong the way a wound is wrong, the canopy hacked back off the old high-water flat where the Wreck lies rotting in the mangrove, the rot-thicket cut and dragged and the bare mud opened to the sky. No thing of my green opens the canopy. The green closes. It grows down over every hole the way the tide comes back over a print, swallows the light, drinks the bare ground in a few lows and leaves no scar — that is the first law of my green, that nothing stays opened. Only men open a thing and hold it open. I read the held-open from a long way off, downwind, low in the drowned roots, and the second mouth pulls up tight and hard under my breastbone the way it pulled when the boats first sat the water wrong.

They have landed where the last men died. They have cut a clearing in the rot that ate the others and they have not smelled the dying still on it. Men have no nose for the thing that takes them. I have read the bones of the dead expedition under the mangrove, the ones the fever took, the ones I do not feel — and the living have set their fires on the same ground, in the same fever-air, and do not know they are already meat. The green does not hurry. It will take them the slow way. But it has them, and has had them since the first foot came off the boat.

---

I go in close on the low.

The body chooses the low for me the way it always chooses — the tide out, the mangrove channels gone to soft mud and warm shallow, the warm shallows where the Mure rise, and I read the Mure-sign as I cross and there is none, the tide-things gone deep and quiet off the men the way the green goes quiet ahead of the Hesh. Even the drowned-ones will not stand where these stand. I take that and lay it on the shelf and go on, low under the cut stumps, into the smell of them.

The smell lands behind my teeth before my eye finds the first shape, and the smell is the hold.

Tar. Smoke that is fed and tended and does not go out. The hot iron-stink of a forge kept small and close. And under all of it, threaded through all of it, the close packed reek of bodies held where they cannot move, the piss and the sour breath and the open sores of the kept — the smell of a thing that is owned. I have not had that smell in my nose since the dark of the hold, since the wet wood sweating salt and the boy warm against my side. The body wore it. The body wears it again now in one breath, and the skin on the back of my neck crawls and tries to get away from a hand that is not there, the closing hand, the slow sure grip that owned the whole of me in the one hold — and I lie in the mud under a cut stump and I make the crawling stop, and I look.

---

There are more of them than the tribe.

I count the way I count the haul of a herd before I choose, low and sure, learning where it bunches thick and where it thins and goes soft. Many tens. They go on two legs, all of them, the upright pack, and they handle things — the endless handling of things that only men do, setting down and taking up and setting down, the boxes, the lashed bundles, the long hooked iron stacked in racks. They have made their dens out of cut hide stretched over poles, the round dark humps of them set close, all the mouths turned in to the kept fires in the middle, and the red of the fires glows low in the seams the way the red glowed across the dark water in a dream I do not let myself keep.

A pack. A herd of men, gathered, warm, tending its own.

The far mouth opens for it. It does not care what they are. It leans me up out of the mud toward the many-bodied warm, toward the bending and the lifting-up, the being taken in against a warm that chose to hold me — and in the same breath the near part of me draws back flat and the lip wants to peel off the teeth, because hands, because hold, because the smell is the smell of the thing that made me a thing. Toward in the chest. Away in the legs. The two of them in the one skin hauling at each other until the pull sings.

I have a tribe now. I have a home with no between in it and a seat the strength walked off of and left me on. The far mouth is full — it has been full since the Kala folded down beside me on the king's seat and the *nothing comes* did not die half-made in her great chest. I tell the leaning that. *You are full. You do not need the fires. You have the high stones.*

The leaning does not hear me. It leans anyway. Because the warm down there is the warm of my own kind, the same shape walking, and there is a hole in me shaped like that warm that the high stones cannot fill, that the Kala's arm over me cannot fill, a man-shaped hole worn smooth by a thing that used to sit there and sits there no longer. The tribe filled the far mouth. It did not fill this. I learn that lying in the mud watching men I have every reason to flee, and it is the worst thing I have learned since the gap behind my teeth came up empty: that I had two holes in me and only knew the one.

---

I learn them the way I learned the tide. By lying still and letting them come clear.

They are not here for the green. A pack that wanted the green would take meat and water and go, the way the dead expedition took and could not keep. These take the wrong things. I watch them haul a thing up the mud from the channel-mouth on poles, a great gray-silver shape gone slack, and the gut drops out of me because for one breath I read it as Vorth — but it is a drowned-one, a Mure, a big one, its web-fingered hands lashed and its pale wet skin going dull. They have taken it alive off the warm shallows and broken it and dragged it up and they cage it. Not kill it. Cage it. The hooked iron is for taking a live thing and not letting it run, and I have the whole shape of it now: they are not hunting the green to eat it. They are hunting it to keep it. To carry it off across the water in the dark of a hold the way they carried me.

I see the cages then, set back where the smell is thickest. Wood and lashed iron, low, too low for the things in them to stand. The lashed Mure in one, and a thing in another I have no name for, lean and wrong-made, a Skell with its jackal-coding broken, lying on its side in its own waste. And there are men in the cages too. Some of the kept are men.

I lie very still when I read that. Men keeping men. The same shape on both sides of the iron, the hand that closes and the neck the hand closes on, and only the iron between them saying which is which — and I know which the iron made me. I wore the inside of it. I have never once, in all the lows since the sea spat me up, let myself think the men who held me were men. I made them the hold come walking, the hand and the ugly sound, a weather without a face. And here is the face. Tens of faces around the kept fires, warm, tending their own — and some of the things they tend are bodies in cages, and the men in the warm do not turn their heads to the men in the iron any more than I turn my head to the bones in the scree.

---

The leader I read last, the way I read the big patient one last off the spread line that walked my green.

He does not handle things. He stands and the handling moves around him. He is not the biggest — it is never the biggest — but the others bend toward him without being struck, the way a tribe leans to the king before the king has done anything, and that leaning tells me he is the one whose hand all the other hands answer to. He has the still of a thing that does not have to hurry to what it is going to take. I have stood under that still on a deck. I have stood under it in a hold. The body knows the slow certain coming-on, and it knows this man is made of it.

He wears a mark.

It is on the breast of him, worked into the hide he wears, and on the iron of the long blade at his hip, and burned into the wood of the cages — the same mark in all the places, the mark that says *mine* to anything that can read a mark. And I can read it. I have read it before. It comes up out of my drowned-deep where no word lives, up from under the long silence, and the whole skin of me draws tight at once: it is the Eye-and-Thorn. The sigil off the relic I took from the Wreck, the one the seer laid the meaning-sound over, the one that resonates in me from a place older than the island and older than the hold — the eye, and the thorn through it, the watching and the keeping-by-pain bound into one shape. The dead expedition bore it. I have it bound at my own chest this low, under the Kala's keeping and the crown I won. And the living men who landed where the dead men died wear it burned into the iron of their cages.

The dead bore my sigil. The living bear it. And they wear it to mean *owned.*

I do not have the head for what that is. The head cannot hold it. I take it off the head and set it on the shelf of wrong things, beside the four and the five and the fever I do not feel and the gap behind my teeth — the deepest thing on the shelf now, the thing that does not come apart in the hands: that the mark drowned in me from before everything, the mark the seer read as kin-omen over my blood, is the brand the slavers burn on the cages they will carry off across the water. Mine and theirs. The same shape. I do not turn it over. I am not strong enough yet. I set it down and keep my eyes on the leader of the men.

---

He comes to the cage with the broken Mure in it, and the pack makes a path for him, and he stands and looks at the drowned-one the way I have watched the seer look at the squared dead — reading it, weighing it, laying it into some order I cannot see. He says a thing. The sound of it carries to me in the mud, low and flat and edged, sounds set against sounds the way the young one's call carried across the water in the old dream, a word, maybe a name — and a smaller man near him answers it with the quick bend of a body that answers because it must, and the others make the low sound a pack makes to the thing it leans to.

And the man-shaped hole leans so hard at the sound of men making words to each other that it hurts worse than the gut ever hurt. Because that is the thing I lost. Not meat. The easy road of it — from down where the wanting lives, up the throat, out, laid clean on the air, thrown to a body that will catch it and throw one back. The Vorth gave me a tongue and a name and an answer; the tribe filled the far mouth with the still-sound and the rains-break sound and the meaning laid over me by a gray finger. But it did not give me back *this.* Men speaking to men in the shapes my mother spoke over me before the ship. I dig my pads into the mud and hold my own shape down by force against the want that wants to pull me up toward the very men who would burn the Eye-and-Thorn on the cage they bent me into.

The leader turns from the Mure-cage, and his still face passes across the green, across the cut stumps, across the dark drowned roots where I lie — and it does not stop on me, it cannot, I am one dark shape in a green of dark shapes and the wind holds true and gives him nothing. But it passes over me, the weighing look, the slow certain reading, and the back of my neck crawls under it the way it crawled under the hand, and I learn the shape of him whole the way I learned the spit and the seep and the loose rocks: he is the hold come walking. Come up out of the dark of memory and across the water onto two legs into my green, with the hooked iron and the burned brand, to fold over the world the way the hand folded over my neck and made me a thing.

His name will be a thing I learn. The kept will give it to me, the way the kept always give the keeper's name, low, behind the teeth, in the dark — Drevan Sool, the captain, the still one whose hand all the other hands answer to. But I do not have it yet, this low. This low he is only the shape, and the shape is enough.

---

I should go.

Every near part of me says it. A thing this many, this armed, that walks into the death I survive and cages what it does not kill — it will cut and take and burn the brand on everything that can be dragged to a boat. I have a tribe to guard. A Kala, a bold heart that rides my shoulder, a high stone full of small ones and the half-grown that crowd the lip. The men are not here for them yet. They are here for the easy green, the channel-mouth, the warm shallows. But the channel is a hand's reach from the high stones, and a thing that opens the green and holds it open does not stop at the line a beast bleeds across the stone. It learns the line, and then it crosses it. I read that off the spread fan of them already moving out from the fires, the cutting-parties with the hooked iron fanning into the rot-thicket the way the spears once fanned across my ground.

I should take the tribe and go deep, up into the high broken stone and the fever-fog where men die and I do not, where no boat reaches and no brand burns, and let the green eat the men the slow way it eats all men, and never once lay my shape where the captain's weighing-look can cut it out of the green.

I do not go.

I lie in the warm mud with the smell of the hold in my nose and the Eye-and-Thorn burning on the cages, and I do not pull back into the deep green where the hand could never reach me. Because the men are my own shape, and the captain wears my own sigil on his iron, and I have to know. What the mark means and whose it is and why it lives in me and in him both. The shapes my own kind make to each other with their mouths, the easy road I lost. A thing that learns a new hole in itself cannot stop leaning toward the only thing shaped to fill it, even when the thing is the hand and the hold and the brand.

The shore gives once, and then it makes you go further. I went all the way up. There was no further up the high stone left to go, only the lip, and I stood on the lip and the body knew, bone-sure, the way it knows a tide before the sky shows it: the highest thing you stand on is the thing the next taking comes for. I read it on the king's seat in the green half-light. I read it again now, lower and surer under all the leaning, the dread of it laid deeper in the gut than the leaning sits, watching the still captain weigh my green: the taking has a face this low, and the face is mine, and it is already in the door.

---

I stay until the light goes.

I watch the kept fires come up red on the cut mud when the green half-light dies into the dark, fat and fed and tended, warm in the killing green where my one banked coal in its fist of fiber is to them what the cat's picked bones are to the running herd. I watch the men gather and throw their shapes at each other, the easy road, the call caught and thrown back, and the man-shaped hole aches at every sound. I watch the cages where the kept do not get the fire — the Mure going dull, the broken Skell on its side, the men in the iron who are the same shape as the men in the warm. I watch the still captain stand among his fires with the Eye-and-Thorn on his breast, the mark that drowns in me, and I lay the whole night of it down on the shelf and behind my teeth, beside the brand I keep only so that I can one day be rid of it.

And under the hand I clap over my own mouth in the dark — the old clap, learned long lows ago, to keep the rasp in, to keep my shape from giving itself up — I want a sound to set against the men's far calls. One sound. Mine. The name my mother set on me before the ship, the one un-stolen thing, the one the slavers never knew to reach for and so never took. I go in behind the teeth to find it and throw it across the cut mud at the fires of my own kind the way the young one threw its name up the shore.

It is there. It is always there, held down hard in the gap where three teeth went, and the warmth comes up slow and lies down in the hollow around it the way it always comes, and the name is mine and whole. But the throwing of it is the thing they tore out. The hands that owned me took the road from the head to the mouth and left me the name and no way to lay it on the air, and this low I learn again what that costs — a man-shaped hole opening wide as a mouth toward the fires, the name held full in the dark behind my teeth, and no one on the whole drowning island shaped to catch it who is not also the hand.

I take my hand off my mouth. There is no sound to keep in. I have the name, and no one to throw it to.

I go back into my green in the full dark, up toward the high stones, toward the Kala and the bold heart and the home that is mine, the king of the wild things going home through the rot. And the far mouth that is full leans behind me the whole way, back toward the red fires, toward the men, toward the brand burned on the iron — full, and leaning anyway, the way a body that has everything still leans toward the one thing that almost killed it, because the one thing that almost killed it was the last warm hands that were the same shape as its own.

I know, the way the body knows a tide before the sky shows it, that I will go back down to the fires. Not this low. But I will go, down to the brand and the cages and the still captain who wears my own mark on his iron, because the man-shaped hole has learned the road to the only thing shaped to fill it, and a hole like that does not unlearn a road. It only counts the lows until it can walk it.
