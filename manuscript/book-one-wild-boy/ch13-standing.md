# Chapter 13: Standing

There is a low when the rot-thicket eats one of the strong.

It is the heavy old male with the torn ear, the one that set himself in my track when the rains lifted, the one whose strength the soft ground would not hold. He does not wait for me this low. The fat-meat beast has come down into the deep rot to wallow, and the old male is sure of his own great weight the way the strong are always sure, and he goes down the bank ahead of me, out onto the skin of the rot, without the eye that reads the ground.

I am behind him on the bank when the ground tells him the truth.

I read it in the soles before the eye — the give coming up under his foot, the dark vein of soup working under the mat of root and rot-leaf. I throw the asking-sound at his torn ear, the rising one, *back, back.* But he is in the rush of his own strength and the beast is a spear-throw off and fat and his, and he does not come back. He sets the whole of his weight down where the ground is a lie.

And the ground opens.

---

It takes him to the chest before the rot-leaf has finished closing over the place his foot went through.

I have read the deep rot's mouths since the first season in my green, the still soup under the green skin that knots and pulls and does not give back what it swallows. The fat-meat beast wallows in it gladly, broad-footed where a heavy hard thing only sinks. The old male is not made for it. The rot reads his weight and his thrashing, and pulls, and he goes down to the chest, the great arms beating the soup to froth, and the more he fights the deeper the wet takes him — a law strength makes worse.

The tribe is on the bank. They have poured down after the beast and stopped at the lip of the thicket where the ground turns to lie, and they do not go out onto the skin of it. They know the rot. They have stood at this exact lip and watched it take things and not followed, because to follow is to be taken with it. They make the low sound, the one with the gone-thing folded into it. He is in the soup to the chest and they are already squaring him in the head, faced to the sun, gone.

I am the lightest thing on the bank.

---

There is no place in me where the deciding sits. The body has it before the head, and the body goes.

I go out onto the skin of the rot at a run, low, my weight thrown wide and quick the way you cross the give before the give can read you, foot finding the root-mat and gone off it before the mat can know a foot was there. The Vorth cannot do this. Their weight is their whole strength, and out here their whole strength is the thing that kills them. Mine is the one thing the deep rot has no law for — a thing too light to pull.

I do not go straight. Straight is the soft line his thrashing has churned to soup. I read the firmer mat where the old root runs under, the way I read the run in the mud, and come to him along it, the long way, the light way, my feet spread on the last of the ground that holds.

He has stopped beating now. That is the worst thing. He has gone to the deep stillness, the soup to the throat, the torn ear back, the small dark eyes rolled to me — the stillness of a thing that has weighed the stronger thing that holds it and stopped its own body. I know that stillness. I have been it, on a deck, in a hold, with the hand closed on the back of the neck. I feel it come off him into me and set my teeth on what it wakes.

I cannot pull him. If I take his hand he will not come up; he will only take me down, the two of us squared in the soup together. The strong cannot reach him. The weak has no arm.

So I do not pull him. I make the green pull him.

---

I have the loop at my belt. I always have the loop at my belt.

But a loop is for the neck of a thing that runs, and there is no green branch out here sprung and waiting. There is only the half-drowned giants the rot has not yet eaten, and the vine.

The vine is the thing. The drowned green is roped through with it, the long woody runners that climb the dead giants and bind the canopy down, thick as my arm, the thing I have climbed and swung on in a green that meant to drop me. I read the nearest great runner where it comes down off a leaning giant along the firm mat, get a turn of it in my two hands, and throw the end out across the churned soup to the old male.

He does not understand it. He is a thing of the arm and the blow and there is no blow in a thrown vine. The end of it lands across his shoulder and the great froth-churned arms do not come up to take it. He only looks at me, the torn ear back, going down.

I make the sound. Not a Vorth sound — I have none for *take it, hold it, I will not pull, the green will pull.* I make the sound the shore made in me, the one with no word in it, the one a body throws across a closing distance that means *here, this, now, live* — and I shake the vine so it slaps his arm, the whole of my want in the shaking, *take it, take it* — and some old thing in him under the strength reads the want if not the word, and the great hand comes up out of the soup and closes on the vine.

And now I make the green the arm.

---

I take my turn of the vine to the great leaning giant and run the runner over the high stub of a broken limb the way I run a loop over a bent branch — over and down, so the limb is the seat the strength turns on, so a pull on my end is a lift on his. I learned it on the shore, with stones too heavy to lift: a weight over a high seat, a long pull, and the thing too heavy to lift comes up.

And then I call the strong.

I throw the asking-sound back at the bank, the rising one, *come, come,* and show them the vine, the turn of it over the high stub, the old male hung on the far end of it — and the big ones read it. They cannot read the ground; that is why they are on the bank and he is in the soup. But they can read a vine in two hands that wants pulling, and they pour out — not onto the rot, onto the firm root-mat I have crossed, set foot for foot behind me where the ground will hold — and they take the vine up the line, hand over great hand, the whole main-strength of them laid at last onto a thing it can use.

I am the eye. They are the arm. I have made the place where the two meet.

The vine goes taut over the high seat and groans, and the deep rot pulls back the way it always pulls back, and for a breath nothing moves, the soup and the strong holding against each other across the dead limb — and then the soup gives him up. Slow. Sucking. The wet climbing off him a hand's width at a time, and the seat lifts and the rot loses its law, and the old male comes up out of the mouth of the green that meant to keep him, hung on a vine the weak thing threw, lifted by a strength the weak thing aimed.

They drag him over the firm mat to the bank and he lies there heaving, the soup pouring off him, the torn ear flat, alive.

---

The whole tribe is on the bank, and the whole tribe saw it.

This is the thing that is different. The loop, the fed fire — those were gifts I handed them, the weak thing's small things laid down for the strong to take or leave. This they watched. The deep rot took the strongest among them, and the strong stood on the bank and could not follow, and the weakest thing went out where the strong could not go and brought him back — not by being strong, by being light, by reading the ground, by making the green the arm. They watched the weak thing do the thing the strong could not, with their own strength in its hands.

The old male gets his legs under him, shaking, the soup drying gray on his hide, and comes to me. He stands over me, the great torn-eared bulk of him, his small dark eyes going over my face the way the Kala's went over the loop, and makes a sound — low, slow up out of the great chest, with a thing in it I have the whole shape of: the thing a strong one lays on a thing that has kept it from the gone-low. Not the gentling, not the asking, not the dismissing sound that followed me the first season. The sound a body makes to the thing that stood between it and the squared dead. He bows the great head. The strong one bows its head to the weak. And then he does the thing I have no shelf for: he takes my wrist in his great hand — the hand that could close and end me, that has only ever moved me off a rock with no thought in it — and he does not move me. He holds the wrist up and shows it to the tribe, the dark scarred small thing in his great fist, lifted for the others to see, and makes the sound again, plain, laid on me where they all can read it.

He is naming me to them. He is saying, in the only tongue there is, *this one. This is the one that did it.*

---

The far mouth stands all the way up.

It is not the near edge of it that the loop fed, that the fire fed, that the seer's bowed head fed. It is deeper. To be handed up before the whole tribe by one of the strong, the wrist lifted, the sound laid plain — *this one* — is to be made a thing worth a sound by more than the one warm bulk that chose me. This is the whole tribe turning, the strong included, reading the weak thing as the thing that kept their strongest from the gone — and the second mouth that leaned out of me my whole life into nothing finds the whole bank turned toward it.

I do not have their word for it. I have all the weather of it. I came up among them the soft weak wrong thing on lent ground that poured away the moment a hand opened — and this low the ground does not pour away. The strong itself has set foot in my track. I have stood on a thing and it has held my weight. I have earned a standing that is not lent.

The Kala is on the high bank above us, the great gray-silver bulk, her small dark eyes on me, the keeping thing in them turned the new way, the proud way — the way a thing looks at the thing it chose when the choosing comes good before all the others. She chose me before there was a thing to choose me for, in the fever-fog, in the rot she folded off me, when I was only a wrong thing the green could not finish. The bank lifts my wrist now. She lifted it first, when it was worth nothing.

---

The king is on the high stones, and the king watches.

Gull-Marr does not come down. He stands up on his broken lip above the drowned thicket, the heavy flat-eyed bulk of him, and watches the whole of it — the weak thing go out where the strong could not, the vine thrown, the strong poured out and aimed like a stone, the old male lifted up out of the green's mouth, the wrist held up. He watches it the way I go over a thing on the shelf of wrong things. Slow. A thing that should not be, and is.

When the king let it stand on the keeper's clean shelf, the rains-low, he laid on it the sound he lays on a thing that *is* — allowed, set into the order, but not chosen. A small thing the strength-law of the stone could hold in its hand and not feel.

This is not that.

I feel the change in him before the head can name it, the way I feel a tide turn in the channel — the whole weight of it before I have any of its parts. The flat eyes do not warm and do not tip the way the old male's did. They go narrow. The king's law is the law of the strong — strength is the way, and the path to the way is more strength, and a thing that has not got the strength has not got the way. That is the stone he stands on. And he has just watched the marrow of his own stone aimed by a hand that could not turn a Vorth's head a finger's width — watched strength take orders from the weak — and he reads the thing under it, the thing the seer half-read in the side of my head: that there is a way that is not strength, and the weak thing has it.

---

He comes down off his broken lip.

The gentling goes out of the high stones the way it went out the rains-low, the small ones feeling it first, the bold one already gone off the press and up the rock. He crosses the bank, the clear bare stone moving with him, and comes to where I stand with the old male's naming still going round, and stops over me, and looks down.

I brace for the arm. I have braced for it the whole season — the arm that turns the weak thing off the ground, the hand that closes, the brand laid in the flat tongue, *mine, be still, come, you are a thing.* The old barb turns in the warm dark behind my teeth. But the arm does not come. The bank has named me; the Kala has kept me; the seer has marked me; even his own word let it stand. He cannot lay the brand on a thing his whole tribe just turned toward, not in front of the turning.

So he does a thing that is worse, and quieter, and I read it whole the moment he does it.

He looks at the old male, alive on the weak thing's reading, and at the wrist the old male lifted. And he makes a sound — not at me. At the old male. Low, and short, and flat, the teeth back in it, the careful teeth that were out of the sound he laid the rains-low. The old male hears it and the torn ear flattens and the great head goes down, and the naming-sound dies in his chest half-made, and he steps back off me, the proud thing gone out of him.

The king has not turned me off the ground. He has reminded the strong whose ground it is.

And then the flat eyes come back to me and hold me, long, the narrow weighing look, no warmth in it and no letting-stand. There is the thing I know in the body before the head can find it — the thing one strong lays on another strong across a stone they both want, the thing the great cat laid on me from the dark the night I learned what I was strong enough to do. It is not the look the king lays on a thing that *is.* It is the look he lays on a thing that *could.* He marks me the way a strong thing marks the thing on its stone it will have to deal with before the stone is sure again.

He does not need a word for *threat.* He has the whole shape of it. I read it off him the way he reads the weather, bone-sure before the sky shows it, and lay it down behind my teeth with the rest, in the gap where the name was, the hardest law the high stones have taught me yet:

*To earn a standing on a strength-stone is to become the one thing the strength-king cannot let stand.*

---

He turns his great heavy body and goes back up onto his broken lip, the bare stone going up with him, and he does not look back, and the not-looking is its own kind of marking.

The bank breaks up slow. The old male will not meet my eye now; the king's small flat stone of a sound is on him, and he carries it. The others go up off the bank, and the turning toward me is still in them, the *this one,* but it is quieter now, gone careful under the thing the king laid down.

I stand a while on the bank over the drowned thicket where the rot still works its slow mouth in the wet. I have earned a thing this low that no low gave me before — a standing the ground did not lend, a place I set my weight on and it held, before the whole tribe. The far mouth has it, deep. And in the same turning I have been marked by the one whose word is the law of the stone — marked the new way, the *could* and not the *is.* The body knows what the head is slow to hold: the thing that turned the tribe toward me is the exact thing that turned the king against me. I could not have the one without the other.

The Kala comes down when the bank has cleared. She folds down beside me at the lip of the rot, the great gray-silver bulk, and lays the arm over me, sure, the keeping arm, and makes the still-sound, soft, falling, *be still, you are held, nothing comes.* And I make it back, low and torn, two falls, scraping over the gap where three teeth were gone. But I make it looking up at the broken lip, where the heavy flat-eyed shape stands black against the high green, not gleaning, not working, only standing, turned to the small scarred thing the strong followed down into the rot.

*Nothing comes,* the Kala says, soft, falling, over the far mouth that is full.

Something comes. A thing is coming off the broken lip toward me as sure as the tide comes up the channel — not this low, not the next, but coming, weighed and marked and bone-sure. I lie in the warm with the still-sound going around, and I do not tell her the thing the body already knows. Behind my teeth, in the gap where the name lies, beside the brand I will give back someday across the water, I lay the new law down and keep it, the way the shore taught me to keep a thing either way it goes.

The shore gives once. Then it makes you go further. The high stones are no different. They have only made the further a thing with flat eyes and a broken lip and the whole law of the strong behind it, waiting up in the high green, marking the place where the weak thing learned to stand.
