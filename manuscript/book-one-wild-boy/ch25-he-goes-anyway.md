# Chapter 25: He Goes Anyway

The bait is the home. That is the thing the keeper learned that no man before him learned, because no man before him stayed long enough on my green to watch where the far mouth leans.

He could not draw my shape out of the still. He set the meat at the thornwood and I did not come, because the near mouth was fed, and a fed beast does not walk onto open ground for a haunch. So he stopped baiting the near mouth. He went up my green and took the Kala.

A bait is a thing you set where the animal already goes. And there was only ever one place I went.

---

The trap is whole in me, every tooth of it, and the having does not turn me back. I do the thing the bait was set for.

I go down toward her.

The law is in my legs the whole way, the first law of the green I learned the first moon and have never once forgotten, screaming up through my soles as I pour myself down off the safe high edge into the scattered fire and the owning shapes. *A thing the world lays out easy is a thing the world means to close on.* The Kala on the cleanest high shelf with two irons in her is the easiest thing the world has ever laid out for me. The whole green I bled into being is screaming the law up into me: *do not go down onto her. Take the small ones and go up, into the fever-fog and the broken stone where the men die and you do not. The home is already on the hook. Going to it only puts you on it beside her.*

That is the law. It has kept me alive on an island that keeps no one.

I go anyway.

The far mouth is bigger than the law and always was. It has its teeth in me now, dragging me down off the one edge where no hand could reach me, toward the net and the hooked iron and the patient one waiting in the green below — and no part of me turns back. Not fooled. Never fooled. I come down the open stone toward the closing hand with my eyes wide and the choosing made, because the thing in the net is the one warm that ever filled the second mouth full, and a body does not leave that on the stone, not for any law the green ever taught.

The shore gives once, and then it makes you go further. I thought the high stone was the far end of going. It is not. The far end of going is this — down onto the open shelf in the light, into the trap, knowing.

---

She sees me come.

She lifts the great gray head off the bloodied stone as far as the net will let it and lays the small dark patient eye on me, and she reads me the way she has read me from the first low, before there was ever a sound between us — and what she reads is *come,* and what she reads is the trap, both at once, and the sound she makes is the lowest I have ever heard out of her. Not the asking-sound. Not *where, where.* The one she makes over a small one straying too near the rot-thicket where the ground will not hold.

*Go back. Go up. Do not come down onto the soft ground after me.*

She is in the net with the irons in her shoulder and her haunch and she is telling me to leave her in it — to take the between I carried up the green on my skin all those lows back up the broken stone, and live.

I go to her.

---

I come fast and low across the scattered fire and the first one never hears me.

I take him at the back of the knee with the killing-stone in my fist and the joint goes sideways under it and he folds with a sound like green wood breaking. His holding-spear rings on the rock. My hand is already out for it, the long iron, the barb wet and gleaming for the length of one heartbeat in my palm —

No. The net comes out of the dark behind me.

It is weighted at the edges with stone and it is on me before the spear is anything but a weight in my hand. The mesh drops over my head and shoulders and the stones swing in and wrap, and the more I surge the more it takes, the way a net is built to take. I am down. The spear is gone, ringing away across the stone. I roll. I bite. I get a fist of the mesh in my teeth and pull and a strand parts and the taste of it floods my mouth — tar. Hold-tar. The black seam-stink that lived in the dark of the ship low after low under the bodies, and the body knows it before the head can shape it, and the buried sound comes up screaming behind my gone teeth at last. The hand. The closing. The brand. It rises out of the place I packed it down and fills my whole skull, and the red comes up all the way behind it, hot, flooding, every edge of me gone to teeth and want.

Good. Let the net try.

I bite through a man's wrist to the cord under it. He howls, high, a sound that does not belong to a thing that carries hooked iron, and the blood that comes is warm in my mouth, alive, singing all the way down into the gut — nothing like meat, nothing like the still cool of the deep green, all rush, all want, all the living heat of him. The second mouth under my breastbone yawns wide for it. It does not want the wrist. It wants all of him, the whole warm living shape of him taken into the place no meat has ever filled, and for one black breath I let the rage and the second mouth run together and there is no boy left in it, only the open throat of the thing.

Then there are four on the net at once.

---

Let the high stones keep the count: three of them out of the fight before they fold me. The one at the knee. The one whose wrist runs red into the seams of the rock. A third I take across the throat with the edge of my hand and he goes down whooping for the air that will not come fast enough. A thing that hunts me carries the marks of having hunted me. That is mine. They cannot take it off me.

I open the leader's face from the eye to the jaw with my nails before they pin the arm, a long furrow welling dark — and he does not cry out. That is the worst of him in the end. Not the size. That he takes the wound and does not break stride. He bears down harder, even and sure, patient with his own face running blood, patient the way the rot is patient with a fallen thing. He has been sure of this ending since the low he first crouched at my line, and a torn face does not move a man who is sure.

But I am one boy. There is no more than a boy's strength in my arms and it runs out the way a boy's runs out — fast, all at once, the deep well of it draining to mud while the red still screams for more. They counted it long ago, lying out on the high ground learning which way I bolt and how long the heat in me lasts. They do not come at me one at a time the way beasts come, each waiting its turn at the kill. They come together, one animal with many backs and many hands, and when the first net is full of me they bring the second, and over that the third, weighting me down against the stone under layer on layer of stone-hung mesh, and then they bring the rope.

A loop closes on my wrist. I twist and bite the hand that set it and taste salt and grease and get a curse for it, but the loop holds. Another loop. My ankle, dragged straight and lashed. They lean their whole weight down the lines and the stone comes up to meet my face and the salt-iron floods my mouth where the rock splits my lip.

And the Kala heaves.

She comes up under her own mountain of net, surging once against all the stone they swung into her and the two irons turning in her, and the high shelf groans under it and for one breath I think the home will stand up out of the trap and bring the whole sky down on them — and they turn the iron on her. Not to kill. I read it even folded in the nets — they do not drive the long iron into the deep of her where the life is. They work it shallow and cruel along the great shoulder where the barb already sits, driving her back down into the stone by the pain of it the way a man drives a beast he means to keep, and her heave breaks and she goes down, and the torn sound comes out of her, and it is the worst sound on the whole of this island.

I scream into the mesh and I cannot reach her. I am many hands deep in the rope, and the between I carried in her arm is the whole of the distance now.

---

They have made me a thing in the net.

Rope at the wrist. Rope at the ankle. A loop drawn cruel around my throat so the more I pull the less the air comes, so my own fighting chokes me. Blood in my eyes, mine and theirs, all of it iron, all of it warm where my wounds should run thin and slow and do not. They get the weight of three nets and four men down onto me and the stone bears up under my cheek, and I claw at it, at my own high country, the ground the tribe bled into being and I bled to keep. I get a fistful of grit and the loose scatter of the kicked-out fire and I clamp down and pull, and it pours out between my fingers, not one grain staying. That is the whole truth of this island in one fist of running ash. It does not hold what I cannot hold. It was never mine to keep — only borrowed back from the killing green a low at a time, and the moment my hand opens it pours away.

I snarl, bitten and bleeding, more beast than boy left in the rope, the words gone, the self gone thin, only teeth and the red and the choke of the loop. And the rope does not care that I am a beast. The stone does not care. The patient one, somewhere up the shelf with the whole still weight of him gathered, does not care.

And here, even here, the body keeps its wrong small secret. The wounds the nets and the stones tore down my arms and side are already drawing their edges in while they haul me flat, knitting faster than a cut has any right to, here on the high stones where every cut on Saqarro festers and goes rot-green and fly-blown in a low. The biting swarms that crawl in a black blanket over the bleeding men do not land on me — a clean hand's-width of air rings my skin and they will not cross it. The brand is in my skull and the wrong small thing in my blood goes on keeping its account in the corner where it always has, asking nothing, agreed to by nothing, while they make me cargo.

---

I see her once more before they turn me.

The Kala, pinned in her mountain of net, the irons standing up out of her shoulder and her haunch, the small dark patient eye finding me across the wall of men. She does not heave again. She lies down into it. And the eye that lays on me is not afraid for herself — no fear in it turned inward, no care for her own opened side. It is wide and dark and it breaks on me, and I understand at last what she has been reading off me since I came down the stone.

She read the down road on my skin and the smoke in my hair and went still around me holding it, and she could not say it whole and I could not hear it whole, and she did the one thing a great slow thing can do — folded the between into her arm and waited. And it came down to this anyway, because the one thing none of it could do was make me leave her on the stone.

The second mouth went still under her once. That is the thing. In all the lows of this green, the one time the ache behind my breastbone lay down and was at peace was in her warm, the small ones heaped against her arm and her great slow tide under my ear and no between in it anywhere. She gave me that with no word and no asking, the only crown I ever wore that was not bled out of the green by my own hands. She made me, the way the deck unmade me. And the hook took the home for bait, and the home is down in the net with the irons in it, and the boy is in the rope, and there was never any other end to it from the low I learned to care.

---

Then the hand closes on the back of my neck.

The hand. The hand in the dark. The hold.

It is here. It is now. It is real and warm through the leather, the broad spread of fingers bearing my cheek into the stone, and it is the exact shape, the exact weight, the exact closing I carried buried under the tar all those lows in the wreck of myself — and the scream tears out of me at last, every low of the hold come up my throat at once.

They made this. They wove the tar-nets and climbed my green in the dark, paying a length of fever and a length of rot for every step, dying slow the whole way up, and dragged the home off its crown — and it was all only ever the long slow making of this one closing. The hand on the back of the boy's neck, folding him down, making him a thing. They came walking across the water onto my green to put it back where it was.

I hold the name they have not found, pressed flat behind the gap where the three teeth went, where no hand has ever reached and no hand reaches now. *Rook.* Whole in me, woken back through a long buried tide and a warm with no between in it. They can have the body in the net. They cannot have that. It is not in the body. It is under the tongue, behind the gone teeth.

They think they have taken an animal. They fold me down into the stone and the hand bears even and sure on the back of my neck, the patient one's whole long work come down to this one weight, and somewhere up the shelf a man laughs, short and low, the laugh of a thing got that has been hunted long. It lands behind my breastbone and stays.

I learned to care, and it caught me, and she is the price, and crouched here under the closing hand with the trap shut whole around me, I know the last clear thing before the red heat closes back over it.

I would do it again.
