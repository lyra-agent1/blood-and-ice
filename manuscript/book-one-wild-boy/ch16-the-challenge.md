# Chapter 16: The Challenge

A thing weighed and not struck does not go away. It comes up the rock toward you a low at a time, slow, the way the tide comes up the channel with no sound and no hurry and no bottom you can stand on, and the waiting for it is its own kind of dying.

I have lived in that waiting since the dead head of the Hesh cooled on the clean stone. The king did not strike then. The whole tribe was turned toward the small scarred thing that brought the apex down, and the strength-king cannot lay his hand on a thing the tribe has turned toward, not in front of the turning. So he went up onto his broken lip and stood with the narrow weighing eyes turned in on me, and let the lows go by, and the letting-go was not a gift. It was the tide reading the bank. He was waiting for the gladness of the killed apex to thin off the high stones the way the rains thin, so that when he came down at last he would come down onto a thing the tribe had set back down.

That is the law I read off him low after low from under the warm of the Kala's arm. *The strength-king does not strike the lifted thing. He waits for it to be set down, and then he strikes the place where it stood.*

So I do not let it be set down.

---

I do not have a word for the thing I do in the lows after the Hesh. I have all the weather of it, and the weather is the same weather I have worked since the green first tried to drop me: I make the weak thing needed, low after low, so the turning toward me cannot go quiet.

I read the country the strength cannot cross, and I keep reading it out loud. When the heavy old male with the torn ear takes the half-grown ones down to the rot-thickets to learn the fat-meat beast, I go down ahead and lay the firm run with peeled root so the light feet can find it, and the big ones come behind me set foot for foot over ground their weight has no law for, and come back up the rock fed off a country that went hungry past them every season before me. When the small one with the quick heart goes high after the sap-nest and the limb gives, I am the thing that reads the give before the eye and is under the falling small one with my arms out, and the Kala makes the long meaning-sound over the small one not-squared, not-faced-to-the-sun, kept. I make the loops. I bank the fire. I mark the deep rot's still mouths with a squared stone, so the heavy ones learn the lie of the ground from a thing the weak thing set down for them.

Every one of these is a small gift laid where the whole rock can see it. I am not laying them for the gladness; I have stopped needing the gladness the near-edge way I needed it the first season. I am laying them across the place where the king means to come down, so that when he comes down the tribe is still turned, and the strength-king cannot strike a turned thing without striking the whole turning.

I learned this on the shore, with the deep rot and the heavy thing too strong to lift. You do not stand against the weight. You make the ground the weight stands on do the standing. The king is the weight. The turning of the tribe is the ground. I keep the ground from going firm under him.

He reads it. He reads me keeping the turning warm the way I read him waiting for it to go cool, and the two readings meet across the high stones low after low and neither one looks away.

---

The seer is the thing that breaks the waiting.

The ash-pale head comes across the crowded rock in the half-dark before a light, the way it came the low I laid the Hesh down, slow, the old bulk moving careful through the sleeping piles, and folds down at the lip where I lie in the warm with the Kala's tide at my back. The pale finger comes to the Eye-and-Thorn bound at my chest, the relic off the dead expedition, the wreck's one made thing that the seer reads as a kin-omen, and makes the long meaning-sound over it, the *means*, the sound that has no edge of asking in it, only the weight of a thing that is.

And then the finger lifts off my chest and turns, the way it turned the low of the Hesh — not out into the dark, there is nothing in the dark anymore, the thing in the dark is in the soup of the tribe's bellies. Up. Toward the broken lip where the heavy flat-eyed shape stands black against the high green, the way it has stood every low since the apex died. The seer makes the meaning-sound up at the king, and then down at me, and then draws the line between the two, slow, the way you draw a run in the mud for a thing that cannot read it yet.

*This one. And that one. And the two are one telling, and the telling is not done.*

I have the whole shape of it. The seer is not asking; the seer is reading a thing already written, the way I read the storm off my tongue before the sky shows it. There is a telling on the high stones between the small scarred thing the tribe turned toward and the strength-king who cannot let it stand, and it has been coming up the rock a low at a time since the Hesh, and the seer is saying the only thing left to say of it: it will be finished. Not by the waiting. It will be finished the way the strong finish a thing, on the clean stone, in front of the turning, body to body, the law of the strength laid down where the whole tribe can read which one of them carries it after.

The seer folds the pale hand back and goes still, and the king on the broken lip has watched the whole of it — the line drawn between us in the dim air, the omen laid on the wrong small thing and then on the king and then on the run between them. He has watched the seer name the telling, and a named thing cannot be left to thin the way the gladness thins.

He comes down off his broken lip.

---

He does not rush. The strong never rush the thing they have already weighed. He crosses the clean stone, the certain weight sliding, and the gentling goes out of the high stones ahead of him the way it goes out before the storm, the small ones pressing back into the piles, the bold one already gone off my shoulder and up the rock. The Kala's tide stops at my back. The great gray-silver bulk of her comes up over me, the keeping arm closing, and she makes a sound I have not heard her make — low, with the teeth in it, set between the king and the thing she chose. She would stand between us. The thing that carried me out of the fever-fog would put her own great body in the run the king is sliding down.

I lay my scarred hand on the gray-silver arm and I make the still-sound back at her, soft, falling, the one she taught me, *be still, you are held* — turned, this low, the wrong way, the small thing telling the great one to be still. Because if she stands between us, the king strikes her, and the law of the strength reads her standing as the proof of the thing he means to prove: that the wrong small thing has no standing of its own, that it stands only inside the arm that keeps it, that the turning toward me is borrowed off her bulk the way I borrowed every day of ground I ever stood on. If she fights for me, I am a kept thing, a thing on lent rock that pours away the moment the great arm is moved.

I will not be a kept thing. I have been a kept thing — the hand closed on the back of the neck, the brand laid in the flat tongue, *mine, be still, come, you are a thing*, the old barb turning in the warm dark behind my teeth where the three are gone. I will not stand inside another arm and let it carry my standing. I take my hand off her arm and move out from under the keeping, onto the clean stone, the smallest thing on the high stones, alone on the open rock in front of the whole turning, with the king sliding his weight down toward the place where I have stood.

The Kala's sound dies in the great chest. She holds. I feel the holding cost her the way the still-sound cost me. She lets me stand out of her arm onto the killing stone, because she has read the same law I have read: that the only standing worth the name is the one no arm carries.

---

He stops over me.

The flat eyes go down the great length of him to the small scarred thing on the open stone, and there is no warmth in them and no letting-stand, only the weighing, the *could* and not the *is*, the look one strong thing lays on another across a stone they both want. He is half again my height and many times my weight, the heavy bulk of the high canopy and the broken ridges, the strength that is the whole law of this rock. He could close the great hand on the back of my neck and end the telling in a breath, the way he has ended every telling that ever came up the rock toward him.

He does not close the hand. That is the thing I read, and the reading sets the whole shape of what is coming. If he closed the hand he would be ending me off-hand, with no thought in it, the way he turned the small ones off the rock the first season — and the tribe has seen the wrong thing bring down the apex the king turned away from, and it would read the closed hand as the king putting away a thing he could not stand beside. He cannot put me away quiet. The seer has named the telling. He has to win it in front of the turning, strength laid against the thing the tribe followed down into the rot, so that when it is done the whole rock reads the law of the strength standing whole over the wrong thing that read the ground.

So he does not close the hand. He lifts the great head and he makes the sound.

It comes up slow out of the deep of the chest, lower than the gentling, lower than the asking, a sound with the whole weight of the high stones in it and the teeth out — not the careful teeth he laid on the old male the low he reminded the strong whose ground it was, the full teeth, the bare ones. It rolls down the clean stone and the whole tribe goes still under it, the way the green goes still under the storm before the storm breaks, and I do not have their word for the sound but I have every grain of its weather, the thing one strong throws at the thing on its stone it means to settle: *here. This stone. You and me. Now, in front of them all.*

The challenge. Laid down bare on the clean stone in front of the whole turning.

And the thing the body does then, the thing it does before the head can find the words to be afraid with — it does not step back.

---

There is a place where the deciding sits, and it is not in the head. The head has all the law. The head knows I am the smallest thing on the high stones, that strength is the king's whole stone and there is more of it in his one arm than in the whole of me, that the body cannot win this the way the body has won everything else, by being light and reading the ground, because there is no ground to read on the clean stone — no rot to make the mouth, no high seat to turn the strength on, only the open rock and the king and the small thing, and the king's whole law that on the open rock the strength is the way and the path to the way is more strength.

The head knows all of it, and the body does not step back.

It does not step back because of the far mouth. The second mouth, the one no kill ever fed, the one that leaned out of me my whole life into the empty and found nothing to lean on — it found a thing to lean on here. The Kala chose me in the fever-fog before there was a thing to choose me for. The old male lifted my wrist before the whole bank and named me. The tribe turned, and the turning was the first ground my whole life that did not pour away the moment a hand opened, the first home the world had not taken from me. A thing too light to fight for itself is not too light to fight for the only home it has ever had. If I step back off the clean stone the king's law reads it true: the wrong thing has no standing of its own, the turning was borrowed, the home is lent. And then the ground pours away, and I am a thing on lent rock again, kept inside another arm, property again, the brand laid back on me in the only tongue this rock knows.

I will not give the home back. I gave a name away once to the hands that took me, all but the one behind the teeth, and I have spent every low since taking the rest of myself back off the killing ground a day at a time.

So I hold the name behind my teeth, in the gap where the three are gone, the one un-stolen thing, *Rook*, the word my mother gave me before the ship, before the hands, and I bite down on it the way I bit it down in the rot beside the Hesh, the stone you bite when the body wants to bolt and the bolting is the death of you. And I lift my own small head to the great flat-eyed bulk over me on the open rock, the smallest thing on the high stones, with nothing in my two hands and no ground to read and no arm to stand inside, and I make the sound back.

It comes up out of me low and torn, scraping over the gap where the teeth are gone, nothing of the king's weight in it and all of his meaning. *Here. This stone. You and me.* The asking thrown back as an answering. The challenge taken.

The whole tribe reads it. The smallest, softest, wrongest thing on the high stones has stood out of the keeping arm onto the open rock and answered the strength-king's challenge in his own bare tongue, and the rock goes so still I can hear the steam working off the green below us and the far small water in the channels and the slow vast tide of the Kala's breath behind me, holding, holding, not coming between.

---

The king holds the weighing look on me a long breath after the answer, and a thing moves far back in the flat eyes that is the nearest the strength-stone comes to warmth or to letting-stand: the thing one strong reads in another when the other does not break. He came down the rock sure the wrong thing would step back, that the turning was borrowed off the Kala's bulk, that one bare challenge laid down full-toothed would set the whole telling back to *is* and not *could*. And the wrong thing did not step back. It stood out of the arm and answered.

I have not won a thing. The answer is not the winning. The strength is still his whole stone and there is still more of it in his one arm than in the whole of me, and the clean stone is still the one ground my reading cannot read. The telling the seer named is not done — it is only begun, laid down bare between us on the open rock, to be finished the way the strong finish a thing, body to body, in front of the turning, the next low or the low after, coming now as sure as the tide, no longer a thing weighed and waiting but a thing said aloud and answered and owed.

He turns his great heavy body and goes back up onto his broken lip, slow, the certain weight sliding, and he does not look back, and the not-looking is not the dismissing not-looking of the first season. It is the not-looking of a strong thing that has been answered on its own stone and must now go and weigh, again, the thing it was sure it had finished weighing.

The Kala folds down over me when the king is up, the keeping arm coming over the small thing that stood out of it, and she does not make the still-sound this low, the *nothing comes*, because the thing that comes is named now and a thing on the high stones does not lie to the kept young about the named thing. She makes the other sound, the low even one with the gladness folded in it, the sound the tribe makes when the killing thing has lifted off it and not taken it this time — only it is laid on me, soft, and there is a thing under the gladness I have no shelf for, the thing a strong one lays on a thing that stood where it did not have to stand. I make it back, low and torn, two falls scraping over the gap where three teeth were gone, the far mouth full, the near one easy.

The bold small one comes back to my shoulder in the dark when the rock has gone quiet, the quick heart gone easy against my neck, the keeping-place become the thing it keeps. I lie in the warm with the near and the far, the small one's quick heart and the Kala's slow vast tide, and I lay the new law down behind my teeth, in the gap where the name lies, beside the brand I will give back someday to the hands that laid it on me, the hardest the high stones have taught me yet:

*A standing no arm carries is a standing you must stand to the end alone. The home worth keeping is the one you have to step out of the keeping arm to keep.*

I do not sleep. I lie in the warm with the second mouth full and the challenge laid down bare on the clean stone, and over all of it I lay the next going-up, the one the seer drew the line to, body to body on the open rock where there is no ground to read and no green to make the arm. The body keeps it, the way the shore taught it to keep a thing either way it goes.

The shore gives once, and then it makes you go further. I have gone as far as the apex of my green and brought its head up the rock, and the high stones have only the one thing left on them stronger than the thing I killed. I have stood on the clean stone and answered it in its own tongue, and there is no further left to go but the one. The strength-king is up on his broken lip in the dark, weighing, and the telling will be finished the next low or the low after, on the open rock where my whole life of reading the ground is worth nothing and the only thing in my two hands is the home I will not give back.
