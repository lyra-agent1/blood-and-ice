# Chapter 12: Belonging

There is a low that comes when the rains break.

It comes after the long season of the standing water, when the green has drowned itself to the chest and the rot-vapor lies thick on the channels and every low is the same drowned green low as the one before it, the sky shut down close over the high stones and the warm rain coming and coming until the body forgets there was ever a thing that was not falling water. Then one dark the rain thins. And the next it stops in the deep of the night, between two breaths, the way a heart stops, and I wake in the kept warm against the Kala with my ear to the great slow tide of her and I do not know what woke me, and then I know. It is the quiet. The rain has gone off the high stones and left them dripping and steaming in the dark, and through the broken roof of the green the lights come back, far and small and many, the lights I counted alone in my green low after low when there was no other thing to count.

I do not count them this dark.

That is the thing I learn, lying still in the steam with the lights come back. I have stopped counting them. I lie in the warm with the rain gone and the whole drowned world breathing out below us, and I do not count the lights between, because I am not in the between. For the first time in all my counting there is no between.

---

The break of the rains is a thing the whole tribe knows in the body the way I knew the turn of the tide.

They wake all down the shelf in the steaming dark, the big ones lifting their great dark-hided heads, the small ones stirring out of the piles of warm where they sleep heaped like a litter of one thing. And they make a sound I have not heard before, low, going from mouth to mouth down the rock — a long even sound with the gladness folded into it, the sound a body makes when the killing thing has lifted off it and not taken it this time. The rains are a killing thing. The standing water brings the wet rot up into the small ones' chests, and the season takes some of them — I have seen the Kala carry a still small one up to the high clean shelf where the squared dead lie faced to the sun, and lay it down small and squared with the rest, and make the long meaning-sound over it, the one for the kept and the gone. The rains are the thing that does that. And now the rains have lifted.

The tribe pours down off the high stones into the steaming green to take what the lifted rains have left.

---

I go down with them, and this low I do not go down behind them.

This is the new thing, and the body knows it before the head can name it. There was a long season when I went down after them, the soft slow weak thing trailing the strong, kept on borrowed stone — going down to set my loops in the runs the heavy foot could not follow, to take the burrow-thing and the clever meat off the bared flats the Vorth go hungry past, and carry it up in my two hands to the fire. That taking bought me my place a day at a time, the way I bought every day of standing I have ever had, borrowed back off the killing ground and pouring away the moment the hand opens. But this low when the tribe pours down, a big one waits at the head of the run.

It waits for me.

It is the heavy old male with the torn ear, the one that stood a whole season at the lip of the rot-thickets and went up empty because the soft ground would not hold him. He has seen me cross that ground — seen the man-shape go out light over the skin of the rot where his own weight goes through into the soup beneath, read the run, bring back the wallowing fat-meat beast the soft ground hid from him. And this low he waits with his torn ear and his small dark patient eyes, and when I come he makes the asking-sound, the rising one, *where, where* — and tips his great head down the bank toward the rot-thicket where the ground will not hold him.

He is asking the weakest thing on the high stones to read him the country his strength cannot cross.

I go ahead of him. Down the bank, out onto the skin of the rot where I am light and he is not, reading the run with my soles, and he comes behind me set in my track, foot for foot where I have shown the ground will hold. I am the eye and he is the hand. I find the thing and he closes on it, the great arm doing in one blow what my whole weight could not, and we bring it up between us, his kill on my reading.

I am not trailing the strong anymore. The strong is set in my track.

---

The bold small one is mine now. The whole tribe knows it.

It started in the firelight, the small dark quick one that took to me the way a thing takes to warm, that rode my chest and laid the world's names on me one at a time. I take them still. But it is more than the names now. It comes up the line of my torn voice when I throw the asking-sound, *here, here,* quicker than it goes to any big one, and it rides my shoulder down to the green and back, and when the half-grown ones cuff it to teach it the order of the stone, it does not run to the Kala. It runs to me. It puts the man-shape between it and the cuffing the way a small thing puts a big one between it and the hurt, and the half-grown one draws up short — not because I could turn its head a finger's width, I could not — but because the man-shape stands between, and the small one is behind it, and that is a thing that means *kept*, and the half-grown one reads the meaning and turns away.

I file it on no shelf. It will not file. A small warm thing has chosen the man-shape for its keeping-place, has run to me out of all the strong of the high stones to be the thing between it and the hurt, and there is no shelf in me for a thing chosen back. The second mouth takes it instead, the far one, the one no kill ever fed — it takes the small one's running-to-me and closes around it, the way a hand closes on a thing it means to keep and not crush.

I am a keeping-place now. I, who have been a kept thing and a stolen thing and a thing a hand closes on, am the thing a small one runs to. The line runs the other way now. Something comes up it to me to be held, and I hold it, and the holding does the thing to the far mouth that no meat has ever done.

---

The Kala lays me with the squared dead.

I do not understand it the low it happens, the way I did not understand the seer's finger on my throat. The high clean shelf where the sun strikes first is the keeper's ground, hers and the seer's and no other living foot's — the still ones lie there, squared and faced to the sun, the gifts set with them, faced and ordered, brought up out of the green and laid down to mean. I have watched her tend them from below the whole season, going up the bare clean rock to square the dead and face the gifts. No foot of mine has gone up there. The body learned it the way it learns the king's bare stone — by being stopped at the edge of it.

But this low she does not stop me at the edge. This low she takes me up.

She comes off the high shelf in the steaming dark after the rains have broken, and she takes my hand in her great dark hand the way she takes a small one's, sure and soft, and turns and goes back up, and does not let my hand go, and so I go up after her onto the clean ground where the dead are faced to the sun. I go up stiff, the body braced for the arm to come down across my chest and turn me, the way the arm turned me off the king's stone the first low — but no arm comes. There is only her hand around mine, leading the man-shape up onto the keeper's ground where it has never set foot, past the squared small ones I have seen her carry up, past the gone old ones in their lines.

And she stops at a clear place among them, a bare squared place at the sun-struck edge, and she lays her great dark hand flat on it, and then she lays my hand on it beside hers, and she makes the long meaning-sound. The one for the kept. The one she makes over the squared dead and the faced gifts. The one the seer made over the eye at my chest and over the torn voice out of my throat. The small dark eyes come down to my face, close, reading me the way she read me the first low, and I understand it at last, the body first and the head a long way after.

She is showing me my place among the laid-down.

She is telling me, in the only tongue there is between us, that when the gone-low comes for me however it comes, this is where I go. Up here. On the clean ground. Squared and faced to the sun with the kept. She has laid the man-shape into the order of the high clean shelf, the deepest order the Vorth have, the one that says *this was ours, this we keep even gone* — and there is a place in it now shaped to me, and her hand is on it, and mine under hers.

The far mouth does not widen at this. It is past widening. It goes still the way it went still the first time against her warm, the deep stillness I have no word for. To be given a place among the kept dead is to be given a place among the living that does not pour away when the hand opens. It is the un-borrowed stone, the first ground I have stood on since before the black that is not lent by a hand that can take it back.

I have never had a place to be laid down. The hold makes a thing and throws it over the side when it stops being meat; the green eats what falls and squares nothing and faces nothing to any sun. To be a thing that will be laid down squared and kept — that is to be a thing that *was.* And I sit on the keeper's clean ground with my hand under the Kala's on the place she cut for me, and I think this is the bottom of it. I think the second mouth, that no kill ever fed, is full.

---

The king comes up onto the clean shelf.

It is the one thing that should not happen, and it happens slow, the way the worst weather comes slow, bone-sure before the sky shows it. The gentling goes out of the high stones. The small ones feel it first, the bold one slipping off my shoulder and away. And Gull-Marr comes up off his broken lip and crosses the bare stone the others leave bare and climbs onto the high clean shelf where the dead are faced, the clear bare stone moving up with him, and he stops over the Kala and the man-shape with its hand on the squared place, and looks down at us, and his flat eyes do not warm and do not tip.

I brace for the arm. I have braced for the arm the whole season. The king has not chosen me, and here I am on the keeper's clean ground with my hand on a cut place among his dead, the soft slow weak wrong thing laid into the order that is the tribe's own marrow, and the king has come up to turn me off it. The body knows the arm is coming. The body has known a hand was coming the whole of its life. The hand on the back of the neck. The closing. The brand. I sit with my hand under the Kala's and the old barb turns in the warm dark behind my teeth, the ugly hold-word that did not leave, *mine, be still, come, you are a thing* — and I wait for the king to lay it on me in his own flat tongue and turn me off the kept ground back onto the borrowed.

The Kala does not move her hand off mine.

She lifts her head to the king, the great heavy silver-shot head, and she holds his flat eyes, and she does not make the keeping-sound she threw across the bare stone the first low. She makes nothing. She only holds his look with her hand flat on my hand on the squared place, the whole still vast bulk of her unmoving between the king and the thing she has laid among her dead, and the seer comes slow up onto the clean shelf on the king's far side, the old pale-furred head low, and lifts one long dark finger and points it — not at me, at the eye bound at my chest, the ringed looking and the thorn driven through it — and makes the long meaning-sound, the one for the kept, the one for a thing that comes from before and means.

And the king looks from the mark to the cut place to the Kala's hand on my hand, and the flat eyes go over the whole of it, slow, the way I go over a thing on the shelf of wrong things — a thing that should not be, and is, and has not been turned over. And he does not lay the brand on me. And he does not turn me off the stone.

He makes a sound. Low, and short, and flat — but the teeth are out of it. Not the careful one he made the first low, the *what is this, why is this on my stone.* Shorter than that, and flatter, with a thing in it I know in the body before the head can find it, the thing a strong one lays on a thing it has weighed and will let stand — not chosen, not warmed-to, but *allowed,* set into the order of the stone by the one whose stone it is. He turns his great heavy body and goes back down off the clean shelf, the bare stone going with him, and he does not turn me off the keeper's ground.

The king has let it stand.

The Kala kept by her own choosing, low after low, against his sufferance — and this low he has stopped suffering it and started letting it stand, which turns borrowed ground into ground that *is.* The seer marked me. The Kala cut me a place among her dead. And the king came up onto the highest order of the high stones and weighed the whole of it and let it stand. Three of them. I am not on borrowed ground anymore. I am laid into the order of the tribe at every height there is — from the small one that runs to me to be held, up through the keeper who cut me a place among her gone, up to the king whose word is the law of the stone.

---

I lie in the warm that dark with the rains gone and the lights come back through the broken green, and I do not count them.

The bold small one is heaped against my chest, half asleep, learning the still-sound after the Kala and me, throwing the clumsy wrong shape of it up at the warm. The Kala folds down beside us, the great warm silver-shot bulk, and lays the arm over me, sure, the keeping arm, and makes the still-sound, soft, falling, *be still, you are held, nothing comes.* And I make it back, low and torn, soft-then-softer, two falls, scraping over the gap where three teeth were torn — and the small one makes it after us both, and the three of us throw the one soft sound back and forth between us in the steaming dark above the drowning green, around and around, and no part of it closes like a hand.

The far mouth is full.

I do not have a head for it. I have only the body, and the body has never known the thing before. The near mouth I have filled a thousand times, with meat and water, low after low; the near mouth is an easy mouth and an honest one and it empties again with the next low and you go and fill it again. But the far one — the second mouth, the far hunger under the breastbone that no kill ever answered, that has leaned out of me toward a thing to turn and see me and make me a thing worth a sound, since before the black, since the mouth that made sounds at me in the dark went down into the deep water and left me a thing no other thing answered — the far mouth is full. Not the near edge of it. The whole drowned-deep of it, where the lost name lies and the buzzing turns over in its sleep — full, and warm, and still.

I have a name behind the teeth that no one has spoken, the one un-stolen thing, my mother's gift, held under the tongue. I have the brand beside it, the ugly closing-hand sound that did not leave, that I will give back someday across the water to the ones it belongs to. And now I have a third thing in the warm dark with them, laid down the deepest I have ever laid a thing: *the far mouth is filled, and the name for it is here, this, mine, kept.*

I do not have their word for it. I have all of the weather of it. A thing turned and saw me and made me a thing worth a sound, and then a tribe did, at every height there is, and the far mouth that leaned out of me my whole life into nothing found, not the near edge of a saving, but the whole of a home.

I lie in the warm with the small one's heart going quick against my chest and the Kala's going slow and vast under my ear, the near and the far, the quick and the slow, and the going-on is not the thing I do anymore to get to the next low. The going-on is the thing I do to stay in this one.

I would do it again. I do not know yet what it will cost me to have learned it — that the far mouth, once filled, is a thing that can be emptied, all at once, by a hand reaching in across the water; that the fullest it has ever been is the exact measure of how much there is now to take. I only know, this low, that the leaning-out is over.

I am held. Nothing comes.

I let the far mouth keep it, and I sleep in the warm, the lights uncounted over the high stones, and for this one low the world is the home and the home is the world, and there is no between.
