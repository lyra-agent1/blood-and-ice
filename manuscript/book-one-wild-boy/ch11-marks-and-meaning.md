# Chapter 11: Marks and Meaning

I take the skins back out at the dark, after the fire, when the high stone is mine and the Kala has folded down into her own still.

I have carried them up from the wreck a moon now, rolled in a sleeve of fat-soaked hide against the wet, and I have not opened them. There was no place in me to open them. The shore gave me only the laws of the shore, and the laws of the shore do not read marks; they read tide and bank and the worn pale line in the mud where the small feet go. But up here among the Vorth a thing has loosened in me. The sounds are coming back, one stolen at a time, laid in the gap behind my teeth — *low*, *high*, *come*, the gentling, the question-sound — and where the sounds come the rest stirs, the drowned-deep place rolls in its sleep, and the body says, the way the body says everything before the head: *now. Try them now.*

So I unroll the skins on the bare dark stone in the red light, careful, the way I lay a thing down that the wet wants, and I bend my face close over the rows the dead men scratched into the dead skin, and I try to make them speak.

They will not.

I knew they would not. They would not at the wreck and they will not here. The marks lie in their rows, ordered the way nothing in the green is ordered, line under line under line, small and dark and patient, and the body knows past all doubt that they are made to mean, that there is a way of taking the rows in through the eye and having them come out the other side as sound and shape and the thing a man wanted to say before he lay down to die. I had that way once. I am sure of it the way I am sure of the name behind the teeth — sure of the warm hollow where it lay, and finding only the warm, and nothing in it. The reading is gone the way the name is gone. The slavers took one and the green took the other and I am left bent over the rows with the buzzing low in the drowned place, the buzzing that is almost a shape and never breaks the brown water.

But I do not roll them up.

---

I work at them.

This is the thing the shore taught me that the green cannot starve out: a thing you do not understand, you do not turn from. You do not turn from the run you cannot read; you crouch by it low after low until the body learns the shape of the small feet that wore it. You do not turn from the wet fire dying; you make it a thousand times until the making is in your hands. So I do not turn from the marks. I make myself their student the way I made myself the Kala's, small, patient, going in through the only door there is.

I find that the rows are not all one thing. The body sees it before the head, the way it sees the run in the mud. Most of the marks are the small busy rows, line on line, the kind I cannot crack — but here and there among them the dead hand laid down other things, larger, standing apart, and these the body half-takes. A shape that is the curve of a coast, the bend of a reach, the bar of black mud — I have walked that bend, I have stood on that bar among the bones. The dead man drew my green. He drew the broken country, the channels and the high stone and the drowned reaches, scratched it small on the skin the way the green is scratched large on my soles, and where his lines bend I know the bend, because the body walked it. This one I can read. Not in the head. In the feet.

And there are the other shapes. The small standing ones the busy rows make way around, the ones a hand drew slow and meaning. And among them, twice — three times — driven into the dead skin in dark deliberate line, the eye.

The eye. Ringed, open, looking. And through it, crossing it, the thorn.

The disc at my chest goes warm against the breastbone, or I think it does, the way a thing you stare at long enough begins to move. I lay the worked metal beside the drawn skin in the red light and they are the same mark, the same hand-truth in them, the true round and the true line — the thing on my chest and the thing the dead man scratched are one thing, made twice, by men, before the green. I put my torn finger on the drawn eye and then on the worked one and the buzzing comes up out of the drowned place, the long no-bottom wave, *I know this, this is mine* — and no when, and no whose, and the brown water closes over it again and leaves me only the two marks and the dark and the red light and the want.

---

The far mouth stands all the way up over the skins.

It does this now. It has done it since the wreck — leaned the whole of itself toward the marks, toward the men, toward the eye that is mine and means nothing — but up here it leans worse, because up here the second mouth has learned what it is to be filled. The Kala fills it. The folding-down beside me in the warm fills it. The heads tipping toward the man-shape that fed the dead red live fills it. I know now, in the body, what the ache is for: it is for a thing to turn and see me and make me a thing worth a sound. The tribe turns and sees me. The tribe makes the sounds.

But the tribe cannot read the marks. The Kala laid her great dark finger on my loop and half-saw the thinking in me; she cannot lay it on the rows and find the man who drew them. The marks are a thing from the before, from the world that took me and the world I came from, both the same kind, all gone now to bone on the bar — and the far mouth leans out toward that world over the dead skin and finds it dead. The eye looks out from my chest and from the drawn rows and tells me nothing, the way the bones told me nothing, the way the name tells me nothing in the gap where the three teeth were torn.

Two things are mine and gone. The name, and the reading. The slavers got the one — though they never knew to reach for the name, only the closing hand on the back of the neck, only the brand, the low ugly sound behind their teeth that meant *mine, be still, come, you are a thing*, and that one stayed when the true one left, lodged in the warm dark like a barb I cannot work out of the flesh. And the green starved the other, the reading, low by low, the way it starved the name. I have the eye and I cannot read it. I have the rows and I cannot read them. I am a man bent over a thing made to speak, and I have lost the mouth that hears it.

I lay the law down behind my teeth with the rest. *A thing made to mean does not stop meaning because you have lost the way of it.* The run is still a run when you cannot read the feet. The reading will come back, or it will not, the way the name will come back, or it will not. But the marks are not dead while I keep them. So I keep them. I roll the skins careful in the fat-soaked sleeve, and I loop the disc back over my neck, the eye out against my chest, the thorn through it, and I do not turn it over, and I do not let it go.

---

The seer comes to me at the marks the way it came to me at the loop.

I do not hear it come. It is old past the others, the body sunk in on itself, the dark hide gone ash and near-white with the years, and it moves the way the oldest things move, slow and without sound, so that I look up from the rows and it is simply there, crouched at the edge of the red light, the milk-pale eyes already on me, already reading, taking the whole shape of a thing before it goes down into it.

It does not look at me first. It looks at the skins.

This is new. The young ones look at the skins and make the dismissing sound, the one laid on a thing that is no use — dark scratches on dead hide, no meat in them, no warm, nothing the body can do with. The seer does not make the dismissing sound. It bends its great near-white head down over the rows, slow, and its eyes go along the lines the way the Kala's finger went along the loop, following the made-thing back to the hand that made it — and it knows, the way it knows everything, that the rows are made. It does not know to read them. But it knows they are a thing that is read. It is the only one of them that knows the difference, and the knowing of the difference is itself a thing I did not think to find up here on the high stone, and the far mouth lifts at it the way it lifts at every saving.

Then the seer lays its finger on the disc at my chest.

Not on me. On the eye. It follows the ring with the old pale fingertip, around, the way it followed the loop, and then the thorn, down the length of the barb that pierces the looking — and it makes the sound. The long low one. The one it made the first low it came to me, the one with the thing in it bent toward, turned up, the not-dismissing sound — and I have it now, near enough, I have stolen enough of their tongue to hold the shape of it. It is the sound they lay on the squared dead and the gifts faced and set on the high stones. It is the sound for a thing that comes from before and means.

The seer reads the eye-and-thorn the way it reads its own old signs. As a thing of the kept. A kin-omen. A mark that means.

---

And then it does the thing it did at the loop, and worse.

It lifts the old finger off the disc, and it lays it against me. Not the side of my head this time, where the thinking is, where it laid it after the loop. Lower. It lays the flat of the worn pale finger against the side of my throat, where the blood goes, where the great pulse runs up under the skin into the head — and it holds it there, light, the way I lay my finger along the worn line in the mud, reading the thing that passes under.

It feels the blood go.

I hold still under it. The beast comes up a little at the touch on the throat, the old place, the place the hand closes, the place the brand lives — but this is not the closing hand, this is the lightest pale pad of a finger laid to listen, and I let the beast settle, and I let the seer read the blood the way I let the Kala fold the green branch down. And the seer goes very still over the finger. Its milk-pale eyes change. The same thing goes over them that went over the Kala's the low she half-saw the thinking, the same thing the old pale head did over the bones of the thing that owned my green — a thing turning toward a thing it did not look for and now half-sees.

It feels something in the blood.

I do not know what it feels. I do not have it to know. But I know my blood is one of the wrong things — I know it the way I know all of them, filed low on the shelf, not turned over: the green that could not eat me, the water that knots every belly but mine, the venom that goes out of me fast and leaves no mark, the wound that draws shut clean and quick in the wet where every other wound goes to rot and fly and gangrene, the fever-air I sleep in and wake whole from, the swarms that ring me and never land. The dead men on the bar drank my water and breathed my air and lay down where they stood and died of the green I live in, every one. The wrongness is in the blood. The seer lays its finger on the throat and feels the blood go and the milk-pale eyes change, and I understand, the body first and the head long after, that it has found the same thing the green found and could not finish — the thing that should not be, running warm and unkilled under the skin of the man-shape it took in.

It looks from the throat to the disc. From the blood to the eye-and-thorn. And it lays them side by side the way it laid the loop and the side of my head, two halves of one strangeness — the mark that is mine from before, and the blood that the green cannot kill — and it bows the great near-white head over both of them at once, slow, the long meaning-sound coming up low out of the sunk old chest, the sound for the kept, the omened, the thing that comes from before and means.

---

I do not understand what it knows.

I crouch under the bowed head with the rows of dead marks spread on the stone and the eye against my breastbone and the seer's old finger gone now from my throat, and the head reaches after the thing and finds nothing it can close on — only the seer bent over me, naming me with the sound it lays on the squared dead, reading the blood I cannot read and the mark I cannot read both at once, sure of a thing about me that I am not sure of, that I have only filed, low, in the dark, on the deepest shelf, the one under all the others. *The green kills my kind and does not kill me.* And under it now, laid down this low, a new thing, deeper: *the old one feels it in the blood, and it is the same as the mark, and the mark is mine from before.*

I do not turn it over. There is no turning it over. The head has no shape for it, the way it had no shape for the bones and the standing and the breathing on the bar. But the body files it, and the far mouth — the second mouth, the one no kill has ever fed, the one the Kala has begun to feed — leans out under it into the red light, and finds, this once, that it is not leaning into nothing.

The bones did not turn. The marks do not speak. But the seer turns. The seer, bent over my blood and my mark, half-reading a thing in me I cannot read in myself — the seer is an eye that is not my eye, turned on me, and it does not look away, and it does not make the dismissing sound. It makes the meaning-sound. It lays me with the kept.

It is not the same as being read. The man who scratched the rows could have read me, the marks and the blood and the name behind the teeth, read me whole — and he is bone on the bar, and the way he had is gone out of the world, or gone only out of me. The seer cannot read me. It can only feel that there is a reading. It lays its finger on the locked thing and knows it for a locked thing that means, and bows its head to the meaning it cannot reach.

But it is more than the bones gave. It is more than the green ever gave. To be felt for and found and named *kept*, by the oldest eye on the high stone, with the wrong blood going warm and unkilled under its finger and the eye-and-thorn looking out from my chest — it is enough. It fills the near edge of the far mouth, the way the loop did, the way the fed fire did. Not the whole of it. The whole of it wants the reading, wants the man who could take the rows in and give them back as my own true shape, wants the name out of the gap and the meaning back into the eye. That hunger does not close. It never has. It never will, until a thing turns and reads me whole.

But the seer turns. And the seer finds. And the far mouth leans into the finding and is, for this low, in the red light, on the high stone, with the marks spread and the Kala's slow vast heart going through the dark beside me — not fed full, but fed.

---

I roll the skins back into the fat-soaked sleeve at the dark when the seer has gone. I loop the disc against my chest, the eye out, the thorn through it. I lay the new law down behind my teeth, in the gap where the name was, beside the stolen sounds and the gentling that is not mine and the warm that chose to hold me and the brand I will give back someday across the water to the ones it belongs to.

*The thing that is locked is still a thing that means. And a thing that means draws an eye, even an eye that cannot read it.*

The marks are made to speak and I have lost the mouth that hears them. The eye is mine and tells me nothing. The blood runs warm where every other blood went to fever and went still, and I do not know why, and I do not turn it over. But the oldest eye on the high stone has laid its finger on all of it and bowed its head, and named me kept, and that naming sits in me now beside the want it cannot fill — the want for the reading, the want to be taken in whole and given back, the want of the second mouth that no kill ever fed.

The Kala folds down beside me in the failing red, the great dark bulk of her, and lays the arm over me, and makes the gentling sound, the keeping one. I lie against the warm that knows it holds me, the rolled marks under my arm, the eye against my breastbone, the wrong blood going warm and unkilled under the seer's gone finger. Somewhere out in the dark of me, behind the gone teeth, in the drowned-deep place, the buzzing turns over once in its sleep — the marks, the eye, the name, all of it locked, all of it mine, all of it waiting on a mouth that can hear.

It will come back, or it will not. The shore taught me to keep the thing either way.

I keep it. The eye looks out from my chest into the dark, and the thorn goes through the looking, and the blood goes warm under the seer's name for it, and I hold the whole of it in the warm where the lost name lies, and I go on.
