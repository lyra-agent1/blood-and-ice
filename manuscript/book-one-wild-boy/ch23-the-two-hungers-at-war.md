# Chapter 23: The Two Hungers at War

I have two mouths, and for the first time in my life they hunger in two directions.

---

I learn it on the spit, low after low, the way the body learns a thing the head will not yet hold: that I am two roads now, and each low I walk both, and each low they pull further apart.

There is the up road. At the bare of the dark I go up off the bone-spit into my green, up the slick rot and the broken stone, to the high shelves where the Kala lies vast and slow with the small ones heaped warm against her and the home with no between in it waits the way it has always waited. I go up and I lie down in the kept warm with my ear to the great tide of her, and the near mouth is fed and the far mouth was full there once, full all the way down, and the body knows that road the way the soles know the runs — it is mine, it is bled into being a day at a time, it is the only crown I have ever worn.

And there is the down road. At the heavy of the next low, when the owning-fire goes thick with grease, I pour myself down off the high stones, down the rot I just climbed, down to the high drowned roots over the spit where I lie poured into the mud and watch the upright pack at its fires. The down road is new. The down road is a handful of lows old against a whole life of the up. But the far mouth leans down it now and will not come up, because at the bottom of the down road there is the man-shaped hole, and the warm of the fires that are not mine, and the owned girl naming the world one stone at a low to the dark she thinks cannot answer.

Two roads. The same body. And the body cannot lie on both ends of the spit at once.

The shore gives once, and then it makes you go further. I went all the way up the green, to the highest stone, and I thought the highest stone was the far end of going. It is not. The down road is further. The down road runs the one way I never thought to go again — back toward the hand, back toward the fires of the men, back toward the brand — and the far mouth has its teeth in me and is dragging me down it, low by low, and the up road is going thin in my mouth while I go.

---

The down road has changed since the keeper read my mark.

I read the change off the camp from above the way I read a turn in the weather. Before, the camp leaned only at its work — the hauling, the cutting, the dragging and lashing of the cages. Now it leans up the spit. At the green. At me. The captain has set watchers where the spit comes in, bodies posted to read the dark for the thing that wears the Eye-and-Thorn, and the fires burn longer, and the owning ones go nowhere now without the iron near the hand. They are looking out for the thing in the green. They are looking out for me.

And the camp is not only watching for me. It is calling — and the calling does not stop, low after low, since the keeper first set the warm thing at my line.

I feel it the way I feel a tide that will not turn back out. A thing goes out from the fires up the spit into the dark, every low, aimed at the green — not the closing brand-sound, not the *come* that means *mine, be still, you are a thing.* The other thing. The meat set out warm at my line and left, the bright kept-water beside it, the welcome laid out in the only road the camp has to lay it. They make the fires bright and they do not drive the swarms off and they let the grease-smoke roll up the spit into my green, and on the smoke comes the smell of the upright pack at its ease, fed and dry and many and warm — the smell of the warm I lost.

The keeper baits me still. He cannot find my shape in the still, so he lays out the thing the second mouth wants and waits for the second mouth to walk the body down onto the open ground where the iron can reach. He does not know the dull-water folded in the welcome slides off me low after low; he thinks he is laying me down heavy. He is wrong about the body and right about the want.

And the worst of it is the thing I had whole on the shelf last low and have no more power over this low than I did then: I know it is bait, and the far mouth leans at it anyway.

---

I have read bait all my life. It is the first law of the green and I learned it the first moon — the still pool that is too clear, the easy meat laid too plain, the trail that runs too good toward the place the ground will not hold. *A thing the world lays out easy is a thing the world means to close on.* I read the captain's bait whole, the meat and the smoke and the let-down guard that is not let down at all, and the near part of me, the green part, the part that made itself a king, says the law plain behind my teeth: *do not go down. Take the tribe and go deep, up into the fever-fog and the high broken stone where men die and you do not, where no boat reaches and no brand burns. A thing the keeper wants is a thing already half in the hold.*

The legs say it. The soles say it, dug into the mud. The whole green I bled into being says it.

And I do not go up. I lie in the streaming roots and I do not go up, and the far mouth holds me down on the down road by force, and I understand at last, the body first and the head long after: the two hungers were one road my whole life. The near mouth and the far mouth wanted the same thing — to live, to be fed, to be kept — and the green gave both at the high stones, the belly and the belonging in the one warm. But the keeper has cut them apart. He has laid the far mouth's exact food down on the open ground where the near mouth's death is. And now they pull against each other inside the one body, the near one hauling up the green toward life and the far one hauling down the spit toward the warm, and the rope between them is me, and it is fraying.

This is the war. Not the iron and the green. The war is in the breastbone, the two hungers turned at last against each other, and there is no eating that ends it.

---

She comes down still.

That is the thing that holds me on the down road past all the law: the keeper shut the road — kept her behind his fires, set the welcome out in his own hand — and still she comes. She slips the thornwood at the bare of the low and comes down the killing spit under the watcher's eye, and stands at my line and lays me the road one stone at a low.

She knows now that she is watched. I read it in her — the strung shape, the looking-back, the carrying-hand kept low against the mud where the watcher at the stumps cannot read it. And the keeper does not haul her back. He has read that the dark moves for her and not for him, and so he lets her slipping go, the way a man lets the hook go down with the bait on it, to see what rises. She knows it. She comes anyway. And while she makes her easy talking-to-the-dark sounds for the watcher to hear — the weed, the snail, the two leaning water-sounds — her low hand against the wet mud lays me the other road, the hand-road, the road under the road that I have read all my life when the mouth-road was shut to me. The eye and the thorn. The flying-apart. The closing hand pointed back at the camp. *The hand is coming. The mark has set them moving. I cannot keep it behind my own teeth so I bring it down to the wall of the green.*

She is the mirror, and I will not lie to the shelf about which she is. She is not the Kala, not the home with no between, not the thing that filled the second mouth full. She is the owned thing on the killing ground, the one who breaks away, the road-carrier with the lifting hand over her bent neck — a thing I look in and see my own neck, and a man can look in a mirror and not be looking at a home. I know which is the home. The home is up the green with the small ones heaped against the Kala's arm.

But the far mouth does not know mirror from home. It only knows that no thing has come down onto my killing ground under a watcher's eye to warn me of the hand since the Kala — and it leans at the mirror, hard, and the leaning is the war made flesh, because every low I go down to take her warning is a low the watcher reads the green a little better, a low the keeper's hook sinks a little deeper, a low the brand walks a step nearer up the spit toward the warm hollow where my name lies.

The far mouth is the door the brand walks through. I knew it. I filed it low on the shelf the low I let her see the green move. And I keep walking down to the door and standing in it, because the want is bigger than the law, and that — *that* — is the most human thing I have ever done, and the most exposed. The beast does not stand in open doors for want. The beast goes deep and lives. Only the man-shaped thing in me leans into the lit mouth of the trap because there is a body inside it that has been hungry the way I have been hungry, naming snails to the dark.

---

I go up to the Kala wrong now.

That is how I know the war is real and not a thing the head made up to chew. I go up the green at the bare of the dark and I lie down in the kept warm, and the Kala lifts her great gray head off the sleep and lays the small dark patient eye on me, and she reads me the way she has always read me — the way she read the thinking in me the first low, before there was a single sound between us. And what she reads now is the down road on me. The grease-smoke in my hair. The man-shape come back up the rot smelling of the fires of the upright pack.

She makes a sound low in the vast slow tide of her chest. Not the asking-sound, the rising *where, where.* A lower one. A settling one with a question folded under it, the sound she makes over a small one that has strayed too near the rot-thickets and come back smelling of the soft ground that does not hold. *Where do you go. What is on you. What is the smell of the thing that is not us.* And she lays the great arm over me the way she has laid it a thousand lows, the home with no between — and for the first low in all my counting there is a between. There is a between in the Kala's arm. I put it there. I carried it up the green on my skin off the down road, the smoke and the warning and the leaning, and now it lies in the warm between her tide and my ear, thin and new and wrong, and she feels it too, because she goes still around me in a way she has never gone still, holding a thing that has started, low by low, to belong to a warm that is not hers.

I have never lied to the Kala. There were no words to lie with. The road between us was the road under the road, the hand and the eye and the weight given, the body that does not lie. And now I lie to her with my whole body every low, going up the green carrying the down road on my skin, and she reads the lie the way she reads everything, plain, in the small patient eye, and she does not have the sounds to ask me whole and I do not have the sounds to tell her, and the between sits in her arm and grows.

This is the cost of the war. Not the iron yet. The iron is not come yet. The cost so far is the between in the Kala's arm where there was never a between, and it is the worst cost I have paid since the hold, and I pay it every low for the leaning of the far mouth at a fire that is not mine.

---

The seer reads it before the Kala can name it.

It comes up to the high shelf in the heavy of a low when the rains have thinned, the old gray one with the slow finger, the keeper of the tribe's old signs that bowed its head over the blood in my throat a hundred lows ago and named me *kept.* It comes to where I lie in the Kala's between-arm and it crouches its old weight down and it lays the gray finger to my chest. Not to the throat this time. To the chest. To the hard flat thing on the gut-cord, the Eye-and-Thorn, the shape the dead men died wearing and the keeper wears on his iron and the owned girl draws on the wet air with her low hand.

And the seer goes into the stillness. The turning-toward stillness, the half-seeing one. Its old finger rests on the eye and the thorn and its breath goes long and even, and it makes the meaning-sound it made the first time, the low one for the kept and the come-from-before — and then it makes a new one under it. A wrong one. A leaning-away one. Its small old eyes come up off the relic and go down the green, down the rot toward the spit, toward the fires it cannot see and has never seen and somehow reads off my skin and the shape on my chest — and the new sound it makes is the sound the tribe makes at the edge of the rot-thicket where the ground will not hold. The danger-sound. The *here, and no further.*

The seer reads the same shape two ways now, the way the keeper does, the way I do. It reads the eye and the thorn as kin-omen, a thing of the kept, a thing of before — and it reads, off the smoke in my hair and the down road on my skin, that the kin-omen has a hand reaching for it up the spit, and the hand is the thing the tribe has no sign for, the thing from across the water, the thing that takes the kept and carries it off into a hold. The seer cannot say the hold. It has never seen one. But it lays its gray finger on the mark that says *owned* and it makes the danger-sound at the green below, and I understand it whole, the body first: *the thing you wear is reaching for you, and the reach is coming from below, and the kept thing is being called down off the high stone toward the hand.*

It knows. The old gray seer knows what the Kala only feels — that I am being drawn down, that the down road ends at the brand, that the far mouth is walking me toward the one death the green could never deal me. And it has no more sounds for it than I do. It lays the finger down, and bows the old head, and goes back down the shelf into the steam, and leaves me lying in the between-arm with the danger-sound ringing under the eye and the thorn.

---

I lie between the two roads in the full dark and I hold the four things behind my teeth and I add no fifth, because the fifth thing has no shape yet, only a pull.

The name that is mine and answers nothing, held in the warm gap where three were torn. The brand that is theirs and means *you are a thing.* The road the owned girl lays me one stone at a low. The warning — the eye flying apart, the camp, the closing hand. Four things in the warm hollow, laid against each other, and over all of them now the danger-sound the seer made at the green, the *here, and no further,* aimed down the spit at the very road my far mouth will not stop walking.

I am most human here. I know it in the body. All the long climb up the green I was going the other way, going beast, going deep, paring the man down to the soles and the teeth until there was barely a man left to take — and here, at the bottom of the down road, with the grease-smoke in my hair and the girl's road behind my teeth and the man-shaped hole leaning open under my breastbone, I am more a man than I have been since the deck. And the man is the soft thing. The man is the thing the keeper can take. The beast goes deep and the green cannot eat it; the man stands in the lit mouth of the trap for the want of a warm that is not his, and the man is the thing already half in the hold.

The two hungers are at war, and I am the ground they fight on, and I cannot feed both without losing one. If I go up the green and stay up — take the tribe deep, never lay my shape where the weighing-look can cut it out of the dark — the near mouth lives and the far mouth starves at the door it will not leave. If I go down the spit and keep going — to the warm, to the road, to the mark that is mine and his both that I have to know the why of — the far mouth leans into the warm and the hand closes on the back of my neck, and I am cargo, and the between in the Kala's arm becomes the whole of the distance, water and a hold and the dark.

I do not choose this low. The body will not let the head choose yet; it only lies between the two ends of the spit and bleeds toward both. But I feel the choosing coming the way I feel a tide turn, slow and sure and from below, and I feel which way the far mouth leans, and I lie in the Kala's between-arm with her great slow tide under my ear and the danger-sound ringing and the down road bright in my mouth, and I am afraid — not of the iron, not of the green, of nothing I can lay a finger to — afraid the way a thing is afraid that already knows it will walk down the lit road anyway, into the warm, into the closing hand, because the far mouth is bigger than the law and always was.

I have been a king of the wild things. I have been a beast the green could not eat. And here at the bottom of my own crown, torn in two by the two hungers that fed me my whole life and have turned at last against each other, I am only what I have always been under all of it — a thing the world keeps reaching for, leaning toward the one warm it should run from, with the hand already coming up the spit out of the before, and my own far mouth holding the door for it.
