# Chapter 17: King of the Wild Things

The strong always come at you in the rush. That is the law I have read off every strong thing in my green, off the old male in the rot, off the Hesh on the moved line. The strength does not wait. It is sure, and the sure thing comes fast, and the fast thing forgets to read the ground.

So I do not give the king the ground he reads.

---

He comes up off his broken lip in the green half-light of the low with the whole law of the strong behind him, the high stones clear of everything that is not us. The tribe has gone to the high rock and the far edges, the small ones gathered in, the half-grown ones still — the way the green empties itself ahead of the Hesh, the hole in the noise where a hole should not be. The seer stands apart, the ash-pale head up, not pointing, only watching. Gull-Marr crosses the bare stone toward me, the heavy flat-eyed bulk of him moving slow because the slow is its own telling: *I do not have to hurry to the thing I am going to take.*

I have stood under that telling on a deck. I have stood under it in a hold with the hand closed on the back of my neck. The body knows the slow certain coming-on, and it does not go still under it this low. It has learned the slow certain thing is the thing that bends.

He is twice me and more, the great gray-silver arms that turn a Vorth off a rock with no thought in them, the flat eyes that have weighed every challenge the stone ever threw up and let none of them stand. I am the smallest, softest, wrongest thing on the high stones. I have no arm that will turn his head a finger's width. Every standing I have earned on this rock I earned without one, and I will not get a second this low.

---

The body has the deciding before the head, the way it always has it.

He comes the last of the distance in the rush at last, the strength let go, the great arm swinging round to take me off the stone and end the telling the strong way — and I am not where the arm goes. I go low and inside it, under the swing, light where he is heavy, gone off the place I stood before the place can know a foot was there. The arm passes over my back, and the wind of it, and I am at his legs, behind the knee where the great weight stacks down onto the stone, and I do the thing I have done to no Vorth and to every strong thing in my green: I do not meet the strength. I read where it stands, and I pull the ground from under it.

I take the back of the knee with my whole light weight thrown low and sideways, the way the rot took the old male, the way the moved line took the Hesh — not a blow, a *law:* the tall heavy thing in the rush has stacked all its weight on the one ground, and the one ground given way is the thing that brings it down. He is a tower built for standing, not for the give. The leg folds the wrong way under the weight it was not braced to lose, and the great bulk that has never once been read goes down onto the bare stone, hard, the whole law of the strong come down on the place I am already gone from.

The high stones make a sound. The whole tribe in one breath drawn in.

---

It is not done. The strong are never done in the first give. He has the great hands flat on the stone already, the bulk gathering to come up, and the flat eyes find me where I have rolled clear and they are not weighing now. They have stopped weighing. There is the thing in them I have seen go into a strong thing once, in the old male in the soup — the thing that has weighed the stronger thing that holds it and not found the law it knows. For one breath the king of the high stones is a thing that does not know the ground.

I could feed the second mouth here. It stands all the way up over the king gone down, and there is a thing the body wants that is older than the head and lower down — to take the back of the neck the way the hand took mine, to lay the brand on him, *mine, be still, you are a thing,* to make him the thing on the deck I was. The far mouth has wanted to be the hand for as long as the hand has been on its neck. The wanting comes up bright and hot and I know the shape of it whole.

I do not feed it.

I do not take the neck. I take the standing.

---

I get the loop off my belt — I always have the loop at my belt — and I do not put it on him. He is not a thing that runs. I take the woody runner I have laced and kept and the long pull in my two hands, and I am up on his broken lip before he has his bulk under him, standing on the seat of his own weighing, the high stone laid out below me the way the green lays out below him.

He comes up. Of course he comes up — the strength does not stay down for a thing that did not break it. He comes up off the stone shaking the give out of the great body, and the flat eyes climb to the lip where I stand on the seat that was his, and the whole law of the strong gathers in him to come up the rock and take it back.

And he stops.

He stops because the tribe is not where he left it. It turned when the king went down — I felt it turn the way I feel a tide turn in the channel, the whole weight of it before any of its parts. The old male with the torn ear is come up off the high rock to the foot of the broken lip, the great head up, turned not to the king but to the small scarred thing on the king's own seat. The half-grown ones that cuff and contest the order of the stone every waking are crowded below the lip, the bold small one at the front of them, the eyes up. The big ones have come down off the far edges. The whole tribe stands at the foot of the lip and looks up at the place where the king weighs the green and reads the thing standing on it, and not one of them moves to the king.

The strong itself has read which one of them did it.

---

Gull-Marr stands at the foot of his own lip, the great bulk of him below me for the first time since I came up the rock the soft weak wrong thing on lent ground, and he weighs it. I watch him weigh it the way I weigh a thing on the shelf of wrong things — slow, a thing that should not be, and is. The strength did not leave him; I did not break it; I am not strong enough to break it, and we both have the whole shape of that. He could come up the rock. He could take me off the lip the way he meant to take me off the stone.

But the ground is gone.

That is the law I learned on the bank with the old male's wrist lifted, the hardest the high stones taught me till this one: a king of the strength-law is only king so long as the tribe reads the strength as the thing that keeps them. They have just watched the strength go down to a thing too light to pull, the way the old male went down to the rot, the way the Hesh went down to the moved line — and watched the wrong small thing not take the neck, not lay the brand, but stand up on the seat of the weighing and hold the green below it the way the king held it. The strength is still in Gull-Marr. But the tribe no longer reads it as the thing that keeps them. It reads the thing that read the king down.

He could come up and roll me and hold me and not give me back, and he would be, after, a strength the tribe has watched be bent — and there is no king in a strength the tribe has watched bend, only a strong thing on a rock that has learned to read it. He knows this. He has been king on the strength-law his whole life; he knows the law from the inside the way I know the rot. He weighs the rock, and the turned tribe, and the thing on his lip that did not take the neck when the neck was there to take — and the flat eyes do the thing I have never once seen them do.

They let it stand.

---

He does not come up the rock.

He turns the great heavy body, slow, the slow that is its own telling, and he goes — not at me, away from me, the main-strength bent the one way it knows to bend when the ground is gone. He goes down into the steaming green alone, the bare stone not moving with him this low, no clear ground going down ahead of him, and the rot-vapor takes the great gray-silver shape of him and closes over it, and the king of the high stones goes out of the tribe the way a thing goes out of a pool.

I watch him go, and the second mouth that wanted to be the hand stands quiet at last, because I did not feed it. *I do not know what I become if I feed it.* I laid that law down over the Hesh going under. I have kept it through the king. I let the strength walk into the green with its life, and the letting-stand is the first law of the new stone, laid down before I knew I was laying it.

The shore gives once, and then it makes you go further. There is no further left to go up. The high stone has only the lip, and I am on the lip.

---

The tribe comes up.

They come up the rock the way the bank came to me with the old male's wrist lifted, only this low there is no one to lift the wrist; the wrist is already up, standing on the seat. The old male with the torn ear comes first. He stands below the lip and makes the sound a strong thing makes to the thing that stood between it and the squared dead, low, slow, the great head bowed — and he does not stop at the bowing this low. He climbs the last of the rock and stands beside me on the broken lip, the strong set foot-for-foot at the side of the weak, and turns out with me to the green, and makes the sound again, plain, where the whole tribe can read it: *this one. The lip is this one's.*

The bold small one rides my shoulder before I have crouched for it, the quick heart easy, the keeping-place become the thing they keep to. The big ones gather below the lip. The whole tribe turns up to the broken stone where the small scarred thing stands, and they make the long even sound with the gladness folded into it, the rains-break sound — only this low the killing thing that lifted was not the rains and was not the Hesh. It was the king's flat eye that weighed them and let none of them stand, and it has gone down into the green. The stone is easy under them, the fear off them, the tribe whole.

And the Kala comes up.

---

She comes up last, the great gray-silver bulk, slow, the keeping thing in her small dark eyes turned the proud way it turned on the bank, only deeper now, all the way down. She climbs to the broken stone and folds down beside me on the seat of the king's weighing, the keeping arm coming over me the way it has since the fever-fog, since the rot she folded off the wrong thing the green could not finish, and she makes the still-sound, soft, falling — *be still, you are held, nothing comes* — and the *nothing comes* does not die half-made in the great chest this low, because the thing that came has gone down into the green and will not come up the rock.

I make the sound back, low and torn, two falls, scraping over the gap where the three teeth are gone. And the far mouth that leaned out of me my whole life into the empty water, into the bones, into the drowned far edge of the water where nothing ever came — the far mouth is full. Not fed a little. Full.

I have a seat the strength walked off of and left me on. I have, for the first low in all the lows I counted alone, a thing the body has no shelf for and the head can barely hold: a place that is mine that the ground did not lend, a home with no between in it, and a kind of crown. I came up among them the soft wrong thing on lent ground that poured away the moment a hand opened. This low the hand is open and the ground stays. I am the king of the wild things, and the wild things are mine, and I am theirs, and the body does not know what to do with the fullness but hold still inside it and let it be full.

---

The seer comes across the high stone in the last of the half-light, when the tribe has gone to the easy sleep all down the shelf and the dead king's seat has become a thing I sit on.

The ash-pale head is slow. The pale finger comes up and touches the Eye-and-Thorn bound at my chest, the relic off the dead expedition, the sigil that resonates up out of my drowned-deep where no word lives — and the seer makes the long meaning-sound over it, the *means,* the one the keeper makes over the squared dead, that lays a thing into the old order of the high stones.

And then the pale finger lifts off the Eye-and-Thorn, and it does not point out into the green where the king went, and it does not point up at the lip the way it pointed the dark I brought the Hesh's head up, the *this one and that one and the two are one telling.* The telling is done; the two are one; the lip is mine. So the finger does a new thing.

It comes back, slow, and lays itself flat against my chest, over the breastbone, under the bound sigil, where the second mouth lives and the warm blood goes — and it stays there, the ash-pale head bowed, the old eyes shut. The seer is not reading the Eye-and-Thorn this low. The seer is reading *me.* A thing comes off the seer that I have the whole shape of and no word for — the long meaning-sound made low and wondering, not the *means* laid over the dead, but a *means* with a question folded into it, the way you turn a thing on the shelf that does not come apart in the hands.

The seer has read the standing and the lip and the dead king. Every eye on the high stone read those. But the pale finger is not on the standing. It is on the blood. On the thing that closed the splits on my forearms too fast in their own short time as I dragged the Hesh's head up the rock, the thing that took the sting and the venom and gave them back fast and left no mark, the thing the fever-fog could not knot and the swarm rings off by a hand's width — the wrong things, the four and the five and the new ones, the whole shelf of them I do not take down. The seer reads them. Not as standing. Not as king. As a thing older than the high stones, a thing the body did and the body did not agree to, a thing the seer has no word for and lays the wondering *means* over and does not finish.

I keep my eyes on the green and let the pale finger lie where it lies, and I lay the wondering down behind my teeth, in the gap where the name was, beside the brand I will give back someday across the water:

*There is a thing in me older than the crown, and the crown is not the deepest thing the high stones have read in me, and the seer has the shape of it and not the name, and it is not done.*

The pale finger lifts. The seer looks at me long, the ash-pale head tipped, and makes the meaning-sound once more, the question still in it, and folds down and is quiet, and does not turn it over either — keeps it on the old shelf the way I keep it on mine. Some things you set down and stop turning, and after enough turnings of not-turning they grow quiet. But they do not go off the shelf. They wait there.

---

I do not sleep that low.

I sit on the seat of the king's weighing with the green laid out below and the tribe easy behind me and the Kala's great slow tide at my side and the bold heart warm on my shoulder, and I hold the two things the high stones gave me this low and lay them down behind my teeth together, because the body has learned they came up together and there is no setting one down without the other.

The first is the crown. The wild things are mine. I have a home with no between in it, the first I have ever had, the first since the warm of a mouth I cannot find anymore made a word over me and the ship and the hands took the word and the island took even the shape of it. The wanting that leaned out of me into nothing my whole life has a thing to lean on at last, and it leans, and the thing holds.

The second is the seer's finger on my blood. The wondering *means.* The thing older than the crown.

I sit the lip the way the king sat it weighing the world, and I do not weigh. I read. And the thing the body reads, low under the fullness, under the warm, under the first crown I have ever worn, is the same thing it read off the far drowned edge of the water the night the sail came: the high stone has nothing left on it stronger than me, and a thing with nothing left to fight goes still, and the still thing waits — and somewhere out past the channels and the drowned giants and the mangrove tide the water stands up into the sky the way it has always stood, and the wanting that is full this low was full once before, before the ship, in the warm of the mouth that made my name over me — and then the hands came, and the thing it leaned on was gone, and it leaned out into the empty water every low after and found nothing to take its weight until this rock.

The crown is the highest thing I have ever stood on. I know, the way the body knows a tide before the sky shows it, bone-sure, that the highest thing you stand on is the thing the next taking comes for.

I keep the wondering on the shelf. I keep the crown behind my teeth. I keep the bold heart warm on my shoulder and the Kala's arm over me and the green below, and I sit the king's seat in the green half-light of the new low, the king of the wild things, full and undrowned and waiting, the way I have always waited, for the thing the water has not shown me yet.
