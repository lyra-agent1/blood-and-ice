# Chapter 10: The Weaker, the Smarter

I am the weakest thing on the high stones.

This is the first law I learn among them, and the body learns it before the head, the way the body learns everything here. It learns it the morning a half-grown one — younger than me, barely come into its strength — takes my wrist in its hand to move me off a warm rock it wants, and moves me, the way I move a stone off its seat at the channel, no thought in it, no malice in it, the whole of my weight nothing to the arm that lifts it. I am set down a body's length off and the young one is already not looking at me. And I crouch where it set me with the wrist still ringing and I understand, all the way down, the thing I have come up among.

I am the longest tooth in my green. I am the thing the green could not eat. And here I am the small one. Here I am the one that gets moved.

The shore taught me one shape of being and the high stones teach me the other. Down in my green I am the longest tooth, the one strength that walks where it wants. Up here strength is a thing that walks past me on two legs and four, that hangs off the rock by one arm with a young one gripped to its chest, that brings down the fat-meat beast with a blow I could not throw if I threw my whole body behind it. Among the Vorth I am soft. I am slow. The arm is short and the back is light and the hand that breaks a thing's neck on the shore would not turn a Vorth's head a finger's width.

So. I am the weakest thing on the high stones.

Good. Let me find the other thing I am.

---

I watch them work the green the way I once watched the herd, counting, learning the shape of it, where it is strong and where the strength runs out.

The Vorth take the fat-meat beast by main strength and the green gives it to them because they are more and they are heavy. They pour down both sides of a channel and close it like a fist and the thing in the channel has nowhere the strength is not. This is how they have always taken it. The young ones learn it off the big ones by the body, by doing the thing the big body does until the small body can do it too. There is no other way in them. The way is strength and the path to it is more strength, and a thing that has not got the strength has not got the way.

But strength runs out where the green goes thin and high. Up in the canopy where the branch will not take the heavy body. Down in the deep rot where the ground is not ground, where the heavy foot goes through the skin of it into the soup beneath and the more you weigh the worse it holds you. Out on the bared channel-flats at low water, where the fat-meat beast is, but also the thing that is not the beast — the long quick thing, the burrow-thing, the clever meat that will not stand and be closed on, that is gone down its hole or off across the mud before the fist can shut.

The Vorth go hungry past those edges. I have seen it. I have seen a whole hand of them stand at the lip of a rot-thicket where a fat-meat beast went down to wallow, and not follow, because the ground will not hold them, and turn and go back up empty while the beast wallows on a spear-throw off, fat and stupid and safe behind nothing but the soft ground.

The green is thin in the exact places strength is no use. And I am light. And I am the thing that learned the channel-flats in the dark with no one to show me, the thing that reads which stone hides a meal worth the reaching.

The body knows it before the head does. There is a place here shaped for me. Not the strong place. The other one.

---

I make my first noose for the burrow-thing.

I have made them on the shore, in the season I learned that some meat will not wait to be hunted and must be made to come to the hunter. A loop of gut, of plaited fiber, set in the run where the thing's own feet have worn the green pale, bent down and held by a sprung green branch so the thing puts its own neck in the loop and its own panic throws the branch and the branch does the killing while I am asleep or away. The shore had no one to teach it to me. I taught it to the green and the green taught it back.

Up here the burrow-thing runs the channel-edge at the low water, fast and low, in and out of its hole, and no Vorth has ever taken one, because no Vorth is fast enough to close on a thing that small and that quick, and the hand that could crush it cannot catch it. I crouch at the channel-edge in the gray light where the young one once found me burning, and I read the run, the pale worn line in the mud where the small feet go, and I set the loop in it, and bend the green branch down and notch it, and lay the trip across the run light as a hair. And I go up the bank and I crouch in the rot-fern and I wait, the way the shore taught me to wait, still as the stone, part of the green.

A big one passes above me on the bank and looks down at the man-shape crouched doing nothing in the fern and makes a short sound, the dismissing sound, the one they lay on a thing that is no use, and goes on. I do not move. The swarm rings me and does not land, drawing its clean bare line around the arm. And the burrow-thing comes up out of its hole and runs its run and puts its own neck in my loop and throws the branch, and the branch snaps up and the thing hangs kicking and then hangs still, and I have meat the green said no Vorth could have.

I carry it up to the high stones in my two hands.

---

They do not know what to do with it.

I lay it down on the gray rock among them, the burrow-thing, the clever meat, the thing they have stood at the lip of the rot and watched go safe down its hole a hundred lows — and the heads come up and tip toward it, and then toward me, and there is a sound that goes around them low, a sound I am learning to read, the sound that is half the dismissing sound and half another thing, a thing with a question bent into it.

The young one that found me comes and crouches by the burrow-thing and turns it over and looks for the wound, for the killing blow, for the place a strength went into the meat — and there is no wound, there is no blow, there is only the loop, the thing the meat did to itself with my green branch for an arm, and the young one cannot find where the strength was because there was no strength, and it makes the question-sound, low, up at me.

And the Kala comes.

She comes down off the high shelf where she keeps the still ones, the great gray-silver bulk of her, and she crouches by the burrow-thing and she does not turn it over looking for the blow. She looks at the loop. She lays one gray finger on the plaited fiber and follows it with the finger, up off the neck of the dead thing, up the length of it to the frayed end where I cut it, and then she lifts the finger and looks at me, at the man-shape that made the loop, and a thing goes over the milk-edged eyes that I have only ever had from her, the keeping one, turned now a new way — turned the way the old gray one turned its head over the eye-and-thorn at my chest. Turned toward a thing it did not look for and now half-sees.

She makes a sound at me. Not the gentling sound. A new one. Short, and up at the end, and laid on me plain, and I do not have it yet, I do not have the word, but I have its shape the way I have the shape of every hole in me, and its shape is *what is this, that you do.*

And I do the thing I am learning to do. I show her.

---

I take her down to the channel-edge at the next low water and I make the noose in front of her.

I do not have the words to say it. The words are coming — slow, stolen off their tongues one by one, *low* and *high* and *come* and *the gentling*, laid in the empty place behind my teeth where the name was torn out, a sound at a time, a true one — but I do not have the words for *this is a run* and *this is a trip* and *the thing kills itself while you sleep*. So I do not say it. I do it, slow, with her great gray head bent down over my two small hands, and I make her watch the hands the way the small ones watch the big ones — by the body, because that is the only way in, and I have turned myself small to come in through it.

I read the run. I show her the pale worn line, lay my finger along it the way she laid hers along the loop. I set the loop. I bend the green branch down, and I take her great hand — and she lets me take it, the hand that could close and end me, she lets the weak thing fold it down to the notch — and I notch it under her hand so she feels the spring of it, the held strength of the bent green waiting to be let go. I lay the trip. And we go up the bank, the two of us, and we crouch in the fern, the great gray bulk of her gone still beside me, still the way I am still, learning the stillness off the weak thing the way the weak thing learned the warm off her.

And the burrow-thing comes, and runs its run, and throws the branch, and the loop takes it, and the Kala makes a sound I have never heard from her, a sound that goes up out of the great chest fast and breaks at the top — and I know it, I know it the way I know the shape of every hole, I have heard the small ones make it when a thing they did not look for comes good, when the green opens its hand and gives. It is the sound of a thing surprised into gladness.

The strongest thing I have ever been held by, and I have made it glad with the weakest thing I have — a loop of plaited fiber and a bent green branch and the patience the shore beat into me. The branch does the killing. The bent green is the arm I do not have. This is the place that is shaped for me: where the strong thing's strength runs out, and I stand in it and hand them what is on the far side.

---

After the loop they let me into the work a new way.

It is slow. I am still the weakest. The young ones still move me off the warm rock, still take what they want out of my hands with no thought in it. But the question-sound follows me now where the dismissing sound followed me before. When a fat-meat beast goes down into a rot-thicket the heavy bodies cannot follow, the heads tip toward me. When a thing they cannot catch runs a run, they look at the man-shape and its loops. I am not strong and they have stopped waiting for me to be. They have begun to wait for the other thing.

I bring them the high-canopy meat the branch will not give the heavy body, because I am light and I learned the climb in a green that meant to drop me. I bring them the burrow-thing and the clever meat off the flats. I bring them, one low, the red light.

This is the thing that turns it.

Their fire is kept — fed, banked, tended on the high gray, the held red light a thing makes and does not let go out, the light I counted across all that drowning green when I had only a coal nursed in a fist of fiber. They have had it longer than the oldest of them has been alive; it came down to them off the dead, hand to hand, low to low, and not one of them knows how it was first lit. But a fire that is only kept is a fire that can die, and theirs has come near it, low and starving in the long wet when the green will not dry and there is nothing to feed the red but more wet green. I have watched them feed it wrong, pile the soaked green on it and watch it sulk and gutter and shrink, because they keep fire, they do not make it, and a thing you only keep you do not understand. You understand a thing by making it. The shore made me make my fire a thousand times before it let me keep one.

So when theirs is dying in the wet I go down into my green and I bring up the dry — the dead heart-wood of the fallen giant that the rot has not reached, the fat-soaked fiber, the rendered fat of the fat-meat beast I have learned to keep, the things that take the flame and hold it through the wet — and I feed their dying red the way the shore taught me, slow, from the heart out, and the red stands up off the gray ash and takes the dry and burns high and clean, the highest it has burned in lows, and the warmth comes off it and lays on all of us up on the high stones in the wet dark.

And the heads, all of them, even the king's, high on the bare gray — the heads tip toward the man-shape that made the dead red live. Not the way the heads tip toward the strong one. A new way. The way a thing tips its head toward a thing it does not understand and wants.

I have given them the one thing the strong cannot take by strength. You cannot be strong at fire. The fat-meat beast will not yield to a blow. There is no main-strength way to make the dead red live in the wet, and I have made it live, the weak thing, the small one that gets moved off the warm rock — and the warm rock is mine now, the low after the fire, because the young one that would have moved me looks at me first, and does not.

---

The seer comes to me again at the dark, after the fire.

The old gray one. The oldest I have seen, the body sunk in on itself, the milk-pale eyes still moving, still reading. It comes to where I sit in the new warmth of the fire I fed, and it crouches a long while, and it reads me the way it read me the first low, slow, taking the whole shape of a thing before it goes down into it. And it lays its gray finger again, not on me, on the disc at my chest — the eye, ringed, looking, and the thorn driven through the looking, the mark that buzzes low in the drowned place of me, *I know this, this is mine,* and no when and no whose.

And then it does a new thing. It turns the gray finger off the disc and lays it against the side of my head. Light. Not where the strength is. Where the other thing is. And it makes a sound, low, the same long sound it made over the mark, the one with the thing in it turned toward, turned up — and it looks from the side of my head to the loop of plaited fiber still hung at my belt, the loop that killed the clever meat, and back to my head, and it makes the sound again. And I understand it the way I understand everything here, the body first and the head after.

It has read the wrong thing in me — the thing on the shelf, the green that could not eat me, the fever-air that drops every other body and leaves me clear, the venom that goes out of me fast and leaves no mark, the wounds that draw shut clean where every other wound goes to rot, the disc that knows me — and it has read the other thing too, the thing in the side of my head, the loop and the trip and the fed fire, and it has set them side by side. The thing that should not be, and the thing that thinks. And it knows them, in whatever way the old gray blood knows, for two halves of one strangeness. It bows the great gray head over both of them at once, slow, the way the Kala bowed hers over the dead small one, the way I once bowed mine over the bones of the thing that owned my green.

I do not understand what it knows. I set it on the shelf with the rest — the mark, the buzzing, the old one's knowing, and now this, the gray finger laid against the side of my head like a thing being named. I do not turn it over. There is no turning it over. But I keep it. The way I keep every light now instead of counting the lights between.

---

The Kala folds down beside me in the firelight when the seer has gone, the great gray-silver bulk of her, and lays the arm over me, and makes the gentling sound, the keeping one. And I lie against the warm that knows it holds me, with the slow vast heart going through my ear, the loop at my belt and the fed fire high and the warm rock mine for the keeping, inside the many that should have swept me, that have begun instead to wait on the thing only I can hand them.

I am still the weakest thing on the high stones. The young one will move me off the rock again, some low, with no thought in it. I will never throw the blow that brings the fat-meat beast down. I will never turn a Vorth's head a finger's width with my hand.

But there is a low pale line worn in the mud at the channel-edge where the clever meat runs, and a bent green branch waits over it with my loop set light across the run, and while I lie here in the warm the branch is the arm I do not have, doing the work the strong cannot do, in the place the strong cannot go. The shore taught me a law and I bring it up to the high stones whole. *Where you are not strong, be the thing the strong needs.*

I lay the law down behind my teeth, in the gap where the name was, beside the stolen sounds and the gentling sound that is not mine and the warm that chose to hold me. And the far mouth, the second mouth, the one no kill ever fed, the one that opened wide and empty over all that drowning green and would not close on meat or water — it lies quiet under my ribs now. Full. Because they have stopped looking past me. They have begun to look at me. Not the longest tooth. The other thing. The thing only I can hand them.

The weaker, the smarter. On the shore the law was hand and tooth and the strongest thing keeps the ground. Here is the new law, brought up whole into the high stones and laid beside the old: *the strong takes what it can reach; the weak learns to make the green hand over the rest.* And the rest is the larger part. The rest is everything past the edge of the arm.

It is enough to be known by. After all the counting, all the lights between — it is more than enough.
