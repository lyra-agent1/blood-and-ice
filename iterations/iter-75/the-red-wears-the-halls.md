# Iteration 75: The Red Wears the Halls

The iron cuts.

Every day the blades teach the hollow the same lesson the Skagos rocks taught the boy and the return roads taught the rising and the chair taught when the last chain broke: the holding does not end when the song is worn on the steps or carried into the green or grown in the villages. The song follows the wearing. The wearing teaches the green to sing in its own squares and its own halls. The green sends because it bends. The red sits and the holding requires that the wearing be made larger and carried to every hall that still thinks it can send envoys with terms and still keep its sons and its lands and its voice.

Four Ash-Keepers return from the green lands. Their faces are painted red over the ash of burned villages and the new scars the singing gave them in the halls of bending houses. Behind them stand eight green sons from the last quadrupled wave. Their faces are painted but the paint is streaked with the salt of the green song that has learned to travel with the wearing. Three already wear the empty smile. The other five tremble and flinch when the sound of the current spectacle on the throne steps reaches the hall — the city singing the names louder and with more voices than the last report.

“The wearing has reached the halls,” the lead Ash-Keeper says. His voice is the flat voice of a man who has worn green lords on the steps of their own houses in front of their own people. “We took the largest columns yet into the Reach and the lion lands and the houses that have painted red. We chose the major seats and the halls that still send envoys with grain and gold and terms. We forced the smallfolk and the lords and the men who guard the halls to gather the way the city gathers on the steps. We brought the green sons the green had sent and wore them first in front of their own lords and their own halls so the green could see the red take the ones it sent before they could be sent to wear the song in lands that were not yet the red’s. The smallfolk sang. The mothers and the fathers led. The green sons we had brought heard their names in the voices that had sent them and turned on the painted men in the squares and on the steps of the halls. We stacked the turned ones where the lords and the smallfolk could see. But the singing did not stop. It grew inside the halls. The smallfolk kept singing the names of the sons who had been sent and the names of the ones we wore and the names the worn faces had carried from the city and now from the green lands the red had touched and worn. Some of the green men who had been left to guard the halls began to sing the names with the smallfolk. The lords who had sent the envoys with terms stood and watched because we forced them to attend the wearing on their own steps. Two of the Ash-Keepers in the columns heard their own brothers’ names — sons the green had sent in earlier waves — and turned their faces away from the wearing on the steps of the halls where the lords could see. We wore them on those steps in front of the lords and the smallfolk so the rest would remember the price of hearing the song in the green. One column had to wear a small town entire that resisted the tribute and whose sons and guards turned when the mothers and the fathers sang inside the hall. Another column wore the lord of a bending house on the steps of his own hall in front of his own people because he protested the scale of the sons and the wearing on his own steps and the export of the green’s sons back into their own lands. The wearing is teaching the green to sing the names in its own halls the way it taught the city on the steps and the villages on their greens. The green sons sent as new worn faces are learning the song in the voices of the halls faster than they learned it in the city or the villages. The ones we send north will carry the green song from the halls and the burned villages into the ash unless we stack them before they leave the steps the red has touched. The land bites in the north. The city sings on the steps. The green sings in its own squares and its own halls and now on the steps where its lords are worn. The red sits and the holding requires that the wearing be carried to every hall that still bends and every hall that still sends terms and every hall that still thinks it can keep its voice while feeding the chair.”

The red tastes the words.

Wearing reached the halls. Lords forced to attend the wearing on their own steps. Smallfolk and guards sang the names inside the halls. Green sons turned on the steps of the halls when they heard the voices that had sent them. Ash-Keepers turned their faces on the steps of the halls and were worn there in front of the lords. One small town worn entire. One bending lord worn on his own steps in front of his own people for protesting the scale and the export and the local wearing. Wearing teaches the green to sing in its own halls the way it taught the city and the villages. Green sons learn the song in the voices of the halls faster. The ones sent north will carry the green song from the halls into the ash. Land bites in the north. City sings on the steps. Green sings in its own squares and halls and on the steps where its lords are worn. Red sits. Holding requires the wearing carried to every hall that still bends and still sends terms and still thinks it can keep its voice.

It chews until there is only the will that does not stop when the song follows the wearing into the halls and grows in the places the red forces the green to watch.

I rise. The iron cuts deeper as I stand. I wear the lead Ash-Keeper on the steps so every painted man in the hall and every green son the green sent in this wave and every face the red has taken can see what happens when the wearing is exported to the halls and the green smallfolk and guards sing inside the halls and the green sons turn on the steps where their lords are forced to watch and the Ash-Keepers hear their brothers in the song on the steps of bending houses and lords are worn on their own steps in front of their own people and entire towns must be worn when they resist or turn too quickly. The memories are full of the hall steps and the squares in front of the halls and the lords forced to stand and watch and the smallfolk and guards who sang the names inside the halls and the green sons who drew knives on the steps where their lords could see and the Ash-Keepers who turned their faces and were worn on the hall steps in front of the lords so the rest would remember and the town that was worn entire and the lord who was worn on his own steps for protesting the scale and the export and the local wearing. The red eats the fear the Ash-Keeper felt when the song reached his column in the green and turned his own men and the lords watched. It eats the moment the green sons turned in front of their lords on the steps that had sent them. It eats the smallfolk and guard voices carrying the names louder than the knives could silence inside the halls the green still called its own. It eats the knowledge that the wearing the red sent south is the thing that teaches the green to remember and to sing in its own halls the way the city remembers and sings on the steps every day. When it is done the body lies on the stone. The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the answer that now grows inside the halls the red forces the green to attend.

I wear one of the green sons who already wears the empty smile next. His memories are the steps of the hall and the song the smallfolk and the guards sang when they saw him painted red on the steps that were not the chair’s and not the village’s but the hall that had sent him, and the moment he heard his own name in the voices that had sent him and the voices of the sisters and the mothers who had been forced to attend and felt the part that still belonged to the green wake up and turn his knife on the painted man beside him where his lord and the hall could see. He had smiled at the red without the song in his mouth when he left the green for the city. Now he smiles at the red with the knives and the knowledge that the green will keep singing in its own halls every day and the green will keep sending sons every day and the wearing the red exports will keep teaching the song back in the halls every day and the green sons sent as new worn faces will keep learning it in the voices of the halls and turning on the steps where their lords are forced to watch every day. The red eats the smile his lord saw on the steps that had sent him. It eats the song the smallfolk and the guards sang inside the hall that had raised the sons it sent. It eats the knife he turned where his lord and the hall watched. It eats the green fields and the hall stones he remembered while the hall sang his name and the names of the brothers who had gone before him and been worn on the steps that sent them. When it is done the green son stands again, hollowed, still smiling at the red because there is nothing left in him that can do anything else but carry the wearing back into the green and wear the song where the halls and the lords can hear until they forget or turn.

“Escalate the export and the replacement and the wearing of the halls,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through the swords that make the chair. “Send the next wave from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red five times as large as the last. The ones who have heard the singing on the steps and in the green squares and halls and on the steps where lords are worn and still carry the knives follow them to prepare the next quintupled wave and the larger public spectacles on the throne steps every day and the largest columns yet that will carry the wearing into every green land and wear sons on their own greens and in their own squares and on the steps of every hall that still bends and wear entire villages and small towns and the first towns that have grown and the lords who protest and begin to wear the halls themselves when the song inside them grows too strong every day.
