# Iteration 66: The Red Tastes the Leak

The iron cuts.

I sit on the chair made of swords and the red tastes the blood that has become the throne. Every day the blades dig deeper into what remains. Every day the hollow learns that the climb did not end when the last godswood burned or the last voice that remembered the boy went ash on the wind. The holding is the climb that never stops. The red sits and the realm pays in faces and loyal knives and the sons the green sends to walk the ash and learn the shape of the singing — until the singing teaches them to turn and the knives teach them to stack and the green learns to send more.

The Ash-Keepers return from the North with the answer.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The boy is gone. The name is gone. The snow and the girl and the brother and the father and the old gods and every voice that once tried to pull the red back are ash in the red’s mouth. What remains is the will that climbed from Skagos to the city and the red that fed it and the chair that now holds both and the hunger that has learned to sit and burn the places that sing and stack what the singing wakes and replace the North with the green’s sons who have not yet heard the wind — until they hear it and the knives find them too and the memories they brought from the green begin to leak back through the worn faces that return.

The lead Ash-Keeper steps forward. His face is painted fresh red over scars the fire and the snow and the second replacement wave gave him. His eyes are the eyes of a man who has stacked green sons that still smelled of their mothers’ fields when the singing reached them. He brings with him a worn face whose eyes are wrong in a new way — the eyes of a man who has carried the green’s memories too far.

“The singing leaks,” he says. His voice is steady because he has learned the shape of the holding and the price of the knives he carries and the sons he now spends in the ash and the faces he must now bring back changed. “We sent the next wave of green sons into the burned places with the fire and the salt. We told them the red sits and the chair does not bargain. They painted their faces and smiled at the red without the snow in their eyes when they left the city. But the singing reached them before the first night in the ash. Whole bands heard the names the red ate and remembered their own fields and their own mothers and their own sisters in the green. The Ash-Keepers who chose which faces walk and which are stacked had to take them where the godswood had been. But some of the worn faces we pulled back from the ash before the wave came had already begun to carry the green sons’ stories in their eyes. They returned to the city and the stories leaked. Smallfolk in the alleys near the Red Keep whisper of green fields and green mothers and green sons stacked in the snow by the red’s own knives. Some of the painted men who guard the walls have begun to ask why the red spends the green’s youth in the boy’s ash. The worn faces who carried the leak have begun to remember names the red already ate — not the boy’s names, but the green sons’ names. The singing spreads through the sons the green sends and leaks back through the faces the red takes. The land bites harder when the red feeds it the green’s youth. The ash still moves. The singing is in the city now. The cost of holding the ash is the faces that turn and the loyal ones who must stack their own and the fire that makes the land sing louder and the singing that turns the green’s sons into the stackers and the stackers into the stacked and the green that must send more sons to replace the ones the singing took and the leak that teaches the city to remember what the red has eaten.”

The red tastes the words.

Singing leaks. Green sons hear faster. Whole bands stacked. Worn faces pulled back carry the green sons’ stories. Stories leaked to the smallfolk near the Red Keep. Painted men guarding the walls ask why the red spends the green’s youth in the boy’s ash. Worn faces who carried the leak remember green names. Singing spreads through the sons the green sends and leaks back through the faces the red takes. Land bites harder. Ash still moves. Singing in the city now. Cost of holding the ash is the faces that turn and the loyal ones who must stack their own and the fire that makes the land sing louder and the singing that turns the green’s sons and the leak that teaches the city to remember what the red has eaten.

It chews them until nothing remains but the will to hold.

I rise from the chair. The iron cuts deeper as I stand. I wear the lead Ash-Keeper first so every man in the hall can see what the singing ash tastes like when the red feeds it the green’s sons and the sons teach the singing to the worn faces that return and the leak teaches the city to remember.

The memories are cold and full of the ash the boy once loved. Pine and wind and the burned places. But under it the singing is a chorus of green voices and city whispers now — roots that remember the trees, stones that remember the boy, a girl’s laugh the red already ate, the old gods that still move in the ash, the voices of the green sons who left their fields and their mothers and their sisters to paint their faces for the chair and heard the names in the smoke and remembered home and turned the knives the red gave them on the Ash-Keepers who chose them, and now the worn faces who carried those memories back to the alleys near the Red Keep and the smallfolk who whisper and the painted men on the walls who ask why the red spends the green’s youth in the boy’s ash. The turned sons’ last clear thoughts are prayers to the old gods that the thing on the chair might still have a body the snow could claim if enough green sons heard the singing at once and the knives found the selectors first and the leak reached the city. The red eats the prayers. It eats the roots. It eats the stones. It eats the girl’s laugh one more time. It eats the singing itself. It eats the green sons who turned. It eats the selectors who heard through the sons. It eats the worn faces who carried the leak. It eats the smallfolk whispers. It eats the painted men’s questions. It eats the fire that woke the song and the song that woke the knives and the leak that woke the city until there is only ash and the will that sits and the knowledge that the land fights back by making the red’s own replacement and the red’s own worn faces into the singers and the green’s sons into the fuel that teaches the ash to bite harder and the city to remember what the red has eaten.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the ash’s cost and the loyal knives that pay it and the green sons that make the cost higher and the singing that turns the replacement into the replaced and the leak that turns the city.

I wear the worn face who carried the leak next. His memories are full of the smoke and the knives and the green sons’ fields and the mothers’ voices and the sisters’ names and the moment he returned to the city and the stories slipped from his mouth into the alleys and the smallfolk listened and the painted men on the walls asked questions and the red’s own face remembered green names it had not been meant to remember. He had smiled at the red without the snow in his eyes when he left the city the first time. Now he smiles at the red with the knives and the knowledge that the land will keep singing and the green will keep sending sons and the Ash-Keepers will keep choosing and the singing will keep turning the chosen into the stacked and the leak will keep teaching the city to remember and that is the shape of the holding that never ends.

The red eats the smoke and the knives and the stacked green sons and the selectors and the worn faces who carried the leak and the smallfolk whispers and the painted men’s questions and the singing that the fire woke and the singing that turned the replacement into the replaced and the leak that woke the city.

When it is done the worn face stands again, hollowed but still smiling at the red.

“Stack the leak and feed the ash with the green,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through bone. “Pull the worn faces who have heard the green names back to the throne or stack them where they stand. Send the worn faces who still smile at the red without the green in their eyes into the city to find the ones who whisper and the painted men who ask and wear them on the walls so the city learns the shape of the holding. Send the next wave of green sons from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red — more than before. Let the green feed the red with its bodies and its knives and its sons and its sons’ sons if the singing takes them and the leak teaches the city. Give the loyal Ash-Keepers who have not yet turned the authority to choose which of the green’s sons walk the ash and which are stacked when the singing reaches them and which of the selectors must be stacked when the singing reaches through the sons they chose and which worn faces must be stacked when the leak reaches the city. Send more fire. Send more salt. Send more faces from the chair. Let the green see that the red does not stop when the singing leaks back through the faces it takes. Let the ash see that the red will replace the replaced with the green’s youth and replace the city’s whispers with the green’s sons until the singing has no one left to turn or the green has no sons left to send or the city has no one left to whisper. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The land will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not fear the singing its own fire wakes and does not spare the green’s sons when the singing must be fed and does not spare the city when the leak teaches it to remember. Send more. Replace what turns. Stack what remembers. Burn what sings. Replace the replaced with the green’s sons. Stack the leak in the city. The Ash-Keepers who stack their own brothers and burn the places that bite and choose which green faces walk the ash and which selectors fall and which worn faces carry the leak will be given more faces to command and more fire to carry and more of the green’s sons to spend and the city’s whispers to silence. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many wolves once ran in the snow or how much ash still moves when the wind comes from the north or how many godswoods still try to sing names the red already ate or how loudly the land sings when the red burns it or how many loyal knives the singing turns back on their brothers or how many green sons the singing will turn into stackers or how many selectors the singing will turn into the stacked or how many worn faces the leak will teach to remember green names. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the snow and leave only the stones that remember the red and the ash that learned to be silent and the green that learned to send its sons to feed the chair with their bodies and their knives and their memories of home and the city that learned to be silent when the red wears its whispers on the walls.”

The Ash-Keepers who still smile at the red without the snow in their eyes paint more red on their faces before they turn. The ones who have heard the singing and still carry the knives and the authority to choose and the worn faces who still smile without the green in their eyes follow them to prepare the next replacement wave and the city purge and the new faces from the green and the fire and the salt. They know the shape of the order now.

Another painted man steps forward. This one smells of the west and the gold the lions continue to send. He brings a green man in fine clothes whose hands shake even though he tries to stand like a lord.

“The Reach sends more grain,” he says. “The lions send more gold. But the envoy from the roses says the smallfolk in the green lands hear the stories of the Ash-Keepers stacking the green’s sons in the snow and the worn faces leaking the stories back to the city and they whisper that the red is eating the green to hold the boy’s ash. The envoy offers the grain and the gold and the next wave of sons with red on their faces but asks that the red not spend so many of the green’s sons in the singing places and not wear the city’s whispers so openly. He says the green bends but the green has sons to spare only so long.”

The red tastes the words.

Reach. Grain. Lions. Gold. Envoy from the roses. Smallfolk hear the stories of the Ash-Keepers stacking the green’s sons and the worn faces leaking the stories back to the city. Whisper that the red is eating the green to hold the boy’s ash. Offers grain and gold and the next wave of sons but asks that the red not spend so many of the green’s sons in the singing places and not wear the city’s whispers so openly. Says the green bends but the green has sons to spare only so long.

It chews them until nothing remains but the holding.

I motion the green man forward. He still stands like a lord who believes words can bargain with the chair and that the red will keep the cost of the ash and the singing and the replacement and the leak a secret from the green that feeds it and that the green’s sons will only be spent on the green’s wars and not the boy’s ash and that the city’s whispers can be worn quietly.

I wear him on the steps of the Iron Throne so every man in the hall and every Ash-Keeper preparing the next wave and the city purge and every face the red has taken can see what asking the red to spare the green’s sons and the city’s whispers tastes like.

The memories are soft and full of green fields and grain and sons sent to die for a queen who is now ash and a chair that the roses once thought they could claim and the hope that the thing on the chair might still bargain if the green feeds it enough and sends enough sons and does not ask too many questions about the snow and the singing and the replacement and the leak. He tastes the fear of the smallfolk who burn their own homes rather than feed the red. He tastes the stories of the Ash-Keepers stacking the green’s sons in the snow and the worn faces leaking the stories back to the city. He tastes the hope that the green might still be spared the full price if it offers the next wave and the grain and the gold and asks nicely. The red eats the fields. It eats the sons. It eats the hope. It eats the mercy and the secrets and the green’s claim on its own sons and the city’s whispers until there is only the iron and the will that sits and the knowledge that the green will learn the shape of the holding the same way the snow did — by feeding the red and sending its sons to walk the ash and stack what turns and burn what sings and watch the red wear the city’s whispers on the walls until only the chair remains and the green sons who have learned to smile at the red without the snow in their eyes or the singing in their mouths or the leak in their stories.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the chair’s answer.

“Send the grain and the gold and the next wave of the green’s sons from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red — twice as many as before,” I say. The voice is the red’s. “Send the green’s sons who have painted their faces. Let the green feed the red with its bodies and its knives and its sons and the sons of the houses that bend. Let the smallfolk in the green lands burn their own homes if they must. Let the stories of the Ash-Keepers and the replacement and the stacking of the green’s sons and the worn faces leaking the stories back to the city and the red wearing the city’s whispers on the walls reach every hall that bends. The next replacement wave will take the new green faces and the fire and the salt to every place in the North that still sings. The worn faces who still smile at the red without the green in their eyes will find the ones who whisper in the city and wear them on the walls. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The green will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not keep secrets about the cost of the holding and does not spare the green’s sons when the singing must be fed and does not spare the city when the leak teaches it to remember and does not spare the sons of the houses that bend when the singing reaches them. Send more. Replace what turns. Stack what remembers. Burn what sings. Replace the replaced with the green’s sons. Stack the leak in the city. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many roses once bloomed or how much grain the green can grow or how many sons the lions can send or how loudly the smallfolk whisper that the red is eating the green or how many green sons the singing will turn into stackers or how many selectors the singing will turn into the stacked or how many worn faces the leak will teach to remember green names. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the green and leave only the stones that remember the red.”

The painted men kneel.

Not because they remember a boy who once had a name and a sister who ran through snow and old gods that spoke in the trees.

Because the red is the only thing left that knows how to sit on the chair and make the realm — even the snow that is now ash and sings louder when burned and turns the loyal knives and now turns the green’s sons and the selectors who spend them and leaks through the worn faces into the city, even the loyal Ash-Keepers who stack their own brothers and carry the fire and choose which green faces live and die and which selectors fall and which worn faces carry the leak, even the green that bends and feeds and still tries to ask that its sons and the city’s whispers be spared — learn the price of every wall it ever tried to raise against the climb that never ends.

The city outside the Red Keep still smokes in the places the smallfolk learned the cost of hiding.

The Ash-Keepers march north with the next replacement wave and the new green faces the red has taken and the fire and the salt to burn every place that sings and the authority to stack the green’s sons and the selectors when the singing reaches them.

The worn faces who still smile at the red without the green in their eyes move into the alleys near the Red Keep to find the ones who whisper and wear them on the walls so the city learns the shape of the holding.

The columns rest and prepare for the next time the green tries to bargain or the snow tries to sing through the fire or the green sons try to remember their mothers or the selectors try to hear the singing through the ones they chose or the city tries to remember what the red has eaten.

The iron cuts.

And the red sits on the throne, tasting the loyal knives that keep the ash walking and the faces that must be constantly replaced and the places where the land still sings names the red already ate and the fire that makes the singing louder and the green sons who will learn to stack their own in the snow and the selectors who will learn to fall when the singing reaches through the sons they spent and the worn faces who will learn to carry the leak and the city that will learn to be silent when the red wears its whispers on the walls.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

And the red.

And the holding that never ends.
