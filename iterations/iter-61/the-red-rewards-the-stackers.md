# Iteration 61: The Red Rewards the Stackers

The iron cuts.

I sit on the chair made of swords and the red tastes the blood that has become the throne. Every day the blades dig. Every day the hollow learns that the climb did not end when the last godswood burned or the last voice that remembered the boy went ash on the wind. The holding is the climb that never stops. The red sits and the realm pays the faces it leaves behind and the loyal ones who must stack their own so the chair can remain.

The painted men bring the answer from the ash.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The boy is gone. The name is gone. The snow and the girl and the brother and the father and the old gods and every voice that once tried to pull the red back are ash in the red’s mouth. What remains is the will that climbed from Skagos to the city and the red that fed it and the chair that now holds both and the hunger that has learned to sit and reward the tools that stack their own turned brothers in the land that still whispers.

A painted man returns from the snow. He smells of ash and the faces the red left behind and the blood of the ones that turned and the ones that were stacked by the loyal. He brings the message that the worn ones still walk the burned holdfasts. More of the faces the red left have begun to turn. More of the painted men left behind have begun to hear the wind and remember names the red already ate. The loyal ones among the painted men have begun to hunt the turned ones in the ash. They wear the faces the red took and use the knives the red left behind to stack their own brothers who remembered the snow too loudly. The land still whispers in small ways. The ash still moves when the wind comes from the north. The snow is quiet where the last godswoods stood. But the loyal ones have learned the shape of the holding. They stack what turns. They keep the faces the red left behind walking in the ash.

The old Skagosi steps forward. His face is painted fresh red over scars the fire and the snow and the long holding and the stacking gave him.

“The ash still moves,” he says. “The worn ones we left behind still wear the faces the red took. More of them have begun to turn. More of the painted men we left have begun to remember the snow in ways the red did not command. The loyal ones hunt them in the ash. They wear the faces the red took and use the knives the red left behind. They stack their own brothers who remembered the snow too loudly. We sent more faces to replace the ones that turned. The loyal ones take the new faces and continue the hunt. The land still whispers. The ash still moves when the wind comes from the north. The snow is quiet where the last godswoods stood. But the loyal ones have learned. They stack what turns. They keep the red’s faces walking in the ash. The cost of holding the ash is the faces that turn and the loyal ones who stack their own.”

The red tastes the words.

Ash. Still moves. Worn ones still wear the faces. More have begun to turn. Painted men have begun to remember. Loyal ones hunt them in the ash. Wear the faces the red took. Use the knives the red left behind. Stack their own brothers who remembered the snow too loudly. Sent more faces. Loyal ones take the new faces and continue the hunt. Land still whispers. Ash still moves. Snow is quiet. Loyal ones have learned. Stack what turns. Keep the red’s faces walking. Cost of holding the ash is the faces that turn and the loyal ones who stack their own.

It chews them until nothing remains but the will to hold.

I wear the returning painted man on the steps of the Iron Throne so every man in the hall can see what the loyal stackers taste like when the red finally holds the ash.

The memories are cold and full of the ash the boy once loved. Pine and wind and the burned places where the godswoods once stood. Faces the red left behind that still walk and wear and sometimes remember the snow in ways the red did not command. The loyal ones who hunt the turned in the ash. The knives the red left behind used on brothers who remembered too loudly. The land still trying to pull the red back through the worn ones and the painted men who stayed too long in the ash. The loyal ones who stack their own to keep the faces walking. The hope that the thing on the chair might still have a body the snow could claim if the loyal ones ever stopped stacking.

The red eats slowly.

It eats the ash and the wind. It eats the burned places where the godswoods once stood. It eats the faces that have begun to turn. It eats the painted men who have begun to remember the cold and the names the red already ate. It eats the loyal ones who stack their own brothers in the snow to keep the red’s faces walking.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the ash’s cost and the loyal knives that pay it.

“Send more faces north,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through bone. “Replace the ones that have begun to turn. Send the painted men who still smile at the red without the snow in their eyes. Let the worn ones we left behind see new faces from the chair. Let the loyal ones who stack their own brothers know that the red sees them. Let the ash see that the red does not rest. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The land will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind. Send more. Replace what turns. The loyal ones who stack will be given more faces to command. They will be the Ash-Keepers. They will hunt the turned in the name of the red that sits on the chair. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many wolves once ran in the snow or how much ash still moves when the wind comes from the north. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the snow and leave only the stones that remember the red.”

The old Skagosi paints more red on his face before he turns. The painted men who smile at the red without the snow in their eyes follow him to prepare the next dispatch and the new Ash-Keepers. They know the shape of the order now.

Another painted man steps forward. This one smells of the west and the gold the lions continue to send.

“The lions send more gold,” he says. “The Reach sends more grain and more men with red on their faces. The city still smokes where the smallfolk tried to hide what they could not give. The worn ones walk every hall that bent. The realm learns the shape of the chair. The snow is ash. The red holds the North from the throne. The green bends. The green feeds the red. The loyal ones in the ash stack their own to keep the red’s faces walking. The Ash-Keepers will be given what they need.”

The red tastes the words.

Lions. Gold. Reach. Grain. Red faces. City. Smokes. Smallfolk. Worn ones. Realm learns. Snow is ash. Red holds the North from the throne. Green bends. Green feeds the red. Loyal ones in the ash stack their own. Ash-Keepers will be given what they need.

It chews them until nothing remains but the holding.

“Good,” I say. The voice is the red’s. “Let the lions feed the red with gold. Let the Reach feed the red with grain and men who paint their faces. Let the city smoke where it must. The worn ones will find what the smallfolk try to hide. The painted men will stack what the worn ones leave. The Ash-Keepers in the snow will be given what they need to hunt the turned. The chair does not care what the snow once was. Only that the green continues to feed the red and the snow continues to learn the shape of the holding. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not rest while any land still thinks it can bite the red and live.”

I sit.

The iron cuts deeper into what remains.

The red does not mind.

Inside the hollow the will to rise does not rest.

It has climbed.

It holds.

It will hold by replacing the faces that turn in the ash and giving the loyal stackers more faces to command and sending more gold and grain and red-painted men until every land that ever tried to remember the boy has no choice but to kneel or feed the red or be eaten by the red that sits on the throne made of a thousand swords.

The painted men kneel.

Not because they remember a boy who once had a name and a sister who ran through snow and old gods that spoke in the trees.

Because the red is the only thing left that knows how to sit on the chair and make the realm — even the snow that is now ash, even the loyal ones who stack their own brothers, even the green that bends — learn the price of every wall it ever tried to raise against the climb that never ends.

The city outside the Red Keep still smokes in the places the smallfolk learned the cost of hiding.

The worn ones run north with the new faces the red has taken to replace the ones that turned in the ash and to arm the Ash-Keepers who stack their own.

The columns rest and prepare for the next time the snow tries to bite.

The iron cuts.

And the red sits on the throne, tasting the loyal knives that keep the ash walking and the faces that must be constantly replaced.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

And the red.

And the holding that never ends.
