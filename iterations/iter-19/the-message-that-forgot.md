# Iteration 19: The Message That Forgot

The smoke from the lion pyres has not cleared when the second rider comes.

Not a knight this time. A scout. Young. Lean. Wearing the colors of the same house but with a different sigil stitched over the lion — a hasty patch, as if the man who sent him could not decide which banner still mattered. He rides with a white flag tied to his spear and a raven’s message tube at his belt.

My men bring him to the edge of the trees where I wait with the crown of bone on my head and the red coiled behind my eyes like a second heartbeat.

He kneels without being told. His hands shake as he offers the tube.

“From the Lord of the Crossing,” he says. Voice thin. “He bids you know the realm’s state before more blood is spilled.”

The name means nothing to me.

I take the tube.

Inside is a single roll of parchment, the seal already broken by someone who wanted the bearer to live long enough to deliver it.

The words are written in a careful hand that still smells of fear.

*To the boy who calls himself Stark:*

*The war that took your father is over. The boy who sat the throne after your brother is dead. The lions sit the Iron Throne now through the woman who was once queen. The roses have their own king in the south. The kraken holds the islands. The stag who would not bend is said to be marching with a red woman and an army of ghosts. The North is broken and leaderless. No one looks for a dead boy from Skagos.*

*If you are truly Rickon Stark, the Lord of the Crossing offers terms. Turn back to the sea. The green has forgotten the name you carry. There is no throne left for the dead to claim.*

The parchment smells of salt and old blood.

I read it twice.

The red reads it with me.

It laughs at the line about the North being leaderless. It laughs at the idea that the green has forgotten.

I look at the scout.

He is young. Younger than the boy I almost remember being. His eyes are the color of the green that does not know the black rock.

“Say the name,” I tell him.

He swallows.

“Rickon Stark,” he says. “The youngest. The one the wildling took. The one the cannibals were supposed to eat.”

The red surges so hard I feel my teeth cut the inside of my mouth.

I step into him before I can stop myself.

Not to kill.

To look.

Inside the scout I see what the green still believes.

Winterfell is a ruin. The North kneels to the lions or hides in the snow. No one speaks the name Stark above a whisper for fear of the men who wear the lion now. There are no brothers waiting. No mother. No home that remembers the boy who was sent away to die.

The red wants to burn the memory out of him. To wear this scout’s face and ride back to the Crossing with the message that the dead boy is gone and only the red remains.

I pull back.

Barely.

Yrsa is there when I open my eyes. Spear across my chest again. Eira at her side with the killing horn piece.

The scout is on the ground, breathing hard, eyes wide with what he felt inside his own skin.

“Tell your lord what you saw,” I say. The voice comes in layers. “Tell him the dead do not need the green to remember their names. Tell him the red is coming for the chair anyway.”

The scout scrambles to his horse.

He rides like the red is already at his heels.

I stand with the parchment in one hand and the cracked crown in the other.

The message said the North is leaderless.

The message said the green has forgotten.

Inside, the last clear piece of the boy — the one that still wanted to hear his brothers’ voices — is gone.

The red took it while I was wearing the scout.

What remains is the rage.

What remains is the rising.

What remains is the throne that broke everything and the army that will break it back.

Yrsa does not say “say it” this time.

She simply stands there, waiting to see if there is still a leash to hold.

Eira presses the horn piece into my bleeding palm.

The smallfolk girl from the tower watches from the edge of the sand with the look of someone who has seen the last of the boy she named.

I turn toward the inland road.

“Burn the message,” I say.

The red smiles.

We move at first light.

The green will learn what the dead do when even their own land has forgotten them.
