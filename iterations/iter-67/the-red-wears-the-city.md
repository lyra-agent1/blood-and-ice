# Iteration 67: The Red Wears the City

The iron cuts.

I sit on the chair made of swords and the red tastes the blood that has become the throne. Every day the blades dig deeper into what remains. Every day the hollow learns that the climb did not end when the last godswood burned or the last voice that remembered the boy went ash on the wind. The holding is the climb that never stops. The red sits and the realm pays in faces and loyal knives and the sons the green sends to walk the ash and the city that must be taught to forget the cost of the sons it sent.

The Ash-Keepers and the worn faces return from the North and the city with the answer.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The boy is gone. The name is gone. The snow and the girl and the brother and the father and the old gods and every voice that once tried to pull the red back are ash in the red’s mouth. What remains is the will that climbed from Skagos to the city and the red that fed it and the chair that now holds both and the hunger that has learned to sit and burn the places that sing and stack what the singing wakes and replace the North with the green’s sons who have not yet heard the wind — until they hear it and the knives find them too and the memories they brought from the green leak back through the worn faces and the city must be worn until it forgets.

The lead Ash-Keeper steps forward with a worn face whose eyes are empty in the way that means the leak has been tasted and silenced. His face is painted fresh red over scars the fire and the snow and the city purge gave him.

“The singing leaks and the city remembers,” he says. His voice is steady because he has learned the shape of the holding and the price of the knives he carries and the sons he spends and the whispers he now wears on the walls. “We sent the doubled wave of green sons into the burned places with the fire and the salt. The Ash-Keepers chose which faces walk and which are stacked when the singing reached them. Whole bands turned before the first week. The selectors who heard the green sons’ mothers through the ones they chose had to be stacked by the loyal. But the worn faces we sent into the alleys near the Red Keep found the whisperers. They wore the smallfolk who spoke of green fields and green mothers and green sons stacked in the snow. They wore the painted men on the walls who asked why the red spends the green’s youth in the boy’s ash. The city saw the worn faces walk with the green sons’ names in their mouths. The city learned the shape of the holding. But the leak is stronger than the wearing. Some of the green sons’ stories survived the knives. Some of the worn faces who carried the leak back from the ash remembered green names even after the city purge. The singing spreads through the sons the green sends and leaks back through the faces the red takes and teaches the city to remember what the red has eaten. The land bites harder when the red feeds it the green’s youth and the city hears the cost. The ash still moves. The singing is in the city’s mouth now. The cost of holding the ash is the faces that turn and the loyal ones who must stack their own and the fire that makes the land sing louder and the singing that turns the green’s sons into the stackers and the stackers into the stacked and the leak that turns the city into another ash that must be worn until it forgets.”

The red tastes the words.

Singing leaks. City remembers. Doubled wave sent. Whole bands stacked. Selectors stacked by loyal. Worn faces wore the whisperers and the painted men who asked. City saw the green sons’ names in worn mouths. City learned. But leak stronger than wearing. Green sons’ stories survived. Worn faces remembered green names after the purge. Singing spreads through sons and leaks through faces and teaches city to remember what the red has eaten. Land bites harder. Ash still moves. Singing in city’s mouth. Cost is faces that turn and loyal who stack and fire that makes land sing and singing that turns sons and leak that turns city into another ash that must be worn until it forgets.

It chews them until nothing remains but the will to hold.

I rise from the chair. The iron cuts deeper as I stand. I wear the lead Ash-Keeper first so every man in the hall can see what the singing ash and the leaking city taste like when the red feeds one and wears the other.

The memories are cold and full of the ash the boy once loved and the city that still tries to remember. Pine and wind and the burned places and the alleys near the Red Keep thick with smoke and the smallfolk who whispered of green fields while the worn faces walked among them and the painted men on the walls who asked the question before the knives found their throats. The green sons’ voices in the smoke, the mothers’ names, the sisters left behind, the fields that would never see them again. The turned sons’ prayers and the selectors’ last thoughts and the worn faces’ memories of carrying the leak and the city’s eyes watching the wearing and still whispering after. The red eats the prayers. It eats the roots. It eats the stones. It eats the girl’s laugh one more time. It eats the singing itself. It eats the green sons who turned. It eats the selectors who heard through the sons. It eats the worn faces who carried the leak. It eats the smallfolk whispers. It eats the painted men’s questions. It eats the city’s memory of the cost. It eats the fire that woke the song and the song that woke the knives and the leak that woke the city and the wearing that tried to silence the leak until there is only ash and the will that sits and the knowledge that the land and the city fight back by making the red’s own tools and the green’s sons and the capital itself into the singers and the rememberers.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the ash’s cost and the loyal knives that pay it and the green sons that make the cost higher and the leak that makes the city another wall.

I wear the worn face who carried the leak and was sent to wear the city next. His memories are full of the smoke and the knives and the green sons’ fields and the mothers’ voices and the sisters’ names and the alleys where the smallfolk listened and the walls where the painted men asked and the moment the knives found the whisperers and the green names still slipped from his mouth even after and the city’s eyes that saw the wearing and still remembered. He had smiled at the red without the snow in his eyes when he left the city the first time. Now he smiles at the red with the knives and the knowledge that the land will keep singing and the green will keep sending sons and the Ash-Keepers will keep choosing and the singing will keep turning the chosen into the stacked and the leak will keep teaching the city to remember and the wearing will keep teaching the city to forget and that is the shape of the holding that never ends.

The red eats the smoke and the knives and the stacked green sons and the selectors and the worn faces who carried the leak and the smallfolk whispers and the painted men’s questions and the city’s memory and the singing that the fire woke and the singing that turned the replacement into the replaced and the leak that woke the city and the wearing that tried to silence it.

When it is done the worn face stands again, hollowed but still smiling at the red.

“Send the next wave and wear the city until it forgets,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through bone. “Pull the worn faces who have heard the green names back to the throne or stack them where they stand. Send the worn faces who still smile at the red without the green in their eyes into every alley and every wall to find the ones who whisper and the ones who ask and wear them so the city learns that even its memory of the green sons will feed the chair. Send the next wave of green sons from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red — twice as many as the last. Let the green feed the red with its bodies and its knives and its sons and its sons’ sons if the singing takes them and the leak teaches the city and the wearing teaches the city to forget. Give the loyal Ash-Keepers who have not yet turned the authority to choose which of the green’s sons walk the ash and which are stacked when the singing reaches them and which of the selectors must be stacked when the singing reaches through the sons they chose and which worn faces must be stacked when the leak reaches the city and which of the city’s whispers must be worn on the walls until the city forgets. Send more fire. Send more salt. Send more faces from the chair. Let the green see that the red does not stop when the singing leaks back through the faces it takes and the city tries to remember. Let the ash see that the red will replace the replaced with the green’s youth and replace the city’s memory with the green’s sons and the wearing until the singing has no one left to turn or the green has no sons left to send or the city has no one left to whisper what the red has eaten. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The land will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not fear the singing its own fire wakes and does not spare the green’s sons when the singing must be fed and does not spare the city when the leak teaches it to remember. Send more. Replace what turns. Stack what remembers. Burn what sings. Replace the replaced with the green’s sons. Stack the leak in the city. Wear the city until it forgets the green sons the way the snow forgot the boy. The Ash-Keepers who stack their own brothers and burn the places that bite and choose which green faces walk the ash and which selectors fall and which worn faces carry the leak and which city whispers are worn on the walls will be given more faces to command and more fire to carry and more of the green’s sons to spend and the city’s memory to silence. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many wolves once ran in the snow or how much ash still moves when the wind comes from the north or how many godswoods still try to sing names the red already ate or how loudly the land sings when the red burns it or how many loyal knives the singing turns back on their brothers or how many green sons the singing will turn into stackers or how many selectors the singing will turn into the stacked or how many worn faces the leak will teach to remember green names or how many city whispers the wearing will silence. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the snow and leave only the stones that remember the red and the ash that learned to be silent and the green that learned to send its sons to feed the chair with their bodies and their knives and their memories of home and the city that learned to be silent when the red wears its whispers and its memory on the walls.”

The Ash-Keepers who still smile at the red without the snow in their eyes paint more red on their faces before they turn. The ones who have heard the singing and still carry the knives and the authority to choose and the worn faces who still smile without the green in their eyes follow them to prepare the next replacement wave and the greater city purge and the new faces from the green and the fire and the salt. They know the shape of the order now.

Another painted man steps forward. This one smells of the west and the gold the lions continue to send. He brings a green man in fine clothes whose hands shake even though he tries to stand like a lord.

“The Reach sends more grain,” he says. “The lions send more gold. But the envoy from the roses says the smallfolk in the green lands hear the stories of the Ash-Keepers stacking the green’s sons in the snow and the worn faces leaking the stories back to the city and the red wearing the city’s whispers on the walls and they whisper that the red is eating the green to hold the boy’s ash and now eating the city’s memory of the eating. The envoy offers the grain and the gold and the next wave of sons with red on their faces but asks that the red not spend so many of the green’s sons in the singing places and not wear the city’s memory so openly. He says the green bends but the green has sons to spare only so long and the city has memory only so long before it breaks.”

The red tastes the words.

Reach. Grain. Lions. Gold. Envoy from the roses. Smallfolk hear the stories of the Ash-Keepers stacking the green’s sons and the worn faces leaking and the red wearing the city’s whispers. Whisper that the red is eating the green to hold the boy’s ash and now eating the city’s memory of the eating. Offers grain and gold and the next wave of sons but asks that the red not spend so many of the green’s sons in the singing places and not wear the city’s memory so openly. Says the green bends but the green has sons to spare only so long and the city has memory only so long before it breaks.

It chews them until nothing remains but the holding.

I motion the green man forward. He still stands like a lord who believes words can bargain with the chair and that the red will keep the cost of the ash and the singing and the replacement and the leak and the wearing a secret from the green that feeds it and that the green’s sons will only be spent on the green’s wars and not the boy’s ash and that the city’s memory can be worn quietly.

I wear him on the steps of the Iron Throne so every man in the hall and every Ash-Keeper preparing the next wave and the greater city purge and every face the red has taken can see what asking the red to spare the green’s sons and the city’s memory tastes like.

The memories are soft and full of green fields and grain and sons sent to die for a queen who is now ash and a chair that the roses once thought they could claim and the hope that the thing on the chair might still bargain if the green feeds it enough and sends enough sons and does not ask too many questions about the snow and the singing and the replacement and the leak and the wearing. He tastes the fear of the smallfolk who burn their own homes rather than feed the red. He tastes the stories of the Ash-Keepers stacking the green’s sons in the snow and the worn faces leaking the stories back to the city and the red wearing the city’s whispers on the walls. He tastes the hope that the green might still be spared the full price if it offers the next wave and the grain and the gold and asks nicely and the city’s memory might still be left alone. The red eats the fields. It eats the sons. It eats the hope. It eats the mercy and the secrets and the green’s claim on its own sons and the city’s memory until there is only the iron and the will that sits and the knowledge that the green will learn the shape of the holding the same way the snow did — by feeding the red and sending its sons to walk the ash and stack what turns and burn what sings and watch the red wear the city’s whispers and the city’s memory on the walls until only the chair remains and the green sons who have learned to smile at the red without the snow in their eyes or the singing in their mouths or the leak in their stories or the memory of the cost.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the chair’s answer.

“Send the grain and the gold and the next wave of the green’s sons from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red — twice as many as the last,” I say. The voice is the red’s. “Send the green’s sons who have painted their faces. Let the green feed the red with its bodies and its knives and its sons and the sons of the houses that bend. Let the smallfolk in the green lands burn their own homes if they must. Let the stories of the Ash-Keepers and the replacement and the stacking of the green’s sons and the worn faces leaking the stories back to the city and the red wearing the city’s whispers and the city’s memory on the walls reach every hall that bends. The next replacement wave will take the new green faces and the fire and the salt to every place in the North that still sings. The worn faces who still smile at the red without the green in their eyes will find the ones who whisper in the city and the ones who remember and wear them on the walls until the city forgets. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The green will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not keep secrets about the cost of the holding and does not spare the green’s sons when the singing must be fed and does not spare the city when the leak teaches it to remember and does not spare the sons of the houses that bend when the singing reaches them. Send more. Replace what turns. Stack what remembers. Burn what sings. Replace the replaced with the green’s sons. Stack the leak in the city. Wear the city until it forgets the green sons the way the snow forgot the boy. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many roses once bloomed or how much grain the green can grow or how many sons the lions can send or how loudly the smallfolk whisper that the red is eating the green and the city’s memory or how many green sons the singing will turn into stackers or how many selectors the singing will turn into the stacked or how many worn faces the leak will teach to remember green names or how many city whispers the wearing will silence. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the green and leave only the stones that remember the red.”

The painted men kneel.

Not because they remember a boy who once had a name and a sister who ran through snow and old gods that spoke in the trees.

Because the red is the only thing left that knows how to sit on the chair and make the realm — even the snow that is now ash and sings louder when burned and turns the loyal knives and now turns the green’s sons and the selectors who spend them and leaks through the worn faces into the city, even the loyal Ash-Keepers who stack their own brothers and carry the fire and choose which green faces live and die and which selectors fall and which worn faces carry the leak and which city whispers are worn on the walls, even the green that bends and feeds and still tries to ask that its sons and the city’s memory be spared — learn the price of every wall it ever tried to raise against the climb that never ends.

The city outside the Red Keep still smokes in the places the smallfolk learned the cost of hiding.

The Ash-Keepers march north with the next replacement wave and the new green faces the red has taken and the fire and the salt to burn every place that sings and the authority to stack the green’s sons and the selectors when the singing reaches them.

The worn faces who still smile at the red without the green in their eyes move into every alley and every wall to find the ones who whisper and the ones who remember and wear them on the walls so the city learns to forget the green sons the way the snow forgot the boy.

The columns rest and prepare for the next time the green tries to bargain or the snow tries to sing through the fire or the green sons try to remember their mothers or the selectors try to hear the singing through the ones they chose or the city tries to remember what the red has eaten.

The iron cuts.

And the red sits on the throne, tasting the loyal knives that keep the ash walking and the faces that must be constantly replaced and the places where the land still sings names the red already ate and the fire that makes the singing louder and the green sons who will learn to stack their own in the snow and the selectors who will learn to fall when the singing reaches through the sons they spent and the worn faces who will learn to carry the leak and the city that will learn to be silent when the red wears its whispers and its memory on the walls.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

And the red.

And the holding that never ends.
