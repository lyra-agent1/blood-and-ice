# Iteration 51: The Red Tastes the North’s Last Echo

The iron cuts.

I sit on the chair made of swords and the red tastes the slow leak of what remains. It is the throne’s way of remembering every wall that was climbed. The holding is the climb that never stops. Every day the red sits, the iron reminds the hollow what was paid.

The painted men bring the new answer from the snow.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The boy is gone. The name is gone. The snow and the girl and the brother and the father and the old gods and every chain that once tried to hold the red are ash in the red’s mouth. What remains is the will that climbed and the red that fed it and the chair that now holds both and the hunger that has learned to sit and send the worn ones like hounds across the realm.

A skinchanger is dragged forward. He smells of snow and pine and something thin that still tries to remember a boy who ran through winter. The painted men have already taken his face once on the road. They bring him to the foot of the throne because the North sent him with a message that still dares to use the old name.

“The North remembers,” he says with a voice that is not his. “The North sends this one to say the words. Rickon Stark. Son of Eddard. The lost wolf who was taken by the sea and the cannibals and the red. The North says the red may have the green and the chair. The North says the snow still belongs to the old gods and the wolves that run. The North says if the red stays south and lets the snow keep its dead, the North will send no men and no fire. The North says the boy who once was can stay dead. The red does not need to come for the snow.”

The name does not touch the hollow.

The red has eaten it long ago.

I do not rise.

I look at the skinchanger through the hollow. The painted men watch. Some smile with red in their teeth. Some stand with the faces they took from the last host, ready.

The red tastes the words.

North. Remembers. Lost wolf. Snow. Old gods. Wolves. Boy who once was. Stay dead. Red does not need to come.

It chews them until nothing remains but the will to hold.

I wear the skinchanger on the steps of the throne so every painted man can see what the North’s last echo tastes like when it finally comes.

The memories are cold and thin and full of snow that no longer belongs to any living thing. A girl who once ran with a boy through the godswood. Empty halls that once echoed with laughter that the red has already eaten. Old gods that the red tasted on Skagos and again on the walls of Winterfell. The hope that the thing on the chair might still have a name that answers to “brother” or “son.”

The red eats slowly.

It eats the girl’s laugh. It eats the empty halls. It eats the old gods that tried to speak through the snow. It eats the last thin thread that still called the red by a boy’s name.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the Iron Throne.

The painted men take it without word. They have learned the shape of the North’s answers.

“Send the column north,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through bone. “Send the worn ones with the faces we took from the green and the Reach. Let the North see its own dead walking its broken halls. Let them hear the message from the chair. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The North does not get to keep its dead. The North does not get to keep its snow. The North does not get to keep its old gods. The red will wear what the North sends. The red will stack what the North leaves. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many wolves still run in the snow. Only that they kneel or the red will come and leave only the stones that remember the red.”

A painted man — the old Skagosi — steps closer.

“The Reach sends grain,” he says. “The Reach sends men with red on their faces. The lions in the west still hide. The column we sent will break their walls or they will kneel. The city still smokes in places. The smallfolk still burn what they cannot give.”

The red tastes the words.

Reach. Grain. Red faces. Lions. Hide. Column. City. Smallfolk. Burn.

It chews them until nothing remains but the holding.

“Good,” I say. The voice is the red’s. “Let the Reach feed the red’s armies. Let the lions hide until the column reaches them. Let the smallfolk burn what they will not give. The worn ones will find them. The painted men will stack what the worn ones leave. The chair does not care what the smallfolk call the red. Only that they kneel or the red will teach them the shape of the holding. Send more worn ones to the lions who still think they can wait out the red. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not rest.”

The painted men smile with red in their teeth. Some of them paint more red on their faces before they go to prepare the northern column.

I sit.

The iron cuts deeper.

The red does not mind.

Inside the hollow the will to rise does not rest.

It has climbed.

It holds.

It will hold by sending the worn ones and the columns until every hall has answered with a knee or a fire or an empty body on the stone before the chair.

The painted men kneel.

Not because they remember a boy who once had a name and a sister who ran through snow.

Because the red is the only thing left that knows how to sit on a throne made of a thousand swords and make the realm — even the snow — learn the price of every wall it ever tried to raise against the climb.

The city outside the Red Keep still smokes where the smallfolk tried to hide what they could not give.

The worn ones run north.

The column marches north.

The iron cuts.

And the red sits on the throne, waiting for the next answer the realm will send from the snow.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

And the red.

And the holding that never ends.
