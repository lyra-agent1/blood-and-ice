# Iteration 1: The Wreck and the First Blood

The sea spat Rickon Stark onto the black rocks of Skagos like a piece of driftwood it had grown tired of chewing.

He woke to the sound of waves grinding stone and the distant cry of something that was not quite a bird. His small body was half-buried in wet sand and shattered driftwood. Shaggydog lay beside him, the great black direwolf breathing in ragged, wet gasps. The beast’s fur was matted with salt and blood—some of it the wolf’s, some of it not. Rickon could not remember whose.

He was nine years old. Or ten. Time had become a loose thing since the Freys.

The boy pushed himself up on shaking arms. His clothes were rags. His feet were bare and cut. In his fist he still clutched the small knife Osha had pressed into his hand the night they fled the Twins. The blade was nicked and dull, but it was his.

Shaggydog lifted his massive head and growled at the mist that clung to the shore. Rickon growled back without thinking. It was the only language that still felt honest.

They had been running for so long that the idea of stopping felt like a trap. But the sea had stopped them for them. The little boat—more a leaking skin boat than anything seaworthy—had come apart in the night. Osha was gone. He did not know if she had drowned or been taken by the current. He only knew the wolf had dragged him onto the rocks and that the cold had nearly finished what the waves started.

Rickon stood on legs that did not want to hold him. The island rose before him in jagged black teeth. Skagos. The name tasted like blood and old stories. Cannibals. Unicorns. Men who wore the skins of their enemies and spoke to the dead. Father had spoken of it once, in the long hall at Winterfell, when the wind howled and the torches guttered. *Skagos is no place for a Stark,* he had said. *Not even a dead one.*

Rickon smiled with too many teeth. He was already dead. The Freys had killed him. The Boltons had killed him. The world had killed Rickon Stark and left this thing wearing his skin.

He and the wolf moved inland as the light died.

They found the first corpse before nightfall.

It lay in a shallow gully, half-eaten by birds and something larger. The man had been big, with arms like tree trunks and a beard braided with bone. His throat had been opened from ear to ear. The wound was old enough that the blood had turned black, but not so old that the meat had gone bad. Rickon stared at the body for a long time. His stomach cramped with hunger. Shaggydog whined low in his throat.

The boy knelt. He used the little knife to saw through the sinew of the man’s thigh. It was hard work. His hands were small and the blade was poor. By the time he had a strip of meat, his arms shook. He built a fire the way Osha had taught him—dry moss, flint from the rocks, patience that hurt. The meat sizzled. It tasted like iron and salt and victory.

That night the dreams came.

He was running through snow that was not snow. The trees had faces. The wind spoke in voices that knew his name. Something old moved beneath the roots, something that had been sleeping since the Long Night. It reached up through the ground and touched the inside of his ribs. It tasted the rage there—the same rage that had let him bite a man’s finger off at the Twins, the same rage that had made him want to kill every Frey in the world and then start on the rest.

The thing inside liked the rage.

When Rickon woke, Shaggydog was standing over him, hackles raised, staring at nothing. The direwolf’s eyes were red in the firelight. For a moment Rickon thought the wolf was going to tear out his throat. Then the beast lowered its head and licked the blood from the boy’s chin.

They moved at first light.

The tribes found them before they found the tribes.

It was a hunting party—four men on foot, one on the back of a unicorn that looked more like a nightmare than a horse. The beast had a single spiraling horn black as jet and eyes that held no mercy. The men wore sealskin and bone. One carried a spear tipped with what looked like a human femur. They spoke a language that sounded like rocks grinding together.

Rickon did not run. Running had never saved him.

The men circled. The one on the unicorn said something sharp. Another laughed. They saw a small, filthy boy and a half-dead wolf. Easy meat.

The first spear came low and fast.

Shaggydog met it with a roar that shook the trees. The wolf took the spear in his shoulder and kept coming. Rickon moved with him. He was small and fast and full of the old anger. The knife found the inside of a man’s thigh. Hot blood sprayed across Rickon’s face. He laughed—a high, broken sound that did not belong to a child.

The unicorn screamed and reared. Its rider tried to bring a club down on the boy’s head. Rickon rolled under the blow and came up inside the man’s guard. He drove the knife up under the ribs, the way Osha had shown him with rabbits. The man made a wet sound and fell.

The last two turned to run.

That was when the thing in the ground woke up properly.

Pain exploded in Rickon’s side. One of the hunters had circled and driven a bone dagger between his ribs. The boy looked down at the hilt protruding from his body and felt nothing at first. Then the cold came. Then the heat.

The world went red.

He heard laughing. It took him a moment to realize it was coming from his own throat. The sound was wrong—too deep, too many voices at once. His vision tunneled. The wound in his side stopped hurting. Instead it felt like an invitation.

He turned on the man who had stabbed him.

The hunter’s eyes went wide. He raised his spear. Rickon caught it with one hand. The wood splintered. He did not remember moving. One moment he was a boy with a knife in his ribs. The next he was something else wearing the boy’s skin. He tore the spear from the man’s grip and used the broken end to open the hunter’s throat from jaw to collarbone.

The last man—the one on the unicorn—tried to flee.

Rickon ran him down on foot. The unicorn was fast, but the thing inside Rickon did not tire. When he caught them he did not use the knife. He used his teeth. The unicorn died screaming. The rider died begging in a language Rickon did not understand and did not care to learn.

When it was over, the red receded like a tide.

Rickon stood in the middle of four corpses and one dead unicorn, panting. Blood—most of it not his—dripped from his chin and hands. The knife was still in his side. He pulled it out slowly. The wound should have killed him. It did not. The flesh around the hole was already knitting, ugly and purple and wrong.

Shaggydog approached carefully, ears flat. For the first time in his life, the direwolf seemed afraid of his boy.

Rickon looked at his hands. They were still small. Still a child’s hands. But they had done this.

He smiled. It was not a nice smile.

“More,” he said to the empty forest. His voice was hoarse. “There will be more.”

The old magic on the island had heard him.

And it had answered.

## Self-Evaluation (Iteration 1)

**Against Success Criteria:**
- **Arrival & early survival on Skagos**: Strong. Establishes the brutal landing, loss of Osha, feral state, and first violent contact with the tribes.
- **Seeds of Darrow-like growth**: Introduced. Rickon is already showing refusal to run, tactical thinking in the fight, and the beginning of building reputation through violence. More development needed in future iterations.
- **Monster inside (Logan Ninefingers style)**: Introduced. The "red" rage, unkillable quality after injury, maniacal laughter, loss of self, fear even from Shaggydog. Feels like a curse already.
- **Tone**: Grimdark, visceral, and in line with ASOIAF + Abercrombie + Red Rising influences.
- **Pacing & hook**: Good opening hook. Ends on a strong note that the magic has noticed him and is responding.

**What worked well:**
- The monster awakening feels earned and terrifying rather than just a power fantasy.
- Rickon’s voice and feral nature come through immediately.
- Action is brutal and personal.

**What needs improvement in future iterations:**
- More internal reflection on his transformation (Darrow-style strategic mind growing alongside the monster).
- Deeper sense of the Skagosi tribes and their culture.
- Rickon should begin to think in terms of conquest and leadership, not just survival and rage.

**Ratchet Decision (self):** This section advances the goal and meets the quality bar. Recommend keeping.

**Next Hypothesis (Iteration 2):** Move into the first real engagement with a tribe. Rickon and Shaggydog are found by a larger group. He begins to use the fear of the "monster" as a weapon while also showing the first sparks of strategic thinking (perhaps sparing someone or making a calculated alliance). Introduce a named Skagosi character who will become important. Deepen the internal conflict between the boy who wants to go home and the thing the island is turning him into.
