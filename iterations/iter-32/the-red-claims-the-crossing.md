# Iteration 32: The Red Claims the Crossing

The north tower does not sleep.

We watch it across the river for two days while the stacked dead on our side grow higher and the fires burn high enough for the walls to read the message. The painted men who wear the south tower dead slip across at night and return with green faces and green voices. They tell us the Lord of the Crossing has closed every inner gate. He has sent riders south for more men. He has put his sons on the walls with bows and told them the dead boy king will not pass.

Inside the hollow the red tastes the news and finds it thin.

*Every lord says the same until the walls fall.*

On the third night the north tower sends a boat.

It comes under a white banner with two young men in black and gold. Frey sons. One older, one barely old enough to hold a sword. They step onto our bank with empty hands and the look of men who have already decided which of them will die first.

The older one speaks.

“Rickon Stark. The Lord of the Crossing offers terms. You may take the south tower and the bridge. You may take the grain and the horses. In return you will leave the north tower and turn your column south or north. The Crossing will not pursue. The lions will not pursue. The dead should stay dead.”

The name does not stir the hollow.

The red has eaten it.

I step forward so they can see the crown against the firelight. No horns. No chains. Only the light shape of what was paid for on Skagos and the ridge.

The younger Frey looks at Eira where she stands behind me. She does not raise her head. The red left her enough to stand and breathe and know what she is. The older Frey’s face goes still when he sees what the red has done to her.

“The Lord offers more,” the older one says quickly. “Gold. Men. A place at his table if you swear to the Crossing. The North is broken. There is no chair for a dead boy in Winterfell. But there can be one here if you are reasonable.”

The red laughs low in the hollow.

*Reasonable.*

I take the older Frey by the face before he can finish.

The red slides in clean. His memories are all fear and the smell of the Twins and a father who counts sons like coins. He has already decided this one will be the sacrifice. He hates the father more than he hates the dead boy king.

I let the red laugh through his mouth.

Then I turn him on his brother.

The younger Frey dies on the bank with his own brother’s hands around his throat. The boatmen try to push off. The unicorns take them in the water.

When it is done I let the older Frey’s body fall.

The north tower horns sound.

I wear the older Frey’s face to the edge of the river so every archer on the far wall can see.

“Open the gates,” I say with his voice. The red makes it carry. “Or I will wear every son you send and every man who stands behind them. The chair does not care which tower stands. Only that we climb.”

The north tower does not open.

They send arrows instead.

The red goes with me when I give the order.

Not leashed. Not held back. The chain is ash on the wind behind us.

We cross the river in the dark. Skinchangers wearing the south tower dead climb the walls first. The unicorns follow. The painted men who smile at the red follow them. I go with them.

The laugh starts in the hollow before the first gate falls.

Inside the north tower the Lord of the Crossing is an old man with too many sons and not enough walls. He tries to rally what is left in the great hall. He calls the name one last time.

“Rickon Stark! The North sent you to die! The sea could not keep you! We will—”

I take him.

I wear him in his own hall in front of the last of his men.

The laugh that comes out is not the Lord’s. It is the one the Skagosi feared on the island. The one that broke the ridge. The one that took the bridge and the south tower.

The last black banners burn.

When it is done the Twins are one thing again.

The stacked dead are higher than they have ever been. The painted men work quickly. They have learned the shape of the message.

I stand in the Lord’s hall with the crown in my hand. The river runs between the towers below. Eira is brought inside. She does not speak. She stands where the red can see her and the army can see what a broken chain looks like when the red is given nothing to fear.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The boy is gone. The name is gone. The anchors are ash and broken meat. The green host that came for us at the ridge is scattered or stacked. The Crossing that thought the dead should stay dead is now the red’s.

A painted man — one of the old Skagosi — steps forward.

“The lions will come,” he says. “The North will remember or they will not. The chair is still far.”

I look at the black walls that are now red.

“Let them come,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through a skull. “We will wear what they send. We will stack what they leave. The rising does not stop at the Twins. The chair does not care how many walls stand between it and us. Only that we climb.”

The column makes the Twins its own.

Fires burn high in both towers.

The green is afraid.

And the red has its first real seat on this side of the water.

Inside the hollow the will to rise does not rest.

It looks south.

It looks north.

It looks at the chair.

And it climbs.
