# Iteration 14: The Sea Remembers

The fleet is twenty-three black ships.

They cut the waves like knives made from the island’s own bones. The prows are carved with the red in the Last Skin’s blood. The crown of bone rides the lead mast, lashed there so every man can see what Skagos paid to make a king.

I stand at the bow of the largest. Salt in my eyes. Salt in the wounds the horn left on my skull. The sea is the color of a blade that has already tasted blood.

Yrsa is at my left. Eira at my right. They have not left my side since we pushed off the black sand. They are the only ones who still call me by a name that feels like it might once have been mine.

“Rickon,” Yrsa says sometimes, when the red is too loud in my head. Just the word. Like a hand on the leash.

The storm finds us on the second night.

It is not wind and rain. It is the sea remembering what the island took from it.

Waves the size of longhouses rise and break. Men are swept overboard screaming names the red has already eaten from me. The black ships groan like dying unicorns. The Last Skin stands at the prow of her own vessel, white paint running, laughing as the water tries to take her.

The red wakes hungry.

I feel it behind my eyes, tasting the fear of every painted man who now calls me king. It wants to step into them one by one and make them row until their hearts burst. It wants to wear the sea itself.

I hold it.

Barely.

A wave takes the second ship. The men on it go under without a sound. I see their faces in the red — brothers, fathers, sons of the island — and for a heartbeat I almost remember what it felt like to have a family that was not made of blood and bone and kneeling.

The memory slips.

I reach for the crown.

The red surges the moment my hand touches the skulls.

I do not fight it this time.

I open the door all the way.

The world goes hot and bright. I am laughing — the wrong laughter, the many-voiced one — and the sea answers. I step into the water the way I stepped into the Stormfather. I am inside the storm. I am the wave and the wind and the thing that drags men down.

For one terrible moment I am everywhere.

I see the fleet from above. I see the faces of the men who follow me. I see Yrsa on the deck, spear planted, mouth open in a shout I cannot hear. I see Eira clutching the broken piece of the killing horn like a talisman.

I see myself.

The boy on the bow.

Small. Painted in blood and salt. Eyes the color of the red when it has fed.

He is looking at the horizon like he still remembers why he is crossing.

I do not.

The red wants to keep the sea. To drown the fleet and start over on the black sand where everything is simple and hungry.

I pull it back.

Not all the way. Enough.

The storm breaks.

The sea goes flat as glass for three heartbeats — long enough for the ships to right themselves, for men to haul their brothers back aboard, for the Last Skin to stop laughing.

When I open my eyes I am on my knees on the deck. Blood runs from my nose into the salt. The crown is in my hands. Cracked deeper than before.

Yrsa hauls me up.

“Say it,” she says. Voice raw. “Say the name before it is gone.”

I look at her.

I know her face. I know she stayed.

“Rickon,” I say.

The word is smaller than it was on the sand.

“Rickon Stark,” I try.

It almost holds.

Eira presses the piece of horn into my palm again. The one she gave me when we left.

“You told me I was yours instead of killing me,” she whispers. “On the path. When the red wanted everything. You chose then. Choose now.”

I close my fist around the horn.

Land appears on the horizon at dawn.

Not the green fields I half-remember from a life that is slipping. Rocks. A small tower. A handful of lights. A ship flying a banner I do not know — a lion, or a rose, or something that once meant power on the other side of the water.

The men look to me.

The red looks through me.

I raise the black-wood spear.

“Take it,” I say.

The voice is mine.

For now.

The fleet turns toward the shore.

The sea remembers what we took from the island.

Westeros is about to learn what the red remembers.
