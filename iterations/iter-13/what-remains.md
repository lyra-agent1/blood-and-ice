# Iteration 13: What Remains

The killing horn lies in pieces on the black sand.

Around it the last free tribes kneel in the surf. Unicorns stamp and scream. Skinchangers with white paint and filed teeth stand beside hunters who once called me meat. The Bone-King is dead — the red took him in the final moments when the horn tried to unmake me. He died smiling, the old man, as if he had been waiting for a wolf who would finally finish what the island started.

I stand at the edge of the water with the crown of bone on my head and blood still running from the cracks.

The sea is the color of iron.

I try to remember the name of the place we are going.

Winterfell.

The word is there. The shape of the walls. The sound of the direwolves in the godswood. But the faces are gone. All of them. I reach for the brother who taught me to fight with wooden swords and find only the red laughing.

I open my mouth to give the order to build the ships.

What comes out is not my voice.

It is the sound the horn made when it broke.

Yrsa grabs my arm. Hard. The way she did on the ridge the night I first held the leash.

“Say your name,” she demands. “Say it now, before the red takes the last piece.”

The tribe watches. Hundreds of them. Painted. Armed. Afraid of their own king.

I look at her. I know the shape of her face. I know she has green eyes even if I cannot see the color. I know she stayed when everyone else ran.

“Rickon,” I say.

The word feels like someone else’s.

“Rickon Stark,” I try again.

The red pushes. It wants the name. It wants to wear it like it wore the Stormfather.

Yrsa slaps me.

The pain is clean. Human.

“Again,” she says.

“Rickon Stark of Winterfell,” I say, and this time the red does not take it all the way.

Eira steps forward from the prisoners who became soldiers. She holds a piece of the killing horn in her hands like an offering.

“You told me I belonged to you instead of killing me,” she says. “On the path. When the red wanted my throat. You chose.”

She presses the broken horn into my palm.

“Choose again. Or there will be nothing left to cross the water.”

I close my fist around the horn.

The red howls inside me.

I do not let it out.

Not yet.

We spend three days burning the dead and building the fleet from the drowned caves’ black wood and the hides of the unicorns we could not save. The Last Skin carves the red into the prows with her own blood. Every ship will carry the island’s hunger.

On the fourth morning I stand at the front of the largest vessel. The crown of bone is lashed to the mast so the sea can see what Skagos made. Shaggydog is at my heel, changed but still mine. Yrsa and Eira stand on either side — the last two people who remember the boy.

I look across the water.

Westeros is a smear on the horizon.

The Iron Throne waits there. The place that killed my family. The place that left me for dead.

I do not remember my mother’s face.

I do not remember the sound of my brothers laughing.

But I remember the rage.

I remember the rising.

I remember what the red is for.

“Row,” I say.

The word is mine.

The red smiles behind it.

The fleet moves.

The island watches us go.

And somewhere on the other side of the water, the game of thrones is about to learn what happens when a wolf who has already died once decides to play.
