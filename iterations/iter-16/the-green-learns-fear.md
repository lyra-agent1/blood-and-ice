# Iteration 16: The Green Learns Fear

The tower is ash by morning.

The bodies on the beach are stacked the Skagosi way — skulls on top, the Lannister lion banner nailed through the highest one so the tide can take the message. My men move through the green like they still smell the black trees. They do not laugh the way they did on the island. The new blood has made them quiet. Hungry in a different way.

I stand on the ridge above the ruin and look inland.

The land is wrong. Too soft. Too many colors that have no place in the red. Trees that do not twist. Grass that does not remember being walked on by unicorns. The smallfolk who survived kneel in a line at the edge of the sand. The girl who said my name is among them. She has not spoken since the tower fell. She watches me the way the Last Skin watched the first time the red took the body.

Yrsa is at my shoulder. Eira has not left the piece of the killing horn since we landed. She holds it like a shield between me and the thing I am becoming.

“The green does not know us yet,” Yrsa says. “They will. But not if we wait for them to bring their lions and their roses.”

The red agrees.

It has tasted the young lord’s blood and the fear in the smallfolk’s eyes. It wants more. It wants the villages further in. It wants to wear the faces of the dead Lannister men and walk their roads until the whole realm screams the name it can barely remember.

I close my eyes.

Winterfell.

The word is still there. The walls. The cold. But when I reach for the face of the man who was supposed to sit the throne after my father, there is only the crown of bone and the laughter.

I open my eyes.

“Send them,” I say.

The Last Skin smiles with her filed teeth.

Six of her skinchangers step into the bodies we left in the tower. They wear the golden cloaks and the lion faces like new skins. They take the Lannister horses. Before the sun is fully up they are riding inland on dead men’s errands, carrying the story of what happened here in the voices of the men who died telling it.

The red likes that.

It likes the idea of the green learning fear from the mouths of their own.

I walk down to the line of smallfolk.

The girl looks up when my shadow falls on her. She is shaking but she does not drop her eyes.

“You said my name last night,” I tell her. The voice comes out in layers. “Say it again.”

She swallows.

“Rickon Stark,” she whispers. “The lost one. The one they said was eaten by cannibals on Skagos.”

The red surges at the word *eaten*.

I feel my hands twitch toward her throat.

Yrsa’s spear is suddenly between us. Not pointed at me. Pointed at the space where the red wants to go.

“Say the rest,” Yrsa says to the girl. Voice like iron on iron. “Say why he came back.”

The girl’s voice breaks but she does not stop.

“Because the world took his father and his brothers. Because the lions and the roses and the wolves that are not you left him for dead. Because the black rock woke something that does not forget.”

I look at her.

For one heartbeat I almost see her face clearly. Almost remember what it felt like to be the boy who would have protected someone like her instead of wearing her fear like a second skin.

The red takes the memory before I can hold it.

I turn to the men who are still unloading the ships.

“Burn the green where it stands,” I say. “Every village between here and the first real road. Wear their faces when you do it. Let them run with stories of dead men who laugh.”

The army moves.

The smallfolk are left kneeling.

The girl is still staring at me when I walk away.

Inside, the name is smaller again.

Rickon.

Stark.

The words feel like they belong to someone who died on the black sand and left only the red wearing his crown.

Yrsa walks beside me without speaking.

Eira keeps the piece of horn in her fist.

They are the only ones who still believe there is something left to save.

The green is learning fear.

The red is learning how much it likes the taste of it.

And somewhere inland, the lions are already riding toward the smoke.
