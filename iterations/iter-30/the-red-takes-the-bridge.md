# Iteration 30: The Red Takes the Bridge

The river is wide and brown and full of the green’s dead.

We reach it on the fifth day after the tower. The painted men call it the Broken Ford. The unicorns smell the water and grow restless. Skinchangers who ran ahead return wearing green faces and speak with green voices.

“A hundred men,” one says. His new mouth smiles wrong. “Freys and lions both. They hold the bridge with archers on the far bank and spears on this side. They have heard the stories. They say the dead boy king will not cross.”

Inside the hollow the red laughs low.

*They are wrong.*

I walk to the edge of the trees where the column can see me against the water. The crown is light on my head. No horns. No chains. The army that remains is quiet. The ones who stayed after the ridge are the ones who smile when the red moves. The ones who fear it more than they fear the green.

A man in Frey colors steps onto the near end of the bridge under a white banner. He is not young. He has the look of someone who has already counted the cost of living and decided the numbers do not favor him.

“Rickon Stark!” he calls across the water. The name carries. It does not stir anything in the hollow anymore. The red ate the last of it on the Stark stones. “The Crossing remembers its oaths. Turn back. The North is broken. The lions hold the Iron Throne. There is no chair for a dead boy here.”

The red tastes the words.

Oaths. North. Chair. Dead boy.

It spits them out.

I do not answer with words.

I send the worn ones first.

Skinchangers who wear the faces we took at the tower and the village slip into the water like shadows. They climb the far bank wearing green men’s skins. The archers on the bridge do not know which of their brothers is still theirs until the first arrows turn.

Then the unicorns charge.

The red goes with me when I give the order.

Not leashed. Not held back. The chain is ash on the wind behind us.

We hit the Frey spears on the near bank like the sea hitting the cliffs on Skagos. I take a man by the throat and wear him before his body knows it is dead. His memories are all fear and the smell of the Twins and a wife who waits for a man who will not come home. The red chews and keeps moving.

The Frey commander on the bridge tries to rally his men. He calls the name again.

“Stark! The North sent us—”

I take him.

Not with a knife. With the red.

I walk across the blood-slick planks wearing his face. The archers see their own lord turn and begin cutting down the men beside him. The laugh that comes out is not the commander’s. It is the one the Bone-King heard. The one that broke the ridge.

The green who remain break.

Some jump into the river. The unicorns take them in the water. Some kneel. The red does not care for kneeling. It wants the message carried.

When it is done the bridge is ours.

The stacked dead are higher on this side than they were at the tower. The painted men work quickly. They have learned the shape of the message.

I stand on the far bank with the crown in my hand. The water runs red beneath the bridge. Eira is somewhere behind the column. She does not speak. She has not spoken since the ridge. She carries what the red left her and keeps the pace. The army watches her the way men watch a storm that has already broken once and might break again.

Inside the hollow the red is not satisfied.

It has tasted the bridge. It has tasted the oaths and the names the green still try to use. It wants the Twins. It wants the walls of the Crossing. It wants to wear the Lord of the Crossing in front of his own sons and make the black banners burn.

A painted man — one of the old Skagosi who still smells of salt and blood — steps forward.

“The green will send more,” he says. “They will close every road.”

I look at him until he looks at the stacked dead instead.

“Let them close them,” I say. The voice is the red’s now. It speaks through the hollow like wind through a skull. “We will wear what they send. We will stack what they leave. The chair does not care how many bridges stand between it and us.”

The column crosses before the light dies.

No one sings. No one prays. The unicorns pull the carts across the planks and the water keeps running red long after we are gone.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The rising does not stop at water.

It does not stop at names.

It does not stop at bridges.

We march.

The Twins wait.

And the red is hungry.
