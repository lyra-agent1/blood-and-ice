# Iteration 23: The Red Waits

The field stinks of opened bellies and the green’s fear.

We stack the dead until the pile reaches higher than a man on horseback. The lord’s gilded plate is hammered flat and nailed to the topmost skull like a second crown. My painted men work in silence. No songs. No laughter. Only the wet sound of spears pulling free and the unicorns tearing at the corpses for the marrow they were promised.

I stand apart.

The crown of bone sits crooked. One of the tines cracked deeper during the wearing of the lord. Blood — not all of it the green’s — crusts the edge where it rests against my brow. Shaggydog circles the pile once, twice, then sits at my feet without a sound. Even the direwolf has forgotten what name to answer to.

Yrsa stands ten paces to my left.

Eira stands ten paces to my right.

The army sees the distance. They see the horn piece still clutched in Eira’s fist like a blade she has not yet decided to drop. They see Yrsa’s spear lowered but not put away. They see their Red-Hand king standing between the only two people who did not run when the red tried to eat one of its own.

A skinchanger from the Last Skin’s old tribe approaches. His face is painted half black, half the color of the lord he wore during the breaking. He kneels in the blood-mud and offers a strip of flesh cut from the dead man’s thigh.

“For the king who walks in other skins,” he says. Voice low. “The green tasted sweet.”

I take the meat. I do not eat it. I hold it until the red grows impatient and forces my jaw to move. The taste is nothing. The memory the red pulls from the bite is the lord’s last thought — *the dead should have stayed dead* — before I opened his throat from the inside.

The skinchanger watches me chew.

“He is still in there,” the man whispers to the others near him. “The boy. Or what is left of him.”

Another voice, further back: “The red almost wore the girl. We all saw.”

A third: “The anchors held. For now.”

They speak as if I cannot hear. As if the red has not already slipped into the speaker and seen the shape of their fear. One of them is already planning how he would put an arrow in my back if the red ever turns fully on the painted men. The red smiles at the thought. It files the man’s face away for later.

I drop the half-eaten strip of flesh into the mud.

“March at first light,” I say. The voice that comes out is mine and not mine. Rough. Hollow. “The Crossing will send more. We meet them on ground we choose.”

No one cheers.

No one argues.

They simply begin to move the way men move when the only thing keeping them in line is the certainty that their king will wear them if they falter.

Yrsa steps closer once the others have drifted back to the fires.

“You let them see,” she says. Not accusation. Not quite. “You let them see you inside her.”

“The red wanted the eyes,” I answer. The truth tastes like ash and old blood. “It wanted to look ahead through her and see what the lions are bringing. It still wants.”

Eira does not move. She holds the killing horn piece so tightly the edge has cut her palm. Blood runs down her wrist in thin red lines that match the paint on my own skin.

“You pulled back,” she says. Quiet. “Again.”

I look at her.

Inside there is almost nothing left that knows why pulling back matters. The red has taken the faces. The red has taken the names. Winterfell is a word without pictures. Robb is a sound without warmth. The chair is the only thing that still has weight.

Yet the horn piece in her hand still pulls at something.

I reach out.

She does not flinch.

I take the horn from her fingers and press it against the cracked tines of my crown until it catches. The piece hangs there now, ugly and small against the bone.

“The red waits,” I tell them both. “It waits to see if the line you drew will hold when the next army comes. When the queen sends her golden men. When the North hears and either rises or sends its own knives.”

Yrsa’s jaw tightens.

“Then we make sure the line holds,” she says. “Or we die on it.”

Eira nods once. The girl who was meat on the black sand is gone. What remains is something harder. Something that chose the monster over the green and is now choosing to leash it anyway.

A scout — one of the skinchangers wearing a dead Frey face — returns from the trees before the last light fades. He carries a message carved on a piece of green leather.

The lions are moving in force. Two thousand men under a banner the scout could not name but that smelled of roses and gold. The Crossing has closed its gates and is sending riders north to warn whatever is left of the Stark bannermen. The smallfolk are already telling stories: the dead boy king walks again, painted in blood, leading cannibals and unicorns and wearing the faces of the men he kills.

The red laughs inside my skull.

It does not need to speak the words anymore.

*They remember now.*

*They fear now.*

*Rise.*

I give the order without looking at the anchors.

“Burn the villages we pass. Wear the faces of the scouts they send. Let every green man who sees us carry the story back in his own skin. We do not hide. We do not wait for them to gather. We break them before they can name the fear.”

The army moves.

Yrsa walks at my left.

Eira walks at my right.

The horn piece rattles against the bone crown with every step.

Behind us the stacked dead watch the road with empty eyes.

The red is patient.

It has waited since the black sand.

It can wait a little longer.

But the chair is closer now.

And the green has finally remembered the name it tried to forget.

At first light the column moves inland again, toward the war the realm has decided it must fight.

The dead boy who is mostly red leads them.

The two women who still hold the last chain walk beside him.

The army follows because terror is the only loyalty left.

And somewhere ahead, the lions and the towers and whatever is left of the North are already sharpening their steel for the thing that crawled out of Skagos.

The rising does not care what they bring.

The red only cares that they come.
