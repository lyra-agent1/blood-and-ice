# Iteration 7: The Retaliation

The Stone-Eaters come for blood the next night.

They do not come like the hunters we took. They come like the island itself — quiet, many, and full of old hate. Torches in the dark. Unicorns screaming. The sound of bone horns that make the red in my blood answer before my mind does.

I am awake before the first shout.

The scar on my side burns. The monster inside stirs, tasting the coming violence like a dog smells meat.

Yrsa is already at the entrance of the longhouse, spear in hand. Eira — the girl we took — is with her. The two new men are nowhere to be seen. Cowards or wise, I do not know.

The Bone-King stands on the ridge above the camp, shouting orders in that grinding tongue. His men move, but they move like men who have lost too many winters. Slow. Afraid.

I walk past them.

The red wants to run ahead. It wants to meet the enemy alone, laughing, tearing until there is nothing left but red and bone.

I hold it.

Not because I am strong. Because I am learning what Darrow learned: the monster is only useful if you point it. If you let it run wild, it eats your own army first.

I find the high ground above the main path. The trees here are black and twisted. Perfect for an ambush that is not really an ambush.

When the first Stone-Eaters break through the mist, I am waiting.

There are more than we expected. Twenty. Thirty. The one in the skin cloak leads them — not the one we killed, but his brother, or his ghost. Same painted face. Same hate.

They see me standing there, small against the dark, the black wolf at my side.

The leader laughs.

The red laughs back inside my skull.

I step forward.

The first spear comes. I let it graze my arm. The pain is sharp and clean. The red surges, but I do not give it the body yet.

I point the broken spear at the leader.

“You want the girl? Come take her.”

He charges.

I meet him halfway.

The red takes me then.

Not all the way. Not the full Laughing Dead that Yrsa warned about. Just enough.

The world goes hot and bright. My laughter comes out wrong, but it is controlled. I am still here, watching from inside the storm.

I take the leader’s spear in my hands and snap it. The sound is loud. The man’s eyes go wide. I drive the broken end into his chest and twist. He dies choking on his own blood.

The others hesitate.

That is their mistake.

Shaggydog hits the flank. The men from our tribe, seeing me still standing, seeing the monster but not losing control, find their courage. They charge.

I do not lose myself.

I direct it.

When one of our own gets too close in the chaos, I pull the red back just enough to spare him. The laughter stops for a heartbeat. The man stares at me like he has seen a god choose mercy.

The Stone-Eaters break.

We take five more prisoners. We lose two of ours. The lame unicorn we took earlier is dead, its horn shattered.

When the red finally recedes, I am standing in the middle of the path, breathing hard, blood on my face that is not all mine.

The Bone-King comes down from the ridge.

He looks at the dead. At the prisoners. At me.

“You held the leash,” he says through Yrsa. His voice is rough. “Even when it wanted to run.”

I nod. My throat is raw from the laughter that was not quite mine.

“The island is testing you, little wolf. It gave you the red to break the tribes. But if you break too many, there will be nothing left to rule.”

I look at the prisoners. At Eira, who is helping bind the wounded even though she is one of the taken.

“I am not here to break the island,” I say. “I am here to make it mine. And then I will take it home.”

The Bone-King studies me for a long time.

Then he does something I did not expect.

He kneels.

Not fully. Just enough that his head is lower than mine.

The men around us go silent.

“The Last Horn yields,” he says. “Not because you are stronger. Because you are the first in many winters who did not let the red eat him whole.”

Yrsa translates, but I already understand.

The tribe is mine now.

Not all of it. Not yet. But the Bone-King has given me the first real chain to pull.

Inside, the red is quiet. Sated for now. But I can feel it smiling.

It knows this is only the beginning.

I have one tribe.

There are more.

And when I have them all, when the monster and I have learned to walk together instead of one riding the other, I will turn my eyes across the water.

To the place that killed my family.

To the throne that should have been my brothers’.

I am Rickon Stark.

I am rising.

And the red is rising with me.
