# Iteration 72: The Red Wears the Song on the Steps

The iron cuts.

Every day the red sits the blades go deeper into the hollow that was once a boy. Every day the holding teaches the same lesson the climb taught on Skagos and the return taught on the road and the chair taught when the last anchor broke: the walls do not stay down. They grow back in the mouths of the smallfolk and the hands of the green’s sons and the eyes of the painted men who were meant to be the red’s own. The daily wearing on the throne steps was meant to silence the song the city learned from the green sons sent into the alleys. Instead the wearing on the steps every day became the place where the city learned to sing the green sons’ names louder. The red made the wearing a ritual. The ritual made the song a spectacle. The spectacle made the city a new kind of ash that sings while it burns.

The lead Ash-Keeper comes forward with the first full report of the daily ritual performed in front of the forced crowd. His face is painted fresh red over the new scars the singing gave him on the steps. Behind him stand three green sons the Reach sent in the last wave. Their faces are also painted red but the paint is streaked with the salt of the city’s song and the blood of the ones they turned on before the knives found them. One of the green sons smiles at the red with the same empty eyes the worn faces wear when the leak has already taken the part that once belonged to the green. The other two do not smile. Their hands shake even with the paint on them.

“The city sang back during the wearing,” the Ash-Keeper says. His voice is steady because he has learned what happens to voices that are not steady on the steps. “We brought the whisperers and the rememberers and the singers from the alleys and the squares and the smallfolk who had been forced to watch the last wearing. We brought the green sons the Reach sent as the new worn faces and made them stand on the steps where the city could see. We wore the whisperers first so the city could watch the red take the ones who had sung the green sons’ names in the smoke. The city was quiet at first. Then one of the smallfolk near the front began to sing the name of a green son we had worn the day before. Others joined. The green sons we had sent into the alleys to wear the song heard the city singing their own names back at them on the steps. One of them drew his knife on the painted man next to him and tried to reach the crowd. The others who heard the song turned with him. We stacked the turned ones on the steps in front of the city so the red could see. But the singing did not stop. The smallfolk kept singing the names of the green sons who had been sent and the names of the ones we wore and the names the worn faces had carried back from the ash. Some of the painted men in the crowd began to mouth the words. The Ash-Keeper who was meant to choose the next batch for the alleys heard his own brother’s name in the song the city sang and turned his face away from the red. We had to wear him on the steps in front of the others so the rest would remember the price of hearing the song. The wearing on the steps every day is teaching the city to sing louder every day. The green sons sent into the city as new worn faces are learning the song faster every day. The ones we send north with the next wave will carry the song back into the ash unless the Ash-Keepers stack them before they reach the burned places. The land bites. The city sings. The green sons turn. The red sits and the holding requires more every day.”

The red tastes the words.

City sang the names on the steps during the wearing. Green sons turned on the steps when they heard their own names sung back by the city they were sent to silence. Painted men in the crowd mouthed the song. Ash-Keeper heard his brother’s name and turned his face. Had to wear the Ash-Keeper on the steps so the rest would remember. Wearing on the steps every day makes the city sing louder every day. Green sons sent as new worn faces learn the song faster every day. The ones sent north will carry the song into the ash. Land bites. City sings. Green sons turn. Red sits. Holding requires more every day.

It chews until there is only the will that does not stop when the song answers.

I rise. The iron cuts deeper as I stand. I wear the lead Ash-Keeper first on the steps so every painted man in the hall and every green son the Reach sent and every face the red has taken can see what happens when the city sings the names back during the wearing and the green sons turn on the steps and the Ash-Keepers hear their brothers in the song. The memories are full of the forced crowd and the smallfolk who sang and the green sons who drew knives on the steps and the painted man who mouthed the words and the brother’s name that made the Ash-Keeper look away. The red eats the fear the Ash-Keeper felt when the song reached his own men. It eats the moment the green sons turned in front of the city. It eats the smallfolk voices carrying the green sons’ names louder than the knives could silence. It eats the knowledge that the wearing on the steps every day is the thing that teaches the city to remember. When it is done the body lies on the stone. The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the answer.

I wear the green son who smiled with empty eyes next. His memories are the alleys and the song the city sang after the last wearing and the moment he heard the names on the steps and felt the part that still belonged to the green wake up and turn his knife on the painted man beside him. He had smiled at the red without the song in his mouth when he left the Reach. Now he smiles at the red with the knives and the knowledge that the city will keep singing every day and the green will keep sending sons every day and the wearing on the steps will keep teaching the song back every day and the green sons sent into the city as new worn faces will keep learning it and turning every day. The red eats the smile. It eats the song he heard on the steps. It eats the knife he turned. It eats the green fields he remembered while the city sang. When it is done the green son stands again, hollowed, still smiling at the red because there is nothing left in him that can do anything else.

“Make the wearing a public spectacle every day on the steps with the city forced to watch in greater numbers,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through the swords that make the chair. “Bring the next wave of green sons the Reach and the lions send and wear them openly on the steps first so the city can see the red take the ones it sent before they are sent into the alleys. Send the ones who survive the wearing into the alleys to wear the song the city sang during the spectacle. Send the next wave from the green three times as large as the last. Send half of every wave north with the Ash-Keepers to feed the ash. Send the other half into the city as the new worn faces to be worn on the steps first and then sent into the alleys to wear the song until the city forgets or turns. Begin sending Ash-Keeper columns into the green lands themselves. Let the green see the wearing on its own steps and in its own squares and in its own fields. Let the smallfolk in the Reach and the lion lands learn that the red does not stop at the city. The song the city sings on the steps will be worn in the green until the green has no sons left to send and no voice left that is not the red’s. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The city will learn that even its song on the steps every day will be worn until it forgets the way the snow forgot the boy. The green will learn that its sons will be worn on the steps every day and sent into the alleys to wear the song they helped create until they forget or turn every day. Send more. Wear the song openly. Send the green’s sons into the city as the new worn faces to wear the song on the steps first every day. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many smallfolk sing the names the red ate or how many green sons the singing turns on the steps or how loudly the city sings the green sons’ names during the wearing every day or how many Ash-Keepers hear their brothers in the song and have to be worn on the steps every day. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the green and leave only the stones that remember the red.”

The painted men who still smile at the red without the song in their mouths paint more red on their faces before they turn. The ones who have heard the singing on the steps and still carry the knives follow them to prepare the next tripled wave and the larger public wearing on the steps every day and the first columns that will carry the wearing into the green lands every day. They know the shape of the order now.

A man in Reach colors is brought forward. His hands are bound but he stands like someone who still believes the green can bargain if it offers enough grain and enough sons and enough gold and asks the red not to wear the song so openly on the steps every day and not to send the green’s sons into the city as new worn faces to be worn on the steps first every day and not to export the wearing into the green lands themselves every day. He was forced to watch the last spectacle. The salt of the city’s song is still on his face.

“The Reach sends more grain and more sons,” he says. “The lions send more gold. But the smallfolk in the green hear the stories of the wearing on the steps every day and the green sons turned and stacked in front of the city and the Ash-Keepers carrying the wearing south. They whisper that the red is eating the green to hold the boy’s ash and now wearing the city’s song of the eating on the steps every day and sending the green’s sons to wear the song in the alleys until they turn every day and now bringing the wearing into the green lands themselves. The lords offer the grain and the gold and the next tripled wave of sons but ask that the red not wear the song so openly on the steps every day and not send the green’s sons into the city as new worn faces to be worn on the steps first every day and not bring the wearing into the green. They say the green bends but the green has sons to spare only so long before the stories reach every hall that still thinks it can bargain with the chair.”

The red tastes the words.

Reach. Grain. Lions. Gold. Smallfolk hear the stories of the wearing on the steps every day and the green sons turned and stacked in front of the city and the Ash-Keepers carrying the wearing south. Whisper that the red is eating the green and wearing the city’s song on the steps every day and sending the green’s sons to wear the song until they turn every day and now bringing the wearing into the green lands. Offer grain and gold and the next tripled wave but ask that the red not wear the song openly on the steps every day and not send the green’s sons into the city as new worn faces to be worn on the steps first every day and not bring the wearing into the green. Green bends but has sons to spare only so long before the stories reach every hall that still thinks it can bargain with the chair.

It chews them until nothing remains but the holding.

I motion the green man forward. He still stands like a lord who believes words can bargain with the chair and that the red will keep the cost of the daily wearing on the steps and the green sons turned and stacked in front of the city and the Ash-Keepers carrying the wearing south a secret from the green that feeds it. I wear him on the steps of the Iron Throne so every man in the hall and every painted man preparing the next tripled wave and the larger public wearing every day and the first columns into the green lands and every face the red has taken can see what asking the red to spare the song on the steps and the green’s sons from being worn on the steps first and the green lands from the wearing tastes like.

The memories are full of green fields and sons sent to die for a queen who is now ash and a chair the roses once thought they could claim and the hope that the thing on the chair might still bargain if the green feeds it enough and sends enough sons and does not ask too many questions about the wearing on the steps every day and the green sons turned in front of the city every day and the Ash-Keepers carrying the wearing south every day. He tastes the fear of the smallfolk who burn their own homes rather than feed the red. He tastes the stories of the wearing on the steps every day and the green sons stacked where the city could see and the Ash-Keepers bringing the wearing into the green. He tastes the hope that the green might still be spared the full price if it offers the next tripled wave and the grain and the gold and asks nicely and the song on the steps might still be left alone and the green’s sons might still be sent into the alleys without being worn publicly first and the wearing might still stop at the city. The red eats the fields. It eats the sons. It eats the hope. It eats the mercy and the secrets and the green’s claim on its own sons and the city’s song until there is only the iron and the will that sits and the knowledge that the green will learn the shape of the holding the same way the snow did — by feeding the red and sending its sons to be worn on the steps every day and stacked in front of the city every day and sent into the alleys to wear the song they helped create every day and watching the red bring the wearing into the green lands themselves every day until only the chair remains.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it.

“Send the grain and the gold and the next tripled wave of the green’s sons from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red,” I say. “Send half the green’s sons north into the ash with the Ash-Keepers. Send the other half into the city as the new worn faces to be worn openly on the steps first so the city can see the red take the ones it sent before they are sent into the alleys to wear the song until the city forgets or turns. Send the first Ash-Keeper columns into the green lands with the wearing. Let the green see the red wear its sons on steps that are not the chair’s. Let the smallfolk in the green lands learn that the red does not stop at the city. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The green will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not keep secrets about the cost of the holding and does not spare the green’s sons from being worn on the steps every day and does not spare the city when the song reaches the green sons sent into the alleys and does not spare the green lands when the Ash-Keepers carry the wearing south. Send more. Wear the song on the steps every day until the city has no voice left that is not the red’s. Send the green’s sons into the city as the new worn faces to be worn on the steps first every day and then into the alleys to wear the song every day until they forget or turn. Send the wearing into the green until the green has no sons left to send and no voice left that is not the red’s. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many smallfolk sing the names the red ate during the wearing on the steps every day or how many green sons the singing turns on the steps every day or how loudly the city sings the green sons’ names while the red wears the song every day or how many Ash-Keepers hear their brothers in the song and have to be worn on the steps every day or how many green lands the Ash-Keepers must carry the wearing into before the green learns. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the green and leave only the stones that remember the red.”

The painted men kneel.

Not because they remember a boy who once had a name and a sister who ran through snow and old gods that spoke in the trees.

Because the red is the only thing left that knows how to sit on the chair and make the realm — even the city that sings the cost on the steps every day and the green that bends and feeds and still tries to ask that its sons not be worn publicly on the steps every day and not be sent into the alleys as new worn faces to wear the song they helped create every day and not have the wearing brought into its own lands every day — learn the price of every wall it ever tried to raise against the climb that never ends.

The city outside the Red Keep sings in the places the smallfolk learned the cost of silence during the last spectacle.

The Ash-Keepers prepare the next tripled wave and the larger public wearing on the steps every day and the first columns that will carry the wearing into the green lands every day.

The worn faces who still smile at the red without the song in their eyes move into the alleys to find the ones who whispered the names during the spectacle and wear them on the steps every day so the city can see the red wear the song the city sang while the red wore the green’s sons.

The green sons the Reach and the lions send are divided — half sent north into the ash with the Ash-Keepers, half sent into the city as the new worn faces to be worn openly on the steps first every day so the city can see and then sent into the alleys to wear the song the city sang during the wearing until the city forgets or the song reaches them and they turn every day.

The columns that will carry the wearing south rest and prepare for the green lands that still think they can bargain with the chair.

The iron cuts.

And the red sits on the throne, tasting the loyal knives that keep the ash walking and the faces that must be constantly replaced and the places where the land still sings names the red already ate and the fire that makes the singing louder and the green sons who will learn to turn on the steps every day and the city that will learn to sing the names during the wearing every day and the Ash-Keepers who will learn to carry the wearing into the green lands themselves every day and the green that will learn to send three times as many sons every day so the red can wear the song on the steps every day and use the green’s sons to wear the song in the alleys every day until they forget or turn every day.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

And the red.

And the holding that never ends.
