# Iteration 22: The Anchor Breaks

The column meets the green’s answer on a field that used to be a lord’s pasture.

They come with fire and steel and the banners of two houses that have never loved each other — the lion and the towers of the Crossing. Three hundred men. Knights in plate. Crossbowmen who have never seen painted cannibals before. A lord in gilded armor at the front, the same sigil the scout wore, but this one has come to end the rumor himself.

My army is smaller. Hungrier. Already half legend and half monster in the eyes of the men who still follow because fear is the only leash left.

I stand at the front with the cracked crown of bone on my head and Shaggydog a black shadow that no longer has a name I remember.

Yrsa is on my left.

Eira is on my right.

They are the last two people on this side of the water who look at me and see anything but the red.

The lord raises his voice across the field.

“By order of the King on the Iron Throne and the Lord of the Crossing, lay down your arms. The boy who calls himself Stark is a lie. The dead stay dead. You are cannibals and wildlings and you will be put down like the dogs you are.”

The red laughs.

I let it have the body.

The world goes scarlet before the first arrow flies.

I am inside the lord before his horse can take three steps. His skin is warm and clean and full of the certainty that the world still belongs to men like him. I make him draw his sword and open his own throat in front of his army. The sound the red makes through his mouth is the sound the island makes when it wakes.

The green breaks.

My painted men pour forward. The Last Skin’s skinchangers wear the dead before they hit the ground. Unicorns scream and trample. The black spears do the work the red does not need to touch.

I step out of the lord’s body and into the battle.

I wear three more men before the red lets me go. Each time I see what they saw — the story of the dead boy who became a cannibal king, the fear that the North might still rise for a Stark even a broken one, the order to burn the rumor out before it reaches the snow.

The red takes each memory as I leave the skin.

When the field is quiet, the green is dead or running.

We stack the bodies the Skagosi way. The lord’s banner is nailed to the highest skull. The message is clear.

I stand in the middle of the dead with blood that is not all mine on my face and the crown crooked on my head.

Yrsa is still there.

Eira is not.

She stands ten paces away with the piece of the killing horn in her fist. The one she has carried since the path on Skagos. The one she pressed into my palm every night when the red wanted more.

Her eyes are clear.

“You are not him,” she says. Voice steady. The voice of the girl who did not run when the red wanted her throat. “The boy who spared me is gone. The red has eaten him. The green was right. The dead should have stayed dead.”

She raises the horn piece.

Not like a weapon.

Like a promise.

“I will not let you wear the North the way you wore the lord,” she says. “I will not let the red have the chair that broke your house. Not like this.”

Yrsa steps between us.

Her spear is not pointed at Eira.

It is pointed at the space where the red lives behind my eyes.

“Choose,” Yrsa says. The same word she has said since the first blood on the black sand. “Choose now, or the red wins and there is nothing left worth rising for.”

The red surges.

It wants the girl who was supposed to be meat. It wants the woman who held the leash for too long. It wants the body that has forgotten every name except the rising.

I feel my hands move.

I feel the laughter that is not mine bubble up.

For one heartbeat I am inside Eira.

I see what she saw the night we took her — the boy with the broken spear who chose instead of killing. I see every night she pressed the horn into my palm. I see the hunter’s death and the march and the moment she decided the monster had to die before it reached the chair.

The red wants to keep her skin.

I pull back.

Not all the way.

Enough.

The laughter stops.

I look at Eira.

I look at Yrsa.

Inside, there is nothing left that remembers why the chair matters more than the red.

Only the rage.

Only the rising.

I take the piece of horn from her hand.

The red pulls back.

Not because it is leashed.

Because the two anchors have finally drawn the line the island itself could not draw.

Yrsa lowers her spear.

Eira does not step back.

The army watches their king stand between the last two people who still believe there is a boy inside the monster.

I turn toward the road.

The lions and the Crossing are coming in force now.

The North will hear the story of the cannibal king who wore his own men and spared the girl twice.

The chair is still there.

The rising is still there.

And for the first time since the black sand, the red is not smiling.

It is waiting to see if the last two anchors will hold when the real war begins.

At first light we march.

The green has sent its army.

The dead boy who forgot his name is coming anyway.
