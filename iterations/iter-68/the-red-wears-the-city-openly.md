# Iteration 68: The Red Wears the City Openly

The iron cuts.

I sit on the chair made of swords and the red tastes the blood that has become the throne. Every day the blades dig deeper into what remains. Every day the hollow learns that the climb did not end when the last godswood burned or the last voice that remembered the boy went ash on the wind. The holding is the climb that never stops. The red sits and the realm pays in faces and loyal knives and the sons the green sends to walk the ash and the city that must be worn until it forgets the cost of the sons it sent — until the wearing itself teaches the city to remember louder.

The Ash-Keepers and the worn faces return from the North and the city with the answer.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The boy is gone. The name is gone. The snow and the girl and the brother and the father and the old gods and every voice that once tried to pull the red back are ash in the red’s mouth. What remains is the will that climbed from Skagos to the city and the red that fed it and the chair that now holds both and the hunger that has learned to sit and burn the places that sing and stack what the singing wakes and replace the North with the green’s sons who have not yet heard the wind — until they hear it and the knives find them too and the memories they brought from the green leak back through the worn faces and the wearing of the city teaches the alleys and the walls to sing the green sons’ names louder than before.

The lead Ash-Keeper steps forward with a worn face whose eyes are empty in the way that means the leak has been tasted and the wearing has made it stronger. His face is painted fresh red over scars the fire and the snow and the greater city purge gave him.

“The wearing wakes the singing in the city,” he says. His voice is steady because he has learned the shape of the holding and the price of the knives he carries and the sons he spends and the whispers he now wears on the walls and the memory he now wears in the squares. “We sent the doubled wave of green sons into the burned places with the fire and the salt. The Ash-Keepers chose which faces walk and which are stacked when the singing reached them. Whole bands turned before the first week. The selectors who heard the green sons’ mothers through the ones they chose had to be stacked by the loyal. The worn faces we sent into every alley and every wall found the ones who whispered the green sons’ stories and the ones who remembered and the ones who asked why the red spends the green’s youth in the boy’s ash. We wore them on the walls so the city could see. We wore them in the squares so the smallfolk could see. The city saw the green sons’ names in the mouths of the worn faces. The city learned the shape of the holding. But the wearing made the stories louder. The smallfolk who saw the wearing began to whisper the green sons’ names in the smoke. The painted men who were meant to help the wearing began to ask the question louder. Some of the worn faces who carried the leak back from the ash and were sent to wear the city remembered the green names even after the knives. The singing spreads through the sons the green sends and leaks back through the faces the red takes and the wearing teaches the city to sing the green sons’ names in the alleys where the red wore the whisperers. The land bites harder when the red feeds it the green’s youth and the city sees the cost and the wearing makes the cost a song. The ash still moves. The singing is in the city’s mouth louder now. The cost of holding the ash is the faces that turn and the loyal ones who must stack their own and the fire that makes the land sing louder and the singing that turns the green’s sons into the stackers and the stackers into the stacked and the leak that turns the city into another ash that must be worn until it forgets and the wearing that teaches the city to remember the green sons louder than the knives can silence.”

The red tastes the words.

Wearing wakes the singing in the city. Doubled wave sent. Whole bands stacked. Selectors stacked. Worn faces wore the whisperers and the rememberers and the questioners on the walls and in the squares. City saw the green sons’ names. City learned. But wearing made the stories louder. Smallfolk who saw the wearing whisper the names in the smoke. Painted men ask the question louder. Worn faces who carried the leak remembered green names after the knives. Singing spreads through sons and leaks through faces and the wearing teaches the city to sing the green sons’ names in the alleys where the red wore the whisperers. Land bites harder. Ash still moves. Singing in city’s mouth louder. Cost is faces that turn and loyal who stack and fire that makes land sing and singing that turns sons and leak that turns city into another ash and wearing that teaches city to remember the green sons louder than the knives can silence.

It chews them until nothing remains but the will to hold.

I rise from the chair. The iron cuts deeper as I stand. I wear the lead Ash-Keeper first so every man in the hall can see what the singing ash and the leaking city and the wearing that wakes the song taste like when the red feeds one and wears the other and the wearing makes the song louder.

The memories are cold and full of the ash the boy once loved and the city that still tries to remember louder than the knives. Pine and wind and the burned places and the alleys near the Red Keep thick with smoke and the smallfolk who whispered of green fields while the worn faces walked among them wearing the whisperers and the painted men on the walls who asked the question louder after seeing the wearing and the worn faces who carried the green sons’ mothers’ names in their mouths even after the knives found the ones they were sent to silence. The green sons’ voices in the smoke, the mothers’ names, the sisters left behind, the fields that would never see them again, now sung in the alleys where the red wore the whisperers so the city could see. The turned sons’ prayers and the selectors’ last thoughts and the worn faces’ memories of carrying the leak and wearing the city and the city’s eyes watching the wearing and singing the names louder after. The red eats the prayers. It eats the roots. It eats the stones. It eats the girl’s laugh one more time. It eats the singing itself. It eats the green sons who turned. It eats the selectors who heard through the sons. It eats the worn faces who carried the leak and the wearing. It eats the smallfolk whispers. It eats the painted men’s questions. It eats the city’s memory of the cost. It eats the fire that woke the song and the song that woke the knives and the leak that woke the city and the wearing that tried to silence the leak and the wearing that made the leak a song until there is only ash and the will that sits and the knowledge that the land and the city fight back by making the red’s own wearing into the singer and the green’s sons into the fuel that teaches the ash and the city to bite harder and remember louder.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the ash’s cost and the loyal knives that pay it and the green sons that make the cost higher and the leak that makes the city another wall and the wearing that makes the wall sing.

I wear the worn face who carried the leak and was sent to wear the city and brought the song back next. His memories are full of the smoke and the knives and the green sons’ fields and the mothers’ voices and the sisters’ names and the alleys where the smallfolk listened and the walls where the painted men asked and the moment the knives found the whisperers and the green names still slipped from his mouth and the city’s eyes that saw the wearing and began to sing the names in the smoke louder than before and the painted men who saw the wearing and asked the question louder. He had smiled at the red without the snow in his eyes when he left the city the first time. Now he smiles at the red with the knives and the knowledge that the land will keep singing and the green will keep sending sons and the Ash-Keepers will keep choosing and the singing will keep turning the chosen into the stacked and the leak will keep teaching the city to remember and the wearing will keep teaching the city to sing the names louder and that is the shape of the holding that never ends.

The red eats the smoke and the knives and the stacked green sons and the selectors and the worn faces who carried the leak and the wearing and the smallfolk whispers and the painted men’s questions and the city’s memory and the singing that the fire woke and the singing that turned the replacement into the replaced and the leak that woke the city and the wearing that tried to silence it and the wearing that made it a song.

When it is done the worn face stands again, hollowed but still smiling at the red.

“Wear the city openly on the throne steps and send the green’s sons to replace the city’s memory,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through bone. “Pull the worn faces who have heard the green names back to the throne or stack them where they stand. Send the worn faces who still smile at the red without the green in their eyes into every alley and every wall and every square to find the ones who whisper and the ones who remember and the ones who ask and wear them on the throne steps so the city can see the red wear the city’s memory of the green sons until the city forgets. Send the next wave of green sons from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red — twice as many as the last. Let the green feed the red with its bodies and its knives and its sons and its sons’ sons if the singing takes them and the leak teaches the city and the wearing teaches the city to sing and the wearing on the throne steps teaches the city to forget. Give the loyal Ash-Keepers who have not yet turned the authority to choose which of the green’s sons walk the ash and which are stacked when the singing reaches them and which of the selectors must be stacked when the singing reaches through the sons they chose and which worn faces must be stacked when the leak reaches the city and which of the city’s whispers and memories must be worn on the throne steps until the city forgets the green sons the way the snow forgot the boy. Send more fire. Send more salt. Send more faces from the chair. Let the green see that the red does not stop when the singing leaks back through the faces it takes and the city tries to remember and the wearing makes the city sing. Let the ash see that the red will replace the replaced with the green’s youth and replace the city’s memory with the green’s sons sent to the capital as new tools and the wearing on the throne steps until the singing has no one left to turn or the green has no sons left to send or the city has no one left to remember what the red has eaten. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The land will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not fear the singing its own fire wakes and does not spare the green’s sons when the singing must be fed and does not spare the city when the leak teaches it to remember and the wearing makes it sing. Send more. Replace what turns. Stack what remembers. Burn what sings. Replace the replaced with the green’s sons. Stack the leak in the city. Wear the city openly on the throne steps until it forgets the green sons the way the snow forgot the boy. The Ash-Keepers who stack their own brothers and burn the places that bite and choose which green faces walk the ash and which selectors fall and which worn faces carry the leak and which city whispers and memories are worn on the throne steps will be given more faces to command and more fire to carry and more of the green’s sons to spend and the city’s memory to silence on the steps. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many wolves once ran in the snow or how much ash still moves when the wind comes from the north or how many godswoods still try to sing names the red already ate or how loudly the land sings when the red burns it or how many loyal knives the singing turns back on their brothers or how many green sons the singing will turn into stackers or how many selectors the singing will turn into the stacked or how many worn faces the leak will teach to remember green names or how many city whispers the wearing will silence or how loudly the city sings the green sons’ names after the red wears the memory on the throne steps. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the snow and leave only the stones that remember the red and the ash that learned to be silent and the green that learned to send its sons to feed the chair with their bodies and their knives and their memories of home and the city that learned to be silent when the red wears its whispers and its memory on the throne steps until it forgets.”

The Ash-Keepers who still smile at the red without the snow in their eyes paint more red on their faces before they turn. The ones who have heard the singing and still carry the knives and the authority to choose and the worn faces who still smile without the green in their eyes follow them to prepare the next replacement wave and the city purge brought to the throne steps and the new faces from the green and the fire and the salt. They know the shape of the order now.

Another painted man steps forward. This one smells of the west and the gold the lions continue to send. He brings a green man in fine clothes whose hands shake even though he tries to stand like a lord.

“The Reach sends more grain,” he says. “The lions send more gold. But the envoy from the roses says the smallfolk in the green lands hear the stories of the Ash-Keepers stacking the green’s sons in the snow and the worn faces leaking the stories back to the city and the red wearing the city’s whispers and memory on the walls and now on the throne steps and they whisper that the red is eating the green to hold the boy’s ash and now eating the city’s memory of the eating on the steps where the red sits. The envoy offers the grain and the gold and the next wave of sons with red on their faces but asks that the red not spend so many of the green’s sons in the singing places and not wear the city’s memory so openly on the throne steps. He says the green bends but the green has sons to spare only so long and the city has memory only so long before it breaks and the stories reach every hall that still thinks it can bargain with the chair.”

The red tastes the words.

Reach. Grain. Lions. Gold. Envoy from the roses. Smallfolk hear the stories of the Ash-Keepers stacking the green’s sons and the worn faces leaking and the red wearing the city’s whispers and memory on the walls and now on the throne steps. Whisper that the red is eating the green to hold the boy’s ash and now eating the city’s memory of the eating on the steps where the red sits. Says the green bends but the green has sons to spare only so long and the city has memory only so long before it breaks and the stories reach every hall that still thinks it can bargain with the chair.

He still stands like a lord who believes words can bargain with the chair and that the red will keep the cost of the ash and the singing and the replacement and the leak and the wearing and the wearing on the throne steps a secret from the green that feeds it and that the green’s sons will only be spent on the green’s wars and not the boy’s ash and that the city’s memory can be worn quietly on the walls and not on the steps where the red sits.

I wear him on the steps of the Iron Throne so every man in the hall and every Ash-Keeper preparing the next wave and the city purge brought to the steps and every face the red has taken can see what asking the red to spare the green’s sons and the city’s memory on the throne steps tastes like.

The memories are soft and full of green fields and grain and sons sent to die for a queen who is now ash and a chair that the roses once thought they could claim and the hope that the thing on the chair might still bargain if the green feeds it enough and sends enough sons and does not ask too many questions about the snow and the singing and the replacement and the leak and the wearing on the walls and the wearing on the steps. He tastes the fear of the smallfolk who burn their own homes rather than feed the red. The red eats the fields. It eats the sons. It eats the hope. It eats the mercy and the secrets and the green’s claim on its own sons and the city’s memory until there is only the iron and the will that sits and the knowledge that the green will learn the shape of the holding the same way the snow did — by feeding the red and sending its sons to walk the ash and stack what turns and burn what sings and watch the red wear the city’s whispers and the city’s memory on the walls and on the throne steps until only the chair remains and the green sons who have learned to smile at the red without the snow in their eyes or the singing in their mouths or the leak in their stories or the memory of the cost.
