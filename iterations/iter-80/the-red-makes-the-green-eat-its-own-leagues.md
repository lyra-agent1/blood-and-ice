# Iteration 80: The Red Makes the Green Eat Its Own Leagues

The iron cuts.

The chair remembers every league that thought it could band what the red had already claimed. Every ring of fields the green burned together. Every hall that closed its gates with brothers and sang the names inside as if the song could build a wall the wearing could not climb. Every son the green kept inside to turn on the painted men rather than feed the chair. The red has tasted the leagues and found them wanting. The green will learn that the only leagues that survive are the leagues the red forces the green to make against itself — leagues of green lords leading green columns to wear green brothers on green steps, leagues of green smallfolk forced to sing with red paint while green sons are stacked where green mothers watch, leagues of green that eat the green until only the red remains in every voice that once called itself free.

The lead Ash-Keeper from the twelve-times column returns with burns that smell of seven seats’ fields set to torch in one great ring and a green lord whose hands still shake from wearing his own league brother on the steps of the hall they had closed together. Behind him stand forty green sons from the Great League of the Reach — the seven seats that had banded, burned every field in a single circle of fire, kept every son inside the joined halls, and sung the names openly in the squares and on the burning fields and inside the halls as the column approached. Their paint is fresh but the song leaks from their eyes and the cracks in their voices. The city smallfolk stand in the square before the throne steps with red paint forced so thick it cracks when they open their mouths to sing.

“The green now makes Great Leagues,” the Ash-Keeper says. His voice is the flat voice of a man who has watched green lords forced to lead green columns against their own banded brothers and then wear the league lords on the very steps they had stood together to refuse. “Seven seats in the Reach closed their gates as one, burned their fields in a single ring that could be seen from the hills, and sent no sons and no grain and no gold.
