# Iteration 8: The First Throne

The morning after the Stone-Eaters break is quiet in the way a battlefield is quiet — the kind of quiet that waits for the next scream.

I stand on the ridge where the Bone-King knelt. Not a full kneel. Enough. His men saw it. The red saw it. That is what matters.

The camp below moves like a body that has been cut open and is trying to decide whether to heal or rot. Hunters drag the dead to the pyres. Women with ash on their faces wail the names of the fallen in that grinding tongue that sounds like bones breaking. The prisoners we took — the five Stone-Eaters and the girl Eira — sit bound near the longhouse. They watch me the way prey watches the thing that spared it.

I feel the scar on my ribs pull when I breathe. The red is still there, curled under the skin like a second heart. It is pleased with last night. It tasted control and liked the flavor.

Yrsa climbs the ridge. She carries two spears — one for me, one for herself. The broken one I took from the first raid is gone. This new one is black wood, the head made from a unicorn’s horn filed to a killing point. She does not speak at first. She stands beside me and looks at the camp.

“The Bone-King has not risen,” she says finally. “The men say he waits for you to come down. Some say you should go to him. Others say you should make him come to you.”

I do not answer. I am listening to the red. It wants me to go down and take the old man’s head while the fear is still fresh. It wants the tribe to see what happens when a king yields and then forgets who holds the leash.

I do not give it the body.

Instead I walk down.

The men part for me. Shaggydog walks at my heel, black and silent, yellow eyes promising death to anyone who steps wrong. The Bone-King’s hall smells of old smoke and newer blood. He sits on a throne made of stacked skulls and whale ribs, wrapped in furs that still smell of the animals they came from. His good eye is open. The other is a white ruin.

He watches me approach.

Varl stands beside him. The Bone-King’s blood. Taller than the old man, broad in the shoulders, a skinchanger’s tattoos crawling up his neck like black veins. He has the look of a man who has already decided how this ends.

“You held the red last night,” the Bone-King says through Yrsa. His voice is like gravel under boots. “My men saw. The Stone-Eaters saw. Even the island saw.”

Varl spits on the packed earth. “The red does not stay leashed. It ate his brothers’ house. It will eat this one too.”

The words land. Some of the hunters shift. I feel the red lift its head at the mention of Winterfell, at the memory of the boy who screamed when the world burned.

I step closer to the throne. Not close enough for Varl to reach me without crossing the space where Shaggydog waits.

“I did not come here to be eaten,” I say. The words are for the tribe as much as for the old man. “I came here because the world beyond the water took everything from me. My father. My brothers. My name. They left me for dead on this rock because they thought the island would finish the work. It did not finish me. It woke something.”

I let the red rise. Just a little. Enough that my voice changes, enough that the laughter that is not quite mine threads underneath the words.

Varl’s hand goes to the knife at his belt.

I smile with teeth that feel too many.

“The red is not a curse you suffer,” I say. “It is a fire you point. Last night I pointed it at the men who came to take what was ours. Today I point it at the ones who still think they can hide behind old bones while the world moves on.”

The Bone-King studies me. Then he does something that makes the red go very still.

He rises from the throne.

Not all the way. Enough to stand on the same level as me. He takes the black-wood spear from Yrsa and holds it out, butt-first, toward me.

“The Last Horn yields the first chain,” he says. “Not because you are the strongest. Because you are the first in many winters who made the red kneel instead of the other way around.”

I take the spear.

The tribe goes silent.

Varl does not.

He moves fast for a big man. The knife flashes toward my throat.

The red is faster.

I do not give it everything. I give it the arm and the eyes and the part of the mind that knows how to end a threat in one motion. My hand catches his wrist. Bones crack. I drive the new spear up under his ribs in the same heartbeat, twisting so the unicorn horn finds the heart. The laughter comes out of me like it did on the path — wrong, many-voiced, delighted.

Varl dies staring into eyes that are not a boy’s.

I pull the spear free before the red can take more. The world comes back sharp and cold. Blood on my face. Blood on the throne. The tribe staring like they have seen the old stories walk.

I plant the spear in the earth between my feet and look at the Bone-King.

“You gave me the chain,” I say. “I will not let it rust.”

He nods once. Then he kneels properly this time. Head lower than mine.

One by one, the hunters follow. Not all. Enough.

Yrsa does not kneel. She stands at my shoulder. Eira, the girl we took, watches from the prisoners with something like awe and terror mixed.

When it is done, the Bone-King rises. He gestures to the hall, to the island beyond the black trees.

“The tribes will not all yield so easily,” he says. “The Stone-Eaters will come again. The Hornless Ones in the high cliffs keep the old magics. They will smell the red on you and come to burn it out.”

I look at the spear in my hand. At the body cooling on the floor. At the men who now call me something in their tongue that Yrsa translates as “Red-Hand” or “Wolf-Who-Laughs.”

“Then we will be ready,” I say. “In three days we move on the next. We do not hide. We do not wait for them to come to us. We rise.”

Inside, the red is quiet again. Sated. But I can feel it smiling in the dark behind my eyes.

It knows this is only the first throne.

There will be more.

When I have them all — when the monster and I have learned to walk side by side instead of one riding the other — I will turn my eyes across the water.

To the place that killed my family.

To the throne that should have been my brothers’.

I am Rickon Stark.

I am rising.

And the red is rising with me.
