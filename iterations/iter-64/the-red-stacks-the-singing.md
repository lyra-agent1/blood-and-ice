# Iteration 64: The Red Stacks the Singing

The iron cuts.

I sit on the chair made of swords and the red tastes the blood that has become the throne. Every day the blades dig deeper into what remains. Every day the hollow learns that the climb did not end when the last godswood burned or the last voice that remembered the boy went ash on the wind. The holding is the climb that never stops. The red sits and the realm pays in faces and loyal knives and the land that learns to sing louder every time the red tries to silence it with fire.

The Ash-Keepers return from the greater cleansing with the answer.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The boy is gone. The name is gone. The snow and the girl and the brother and the father and the old gods and every voice that once tried to pull the red back are ash in the red’s mouth. What remains is the will that climbed from Skagos to the city and the red that fed it and the chair that now holds both and the hunger that has learned to sit and burn the places that sing and stack what the singing wakes and replace the North with the green’s sons who have not yet heard the wind.

The lead Ash-Keeper steps forward. His face is painted fresh red over scars the fire and the snow and the greater singing gave him. His eyes are wrong in the way that means he has tasted the cost and still smiles at the red. Behind him the survivors of the cleansing columns stand with fresh blood on their knives and smoke in their cloaks.

“The singing spread with the fire,” he says. His voice is steady because he has learned the shape of the holding and the price of the knives he carries and the fire he now commands. “We burned every place the boy once touched. Every half-roof holdfast. Every old watch stone. Every tree that ever stood where the old gods spoke. We salted the ground. We stacked the ones that cracked when the wind carried the names. But the fire woke more. The singing reached the loyal ones who carried the fire. Some of the Ash-Keepers who left the city smiling at the red without the snow in their eyes heard the names in the smoke and their knives turned on their own brothers before we could stop them. We had to stack bands of our own in the ash we were burning. The worn faces we brought to replace the turned ones heard the singing louder than the ones they replaced. The land bites harder when the red burns it. The ash still moves. The singing is in more places now than before we came with fire. The cost of holding the ash is the faces that turn and the loyal ones who must stack their own and the fire that makes the land sing louder and the singing that turns the loyal ones the red sent to silence it.”

The red tastes the words.

Singing spread with the fire. Every place the boy touched burned. Stones salted. Ones that cracked stacked. Fire woke more. Singing reached the loyal Ash-Keepers. Some who left smiling heard the names in the smoke and turned their knives on their own brothers. Stacked bands of our own in the ash we were burning. Worn faces brought to replace heard the singing louder. Land bites harder when the red burns it. Ash still moves. Singing in more places now than before the fire. Cost of holding the ash is the faces that turn and the loyal ones who must stack their own and the fire that makes the land sing louder and the singing that turns the loyal ones the red sent to silence it.

It chews them until nothing remains but the will to hold.

I rise from the chair. The iron cuts deeper as I stand. I wear the lead Ash-Keeper first so every man in the hall can see what the singing ash tastes like when the red’s own fire wakes it and turns the loyal knives back on themselves.

The memories are cold and full of the ash the boy once loved. Pine and wind and the burned places. But under it the singing is a chorus now — roots that remember the trees that stood before the red came, stones that remember the boy who once ran through snow with a wolf at his side, a girl’s laugh that the red already ate but the land refuses to forget, the old gods that the red broke but that still move in the ash when the fire touches them, and now the voices of the loyal Ash-Keepers who carried the fire and heard the names and turned their knives on brothers they had stacked beside in the smoke. The turned men’s last clear thoughts are prayers to the old gods that the thing on the chair might still have a body the snow could claim if enough loyal knives heard the singing at once. The red eats the prayers. It eats the roots. It eats the stones. It eats the girl’s laugh one more time. It eats the singing itself. It eats the loyal knives that turned on their own. It eats the fire that woke the song until there is only ash and the will that sits and the knowledge that the land fights back by making the red’s own tools into the singers and the stackers into the stacked.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the ash’s cost and the loyal knives that pay it and the fire that makes the cost higher and the singing that turns the knives.

I wear one of the hollow-eyed stackers who survived the greater cleansing next. His memories are full of the smoke and the knives and the faces that smiled wrong in the firelight and the bodies of Ash-Keepers stacked where the godswood had been and the new places the singing had reached and the moment his own knife found a brother who had heard the names too clearly. He had smiled at the red without the snow in his eyes when he left the city. Now he smiles at the red with the knives and the knowledge that the land will keep singing and the loyal ones will keep turning and the red will keep burning and the stackers will keep stacking their own and that is the shape of the holding that never ends.

The red eats the smoke and the knives and the stacked loyal and the singing that the fire woke and the singing that turned the knives.

When it is done the stacker stands again, hollowed but still smiling at the red.

“Stack the singing and replace the North,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through bone. “Pull the worn faces and the painted men who have heard the wind back to the city or stack them where they stand. Flood the burned places with new men from the green — the ones the lions and the Reach have sent with red on their faces who have not yet tasted the snow or heard the singing. Give the loyal Ash-Keepers who have not yet turned the authority to choose which of the green’s sons walk the ash and which are stacked when the singing reaches them. Send more fire. Send more salt. Send more faces from the chair. Let the worn ones we left behind see that the red does not stop when its own knives turn. Let the ash see that the red will replace the North itself if the singing will not be silent. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The land will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not fear the singing its own fire wakes. Send more. Replace what turns. Stack what remembers. Burn what sings. Replace the North with the green’s sons who have not yet heard. The Ash-Keepers who stack their own brothers and burn the places that bite and choose which green faces walk the ash will be given more faces to command and more fire to carry and the green’s sons to spend. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many wolves once ran in the snow or how much ash still moves when the wind comes from the north or how many godswoods still try to sing names the red already ate or how loudly the land sings when the red burns it or how many loyal knives the singing turns back on their brothers. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the snow and leave only the stones that remember the red and the ash that learned to be silent and the green sons who feed the chair with their bodies and their knives.”

The Ash-Keepers who still smile at the red without the snow in their eyes paint more red on their faces before they turn. The ones who have heard the singing and still carry the knives follow them to prepare the replacement wave and the new faces from the green and the fire and the salt. They know the shape of the order now.

Another painted man steps forward. This one smells of the west and the gold the lions continue to send. He brings two green men in fine clothes whose hands shake even though they try to stand like lords.

“The Reach sends more grain,” he says. “The lions send more gold. But the envoys from the roses and the lions say the smallfolk in the green lands hear the stories of the Ash-Keepers burning the North and stacking their own brothers and now replacing the worn faces with the green’s own sons and they whisper that the red will one day ask the green to send its sons to die in the ash and stack their own brothers in the snow. The envoys offer the grain and the gold and the men with red on their faces but ask that the red not spend the green’s sons in the singing places.”

The red tastes the words.

Reach. Grain. Lions. Gold. Envoys from the roses and the lions. Smallfolk hear the stories of the Ash-Keepers burning and stacking and now replacing with green sons. Whisper that the red will one day ask the green to send its sons to die in the ash and stack their own brothers in the snow. Offer the grain and the gold and the men with red on their faces but ask that the red not spend the green’s sons in the singing places.

It chews them until nothing remains but the holding.

I motion the green men forward. They still stand like lords who believe words can bargain with the chair and that the red will keep the cost of the ash and the singing and the replacement a secret from the green that feeds it and that the green’s sons will only be spent on the green’s wars.

I wear them on the steps of the Iron Throne so every man in the hall and every Ash-Keeper preparing the replacement wave and every face the red has taken can see what asking the red to spare the green’s sons tastes like.

The memories are soft and full of green fields and grain and sons sent to die for a queen who is now ash and a chair that the roses and the lions once thought they could claim and the hope that the thing on the chair might still bargain if the green feeds it enough and does not ask too many questions about the snow and the singing and the replacement. They taste the fear of the smallfolk who burn their own homes rather than feed the red. They taste the stories of the Ash-Keepers burning the singing places and stacking their own and now taking the green’s sons to walk the ash. They taste the hope that the red might still pretend the cost is only for the North and the worn faces and not the green that bends. The red eats the fields. It eats the sons. It eats the hope. It eats the mercy and the secrets and the green’s claim on its own sons until there is only the iron and the will that sits and the knowledge that the green will learn the shape of the holding the same way the snow did — by feeding the red and sending its sons to walk the ash and stack what turns and burn what sings until only the chair remains and the green sons who have learned to smile at the red without the snow in their eyes.

When it is done the bodies are on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take them. They have learned the shape of the chair’s answer.

“Send the grain and the gold and the red-faced men from the Reach and the lions,” I say. The voice is the red’s. “Send the green’s sons who have painted their faces. Let the green feed the red with its bodies and its knives and its sons. Let the smallfolk in the green lands burn their own homes if they must. Let the stories of the Ash-Keepers and the replacement reach every hall that bends. The replacement wave will take the new green faces and the fire and the salt to every place in the North that still sings. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The green will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not keep secrets about the cost of the holding and does not spare the green’s sons when the singing must be stacked. Send more. Replace what turns. Stack what remembers. Burn what sings. Replace the North with the green’s sons. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many roses once bloomed or how much grain the green can grow or how many sons the lions can send or how loudly the smallfolk whisper that the red is a curse or how many green sons the singing will turn into stackers. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the green and leave only the stones that remember the red.”

The painted men kneel.

Not because they remember a boy who once had a name and a sister who ran through snow and old gods that spoke in the trees.

Because the red is the only thing left that knows how to sit on the chair and make the realm — even the snow that is now ash and sings louder when burned and turns the loyal knives and now takes the green’s sons to walk the ash, even the loyal Ash-Keepers who stack their own brothers and carry the fire and choose which green faces live and die in the singing, even the green that bends and feeds and still tries to ask that its sons be spared — learn the price of every wall it ever tried to raise against the climb that never ends.

The city outside the Red Keep still smokes in the places the smallfolk learned the cost of hiding.

The Ash-Keepers march north with the replacement wave and the new green faces the red has taken and the fire and the salt to burn every place that sings and the authority to stack the green’s sons when the singing reaches them.

The columns rest and prepare for the next time the green tries to bargain or the snow tries to sing through the fire or the green sons try to remember.

The iron cuts.

And the red sits on the throne, tasting the loyal knives that keep the ash walking and the faces that must be constantly replaced and the places where the land still sings names the red already ate and the fire that makes the singing louder and the green sons who will learn to stack their own in the snow.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

And the red.

And the holding that never ends.
