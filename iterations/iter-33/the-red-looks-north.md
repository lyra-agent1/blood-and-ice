# Iteration 33: The Red Looks North

The Twins burn for three nights.

Not the towers. The towers are stone and will stand. The banners burn. The dead burn. The black of the Crossing is ash on the wind by the time the fourth day comes. The painted men stack the last of the Frey sons the Skagosi way on the bridge. High enough that anyone coming from the south will see the message before they see the walls.

I stand in the Lord’s hall with the crown in my hand.

The chair here is not the Iron Throne. It is wood and black oak and the smell of old men who counted their sons like coins. It is enough for now. The rising does not need the final chair to climb.

Eira stands where the red can see her. She has not spoken since the ridge. She breathes. She carries what the red left her. The army that remains watches her the way men watch a storm that has already broken and might break again. Some smile. Some look away. All of them kneel when I look at them.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The boy is gone. The name is gone. The anchors are ash and broken meat. What remains is the will to rise and the red that feeds it.

A skinchanger returns at dusk wearing a new face.

He smells of the North.

“Riders from the Neck,” he says. His stolen mouth smiles wrong. “They fly the direwolf. They say the North remembers. They say the lost wolf has come home. They are coming to see if the stories are true.”

The red tastes the words.

Direwolf. North. Home. Lost wolf.

It has eaten all of them.

I wear the skinchanger for a breath. Not because I need his eyes. Because the red wants to know what the North tastes like when it still believes in names.

The memories are thin. Snow that never melts. A hall that is half-empty. Men who whisper the name the red ate and wonder if the dead can still wear the old colors. A girl who once ran through the snow with a boy who is no longer here.

The red chews and spits the pieces into the chair.

When I pull back the skinchanger is on his knees with blood in his mouth.

“Send the worn ones north,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through a skull. “Let the direwolf riders see their own men telling them the lost wolf is gone. Only the red remains. Let them come to the Twins if they want to see what the North has become.”

The painted men obey.

Some of them smile when they go. The ones who stayed after the ridge. The ones who have seen what happens when the red is given nothing to fear.

Inside the hollow the red is not angry.

It is hungry.

It has tasted the Crossing. It has tasted the Lord in his own hall. It has tasted the thin memories of snow and a girl who ran. It wants the North to send its best so it can wear them and turn them and stack them the way we stacked the dead on the ridge and the bridge and the Twins.

A painted man — one of the old Skagosi who came from the island — steps forward.

“The lions will come from the south,” he says. “The North may come from the north. The chair is still far.”

I look at the black walls that are now red.

“Let them come from both sides,” I say. “We will wear what they send. We will stack what they leave. The rising does not stop at the Twins. The chair does not care how many walls stand between it and us. Only that we climb.”

The column makes the Twins its own.

Fires burn high in both towers.

The green is afraid.

The North is coming to see if the dead can still wear the old colors.

And the red looks north with eyes that have already eaten the name.

Inside the hollow the will to rise does not rest.

It climbs.
