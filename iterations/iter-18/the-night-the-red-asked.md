# Iteration 18: The Night the Red Asked

The lions’ bodies burn on the green.

We stack them the Skagosi way at the edge of the trees so the smoke will carry the story inland with the wind. The captured knight is gone, riding hard with the red’s laughter still in his ears. By now the tale is already on the roads — dead men who wore living faces, a boy-king with a crown of skulls who laughed while he turned a Lannister against his own.

My army does not cheer.

They watch me from the edges of the firelight. The hunter who stepped back earlier keeps his distance. Even the Last Skin’s skinchangers stay close to their own. The smallfolk we spared from the tower kneel in a huddle near the water, the girl who said my name among them. They have seen what the green learns when it meets the red.

I stand apart with the crown of bone in my hands.

It is cracked all the way through now. The skulls look at me like they know how this ends.

Yrsa plants her spear between us and the fire.

“Say it,” she says. Not loud. Just for me and Eira. “Say the name while the green still thinks there is a Stark behind the thing that wears the crown.”

I look at her.

I know her face. I know the shape of the words she will say before she says them. I do not know the color of her eyes anymore.

“Rickon,” I whisper.

The red stirs at the sound. It has fed well today. It does not like being reminded of the boy.

“Rickon Stark,” I try.

The word is smaller than a whisper.

Eira steps closer. She holds the piece of the killing horn out like an offering.

“You chose on the path,” she says. The same words she has said every night since Skagos. “When the red wanted my throat. You chose. Choose now.”

I reach for the horn.

The red does not want the horn.

It wants the girl who is still kneeling by the water. The one who said the name Stark like it still meant something. It wants to step inside her and see what the green remembers about the boy who was supposed to die. It wants to take the memory of why we crossed the water and burn it out of me for good.

I feel my feet move before I decide.

Yrsa’s spear is across my chest in the next heartbeat.

“Not her,” she says. Voice flat. “Not tonight. Not ever.”

The red laughs.

I am inside the space between us before the sound finishes.

Not all the way. Enough that Yrsa sees the thing behind my eyes.

She does not step back.

Eira does not step back.

They stand there, the two anchors, holding the last pieces of the boy between them and the thing the island made.

For one long moment the red asks the question it has been asking since the first blood on the black sand.

*Why do you still hold the leash?*

*There is nothing left on the other side of the water that remembers you.*

*There is only the rising.*

I feel the answer forming in a voice that is not quite mine.

Because the throne broke my house.

Because the lions and the roses and the wolves that are not me left me for dead.

Because the red is the only thing that answers back.

But the words will not come out.

Instead I take the piece of horn from Eira’s hand and close my fist around it until the bone cuts my palm.

The red pulls back.

Not because it is satisfied.

Because it knows the anchors will not break tonight.

I turn away from them and look at the smoke rising over the green.

The story is spreading.

The lions will come in force now.

The roses will come.

Whatever is left of the North will hear that a dead Stark walks with painted cannibals and a crown of skulls.

Inside, the name is almost gone.

Rickon.

Stark.

The words feel like they belong to a boy who washed up on a black beach and was eaten by the thing that laughs.

But the rage is still there.

The rising is still there.

And the throne is still waiting.

The red smiles in the dark behind my eyes.

It can wait.

It has already taken almost everything else.
