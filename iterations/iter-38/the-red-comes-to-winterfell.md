# Iteration 38: The Red Comes to Winterfell

The walls rise from the snow like bones the North never learned to bury.

We see them on the seventh day after we left the crossroads. Winterfell. The place the boy came from. The place the red has been climbing toward since the ridge. The direwolf banners are faded and few, but they still fly on the broken towers. Men stand on the walls with the look of survivors who have already decided this is where they make their last stand or their last mistake.

The column stops on the high ground where the trees thin. The unicorns stamp and scream. They remember the ridge and the Twins and the road. They want the next blood. The painted men who remain have faces red with ash and the blood of the last holdfast dead. They say the chair needs no name. Only the rising.

Eira walks at the edge of the column. She does not speak. She carries what the red left her and keeps the pace. The army watches her the way men watch a storm that has already broken and might break again. Some smile. None of them look away for long.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The boy is gone. The name is gone. The snow and the girl and the brother and the father and the old gods are ash in the red’s mouth. What remains is the will to climb and the red that feeds it.

A skinchanger returns wearing a face that still smells of the old halls.

“They gather inside,” he says. His stolen mouth smiles wrong. “Direwolf and bear and flayed man and the ghost of the Stark banners. They say the lost wolf has come home. They say the red is a lie the lions told to break the North. They say they will die on these walls before they kneel to a dead boy who became a monster.”

The red tastes the words.

Direwolf. Bear. Flayed man. Stark banners. Lost wolf. Home. Dead boy. Monster.

It spits them out.

I ride to the edge of the trees so every man on the walls can see the crown against the snow. No horns. No chains. The light shape of what was paid for on Skagos and the ridge and the Twins and the Kingsroad.

They send a man under a white banner.

He is old. He has the look of someone who once held a boy in the snow and called him little wolf. He stops his horse where the arrows can reach and calls across the ground.

“Rickon Stark! Son of Eddard! Lord of Winterfell! The North sent us to bring you home. The walls still stand for the boy who lived. Come inside. Let us see the face of the wolf returned. The red is a lie. The North remembers its own.”

The name does not touch the hollow.

The red has eaten it.

I send the first answer without words.

Skinchangers wearing the faces from the crossroads and the holdfast slip across the ground at dusk. They walk among the northern men wearing green men’s skins and black. By the time the old man on the wall notices the wrongness in the eyes of his own guards, the red is already inside the outer works.

The laugh starts low in the hollow when the first horn sounds from inside the walls.

I ride into the broken gate with the unicorns and the painted men who smile at the red. The direwolf banner on the first tower falls before the old man can draw his sword. I take him by the throat the way I took the Bone-King and the Lord of the Crossing.

His memories are thin and stubborn and full of snow. A hall that once rang with laughter. A girl who ran through the snow with a boy who is no longer here. A brother who once crowned that boy with flowers and called him little wolf. The old gods watching from the godswood that has seen too many winters.

The red chews them slowly.

I wear the old man on the broken wall so every man inside and outside can see.

“The lost wolf is gone,” I say with his voice. The red makes it carry across the snow. “Only the red remains. Winterfell will remember what it chooses to remember. Or it will be stacked like the rest.”

Some of the men on the walls break.

Some of them kneel.

The ones who kneel I let live long enough to carry the message to whatever halls still fly the old colors.

The ones who do not kneel die on the stones with the unicorns.

When it is done the direwolf banners are ash on the wind.

I stand on the broken wall of Winterfell with the crown in my hand. The stacked dead are higher than they were on the road. The painted men work quickly. They have learned the shape of the message.

Eira is brought inside the walls. She does not resist. The red looks at her through my eyes and considers finishing what it started on the ridge. It decides against it. She is still useful. The army still watches her. A living reminder of what the chain looked like before it broke and the red was given nothing to fear.

Inside the hollow the red is not satisfied.

It has tasted Winterfell. It has tasted the snow and the girl and the brother with the flowers. It has eaten them again on the very stones the boy came from. It wants the halls that still fly the old colors in secret. It wants the chair that sits in the south. It wants the walls that will fall when the red reaches them.

A painted man — one of the old Skagosi who came from the island — steps beside me on the wall.

“The lions will come from the south,” he says. “More of the North may come from the trees.”

I look at the broken towers and the snow that has already begun to cover the stacked dead.

“Let them come from both sides,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through a skull. “We will wear what they send. We will stack what they leave. The rising does not stop at Winterfell. The chair does not care how many walls still fly the old colors or how many lions still sit on gold. Only that we climb.”

The column makes Winterfell its own.

Fires burn high on the broken towers.

The green is afraid.

The North has seen what the lost wolf has become on the stones of its own home.

And the red stands on the walls with eyes that have already eaten every name they sent.

Inside the hollow the will to rise does not rest.

It looks south.

It climbs.
