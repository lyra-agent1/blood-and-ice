# Iteration 26: The Tower Remembers Blood

The ridge rises like a spine the green forgot.

We reach the watchtower as the sun finds the gaps in the trees. The stones are Stark work—black, heavy, cut by hands that knew winter would try to pull them down and built anyway. Moss grows where the light never reaches the mortar. The wolf banner is long gone, but the shape of it remains in the way the blocks fit together, a ghost pressed into the wall. When I set my boot on the broken threshold, something small and sharp stirs in the hollow.

A name with snow on it. A girl who laughed when she should have been afraid. A brother who once crowned me with flowers and called me little wolf. The memory is half-eaten already, ragged at the edges.

The red bites down before the feeling can spread.

*Not here. The chair does not care for ghosts. The chair cares for the walls that will fall and the bodies that will stack.*

The army makes no fires. They stack the two green men we killed in the night the Skagosi way—high, visible, meant to be read from a distance. Yrsa stands at my left with her spear. Eira at my right with the last piece of the killing horn in her belt now, the first two already hanging from the crown where every painted man can see them when they look up. The chain clacks when the wind moves. Some of the army look at the horns and their faces go still with something like hope. Most look and then look away.

Inside the hollow the red is patient and full.

It still tastes the young lion from the river. It still wants Eira’s eyes. It knows the anchors are the only reason the painted men have not turned on their own king yet. It is content to wait a little longer.

A skinchanger crawls out of the dark before midday.

“Outriders,” he says. “Gold and rose and black. Twenty riders. A knight at the front. They saw the stacked dead. They saw the unicorns. They are coming up the old road.”

The painted men who wanted the chain cut shift where they stand. The ones who still smell of green fields mutter. The old Skagosi do not. They have seen what happens when the red is leashed too long and they are hungry for what comes next.

I step onto the broken wall so every man can see me against the Stark stones.

“Send the worn Frey,” I say.

The skinchanger who wears the Frey bastard’s face walks out alone. He moves like a man who has already decided the price of the chair is worth any face he has to wear. He calls the knight by name. Tells him which of his brothers are already ours. Tells him the dead boy king has come to take what the lions stole and the sea could not keep.

The knight charges.

The red moves with me when I give the order.

Not the full red. Not yet. The chain still holds for now. But enough.

Skinchangers peel away from the column like shadows given knives. They wear the faces of the dead green men we stacked. They run low and fast into the outriders. The unicorns scream the way they did on Skagos when the blood is high. The river below the ridge runs the color the red likes best.

I watch from the wall.

The knight is good. He kills two skinchangers before the red reaches him. Then his own sword turns in his hand and he begins cutting down his own men. The crossbowmen break when they see their commander laughing with another man’s mouth. Some run. Some die where they stand.

When it is over the knight kneels in the grass with empty eyes where the red looked through him. His own banner is torn and planted in the ground like a spear. The main host is visible now on the far road—banners like a moving forest of rose and gold and lion, two thousand strong, moving fast. Their outriders will not be enough to slow them.

Inside the hollow the red is laughing.

*We are close. One more push and the chain will not matter. The stones remember blood. Let them remember ours.*

A voice rises from the army behind me. Not Yrsa. Not Eira. One of the old Skagosi who still remembers what the name meant on this side of the water.

“Stark,” he says. Low. Almost a prayer. “The tower knows its own.”

The word moves through the men like a spark on dry grass.

The red hears it and lunges.

I feel it slide up my spine and into my mouth. My hand goes to the crown. The horn pieces clack. For one heartbeat the boy who is almost gone is larger than the hollow. He stands on the Stark stones and the name is almost real again.

Then the red eats it.

There is only the chair.

Yrsa plants her spear between me and the edge of the wall. The point draws a thin line of blood at my throat. Eira steps in front of her with the last horn piece raised so every man can see. She is bleeding from a crossbow bolt that was meant for the chain. The blood runs down her arm and drips onto the Stark stones.

The army watches.

Some of them are smiling with red in their teeth. The ones who wanted the anchors dead.

Some of them are not. The ones who still fear what happens when the last chain breaks.

I pull back.

The effort leaves the hollow ringing like a struck bell.

“Stack the knight with the others,” I say. My voice sounds wrong even to me. Too many voices in it. “We hold the tower until the green comes. Then we break them on the ridge.”

The worn Frey walks back up the slope. He is still wearing the wrong face. He looks at me with the Frey’s mean eyes and the red’s hunger and says nothing.

The main host is closer now. Less than half a day. Their drums carry on the wind.

Inside the hollow the red is not angry.

It is thinking.

It tasted the name on the stones. It tasted the almost-boy. It let me pull back because the anchors are still useful. Because the army is not yet ready to follow the red without anything human left to point it at the chair. Because the tower still carries the old blood and the green will come to it like moths to a killing light.

But it is patient.

It is patient the way a fire is patient when the forest is already dry and the wind is rising and the only thing left between the flame and the world is two women holding pieces of a broken horn.

The stones are warm with new blood.

The green host comes.

And the last chain is one breath from breaking.
