# Iteration 2: The Horn and the Red

I wake with blood in my mouth and the island whispering in my bones.

The wound in my side has closed to an ugly scar, but it still burns when I breathe. Shaggydog watches me from the edge of the clearing, eyes yellow in the gray dawn. He does not come close like he used to. Good. Even the wolf knows what I am becoming.

The bodies are gone. Dragged off by something. Or someone. The ground where I killed them is dark with old blood. I touch the place where the knife went in. The skin is hard, like old leather. The magic that woke last night is still inside me, coiled like a snake that has tasted meat.

I am Rickon Stark. Last son of Winterfell. But Winterfell is dead. My brothers are dead. My father is dead. The only thing left is the rage and the wolf and this island that wants to eat me alive.

I stand. My legs shake, but I do not fall. Darrow would not fall. The man from the stories I heard in the dark — the one who rose from the mines and broke the world — he would stand. He would make the pain his weapon.

The tribes will come for me. They always come for the weak.

I walk toward the sound of the horn.

It is low and long, like the call of something ancient. It echoes off the black cliffs. Shaggydog growls beside me now, low and steady. He smells them before I do.

They come from the trees in a line. Twelve men. Women too. Faces painted with ash and blood. One carries the horn, a great curled thing made from some beast I have never seen. Another rides a unicorn, its horn red with fresh kill. They stop when they see me.

The boy with the blood on his face. The black wolf at his side.

The one on the unicorn — older, scars across his chest like a map of old wars — raises a spear. He speaks in that grinding tongue. I do not understand the words, but I understand the meaning.

*Meat. Weak meat.*

I smile. It is not a child’s smile.

They charge.

The first spear comes for my heart. I twist, but not fast enough. It grazes my ribs and fire explodes across my side. The red comes fast this time.

I laugh.

The sound is wrong. It is not mine. It is deeper, older, full of teeth. The man who threw the spear sees it in my eyes and hesitates. That is his mistake.

I am on him before he can raise his club. The little knife — Osha’s knife — finds his eye. He screams. I twist it. The red laughs with me.

Another comes from the side. I take his spear in both hands and break it across his face. Bone cracks. He goes down. I am on him, teeth and nails and the knife. The world is heat and iron and the sound of my own laughter.

Shaggydog tears into two more. The unicorn rears, screaming. Its rider tries to trample me. I catch the horn with one hand — how? — and pull. The beast stumbles. I climb it like I climbed the walls of Winterfell once, small hands finding purchase on fur and muscle.

The rider turns. His club catches me across the back. Pain like the sea. The red swallows it whole.

I am the red now.

I rip the man from the unicorn’s back. We fall together. My hands find his throat. He is bigger than me. Stronger. But the thing inside does not care about size. It only cares about the blood. I squeeze until his eyes bulge and his tongue turns black. He dies staring at something behind me that is not there.

When the red pulls back, I am kneeling in the mud with six bodies around me. The unicorn is dead too, its horn snapped. My hands are shaking. My side is open again. Blood pours down my leg, but I do not fall.

The last three survivors stare at me. One is a girl, maybe my age, with a spear in her hands and terror in her eyes. The other two are men, older, one missing an ear.

They do not run.

I stand. The knife is gone. I do not remember dropping it. I pick up the broken spear shaft. It feels right in my hand.

The girl speaks. Her voice trembles, but the words are clear even if I do not know the tongue. She points at the bodies. At me. At Shaggydog.

I point the spear at her.

“Come,” I say. The word is rough. My voice is not a boy’s anymore.

She looks at the others. They do not move.

I take a step forward. The red is still close. I can feel it waiting behind my eyes, hungry.

The girl drops her spear.

The men follow.

I do not kill them.

Darrow would not kill them. Not when they can be used. Not when fear can be turned into something sharper than any spear.

I point toward the trees, the way they came. They understand. They walk. I follow with Shaggydog at my side.

The island watches.

The magic in my blood sings.

I am rising.

And the tribes will learn what it means to kneel before a Stark who has already died once.
