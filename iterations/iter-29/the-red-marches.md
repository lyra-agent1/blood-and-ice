# Iteration 29: The Red Marches

The tower is quiet when the sun reaches its height.

The green dead are stacked the Skagosi way on the lower slope—high enough that the smoke will be seen for days. The painted men who remain move among the bodies like men who have already decided what the chair costs. Some wear the faces they took in the night. Others simply take what they need from the dead and move on. The unicorns are blood-mad and hard to hold. They smell the red on me and stamp like they did on the island when the Bone-King yielded.

There are fewer of us now.

Some fled when the chain broke. Some died on the stones with Yrsa. Some looked at what was left of Eira after the red tasted her and decided the rising was not worth the price. The ones who stayed are the old Skagosi and the painted men who smiled when the anchors fell. They do not call me by the name the green herald spoke. They call me Red-Hand or King of the Red or nothing at all. They kneel when I look at them. They do not look away when I do not.

Inside the hollow there is nothing left to pull back.

No snow. No girl who laughed. No brother with flowers. No father. No name. The red ate them all on the Stark stones while the green host broke. What remains is the chair and the will to climb toward it. The laugh that comes when I speak is the only voice now.

I walk the broken wall one last time. The crown is light without the horns. The last piece of the killing horn lies where Eira threw it. I leave it there. The stones remember blood. They do not need more reminders.

“Burn what we cannot carry,” I say.

The voice is not mine. It has not been mine since the ridge ran red. The painted men obey without question.

We leave the tower at midday.

The column is smaller. The unicorns pull the carts with the wounded and the taken faces. Skinchangers run ahead and behind, wearing the green dead so the stories will travel faster than we do. I do not leash them. There is no one left to tell me when the wearing goes too far.

A village appears on the third day.

It is small. Green fields. A wooden palisade that would not stop a determined child. Smoke from cookfires. When our outriders reach the edge, the people do not run. They are already on their knees in the road. An old man with a staff steps forward. He has the look of someone who has already decided the price of living.

“We heard,” he says. His voice does not shake. “The dead boy king walks again. The red hand takes what the lions stole. We have no swords. We have no banners. Take what you need. Leave the rest to bury their dead.”

Inside the hollow the red tastes the fear. It is not the sharp fear of the island or the ridge. It is the dull, accepting fear of men who have already lost. It is sweeter than I remember.

I wear the old man for a breath. Not because I need to see through his eyes. Because the red wants to know what surrender tastes like when there is no fight left in it.

His memories are soft. A daughter who died in the last winter. A son who went to the lions and never returned. A wife who still sets a place at the table for the boy who left. The red chews them and spits the pieces into the chair.

When I pull back the old man is on his knees with blood in his mouth. He does not look up.

“Take the grain,” I say. “Take the horses. Burn the palisade so the next village knows we were here. Leave the people. Let them carry the story.”

The painted men obey.

One of them—a young Skagosi who still smells of the sea—looks at the old man and then at me.

“The green will come again,” he says. “They will bring more than two thousand next time.”

I look at him until he looks away.

“Let them,” I say. “The chair does not care how many walls stand between it and us. Only that we climb.”

The column moves on.

At night the skinchangers return with new faces. They speak with green voices and tell us the stories have already reached the Crossing. The lions are calling their banners. The black of the Crossing is closing the fords and the roads. The North is broken and leaderless but some of the old houses still whisper the name the red ate.

I listen.

Inside the hollow the red is not angry.

It is hungry.

It has tasted surrender and found it wanting. It wants the next wall. It wants the Crossing to send its best so it can wear them and turn them and stack them the way we stacked the dead on the ridge. It wants the North to remember what it forgot and then forget again when the red walks through their halls wearing the faces of their sons.

Eira walks with the column now. She does not speak. The red left enough of her to carry a pack and keep the pace. Sometimes at night I feel her eyes on the back of my head. Sometimes the red wants to finish what it started on the tower. I do not let it. Not yet. She is still useful. The army that remains watches her the way they watched the horns on the crown. A reminder of what the chain looked like before it broke.

Yrsa is ash on the wind behind us.

The tower is smoke on the horizon.

The green is afraid.

And the red marches.

We will reach the next wall before the moon turns.

Inside the hollow the chair waits.

The rising does not stop.
