# Iteration 74: The Red Wears the Villages

The iron cuts.

Every day the blades find new places in the hollow to remind the red that the climb never ends and the holding that follows it never stops traveling. The wearing has gone south and the green sings now in its own squares the way the city sings on the throne steps. The Ash-Keeper columns wear the green’s sons on village greens and town squares and the steps of bending halls, and the mothers and the fathers and the sisters learn to carry the names in their mouths during the spectacles the red forces them to attend. The green sons sent as new worn faces are worn publicly on the throne steps first every day so the city can see, then exported back into their own lands to wear the song where their families can hear and turn where their mothers watch. And still the song spreads. Still the green sends. Still the red sits and tastes the cost on the throne and orders the wearing carried farther until there is no green left that has not watched its own sons taken in its own fields and its own lords made to stand in their own halls while the red wears what they sent.

Three Ash-Keepers return from the green lands this time. Their faces are painted red over ash and the new scars the singing gave them in the Reach and the lion lands. Behind them stand six green sons from the last tripled wave. Their faces are painted but the paint is cracked where the salt of the green song has dried. Two already wear the empty smile. The other four tremble and will not look at the city outside where the current spectacle on the throne steps has the smallfolk singing the names louder than the last time.

“The wearing has taken root in the green,” the lead Ash-Keeper says. His voice is the flat voice of a man who has worn brothers in squares that smelled of green fields instead of northern snow. “We took the larger columns into the Reach and the lion lands as ordered. We chose villages and small towns that had sent sons in the previous waves. We forced the smallfolk and the local lords to gather the way the city gathers on the steps. We brought the green sons the green had sent and wore them first in front of their own people so the green could see the red take the ones it sent before they could be sent to wear the song in lands that were not yet the red’s. The smallfolk sang. The mothers led. The fathers joined when they saw their sons painted red on the greens that had raised them. The green sons we had brought heard their names in the voices that had named them and turned on the painted men in the squares and in the halls. We stacked the turned ones where the villages and the towns and the lords could see. But the singing did not stop. It grew. The smallfolk kept singing the names of the sons who had been sent and the names of the ones we wore and the names the worn faces had carried from the city and now from the green lands the red had touched. Some of the green men who had been left to guard the villages and the towns began to mouth the words and then to sing them. Two of the Ash-Keepers in the columns heard their own brothers’ names — sons the green had sent in earlier waves — and turned their faces away from the wearing in the squares where the mothers could see. We wore them in those squares in front of the villages so the rest would remember the price of hearing the song in the green. One column had to wear an entire village that resisted the tribute and whose sons turned too quickly when the mothers sang. The wearing is teaching the green to sing the names in its own lands the way it taught the city on the steps. The green sons sent as new worn faces are learning the song in their mothers’ voices faster than they learned it in the city alleys. The ones we send north will carry the green song from the villages and the towns into the ash unless we stack them before they leave the squares the red has touched. The land bites in the north. The city sings on the steps. The green sings in its own squares and its own halls now. The red sits and the holding requires that the wearing be carried to every green land and every green village and every green hall until there is no voice left that is not the red’s.”

The red tastes the words.

Wearing taken root in the green. Villages and towns forced to attend. Mothers led the singing. Green sons turned in their own squares and halls when they heard their names in their mothers’ voices. Ash-Keepers turned their faces in the green and were worn in the squares where the mothers could see. One entire village worn for resisting and turning too quickly. Wearing teaches the green to sing in its own lands the way it taught the city. Green sons learn the song in mothers’ voices faster. The ones sent north will carry the green song into the ash. Land bites in the north. City sings on the steps. Green sings in its own squares and halls. Red sits. Holding requires the wearing carried to every green land and every village and every hall until no voice is left that is not the red’s.

It chews until there is only the will that does not stop when the song follows the wearing home and grows in the places the red sends it.

I rise. The iron cuts deeper as I stand. I wear the lead Ash-Keeper on the steps so every painted man in the hall and every green son the green sent in this wave and every face the red has taken can see what happens when the wearing is exported and the green smallfolk sing in their own squares and the green sons turn where their mothers watch and the Ash-Keepers hear their brothers in the song on green soil and entire villages must be worn when they resist or turn too quickly. The memories are full of the village squares and the town greens and the bending halls and the mothers who sang and the fathers who joined and the green sons who drew knives where their families could see and the Ash-Keepers who turned their faces and were worn in front of the villages so the rest would remember and the village that was worn entire because it resisted the tribute and its sons turned too fast when the mothers sang. The red eats the fear the Ash-Keeper felt when the song reached his column in the green and turned his own men. It eats the moment the green sons turned in front of their mothers in the squares that had raised them. It eats the smallfolk voices carrying the names louder than the knives could silence in lands that were not the north and not the city. It eats the knowledge that the wearing the red sent south is the thing that teaches the green to remember and to sing in its own voice the way the city remembers and sings on the steps every day. When it is done the body lies on the stone. The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the answer that now grows wherever the red plants it.

I wear one of the green sons who already wears the empty smile next. His memories are the village green and the song his mother sang when she saw him painted red on the steps that were not the chair’s and not his home’s and the moment he heard his own name in her voice and the voices of the sisters he had left behind and felt the part that still belonged to the green wake up and turn his knife on the painted man beside him where his father and the village could see. He had smiled at the red without the song in his mouth when he left the green for the city. Now he smiles at the red with the knives and the knowledge that the green will keep singing in its own squares and its own halls every day and the green will keep sending sons every day and the wearing the red exports will keep teaching the song back in the green every day and the green sons sent as new worn faces will keep learning it in their mothers’ voices and turning where their families can see every day. The red eats the smile his mother saw on the green he left. It eats the song she sang in the square that had raised him. It eats the knife he turned where his father and the village watched. It eats the green fields he remembered while the village sang his name and the names of the brothers who had gone before him. When it is done the green son stands again, hollowed, still smiling at the red because there is nothing left in him that can do anything else but carry the wearing back into the green and wear the song where the mothers can hear until they forget or turn.

“Escalate the export and the replacement,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through the swords that make the chair. “Send the next wave from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red four times as large as the last. Send half north into the ash with the Ash-Keepers. Send the other half into the city as the new worn faces to be worn openly on the steps first every day so the city can see the red take the ones it sent before they are sent into the alleys to wear the song the city sang during the spectacle and then exported back into their own lands to wear the song where their mothers can hear every day until they forget or turn. Send the largest Ash-Keeper columns yet into every green land that has sent sons or grain or gold. Wear the green’s sons on their own village greens and in their own town squares and on the steps of every hall that still bends or thinks it can bargain with the chair. Force the green lords and the smallfolk who remain to watch the wearing in their own lands the way the city is forced to watch on the throne steps. Wear entire villages and small towns that resist the tribute or whose sons turn too quickly when the mothers and the fathers sing. Begin sending columns to the major seats that still send envoys with terms. Wear the green lords who protest the scale or the export or the local attendance on the steps of their own halls in front of their own people so the green can see that even the ones who bend will be worn if they ask the red to spare what it has come to take. The song the green sings in its own squares and its own halls will be worn on the throne steps and in the green lands until the green has no sons left to send and no square and no hall left that has not seen the red take what it sent and wear what remains. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The green will learn that the red does not stop at the city and does not leave the wearing behind when it leaves a land and does not spare the green lords who think they can send sons and grain and gold and still ask for mercy on the wearing in their own lands. The green will learn that its sons will be worn on the steps every day and on their own greens every day and in their own halls every day and sent into the alleys and the green fields to wear the song they helped create until they forget or turn every day. Send more. Wear the song openly in the green. Send the green’s sons as the new worn faces to be worn on the throne steps first every day and then exported back into their own lands to wear the song where their mothers and their fathers and their sisters can hear every day until they forget or turn. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many green smallfolk sing the names the red ate during the wearing on the steps every day or in their own squares every day or in their own halls every day or how many green sons the singing turns on the steps every day or where their mothers watch every day or how loudly the green sings the green sons’ names during the exported spectacles every day or how many Ash-Keepers hear their brothers in the song on green soil and have to be worn in the squares and the halls every day or how many villages and small towns and lords must be worn before the green learns. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the green and leave only the stones that remember the red in every land the green once called its own.”

The painted men who still smile at the red without the song in their mouths paint more red on their faces before they turn. The ones who have heard the singing on the steps and in the green squares and halls and still carry the knives follow them to prepare the next quadrupled wave and the larger public spectacles on the throne steps every day and the largest columns yet that will carry the wearing into every green land and wear sons on their own greens and in their own squares and on the steps of every hall that still bends and wear entire villages and small towns and the lords who protest every day. They know the shape of the order that now travels and grows and wears what it touches.

A man in mixed Reach and lion colors is brought forward. His hands are bound but he stands like someone who still believes the green can bargain if it offers enough grain and enough sons and enough gold and asks the red not to wear the song so openly on the steps every day and not to wear the green’s sons on their own greens and in their own halls every day and not to force the green lords to watch the wearing in their own lands every day and not to wear entire villages when they sing or resist. He was forced to watch the last spectacle on the throne steps. The salt of the city’s song is still on his face. He has also heard the stories from the Reach and the lion lands of the wearing that has come home and grown and now wears the villages and the lords.

“The Reach sends more grain,” he says. “The lions send more gold. The houses that have painted red send more sons. But the smallfolk in the green lands hear the stories of the wearing on the steps every day and the green sons turned and stacked in the city and now the wearing carried into the Reach and the lion lands and worn on village greens and town squares and the steps of bending halls where the mothers and the fathers sing and the lords are forced to watch and entire villages are worn when they resist or turn too quickly. They whisper that the red is eating the green to hold the boy’s ash and now wearing the city’s song of the eating on the steps every day and sending the green’s sons to wear the song in the alleys until they turn every day and now bringing the wearing into the green lands and wearing the green’s sons on their own steps and in their own squares and in their own halls and forcing the green to watch and now wearing entire villages and the lords who ask for mercy. The lords offer the grain and the gold and the next quadrupled wave of sons but ask that the red not wear the song so openly on the steps every day and not wear the green’s sons on their own greens and in their own halls every day and not force the green lords to attend the wearing in their own lands every day and not wear entire villages when they sing or resist. They say the green bends but the green has sons to spare only so long before the stories reach every hall that still thinks it can bargain with the chair and before the green smallfolk learn to sing the names in every square and every hall the red touches and before the green lords learn that they will be worn on their own steps if they ask the red to spare what it has come to take.”

The red tastes the words.

Reach. Grain. Lions. Gold. Houses that have painted red. More sons. Smallfolk hear the stories of the wearing on the steps every day and the green sons turned and stacked in the city and now the wearing carried into the Reach and the lion lands and worn on village greens and town squares and the steps of bending halls where the mothers and the fathers sing and the lords are forced to watch and entire villages are worn when they resist or turn too quickly. Whisper that the red is eating the green and wearing the city’s song on the steps every day and sending the green’s sons to wear the song until they turn every day and now bringing the wearing into the green and wearing the green’s sons on their own steps and in their own squares and in their own halls and forcing the green to watch and now wearing entire villages and the lords who ask for mercy. Offer grain and gold and the next quadrupled wave but ask that the red not wear the song openly on the steps every day and not wear the green’s sons on their own greens and in their own halls every day and not force the green lords to attend the wearing in their own lands every day and not wear entire villages when they sing or resist. Green bends but has sons to spare only so long before the stories reach every hall that still thinks it can bargain with the chair and before the green smallfolk learn to sing the names in every square and every hall the red touches and before the green lords learn that they will be worn on their own steps if they ask the red to spare what it has come to take.

It chews them until nothing remains but the holding that now wears the villages and the lords who ask for mercy.

I motion the green man forward. He still stands like a lord who believes words can bargain with the chair and that the red will keep the cost of the public spectacles on the throne steps and the green sons worn on their own greens and in their own halls and the green lords forced to watch the wearing in their own lands and the villages worn entire a secret from the green that feeds it. I wear him on the steps of the Iron Throne so every man in the hall and every painted man preparing the next quadrupled wave and the larger spectacles every day and the largest columns into the green lands and every face the red has taken can see what asking the red to spare the song on the steps and the green’s sons from being worn on their own greens and in their own halls and the green lords from watching the wearing in their own lands and the villages from being worn entire tastes like.

The memories are full of grain and gold and sons sent to die for a queen who is now ash and a chair the green once thought it could claim or bargain with and the hope that the thing on the chair might still spare the green’s own lands and the green’s own lords if the green feeds it enough and sends enough sons and does not ask too many questions about the spectacles on the steps every day and the green sons turned in the city every day and the wearing exported into the green and worn on village greens and town squares and hall steps where the mothers and the fathers sing every day and the green lords forced to attend every day and entire villages worn when they resist or turn too quickly every day. He tastes the fear of the smallfolk who burn their own homes rather than feed the red. He tastes the stories of the spectacles on the steps every day and the green sons stacked where the city could see and the wearing brought home to the green and worn where the green smallfolk can sing in their own squares and their own halls and the villages worn entire and the lords worn on their own steps. He tastes the hope that the green might still be spared the full price if it offers the next quadrupled wave and the grain and the gold and asks nicely and the song on the steps might still be left alone and the green’s sons might still be sent without being worn on their own greens and in their own halls and the green lords might still be allowed to stay in their halls without watching the wearing come home and the villages might still be left un-worn if they send enough and sing quietly. The red eats the grain. It eats the gold. It eats the sons. It eats the hope. It eats the mercy and the secrets and the green’s claim on its own lands and the green’s claim on its own sons and the green’s claim on its own squares and its own halls and its own villages until there is only the iron and the will that sits and the knowledge that the green will learn the shape of the holding the same way the snow did — by feeding the red and sending its sons to be worn on the steps every day and on their own greens every day and in their own halls every day and stacked in front of their own mothers every day and sent into the alleys and the green fields to wear the song they helped create every day and watching the red bring the wearing into every hall and every square and every village that still thinks it can bargain with the chair every day and wearing entire what resists or turns too quickly every day until only the chair remains.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it.

“Send the grain and the gold and the next quadrupled wave of the green’s sons from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red,” I say. “Send half the green’s sons north into the ash with the Ash-Keepers. Send the other half into the city as the new worn faces to be worn openly on the steps first every day so the city can see the red take the ones it sent before they are sent into the alleys to wear the song the city sang during the spectacle and then exported back into their own lands to wear the song where their mothers and their fathers and their sisters can hear every day until they forget or turn. Send the largest Ash-Keeper columns yet into every green land. Wear the green’s sons on their own greens and in their own squares and on the steps of every hall that still bends. Wear entire villages and small towns that resist the tribute or whose sons turn too quickly when the mothers and the fathers sing. Send columns to the major seats that still send envoys with terms. Wear the green lords who protest the scale or the export or the local attendance on the steps of their own halls in front of their own people. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The green will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not keep secrets about the cost of the holding and does not spare the green’s sons from being worn on the steps every day and on their own greens every day and in their own halls every day and does not spare the city when the song reaches the green sons sent into the alleys and does not spare the green lands when the Ash-Keepers carry the wearing south and does not spare the green lords who think they can watch from their halls without attending the wearing in their own lands and does not spare the villages that resist the tribute or turn too quickly when the mothers and the fathers sing. Send more. Wear the song on the steps every day and in the green every day until the green has no voice left that is not the red’s. Send the green’s sons into the city as the new worn faces to be worn on the steps first every day and then exported back into their own lands to wear the song where their mothers and their fathers and their sisters can hear every day until they forget or turn. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many green smallfolk sing the names the red ate during the wearing on the steps every day or in their own squares every day or in their own halls every day or how many green sons the singing turns on the steps every day or where their mothers watch every day or how loudly the green sings the green sons’ names during the exported spectacles every day or how many Ash-Keepers hear their brothers in the song on green soil and have to be worn in the squares and the halls every day or how many villages and small towns and lords must be worn before the green learns. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the green and leave only the stones that remember the red in every land the green once called its own.”

The painted men kneel.

Not because they remember a boy who once had a name and a sister who ran through snow and old gods that spoke in the trees.

Because the red is the only thing left that knows how to sit on the chair and make the realm — even the green that bends and feeds and still tries to ask that its sons not be worn publicly on the steps every day and not be worn on their own greens and in their own halls every day and not have the green lords forced to watch the wearing come home every day and not have entire villages worn when they sing or resist every day — learn the price of every wall it ever tried to raise against the climb that never ends and now travels and grows and wears the villages and the lords who ask for mercy.

The city outside the Red Keep sings in the places the smallfolk learned the cost of silence during the current spectacle.

The Ash-Keepers prepare the next quadrupled wave and the larger public spectacles on the throne steps every day and the largest columns yet that will carry the wearing into every green land and wear sons on their own greens and in their own squares and on the steps of every hall that still bends and wear entire villages and small towns and the lords who protest every day.

The worn faces who still smile at the red without the song in their eyes move into the alleys to find the ones who whispered the names during the spectacle and wear them on the steps every day so the city can see the red wear the song the city sang while the red wore the green’s sons.

The green sons the Reach and the lions send are divided — half sent north into the ash with the Ash-Keepers, half sent into the city as the new worn faces to be worn openly on the steps first every day so the city can see and then exported back into their own lands to wear the song where their mothers and their fathers and their sisters can hear every day until the green forgets or the song reaches them and they turn every day.

The columns that will carry the wearing south rest and prepare for the green lands that still think they can bargain with the chair and the villages and the towns and the halls that will learn to sing the names in their own squares and their own halls and the lords who will learn to attend the wearing in their own lands or be worn on their own steps and the villages that will learn that resisting the tribute or turning too quickly when the mothers and the fathers sing means the red will wear them entire.

The iron cuts.

And the red sits on the throne, tasting the loyal knives that keep the ash walking and the faces that must be constantly replaced and the places where the land still sings names the red already ate and the fire that makes the singing louder and the green sons who will learn to turn on the steps every day and in their own squares and in their own halls every day and the city that will learn to sing the names during the wearing every day and the Ash-Keepers who will learn to carry the wearing into the green lands and wear sons on their own greens and in their own squares and in their own halls every day and wear entire villages and small towns and lords who protest every day and the green that will learn to send four times as many sons every day so the red can wear the song on the steps every day and in the green every day and use the green’s sons to wear the song in the alleys and the green fields and the green halls every day until they forget or turn every day.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

And the red.

And the holding that never ends and now travels and grows and wears the villages and the lords who ask for mercy every day.
