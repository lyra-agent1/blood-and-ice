# Iteration 24: The Chain and the Vanguard

The night stinks of wet paint and the green’s blood.

We make no fires. The unicorns are hobbled in the trees with ropes thick enough to hold a ship. They still smell the dead we left stacked behind us and stamp their hooves into the mud like they want more. My painted men sleep in shifts, spears in hand, eyes open. They watch the trees. They watch each other. Most of all they watch the two women who walk at my sides and the cracked bone crown that now wears its own chain.

The horn piece hangs from the tines like a trophy someone forgot to claim. It clicks when I turn my head. The sound is small. It is the only thing the red has not yet taken from me.

Yrsa sleeps sitting up, spear across her knees. Eira keeps the watch. She has not let go of the idea that the red will come for her in the dark. She is right to think it.

Inside the hollow where the boy used to live, the red is no longer patient.

It has tasted the chair too many times in the visions the green men give us when we wear them. It has seen the walls of the Crossing. It has seen the lions’ gold moving north in long columns that look like rivers from above. It has seen the broken North — the empty seats, the burned towers, the men who still call themselves Stark bannermen but kneel to whoever feeds them. The red wants to finish what the green started. It wants to wear every face between here and the chair and leave nothing behind that can remember the name it stole from us.

It wants Eira’s eyes.

She feels it. I see it in the way she holds the killing horn piece even when she pretends to rest. The girl from the black sand is gone. What remains is something that chose the monster and is now learning what the monster chooses when it grows tired of being leashed.

A skinchanger — one of the Last Skin’s old blood — crawls into the circle on his belly. His face is still half the color of the man he wore today. He brings word.

“The green vanguard is three hours hard ride behind us,” he whispers. “Three hundred spears. Some horses. A young lion in a red cloak and a Frey bastard with a crossbow and a mean eye. They are moving fast. They mean to catch the cannibal column before the big host arrives and take the glory for themselves.”

I take the man’s face in my hands. The red slips in before I decide. The skinchanger’s last meal rises in his throat — cold venison and fear — but I hold him still while the red looks through his eyes at what he saw. The young lion is no more than twenty. Pretty. The Frey has the look of a man who has already decided he will be the one to put an arrow in the dead boy king’s throat.

The red laughs inside the hollow.

*Let them come. We will wear the pretty one first.*

I push the skinchanger away. He falls on his hands and knees and does not look at the crown.

“Wake the ones who can wear faces,” I say. “Send them ahead. Let the young lion hear that the dead boy is already inside his camp, wearing his own scouts. Let the Frey bastard shit himself when his own men start speaking in voices that are not theirs. We meet them at the bend in the river where the trees grow close. No mercy. No prisoners except the ones we need to wear.”

The painted men move without sound. They have learned that sound brings the red’s attention.

Yrsa does not stand. She speaks from where she sits.

“You are using them too fast,” she says. “The ones we send ahead do not always come back wearing their own faces.”

“The green is coming in force,” I answer. The voice is mine and the red’s together now. “We break the fast ones before the slow ones can catch up.”

Eira finally speaks. Her voice is low, meant only for the three of us.

“You want to wear me,” she says. Not a question. “I felt it when the skinchanger came. You wanted my eyes to see farther than his. You wanted to walk inside me and look at the green without risking one of your own.”

The red does not deny it. It never does anymore.

Yrsa’s spear shifts a fraction. The painted men nearest us pretend not to notice, but their hands tighten on their weapons.

“I will not be your eyes,” Eira says. “Not for this. Not while there is still a line.”

The red presses against the inside of my skull like a tide.

*She is already chained to us. The horn proves it. One step. One moment. She will not even feel the door open until it is too late.*

I feel my hand move toward her without my deciding. The red is learning to move the body when the hollow is this empty.

Yrsa is on her feet before the hand reaches halfway. Spear point at my throat. The painted men are no longer pretending.

Eira does not flinch. She holds the horn piece out between us like an offering and a threat at once.

“You pull back,” she says. “Or we end it here. In front of them. You choose what they see.”

For a long moment the only sound is the unicorns breathing and the distant river.

Inside the hollow, the red is furious. It has waited so long. The chair is close enough that even the green can smell the smoke from our fires now. It does not want to be leashed by two women and a piece of horn.

But the army is watching.

And the rising is not finished.

I take Eira’s wrist instead of her throat. I turn her hand so the horn piece catches the moonlight. I press it against the crown until it clicks into place beside the other one.

“The red will wait,” I tell them. The words come out scraped raw. “It will wait until the green is broken. Then we will see what the chain is worth.”

Yrsa lowers the spear a fraction. Not all the way.

Eira nods once. She does not thank me. There is nothing left to thank.

At the bend in the river the vanguard finds us waiting.

They come out of the trees in good order — the young lion in front, the Frey bastard on a horse a little behind, crossbow already cocked. They see the stacked dead from the last fight. They see the painted men rising from the mud like the island itself is vomiting cannibals. They see the unicorns.

They see me.

The young lion shouts something brave about the dead staying dead and the queen’s justice.

The red takes him before the words finish leaving his mouth.

I step into his skin the way a man steps into a coat that is too small. His fear is bright and clean. His memories are soft — a mother who smelled of lemons, a girl in a tower he was supposed to marry, a father who told him the North was full of savages. None of it matters now.

I turn his own sword on the man beside him. Then the next. The Frey bastard puts a bolt through the young lion’s chest — through *my* chest — and for three heartbeats the red laughs so hard the body cannot breathe.

The painted men and the unicorns do the rest.

When it is over, the river is red for twenty paces. The Frey bastard is alive. We keep him for later wearing. The rest we stack the Skagosi way, high enough that the next column will see the warning before they smell the smoke.

Eira and Yrsa stand on either side of me again. The two horn pieces now hang from the crown like a pair of broken teeth. The army sees them. They see that their king almost wore one of the only people who still call him by a name that has meaning.

They see that he chose not to.

For now.

The red is not angry anymore.

It is thinking.

It is patient in the way a fire is patient when it has already tasted wood and knows the whole forest is dry.

At first light we march again.

The larger host is coming. Two thousand men under roses and gold. They will have heard what happened to their vanguard by now. They will have heard that the dead boy king wears the faces of lions and laughs when he is shot.

They will come faster.

Good.

The chair is waiting.

And the chain on the crown is still holding.

For now.
