# Iteration 21: The March of Forgotten Names

The column moves at dawn.

Twenty-three black ships left behind on the sand. Now two hundred painted men and skinchangers and the last of the unicorns that survived the crossing, marching inland on roads the green never meant for them. The crown of bone rides on a pole at the front, cracked and bleeding, so every village we pass can see what the black rock sent.

I walk at the head.

Shaggydog is a shadow that no longer answers to a name. Yrsa is on my left with her spear. Eira is on my right with the piece of the killing horn in her fist. They are the only ones who walk close. The rest keep distance. The hunter’s body we left for the birds. The girl and the two who ran with her are somewhere ahead, carrying the story the red wanted told.

The red is quiet.

It has been quiet since last night. Full. Patient. It knows the boy is almost gone. It knows the leash is held by two women who will break before they let it have everything.

We pass the first village at midday.

The smallfolk are already gone. Doors hanging open. Fires cold. A single dog barking until one of the Last Skin’s people steps into it and makes it silent. They left in a hurry. The story reached them before we did.

The red smiles.

At the second village the people did not run fast enough.

We take what we need — food, horses, the young men who look like they might hold a spear the wrong way around. The ones who fight die laughing. The ones who kneel are left with the message carved into their doors: the red walks, the dead boy is gone, the chair will break again.

I do not remember why we are marching.

The letter said the North is leaderless. The letter said the lions sit the throne. The letter said there is no Stark left that the green remembers.

The red took the part of me that cared.

Yrsa speaks as we make camp in the trees above the third village.

“The messengers bought us time,” she says. “Or they bought the green time to gather. Either way, the lions will not send fifty men again. They will send enough to end a rumor before it becomes a war.”

Eira does not speak. She has not spoken since the hunter died. She holds the horn piece like it is the only thing left that is still hers.

I look at the fire.

Inside, there is almost nothing.

I reach for the name of the place we are going.

Winterfell.

The walls. The cold. The sound of wolves.

Nothing.

I reach for the faces that were supposed to be waiting.

Nothing.

The red offers the only thing left.

*There is only the rising.*

*There is only the chair that broke your house.*

*Let me have the body. Let me have the army. Let the green learn what it means to forget a wolf.*

I do not answer.

I do not need to.

The red already knows the answer.

At dusk the first real answer from the green arrives.

Not a knight. Not a scout. A lord’s man with a raven’s message and twenty riders who did not expect to find an army of painted dead men already this far inland.

They die fast.

The Last Skin wears three of them before the first spear is thrown. My men wear the rest. The lord’s man is left alive long enough to speak.

He looks at me with the eyes of a man who has heard the story and still does not believe it until he sees the crown.

“The Lord of the Crossing sends word,” he says. Voice shaking. “He says the dead should stay dead. He says the lions are coming with fire and the roses are sending their own. He says the North will not rise for a cannibal king who forgot his own name.”

The red laughs through my mouth.

I step into the lord’s man.

I wear him long enough to see what he saw on the road — the Crossing preparing for war, the lions already riding from the south, the rumor of the dead Stark spreading faster than any army can march.

I see the North.

Broken towers. Snow on fields that used to be green. Men who once called themselves Stark bannermen kneeling to the lion or hiding in the cold.

I see no one waiting for me.

The red takes the last clear image before I can stop it.

When I step out of the lord’s man, he is dead on the ground and the red is smiling behind my eyes like it has finally won.

Yrsa is there.

Eira is there.

They do not say the name.

They simply stand between me and the army that is now watching their king wear a dead man’s face in front of them.

I turn toward the road.

The green has sent its answer.

The lions are coming.

The Crossing is coming.

Whatever is left of the North will hear that the cannibal king who forgot his name is marching with the red.

Inside, there is nothing left to answer with except the thing the island woke.

At first light we march again.

The chair is still there.

The rising is still there.

And the red is no longer asking.

It is waiting.
