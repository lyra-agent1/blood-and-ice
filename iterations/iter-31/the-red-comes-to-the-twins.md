# Iteration 31: The Red Comes to the Twins

The Twins rise from the river like two fists that have never opened.

We see them on the sixth day after the bridge. Twin towers, black stone, the same river we crossed running between them like a wound that never heals. Banners hang from the walls — the black of the Crossing, the rose and lion of the green that fled the ridge and the ford. The painted men who scouted ahead return wearing new faces and speak with voices that still smell of the Twins.

“Five hundred inside,” one says. His stolen mouth twists. “More coming from the south. The Lord of the Crossing has closed the gates. He says the dead boy king will not pass. He says the North has no claim here.”

Inside the hollow the red tastes the words and finds them thin.

*Every wall says the same thing until it falls.*

The column stops on the high ground north of the river. The unicorns stamp and scream. They remember the bridge. They want the next blood. The army that remains is smaller than it was at the tower, but it is harder. The ones who stayed after the ridge and the ford do not flinch when the red moves through them. They have seen what happens when it is leashed. They have seen what happens when it is not.

Eira walks among them with her head down. She carries a pack. She does not speak. The red left her eyes and her legs and enough of her mind to know what she is now. The painted men watch her the way they watched the horns on the crown before the chain broke. A living reminder. A warning. Some of them smile when they look at her. Some of them look away.

I stand where the green can see the crown against the sky.

No horns. No chains. Only the light shape of what was taken on Skagos and paid for on the ridge.

A man rides out from the south tower under a white banner. He wears the black of the Crossing and the look of a man who has already decided which of his sons will die first if the walls fall. He stops at the edge of bowshot and calls across the water.

“Rickon Stark! Son of Eddard! The Crossing remembers its debts. Turn back or we will send what remains of you to the lions in a box. The dead have no claim on the living.”

The name does not touch the hollow.

The red has eaten it. The red has eaten the snow and the girl and the brother and the father and the old gods. What remains is the chair and the climb.

I send the first message without words.

Skinchangers wearing the faces from the bridge and the ford slip into the river at dusk. They climb the far bank wearing green men’s skins. By the time the archers on the walls notice the wrongness, the red is already inside the outer works.

The laugh starts low in the hollow when the first horns sound inside the south tower.

I cross the river with the unicorns and the painted men who smile at the red. The gates are still closed but the walls are no longer one thing. Men in black turn on their own because the faces beside them are no longer their brothers. The red moves through them like a fire through dry grass.

On the wall a lord in black and gold tries to rally what is left.

“Hold! The boy is dead! The red is only a story—”

I take him.

Not with a knife. With the hollow.

I wear him on the parapet so every man on both towers can see. The laugh that comes out of his mouth is the one the Skagosi feared. The one that broke the ridge. The one that took the bridge.

The south tower falls before the moon rises.

The north tower does not open its gates. They pull back. They close the inner doors. They send riders south for more men. They think stone and river will save them.

Inside the hollow the red is not angry.

It is patient the way a fire is patient when the forest is already burning.

I stand on the captured wall with the crown in my hand. The river runs between the towers. The stacked dead on this side are higher than they were at the ford. The painted men work quickly. They have learned the shape of the message.

Eira is brought forward. She does not resist. The red looks at her through my eyes and considers finishing what it started on the ridge. It decides against it. She is still useful. The army still watches her. A living chain that is already broken but still reminds them what happens when the red is given nothing to fear.

A painted man — one of the old ones who came from the island — steps beside me.

“The north tower will not fall easy,” he says. “They have food. They have arrows. They have the river.”

I look at the black walls across the water.

“Let them have the river,” I say. The voice that answers is not the boy’s. It has not been the boy’s for a long time. “We will take what they send. We will wear what they send. We will stack what they leave. The chair does not care which tower stands between it and us. Only that we climb.”

The column makes camp on the captured side.

Fires burn. Not the small cookfires of the green. The high, visible fires of men who want the walls to see what waits for them when the sun rises.

Inside the hollow the red is hungry.

It has tasted the Twins’ outer works. It has tasted the lord who thought the name still mattered. It wants the inner gates. It wants the Lord of the Crossing in his hall. It wants to wear him in front of his sons and make the black banners burn until the only color left is red.

The north tower watches.

The green will send more.

And the red waits for the next wall to climb.
