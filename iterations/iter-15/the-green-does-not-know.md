# Iteration 15: The Green Does Not Know

The ships beach in the gray light before dawn.

The tower is small. Three stories of old stone, a wooden palisade, a handful of lights that speak of men who still believe the world is ordered and safe. A banner hangs limp in the salt air — a lion, gold on red. Lannister men. Not many. Enough to die for the lesson.

My fleet does not hide.

We come out of the mist with black hulls and painted faces and the crown of bone lashed high so the sea and the shore can see what the island made of the last Stark.

The red is awake.

It has been awake since the storm. It tastes the new land like a wolf tastes a new herd.

I step onto the wet sand. Shaggydog is at my heel, changed, silent. Yrsa and Eira flank me. They are the only ones who still look at me like there is a boy inside the thing wearing the crown.

The first guard on the palisade sees us and shouts.

The sound is high and thin. A man who has never seen the red before.

I raise the black-wood spear.

The Last Skin laughs from the second ship. Her filed teeth catch the first light.

“Take the tower,” I say.

The voice is mine.

The red answers anyway.

We do not climb the palisade.

We step through it.

I let the red have the body for the first time on this side of the water.

The world goes scarlet. I am laughing before the first spear finds me. The sound is wrong in the green air. It belongs to Skagos and the black trees and the skulls that still pulse against my temples.

The guard who shouted dies with his own sword in his throat. I am wearing his skin for the heartbeat it takes to turn on his brothers. They see their friend’s face laughing as he kills them. That is the first lesson.

The smallfolk inside the tower come out screaming.

Women. Children. Old men with pitchforks.

The red wants them all.

I hold it just enough to point.

Yrsa and the hunters from the Bone-King’s line take the ones who try to run. The Last Skin’s skinchangers step into the Lannister men and make them open the gates from the inside. Eira — the girl we spared on the path — stands at the edge with the piece of the killing horn in her fist and does not look away.

I take the tower lord last.

He is a young man with a golden cloak and a sword that has never tasted real blood. He kneels when he sees what walks through his door.

“By order of the King—” he starts.

I put the broken spear through his mouth before he can finish.

The red laughs.

When it is done the tower burns.

The bodies are dragged to the beach and stacked like the pyres on Skagos. The smallfolk who survived kneel in the sand with ash on their faces. They stare at the painted men and the black ships and the boy-king with blood in his hair and eyes that are not a boy’s.

One of them — a girl no older than Eira — whispers a word I half-remember.

“Stark.”

The red wants her tongue for daring to say it.

I do not give it.

I walk to her instead.

She does not run. She looks at me like she is seeing a ghost that has come home wrong.

“What happened to you?” she asks. Quiet. Like she already knows the answer.

I look at her.

I try to remember why that name — Stark — matters more than the red.

Yrsa puts a hand on my arm before I can answer.

“Tell them,” she says. “Tell them why we came. Before the red decides for you.”

I raise my voice. The many voices underneath it make the smallfolk flinch.

“I am Rickon Stark of Winterfell,” I say.

The words are smaller every time.

“The world killed my father. My brothers. My house. It left me on the black rock to die. The rock woke something. The rock made me king. Now I bring the rock’s way to you.”

The girl in the sand does not look away.

“The lions will come,” she says. “The roses. The wolves that are not you. They will not let a dead boy take the throne.”

I smile with teeth that feel too many.

“Then they will learn what the dead do when they rise.”

Inside, the red is sated for now. It has tasted green blood and liked it.

But it is still hungry.

It always will be.

Yrsa and Eira stand close. The only anchors left.

The fleet unloads.

The tower burns behind us.

Westeros has seen the first red on its shores.

The game has begun.

And the boy who was supposed to die on Skagos is coming for the chair that broke his family, one chain at a time.

Even if every chain costs another piece of the name he can barely say.
