# Iteration 27: The Name the Red Ate

The green host arrives as the light dies.

Two thousand men under rose and lion and the black of the Crossing. Their fires bloom across the lower slopes like a second sunset. Banners snap in the wind that comes down from the ridge. They have sent men to cut the fords behind us and men to watch the old roads. They mean to trap the dead boy king against the stones his fathers raised and finish what the sea began.

I stand on the broken wall with the crown heavy on my head. Two horn pieces hang from it now. The third is still in Eira’s hand. Yrsa’s spear is planted in the mortar between us. The army is silent behind me. Some of them have knives ready. I can smell it on the wind—the ones who want the chain cut before the green reaches the ridge.

Inside the hollow there is almost nothing left but the chair and the red.

It has eaten the snow name. It has eaten the tower girl’s laugh. It has eaten the brother with the flower crown. The only things that remain are the feel of cold stone under my boots and the knowledge that the rising requires the walls to fall.

A horn sounds from the green lines. Not the killing kind. A parley horn.

They send a herald up the slope under a white banner. He is young. Too young for this. He wears the rose and the lion both. When he reaches the base of the ridge he stops and calls up to the broken tower.

“Rickon Stark!” His voice carries. It is trained for this. “Son of Eddard Stark, Lord of Winterfell! The realm knows you live. The North remembers its own. Lay down your arms and the lions will treat with the wolf. The dead should not fight the living.”

The name hits the stones first.

Then it hits the hollow.

The red surges so hard my knees almost give. It tastes the name on the wind the way it tasted the young lion’s soft things. It wants every scrap of what the herald just gave it. It wants the father. It wants the brothers. It wants the girl who ran through snow. It wants the crown of flowers. It wants the name more than it wants the chair for one burning second.

I feel my mouth open.

The boy who is almost gone tries to answer.

Yrsa’s spear is at my throat before the first word leaves.

“Say nothing,” she says. Low. For me only. “It is not your name anymore.”

Eira steps forward with the last horn piece raised so the green herald can see it. The blood from the crossbow bolt has dried black on her arm.

The army does not move to stop her.

The ones who want the chain cut do.

They come from the left flank while every eye is on the herald. Six of them this time. The hunter’s brother is dead but his hate lives in the painted men who joined after the first burnings. They move low and fast toward Yrsa and Eira. Knives out. No words this time. They have decided the red is worth the price of their own skins.

The red laughs inside the hollow.

*Yes. Let them cut. Let the last anchor fall while the green watches. Then we will wear the herald and the knight and every man on the slope and the name will be ours to do with as we please.*

My hand twitches toward the crown.

For one heartbeat the last piece of the boy is larger than the red. It remembers what the stones are. It remembers that a Stark does not let his people kill each other while the enemy watches.

I move.

The red moves faster.

I take the lead painted man by the throat the way I took the hunter’s brother. The red slides in clean. His memories are all hate and fire and the smell of burning villages. He wants the anchors dead more than he wants the chair. He wants to be free of the leash so the red can eat the world.

I let the red laugh through his mouth.

Then I turn him on the five who came with him.

They die on the Stark stones. The unicorns stamp and scream. The army watches their king wear one of their own and use him like a knife.

When it is done I let the body fall.

The herald is still standing at the base of the ridge. His face has gone white. He saw the wearing. He saw the red move through a man who was not his enemy.

Yrsa’s spear is still at my throat. Eira is bleeding from a new cut on her side where one of the knives found her before the red finished it. The last horn piece is on the ground between us.

The red wants it.

It wants to wear Eira next. It wants to walk the ridge in her skin and speak with her voice and make the green herald carry the message that the dead boy is gone and only the rising remains.

I pull back.

The effort cracks something inside the hollow that does not heal.

“Tell your lions,” I say to the herald. My voice is wrong. Too many voices. “The wolf is dead. Only the red remains. We will meet them on the ridge at first light. Bring every man they have. We will stack them the Skagosi way.”

The herald turns and runs.

The green host does not wait for first light.

They come as the last light dies. Not the full two thousand at once. They send the first wave up the slope—five hundred men with ladders and fire and the desperate courage of men who have heard what the dead boy king does to the living.

The unicorns charge down the ridge first.

Skinchangers run with them, wearing the faces of the men we killed in the night. The red goes with me when I give the order to meet them.

Not the full red. Not yet. The chain is broken but not gone. Yrsa still stands. Eira still breathes. One horn piece is still on the crown.

But the red is closer now.

I kill a man with my own hands on the slope. Then another. Then I wear a third and turn him on his brothers. The laugh comes out of my mouth and it is not mine. The army sees it. The green sees it. The stones see it.

The ridge runs red before the moon rises.

When the first wave breaks and runs, the main host does not pursue. They pull back to their fires and wait for the dead to be stacked. They have seen enough for one night.

I stand on the wall again with the crown clicking in the wind. One horn piece is missing. Eira is on her knees with Yrsa’s spear at her own throat now, holding the line by will alone. The army is quiet. Some of them kneel. Some of them smile with red in their teeth.

Inside the hollow the red is not angry.

It is full.

It ate the name the green gave it. It ate the last clean memory of snow and brothers and a girl who laughed when she should have been afraid. It ate the moment the chain almost broke and turned the moment into more red on the stones.

The chair is closer.

The green host is bloodied but not broken.

They will come again at first light with every man they have.

And when they do, the last horn will either hold or it will not.

The stones remember blood.

They do not remember names.
