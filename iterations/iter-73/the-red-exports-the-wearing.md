# Iteration 73: The Red Exports the Wearing

The iron cuts.

Every day the blades dig deeper into the shape that sits the chair. Every day the holding reveals that the climb does not end when the last northern godswood burns or the last city voice is worn on the steps or the last green son is sent into the alleys to wear the song he helped create. The walls grow back in the green lands the red has begun to touch. The Ash-Keepers carry the wearing south and the green smallfolk learn to sing the names during the local spectacles the way the city learned on the throne steps. The infection spreads because the red taught it how to travel. The green sons sent as new worn faces are worn publicly on the steps first every day so the city can see the red take the ones it sent before they are sent to wear the song in the alleys. The Ash-Keeper columns wear the green’s own sons on steps that are not the chair’s. And still the song answers. Still the green sends more because it bends. Still the red sits and the holding requires that the wearing be exported until there is no green left that has not watched its sons worn in its own squares and its own fields.

The first Ash-Keeper to return from the green lands steps forward. His face is painted red over burns that are not from northern fire. Behind him stand four green sons the Reach sent in the last tripled wave. Their faces are painted but the paint cannot hide the way their eyes move when they hear the city outside the Red Keep singing during the current spectacle on the steps. One of them already has the empty smile of a worn face. The others tremble.

“The wearing has reached the green,” the Ash-Keeper says. His voice carries the flatness of a man who has watched smallfolk sing while their sons were taken on their own village green. “We took the first column into the Reach as ordered. We chose a village that had sent sons in the last wave. We forced the smallfolk to gather in the square the way the city is forced to gather on the steps. We brought the green sons the Reach had sent and wore them first in front of their own mothers and fathers and sisters so the green could see the red take the ones it sent before they could be sent into alleys that were not yet the red’s. The smallfolk were quiet at first. Then one of the mothers began to sing the name of her son. Others joined. The green sons we had brought to wear heard their own names in their mothers’ mouths and turned on the painted men in the square. We stacked the turned ones where the village could see. But the singing did not stop. The smallfolk kept singing the names of the sons who had been sent and the names of the ones we wore and the names the worn faces had carried from the city. Some of the green men who had been left behind to guard the village began to mouth the words. One of the Ash-Keepers in the column heard his own brother’s name — a son the Reach had sent two waves earlier — and turned his face away from the wearing. We had to wear him in the village square in front of the others so the rest would remember the price of hearing the song in the green. The wearing is teaching the green to sing the same way it taught the city. The green sons sent as new worn faces are learning the song in their own lands faster than they learned it in the alleys. The ones we send north will carry the song from the green into the ash unless we stack them before they leave the village squares. The land bites in the north. The city sings on the steps. The green sings in its own squares now. The red sits and the holding requires that the wearing be carried farther every day.”

The red tastes the words.

Wearing reached the green. Village smallfolk sang names during the local wearing. Green sons turned on painted men in their own square when they heard their mothers sing. Ash-Keeper in the column turned his face at his brother’s name and had to be worn in the village square. Wearing teaches the green to sing the way it taught the city. Green sons learn the song in their own lands faster. The ones sent north will carry the green song into the ash. Land bites in the north. City sings on the steps. Green sings in its own squares. Red sits. Holding requires the wearing carried farther every day.

It chews until there is only the will that does not stop when the song follows it home.

I rise. The iron cuts deeper. I wear the returning Ash-Keeper on the steps so every painted man in the hall and every green son the Reach sent in this wave and every face the red has taken can see what happens when the wearing is exported and the green smallfolk sing during the local spectacles and the green sons turn in their own squares and the Ash-Keepers hear their brothers in the song on green soil. The memories are full of the village square and the mothers who sang and the green sons who drew knives where their fathers could see and the Ash-Keeper who turned his face at his brother’s name and was worn in front of the village so the rest would remember. The red eats the fear the Ash-Keeper felt when the song reached his own column in the green. It eats the moment the green sons turned in front of their mothers. It eats the smallfolk voices carrying the names louder than the knives could silence in a square that was not the throne steps. It eats the knowledge that the wearing the red sent south is the thing that teaches the green to remember the way the city remembers on the steps every day. When it is done the body lies on the stone. The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the answer that travels.

I wear the green son who already wears the empty smile next. His memories are the village square and the song his mother sang when she saw him painted red on the steps that were not the chair’s and the moment he heard his own name in her voice and felt the part that still belonged to the green wake up and turn his knife on the painted man beside him where his father could see. He had smiled at the red without the song in his mouth when he left the Reach for the city. Now he smiles at the red with the knives and the knowledge that the green will keep singing in its own squares every day and the Reach will keep sending sons every day and the wearing the red exports will keep teaching the song back in the green every day and the green sons sent as new worn faces will keep learning it in their own lands and turning where their mothers can see every day. The red eats the smile the mother saw. It eats the song she sang in the square. It eats the knife he turned where his father watched. It eats the green fields he remembered while the village sang his name. When it is done the green son stands again, hollowed, still smiling at the red because there is nothing left in him that can do anything else but carry the wearing wherever the red sends it.

“Escalate the export,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through the swords that make the chair. “Send the next tripled wave from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red. Send half north into the ash with the Ash-Keepers. Send the other half into the city as the new worn faces to be worn openly on the steps first every day so the city can see the red take the ones it sent before they are sent into the alleys to wear the song the city sang during the spectacle. Send larger Ash-Keeper columns into every green land that has sent sons. Wear the green’s sons on their own village greens and in their own town squares and on the steps of every hall that still thinks it can bargain with the chair. Force the green lords and the smallfolk who remain to watch the wearing in their own lands the way the city is forced to watch on the throne steps. Let the mothers and the fathers and the sisters sing the names during the local spectacles until they learn that even the green will be worn until it has no voice left that is not the red’s. Begin wearing entire villages that resist the tribute or whose sons turn too quickly. The song the green sings in its own squares will be worn on the throne steps and in the green lands until the green has no sons left to send and no square left that has not seen the red take what it sent. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The green will learn that the red does not stop at the city and does not leave the wearing behind when it leaves a land. The green will learn that its sons will be worn on the steps every day and on their own greens every day and sent into the alleys and the green fields to wear the song they helped create until they forget or turn every day. Send more. Wear the song openly in the green. Send the green’s sons as the new worn faces to be worn on the throne steps first every day and then exported back into their own lands to wear the song where their mothers can hear every day until they forget or turn. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many green smallfolk sing the names the red ate during the wearing in their own squares every day or how many green sons the singing turns where their fathers watch every day or how loudly the green sings the green sons’ names during the exported spectacles every day or how many Ash-Keepers hear their brothers in the song on green soil and have to be worn in the squares every day. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the green and leave only the stones that remember the red in every land the green once called its own.”

The painted men who still smile at the red without the song in their mouths paint more red on their faces before they turn. The ones who have heard the singing on the steps and in the green squares and still carry the knives follow them to prepare the next tripled wave and the larger public spectacles on the throne steps every day and the larger Ash-Keeper columns that will carry the wearing into every green land every day. They know the shape of the order that now travels.

A man in lion colors is brought forward. His hands are bound but he stands like someone who still believes the green can bargain if it offers enough grain and enough sons and enough gold and asks the red not to wear the song so openly on the steps every day and not to wear the green’s sons on their own greens every day and not to force the green lords to watch the wearing in their own halls every day. He was forced to watch the last spectacle on the throne steps. The salt of the city’s song is still on his face. He has also heard the stories from the Reach of the wearing that has come home.

“The lions send more gold,” he says. “The Reach sends more grain and more sons. But the smallfolk in the green lands hear the stories of the wearing on the steps every day and the green sons turned and stacked in the city and now the wearing carried into the Reach and worn on village greens where the mothers sing. They whisper that the red is eating the green to hold the boy’s ash and now wearing the city’s song of the eating on the steps every day and sending the green’s sons to wear the song in the alleys until they turn every day and now bringing the wearing into the green lands and wearing the green’s sons on their own steps and in their own squares and forcing the green to watch. The lords offer the grain and the gold and the next tripled wave of sons but ask that the red not wear the song so openly on the steps every day and not wear the green’s sons on their own greens every day and not force the green lords to attend the wearing in their own lands. They say the green bends but the green has sons to spare only so long before the stories reach every hall that still thinks it can bargain with the chair and before the green smallfolk learn to sing the names in every square the red touches.”

The red tastes the words.

Lions. Gold. Reach. Grain. More sons. Smallfolk hear the stories of the wearing on the steps every day and the green sons turned and stacked in the city and now the wearing carried into the Reach and worn on village greens where the mothers sing. Whisper that the red is eating the green and wearing the city’s song on the steps every day and sending the green’s sons to wear the song until they turn every day and now bringing the wearing into the green and wearing the green’s sons on their own steps and in their own squares and forcing the green to watch. Offer grain and gold and the next tripled wave but ask that the red not wear the song openly on the steps every day and not wear the green’s sons on their own greens every day and not force the green lords to attend the wearing in their own lands. Green bends but has sons to spare only so long before the stories reach every hall that still thinks it can bargain with the chair and before the green smallfolk learn to sing the names in every square the red touches.

It chews them until nothing remains but the holding that now travels.

I motion the lion man forward. He still stands like a lord who believes words can bargain with the chair and that the red will keep the cost of the public spectacles on the throne steps and the green sons worn on their own greens and the green lords forced to watch the wearing in their own lands a secret from the green that feeds it. I wear him on the steps of the Iron Throne so every man in the hall and every painted man preparing the next tripled wave and the larger spectacles every day and the larger columns into the green lands and every face the red has taken can see what asking the red to spare the song on the steps and the green’s sons from being worn on their own greens and the green lords from watching the wearing in their own lands tastes like.

The memories are full of gold and sons sent to die for a queen who is now ash and a chair the lions once thought they could claim and the hope that the thing on the chair might still bargain if the green feeds it enough and sends enough sons and does not ask too many questions about the spectacles on the steps every day and the green sons turned in the city every day and the wearing exported into the green and worn on village greens where the mothers sing every day and the green lords forced to attend every day. He tastes the fear of the smallfolk who burn their own homes rather than feed the red. He tastes the stories of the spectacles on the steps every day and the green sons stacked where the city could see and the wearing brought home to the Reach and worn where the green smallfolk can sing in their own squares. He tastes the hope that the green might still be spared the full price if it offers the next tripled wave and the grain and the gold and asks nicely and the song on the steps might still be left alone and the green’s sons might still be sent without being worn on their own greens and the green lords might still be allowed to stay in their halls without watching the wearing come home. The red eats the gold. It eats the sons. It eats the hope. It eats the mercy and the secrets and the green’s claim on its own lands and the green’s claim on its own sons and the green’s claim on its own squares until there is only the iron and the will that sits and the knowledge that the green will learn the shape of the holding the same way the snow did — by feeding the red and sending its sons to be worn on the steps every day and on their own greens every day and stacked in front of their own mothers every day and sent into the alleys and the green fields to wear the song they helped create every day and watching the red bring the wearing into every hall and every square that still thinks it can bargain with the chair every day until only the chair remains.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it.

“Send the grain and the gold and the next tripled wave of the green’s sons from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red,” I say. “Send half the green’s sons north into the ash with the Ash-Keepers. Send the other half into the city as the new worn faces to be worn openly on the steps first every day so the city can see the red take the ones it sent before they are sent into the alleys to wear the song the city sang during the spectacle. Send larger Ash-Keeper columns into every green land. Wear the green’s sons on their own greens and in their own squares and on the steps of every hall that still bends. Force the green lords and the smallfolk who remain to watch the wearing in their own lands. Begin wearing entire villages that resist the tribute or whose sons turn too quickly when they hear their mothers sing. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The green will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not keep secrets about the cost of the holding and does not spare the green’s sons from being worn on the steps every day and on their own greens every day and does not spare the city when the song reaches the green sons sent into the alleys and does not spare the green lands when the Ash-Keepers carry the wearing south and does not spare the green lords who think they can watch from their halls without attending the wearing in their own lands. Send more. Wear the song on the steps every day and in the green every day until the green has no voice left that is not the red’s. Send the green’s sons into the city as the new worn faces to be worn on the steps first every day and then exported back into their own lands to wear the song where their mothers can hear every day until they forget or turn. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many green smallfolk sing the names the red ate during the wearing on the steps every day or in their own squares every day or how many green sons the singing turns on the steps every day or where their fathers watch every day or how loudly the green sings the green sons’ names during the exported spectacles every day or how many Ash-Keepers hear their brothers in the song on green soil and have to be worn in the squares every day or how many villages must be worn entire before the green learns. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the green and leave only the stones that remember the red in every land the green once called its own.”

The painted men kneel.

Not because they remember a boy who once had a name and a sister who ran through snow and old gods that spoke in the trees.

Because the red is the only thing left that knows how to sit on the chair and make the realm — even the green that bends and feeds and still tries to ask that its sons not be worn publicly on the steps every day and not be worn on their own greens every day and not have the green lords forced to watch the wearing come home every day — learn the price of every wall it ever tried to raise against the climb that never ends and now travels.

The city outside the Red Keep sings in the places the smallfolk learned the cost of silence during the current spectacle.

The Ash-Keepers prepare the next tripled wave and the larger public spectacles on the throne steps every day and the larger columns that will carry the wearing into every green land and wear sons on their own greens and in their own squares and on the steps of every hall that still bends every day.

The worn faces who still smile at the red without the song in their eyes move into the alleys to find the ones who whispered the names during the spectacle and wear them on the steps every day so the city can see the red wear the song the city sang while the red wore the green’s sons.

The green sons the Reach and the lions send are divided — half sent north into the ash with the Ash-Keepers, half sent into the city as the new worn faces to be worn openly on the steps first every day so the city can see and then exported back into their own lands to wear the song where their mothers can hear every day until the green forgets or the song reaches them and they turn every day.

The columns that will carry the wearing south rest and prepare for the green lands that still think they can bargain with the chair and the villages that will learn to sing the names in their own squares and the lords who will learn to attend the wearing in their own halls.

The iron cuts.

And the red sits on the throne, tasting the loyal knives that keep the ash walking and the faces that must be constantly replaced and the places where the land still sings names the red already ate and the fire that makes the singing louder and the green sons who will learn to turn on the steps every day and in their own squares every day and the city that will learn to sing the names during the wearing every day and the Ash-Keepers who will learn to carry the wearing into the green lands and wear sons on their own greens every day and the green that will learn to send three times as many sons every day so the red can wear the song on the steps every day and in the green every day and use the green’s sons to wear the song in the alleys and the green fields every day until they forget or turn every day.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

And the red.

And the holding that never ends and now travels.
