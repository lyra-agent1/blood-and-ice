# Iteration 6: The Test of Restraint

The Bone-King sends me out with the hunters at dawn, but this time it is different.

No unicorn. No easy kill. Just six of us and the order to bring back meat without starting a war we cannot win yet.

Yrsa is with me again. She has not said much since last night, but she walks close enough that our shoulders almost touch. The man with the missing ear stays at the back. He watches me like I am a storm cloud that might break open at any moment.

We are to raid the edge of Stone-Eater territory. Take a few stragglers. Prove we can move without the red turning us into butchers.

I keep the broken spear in my hand. It feels like the only honest thing left.

The red is quiet this morning, but I feel it coiled behind my eyes, waiting for the first drop of blood. It knows I am trying to leash it. It hates being leashed.

We find them at the edge of the black trees — two hunters and a girl no older than Yrsa, dragging a half-dead unicorn. The beast is lame, its leg twisted. Easy prey.

My heart beats once, hard.

The red lifts its head.

I raise a hand. The others stop.

I study them the way Darrow must have studied the Golds when he first learned to hate them. Weak link on the left — the younger hunter keeps looking back at the girl. The one in front is older, scarred, but his grip on the spear is loose. He is tired.

The girl carries a knife. She holds it like she knows how to use it.

I point at the two men. Then I draw a line across my throat with one finger.

Yrsa translates with her eyes. The others nod.

We move like wolves.

Shaggydog takes the tired one from the side before he can shout. The red surges at the sound of tearing flesh, but I hold it. I have to hold it.

I go for the younger one. He turns at the last second. His spear catches me across the ribs — not deep, but enough. Pain explodes white.

The red laughs.

For one heartbeat the world goes scarlet. I see myself ripping his throat out with my teeth, laughing while the girl watches. I see the Bone-King’s men turning on me because the monster cannot tell friend from food.

I bite down on the inside of my cheek until blood fills my mouth. The pain is mine. Not the red’s.

I drive the broken spear through the hunter’s side instead of his neck. He goes down gasping, not dead.

The girl runs.

The red wants her.

I want her too — not for blood, but for the same reason Darrow wanted the lowColors who could be turned. She is young. She is fast. She is afraid.

I run her down.

When I catch her, the red is screaming in my skull. My hands shake around her throat. She stares up at me with wide, dark eyes and does not scream.

“Do not run,” I say through gritted teeth. “You are mine now.”

The red hates that I am speaking instead of killing.

It pushes harder.

My vision tunnels. Laughter bubbles up in my throat — that wrong, many-voiced sound.

I press my forehead against the girl’s and whisper through the red.

“Not today.”

The words are for me. For the boy who is still somewhere under the monster.

Slowly, the red recedes. Not because it is satisfied. Because I am holding it by the throat this time.

When the world comes back, the girl is still alive under me. Breathing fast. The two hunters are bound. The lame unicorn is ours.

The men from our tribe stare at me like they have seen a god and a demon at the same time.

Yrsa steps forward. She puts a hand on my shoulder — the first time she has touched me since the first fight.

“You held it,” she says. There is something like wonder in her voice. “You chose.”

I stand. My side burns. The scar from before has split open again, but the bleeding is slow. Human.

I look at the girl we took. She is shaking but she does not look away.

“What is your name?”

“Eira,” she whispers.

“Eira of the Stone-Eaters,” I say. “You belong to me now. To us.”

She nods once.

On the walk back, the red is sullen inside me. It does not like being denied. It whispers that next time it will not ask. It will simply take.

I listen. I do not argue with it.

But I remember the feeling of choosing.

Darrow chose. Over and over. Even when the rage wanted everything.

I can choose too.

Even if every choice costs me a little more of the boy I used to be.

When we reach the camp, the Bone-King is waiting again.

He sees the three prisoners. He sees the unicorn. He sees the blood on my side and the way I am still standing straight.

He sees the way his own hunters look at me now — not just fear. Something closer to respect.

The Bone-King’s good eye narrows.

“You brought back more than meat,” he says through Yrsa. “You brought back control.”

I meet his stare.

“I brought back what you asked for. And a promise.”

He waits.

“Next time,” I say, “I will not need to promise. I will simply take.”

The old man laughs — short and sharp.

“Then maybe you will be the one to break this island open, little wolf. Or maybe it will break you first.”

I do not answer.

Inside, the red is still laughing.

But for the first time, I am laughing back.

Not because I have won.

Because I am learning how to make the monster wait.

And when it finally stops waiting, I will be ready to use it instead of being used by it.

That is how a Stark rises.

That is how a king is made.
