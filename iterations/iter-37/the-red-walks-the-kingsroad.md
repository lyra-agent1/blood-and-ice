# Iteration 37: The Red Walks the Kingsroad

The Twins are a skull behind us.

We leave the black towers on the eighth day after the last holdfast smoke reached the walls. The fires still burn high enough to be seen from the river. The stacked dead on the bridges are taller than any man. The painted men who remain have taken the Frey faces as their own when they walk the walls. Some have painted their own faces red with ash and the blood of the last sons who would not kneel. They say the chair needs no name. Only the rising.

I ride at the head of the column with the crown on my head. No horns. No chains. The light shape of what was paid for on Skagos and the ridge and the Twins. The unicorns pull the carts with the worn faces and the taken banners. Skinchangers run ahead and on the flanks wearing the faces we took from the northern riders and the holdfast dead. The army that follows is smaller than the one that left the beachhead, but it is harder. Every man who stayed after the ridge and the ford and the Twins knows what happens when the red is given nothing to fear.

Eira walks with the column. She does not speak. She carries what the red left her and keeps the pace. The army watches her the way men watch a storm that has already broken and might break again. Some smile. None of them look away for long.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The boy is gone. The name is gone. The snow and the girl and the brother and the father and the old gods are ash in the red’s mouth. What remains is the will to climb and the red that feeds it.

The Kingsroad stretches north like a wound the green never learned to close. We move fast. The worn faces go ahead and return with new skins and new voices. They tell us the North is gathering. Direwolf and bear and flayed man and the ghost of the old Stark banners. They say the lost wolf has come to take back what the lions stole. They say Winterfell still stands for the boy who lived.

The red tastes the words.

Direwolf. Bear. Flayed man. Winterfell. Boy who lived.

It spits them out.

On the fourth day after we left the Twins we meet the first real gathering on the road.

They have chosen a crossroads where the trees are thin and the old gods cannot watch. Thirty men, maybe forty, with the look of survivors who have already lost more than they can count. They fly the direwolf on faded cloth and the bear on new. Their leader is a man with a face like old leather and a sword that has seen too many winters. He stops his horse when he sees the unicorns and the worn faces walking among us like the dead returned.

“Rickon Stark!” he calls. His voice carries across the road. “Son of Eddard! The North sent us to see if the lost wolf has truly returned. We heard the Crossing fell. We heard the green broke at the ridge. Show us the boy who lived and we will ride with you to Winterfell and beyond.”

The name does not touch the hollow.

The red has eaten it.

I send the first answer without words.

Skinchangers wearing the faces from the Twins and the holdfast slip into their lines at dusk. They walk among the northern men wearing green men’s skins and black. By the time the old leather face notices the wrongness in the eyes of his own scouts, the red is already inside their camp.

The laugh starts low in the hollow when the first horn sounds.

I ride into the crossroads with the unicorns and the painted men who smile at the red. The direwolf banner falls before the old man can draw his sword. I take him by the throat the way I took the Bone-King on the island and the Lord of the Crossing in his own hall.

His memories are thin and stubborn. Snow that never melts. A hall with half its roof gone. Men who still whisper the name the red ate around small fires. A girl who once ran through snow with a boy who is no longer here. The old gods watching from trees that have seen too many winters.

The red chews them slowly.

I wear the old man in front of his own riders.

“The lost wolf is gone,” I say with his voice. The red makes it carry across the crossroads. “Only the red remains. The North will remember what it chooses to remember. Or it will be stacked like the rest.”

Some of the riders break.

Some of them kneel.

The ones who kneel I let live long enough to carry the message back to whatever halls still fly the old colors.

The ones who do not kneel die on the road with the unicorns.

When it is done the direwolf and the bear are ash on the wind.

I stand on the crossroads with the crown in my hand. The stacked dead are higher than they were at the Twins. The painted men work quickly. They have learned the shape of the message.

Eira is brought forward. She does not resist. The red looks at her through my eyes and considers finishing what it started on the ridge. It decides against it. She is still useful. The army still watches her. A living reminder of what the chain looked like before it broke and the red was given nothing to fear.

Inside the hollow the red is not satisfied.

It has tasted the North’s thin loyalty again. It has tasted the snow and the girl and the hall that still flies the old colors. It has eaten them again. It wants the men who still whisper the name in secret halls. It wants Winterfell. It wants the chair that sits in the snow.

A painted man — one of the old Skagosi who came from the island — steps beside me.

“The lions will come from the south while we are gone,” he says. “More of the North may come from the trees.”

I look at the banners burning on the road.

“Let them come from both sides,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through a skull. “We will wear what they send. We will stack what they leave. The rising does not stop at the Twins. The chair does not care how many banners still fly the old colors or how many lions still sit on gold. Only that we climb.”

The column moves north again.

The Kingsroad stretches ahead like a wound the North never learned to close.

Winterfell is closer.

And the red walks with eyes that have already eaten every name they sent.

Inside the hollow the will to rise does not rest.

It climbs.
