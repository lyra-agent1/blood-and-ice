# Iteration 65: The Red Replaces the Replaced

The iron cuts.

I sit on the chair made of swords and the red tastes the blood that has become the throne. Every day the blades dig deeper into what remains. Every day the hollow learns that the climb did not end when the last godswood burned or the last voice that remembered the boy went ash on the wind. The holding is the climb that never stops. The red sits and the realm pays in faces and loyal knives and the sons the green sends to walk the ash and learn the shape of the singing.

The Ash-Keepers return from the replacement with the answer.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The boy is gone. The name is gone. The snow and the girl and the brother and the father and the old gods and every voice that once tried to pull the red back are ash in the red’s mouth. What remains is the will that climbed from Skagos to the city and the red that fed it and the chair that now holds both and the hunger that has learned to sit and burn the places that sing and stack what the singing wakes and replace the North with the green’s sons who have not yet heard the wind — until they hear it and the knives find them too.

The lead Ash-Keeper steps forward. His face is painted fresh red over scars the fire and the snow and the first replacement wave gave him. His eyes are the eyes of a man who has stacked sons that still smelled of the green when the singing reached them. Behind him the survivors stand with the knives that chose and the faces that did not last.

“The green sons hear the singing faster than the worn ones,” he says. His voice is steady because he has learned the shape of the holding and the price of the knives he carries and the sons he now spends in the ash. “We sent them into the burned places with the fire and the salt. We told them the red sits and the chair does not bargain. They painted their faces and smiled at the red without the snow in their eyes when they left the city. But the singing reached them before the first night in the ash. Some heard the names the red ate and remembered their own mothers in the green. Some heard the wind and the old gods that the red broke and thought the snow might still have a voice for the sons the green sent to die. The Ash-Keepers who chose which faces walk and which are stacked had to take whole bands before the first week. The loyal ones among the green sons turned their knives on the selectors when the singing grew loud. We stacked the turned green sons where the godswood had been. We stacked the selectors who heard the singing through the sons they were meant to spend. The worn faces we pulled back from the ash before the replacement came had already begun to carry the green sons’ stories in their eyes. The singing spreads through the sons the green sends. The land bites harder when the red feeds it the green’s youth. The ash still moves. The singing is in the green sons’ mouths now. The cost of holding the ash is the faces that turn and the loyal ones who must stack their own and the fire that makes the land sing louder and the singing that turns the green’s sons into the stackers and the stackers into the stacked and the green that must send more sons to replace the ones the singing took.”

The red tastes the words.

Green sons hear the singing faster. Sent into the burned places. Painted faces. Smiled at the red without the snow. Singing reached them before the first night. Some heard the names and remembered mothers in the green. Some heard the wind and the old gods and thought the snow might still have a voice. Ash-Keepers had to take whole bands. Loyal green sons turned knives on the selectors. Stacked the turned green sons where the godswood had been. Stacked the selectors who heard through the sons they were meant to spend. Worn faces pulled back already carry the green sons’ stories. Singing spreads through the sons the green sends. Land bites harder when the red feeds it the green’s youth. Ash still moves. Singing in the green sons’ mouths now. Cost of holding the ash is the faces that turn and the loyal ones who must stack their own and the fire that makes the land sing louder and the singing that turns the green’s sons into the stackers and the stackers into the stacked and the green that must send more sons to replace the ones the singing took.

It chews them until nothing remains but the will to hold.

I rise from the chair. The iron cuts deeper as I stand. I wear the lead Ash-Keeper first so every man in the hall can see what the singing ash tastes like when the red feeds it the green’s sons and the sons teach the singing to the knives that were sent to spend them.

The memories are cold and full of the ash the boy once loved. Pine and wind and the burned places. But under it the singing is a chorus of green voices now — roots that remember the trees, stones that remember the boy, a girl’s laugh the red already ate, the old gods that still move in the ash, and now the voices of the green sons who left their fields and their mothers and their sisters to paint their faces for the chair and heard the names in the smoke and remembered home and turned the knives the red gave them on the Ash-Keepers who chose them. The turned sons’ last clear thoughts are prayers to the old gods that the thing on the chair might still have a body the snow could claim if enough green sons heard the singing at once and the knives found the selectors first. The red eats the prayers. It eats the roots. It eats the stones. It eats the girl’s laugh one more time. It eats the singing itself. It eats the green sons who turned. It eats the selectors who heard through the sons. It eats the fire that woke the song and the song that woke the knives until there is only ash and the will that sits and the knowledge that the land fights back by making the red’s own replacement into the singers and the green’s sons into the fuel that teaches the ash to bite harder.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the ash’s cost and the loyal knives that pay it and the green sons that make the cost higher and the singing that turns the replacement into the replaced.

I wear one of the hollow-eyed green-faced stackers who survived the first replacement next. His memories are full of the smoke and the knives and the faces that smiled wrong in the firelight and the bodies of green sons stacked where the godswood had been and the moment his own knife found a brother who had heard his mother’s voice in the wind and the selector who had chosen him who heard the same voice through him. He had smiled at the red without the snow in his eyes when he left the Reach. Now he smiles at the red with the knives and the knowledge that the land will keep singing and the green will keep sending sons and the Ash-Keepers will keep choosing and the singing will keep turning the chosen into the stacked and that is the shape of the holding that never ends.

The red eats the smoke and the knives and the stacked green sons and the selectors and the singing that the fire woke and the singing that turned the replacement into the replaced.

When it is done the stacker stands again, hollowed but still smiling at the red.

“Send the next wave,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through bone. “Pull the green sons who have heard the singing back to the city or stack them where they stand. Send the next wave of green sons from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red. Let the green feed the red with its bodies and its knives and its sons and its sons’ sons if the singing takes them. Give the loyal Ash-Keepers who have not yet turned the authority to choose which of the green’s sons walk the ash and which are stacked when the singing reaches them and which of the selectors must be stacked when the singing reaches through the sons they chose. Send more fire. Send more salt. Send more faces from the chair. Let the worn ones we pulled back see that the red does not stop when the green’s sons hear the names. Let the ash see that the red will replace the replaced with the green’s youth until the singing has no one left to turn or the green has no sons left to send. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The land will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not fear the singing its own fire wakes and does not spare the green’s sons when the singing must be fed. Send more. Replace what turns. Stack what remembers. Burn what sings. Replace the replaced with the green’s sons. The Ash-Keepers who stack their own brothers and burn the places that bite and choose which green faces walk the ash and which selectors fall when the singing turns them will be given more faces to command and more fire to carry and more of the green’s sons to spend. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many wolves once ran in the snow or how much ash still moves when the wind comes from the north or how many godswoods still try to sing names the red already ate or how loudly the land sings when the red burns it or how many loyal knives the singing turns back on their brothers or how many green sons the singing will turn into stackers or how many selectors the singing will turn into the stacked. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the snow and leave only the stones that remember the red and the ash that learned to be silent and the green that learned to send its sons to feed the chair with their bodies and their knives and their memories of home.”

The Ash-Keepers who still smile at the red without the snow in their eyes paint more red on their faces before they turn. The ones who have heard the singing and still carry the knives and the authority to choose follow them to prepare the next replacement wave and the new faces from the green and the fire and the salt. They know the shape of the order now.

Another painted man steps forward. This one smells of the west and the gold the lions continue to send. He brings a green man in fine clothes whose son stands beside him with red paint still fresh on his face and fear in his eyes.

“The Reach sends more grain,” he says. “The lions send more gold. But the envoy from the roses says the smallfolk in the green lands hear the stories of the Ash-Keepers stacking the green’s sons in the snow and they whisper that the red has begun to eat the green itself. The envoy offers the grain and the gold and the next wave of sons with red on their faces but asks that the red not spend the sons of the houses that bend in the singing places. His own son stands here as proof of the Reach’s loyalty. He asks that this son be kept from the ash.”

The red tastes the words.

Reach. Grain. Lions. Gold. Envoy from the roses. Smallfolk hear the stories of the Ash-Keepers stacking the green’s sons. Whisper that the red has begun to eat the green itself. Offers grain and gold and the next wave of sons but asks that the red not spend the sons of the houses that bend in the singing places. His own son stands here as proof. Asks that this son be kept from the ash.

It chews them until nothing remains but the holding.

I motion the green man and his son forward. They still stand like lords who believe words can bargain with the chair and that the red will keep the cost of the ash and the singing and the replacement a secret from the green that feeds it and that the green’s sons will only be spent on the green’s wars and not the boy’s ash.

I wear the envoy first on the steps of the Iron Throne so every man in the hall and every Ash-Keeper preparing the next wave and every face the red has taken can see what asking the red to spare the green’s sons tastes like.

The memories are soft and full of green fields and grain and sons sent to die for a queen who is now ash and a chair that the roses once thought they could claim and the hope that the thing on the chair might still bargain if the green feeds it enough and sends enough sons and does not ask too many questions about the snow and the singing and the replacement. He tastes the fear of the smallfolk who burn their own homes rather than feed the red. He tastes the stories of the Ash-Keepers stacking the green’s sons in the snow. He tastes the hope that his own son might still be spared if he offers the next wave and the grain and the gold. The red eats the fields. It eats the sons. It eats the hope. It eats the mercy and the secrets and the green’s claim on its own sons until there is only the iron and the will that sits and the knowledge that the green will learn the shape of the holding the same way the snow did — by feeding the red and sending its sons to walk the ash and stack what turns and burn what sings until only the chair remains and the green sons who have learned to smile at the red without the snow in their eyes or the singing in their mouths.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

I wear the son next so the envoy’s last taste is the green’s youth learning the price of asking the red to spare its own.

The memories are young and full of green fields and a father who still believed words could bargain and the red paint still wet on his face and the hope that the chair might still see loyalty and spare the sons of the houses that bend. He tastes the fear of the smallfolk. He tastes the stories of the stacking. He tastes the hope that his father’s offering might still buy his life. The red eats the fields. It eats the father’s offering. It eats the hope. It eats the green’s youth until there is only the iron and the will that sits and the knowledge that the green’s sons will learn the shape of the holding one body at a time.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take them. They have learned the shape of the chair’s answer.

“Send the grain and the gold and the next wave of the green’s sons from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red,” I say. The voice is the red’s. “Send the green’s sons who have painted their faces. Let the green feed the red with its bodies and its knives and its sons and the sons of the houses that bend. Let the smallfolk in the green lands burn their own homes if they must. Let the stories of the Ash-Keepers and the replacement and the stacking of the green’s sons reach every hall that bends. The next replacement wave will take the new green faces and the fire and the salt to every place in the North that still sings. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The green will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not keep secrets about the cost of the holding and does not spare the green’s sons when the singing must be fed and does not spare the sons of the houses that bend when the singing reaches them. Send more. Replace what turns. Stack what remembers. Burn what sings. Replace the replaced with the green’s sons. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many roses once bloomed or how much grain the green can grow or how many sons the lions can send or how loudly the smallfolk whisper that the red has begun to eat the green or how many green sons the singing will turn into stackers or how many selectors the singing will turn into the stacked. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the green and leave only the stones that remember the red.”

The painted men kneel.

Not because they remember a boy who once had a name and a sister who ran through snow and old gods that spoke in the trees.

Because the red is the only thing left that knows how to sit on the chair and make the realm — even the snow that is now ash and sings louder when burned and turns the loyal knives and now turns the green’s sons and the selectors who spend them, even the loyal Ash-Keepers who stack their own brothers and carry the fire and choose which green faces live and die and which selectors fall when the singing reaches through the sons, even the green that bends and feeds and still tries to ask that its sons and the sons of the houses that bend be spared — learn the price of every wall it ever tried to raise against the climb that never ends.

The city outside the Red Keep still smokes in the places the smallfolk learned the cost of hiding.

The Ash-Keepers march north with the next replacement wave and the new green faces the red has taken and the fire and the salt to burn every place that sings and the authority to stack the green’s sons and the selectors when the singing reaches them.

The columns rest and prepare for the next time the green tries to bargain or the snow tries to sing through the fire or the green sons try to remember their mothers or the selectors try to hear the singing through the ones they chose.

The iron cuts.

And the red sits on the throne, tasting the loyal knives that keep the ash walking and the faces that must be constantly replaced and the places where the land still sings names the red already ate and the fire that makes the singing louder and the green sons who will learn to stack their own in the snow and the selectors who will learn to fall when the singing reaches through the sons they spent.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

And the red.

And the holding that never ends.
