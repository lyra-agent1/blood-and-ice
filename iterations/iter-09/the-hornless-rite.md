# Iteration 9: The Hornless Rite

Three days pass like a held breath.

The tribe calls me Red-Hand in the dark when they think I cannot hear. They bring me the first meat from the kills. They step aside when I walk the camp at night, Shaggydog a black shadow at my heel. The Bone-King’s throne sits empty more often than not. He watches from the edges now, one good eye narrowed, waiting to see if the boy who took his spear will break or become something the island has not seen in a thousand winters.

I spend the days learning their tongue in jagged pieces. Yrsa teaches me between raids. Eira — the Stone-Eater girl we spared — sits at the edge of the fire and listens. She has not tried to run. She has not tried to kill me in my sleep. That is more than most would give.

The red inside me is patient. Too patient. It smiles when I speak the words that make men kneel. It smiles when I do not need to call it at all.

On the third night the horn comes from the cliffs.

It is not the Bone-King’s horn. It is older. Longer. The sound slides under the skin and wakes the thing that sleeps behind my eyes. Shaggydog lifts his head and howls once, a raw sound that is not wolf and not man.

“The Hornless,” the Bone-King says when I find him. “They keep the old magics. The ones that were here before the unicorns learned to bleed. They have smelled the red on you. They will try to take it.”

I look at the black cliffs. The trees up there grow twisted, like they remember being something else.

“Then we go to them,” I say. “Before they come for us.”

He does not argue. Twenty hunters. The best he has left. No unicorns — the paths are too steep and the Hornless have no love for the horned beasts. We climb at first light.

The ruins wait at the top of the world.

Black stone carved with faces that have no eyes. Skulls grown through with roots that pulse when you are not looking. The air is thick with the taste of iron and something sweeter, like meat left too long in the sun. The red in my blood answers it like a dog to a whistle it has not heard since it was a pup.

They come out of the mist without sound. Five of them. Painted white from scalp to heel. Eyes the color of old milk. The leader wears a direwolf pelt that still moves as if something inside it is breathing.

She looks at me and smiles with a mouth full of filed teeth.

“The red boy comes to steal the island’s heart,” she says. The words are old. Yrsa translates them in a voice that shakes once and then steadies.

I feel the red uncoil.

I hold it.

The woman — they call her the Last Skin — lifts her hands. The air bends. Shaggydog makes a sound I have never heard from him and then goes still, eyes rolling white. She is reaching inside my wolf. Taking what is mine.

The red does not ask this time.

It takes the body.

I am across the space between us before the others can raise their spears. My hands find her throat. But instead of crushing, the red shows me the door.

Skinchange.

I fall.

For one heartbeat I am inside her. I see what she has seen — the island when the trees were green and the Starks came raiding with fire and iron. I see the price the first wolves paid to walk in other skins. I see the thing they woke and then tried to forget.

The red takes the memory from her and from me in the same motion.

When I open my eyes I am back in my own body, kneeling in the black dirt. The Last Skin is on her knees in front of me, eyes clear for the first time in years. She looks at me like she is seeing a ghost that has finally come home.

“You are not the first wolf to wear a man’s skin,” she whispers. “But you are the first the red has chosen to wear it back.”

The other Hornless kneel. One by one. The white paint runs in the rain that has started to fall.

I stand. My hands are shaking. Not from the fight. From what the red took while it was inside her.

When I reach for Robb’s face, it is blurred at the edges. When I try to remember the sound of Sansa’s singing, there is only the island and the blood and the rising. A piece of the boy I was is gone. Not broken. Eaten.

The red is not sated.

It is hungrier.

Yrsa steps forward. She does not kneel. She puts a hand on my arm, fingers digging in until it hurts.

“You are still here,” she says. Not a question. A command. “The boy who held the leash on the path is still here.”

I look at her. At Eira watching from the edge of the hunters with wide dark eyes. At the Bone-King’s men who now call me something in their tongue that means both king and curse.

“I am still here,” I say.

But inside, the red laughs, and for the first time I laugh with it on purpose.

Because I have the second chain now.

The Hornless will follow. The island is learning.

And every chain I take costs another piece of the boy who died at Winterfell.

That is the price of rising.

That is how a king is made.
