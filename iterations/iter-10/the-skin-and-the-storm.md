# Iteration 10: The Skin and the Storm

The Hornless kneel for three nights.

On the fourth, I dream of Winterfell.

Not the real one. The one the red has left me.

I stand on the broken tower and look out over fields that are not green. The walls are black stone like the ruins. The faces carved into them have no eyes. When I try to remember my mother’s voice, there is only the sound of the horn that woke the red the first time. When I reach for Arya’s laugh, I find the Last Skin’s filed teeth instead.

I wake with blood in my mouth and the red laughing behind my eyes.

Yrsa is already awake. She watches me from the other side of the fire. Eira sleeps between us now, the girl we took who has not run. The two new men from the first raid stand guard at the edge of the camp. They call me Red-Hand when they think I sleep.

“You left again last night,” Yrsa says. Not a question. “The wolf went somewhere the boy could not follow.”

I do not answer. I reach for Shaggydog. He comes, but slower than he used to. Even the direwolf smells the change.

The Bone-King’s hunters have swelled our numbers. Word travels on the island faster than men. The red boy who wears other skins has taken the Hornless. The tribes that still stand alone are choosing sides.

Some come to us.

Some send knives in the dark.

Tonight the knives win.

They come at the hour when the black trees look like men. Not the Stone-Eaters this time. A new tribe. The ones who live in the drowned caves at the edge of the sea. They bring the storm with them — real wind, real rain, and something older riding the lightning.

I am on my feet before the first scream.

The red is already at the door.

I do not open it all the way. I crack it. Enough to move like the thing inside the Last Skin moved.

The first attacker is on me before I can call a name. A woman with salt in her hair and a knife made from a whale’s tooth. She drives it toward my throat.

I step into her.

Not with my body.

With the red.

For one terrible heartbeat I am inside her skin. I feel the storm in her blood. I see her tribe’s plan — they mean to take the Last Skin and burn the red out of me with old fire while I sleep. I see the face of the man they call Stormfather, the one who waits on the beach with the last true unicorn horn that can kill what the island woke.

I take the knife from her hand and turn it.

She dies wearing my face for the last second of her life.

The red wants more.

I give it the leash.

I move through the camp like smoke wearing different faces. One moment I am the drowned-cave woman. The next I am inside a hunter who has already fallen, seeing his own death from inside his eyes. The laughter comes out of other men’s throats. My own men begin to shout my name and the old word for curse in the same breath.

By the time the storm breaks, the drowned-cave tribe is broken.

We lose four. We take seven prisoners who kneel before the red has finished with them.

I stand in the rain with blood that is not all mine running down my arms. The Last Skin’s white paint has washed into my hair. Shaggydog is at my side again, but he does not press against my leg the way he did when I was still only a boy.

Yrsa finds me.

She does not touch me this time.

“You wore them,” she says. Her voice is flat. “You wore their skins like cloaks and made them kill their own.”

“I wore the ones who came to kill us,” I say. The words taste like iron. “Darrow would have done the same.”

She looks at me for a long time.

“Darrow broke the Golds,” she says. “He did not become them.”

The red smiles.

I do not argue.

Eira is waiting when we return to the longhouse. She has bound the wounded with strips of her own dress. When she sees me, something in her eyes shutters.

“You are farther away tonight,” she says. Quiet. Like she is afraid the red will hear. “The boy who spared me on the path is smaller inside you.”

I want to tell her she is wrong.

I want to tell her I am still Rickon Stark, last son of a dead house, rising to take back what was stolen.

But when I open my mouth, the only sound that comes is the laughter that belongs to the island.

Inside, another piece of the boy I was is gone.

I cannot remember the color of my father’s eyes.

Only the red.

Only the rising.

Only the storm I am becoming.
