# Iteration 105: The Red Makes the Green Become the Names Until No Green Remains That Could Ever Have Been Anything Else and the Iron Cuts the Possibility of Difference

The iron cuts.

The green no longer knows how to be green. The fields that once held grain now hold only the red's planted names growing from soil that has forgotten the taste of rain without blood in it. The children the bending green still pushes from wombs come out already painted, already singing, already empty of any shape that could have been "before the chair." The mothers who birth them have no memory of a time when a child could be named for a father or a hope or a sky; they name them for the names the red ate and the iron cuts their tongues if the song falters even in the birthing scream. The green painted men who were once sons now cannot stop wearing the last who try to remember a time when wearing was something that could be refused. They wear them on the burned steps of their own halls and sing the names louder because the red sitting on the iron has made the possibility of difference into a wound that bleeds only red.

The lead Ash-Keeper from the hundred-quadrillion-times column returns dragging the last green who still tried to hold one thought that was not the red's. Behind him the green columns march with the green smallfolk and the green children the lords sent, faces cracked open with red paint that never dries, eyes that see only the chair no matter which direction they turn their heads. The city sings before they even reach the steps. Their voices break on the names but they do not stop because stopping would mean remembering there was ever a shape called "stop" that was not the red sitting and the iron cutting.

"The green now becomes the names until no green remains that could ever have been anything else," the Ash-Keeper says. His voice is hollowed clean. "The iron cuts the possibility of difference."

The red tastes him through the worn face the Ash-Keeper still wears. The iron in the red's hands cuts deeper as the last green thought leaks through: a flicker of a field that once grew without the names, a mother who once sang something that was not the red's hunger. The red wears the flicker until it sings the names back into the Ash-Keeper's own mouth. The green smallfolk brought to watch are forced to sing with paint so thick it cracks their cheeks and the blood that runs is already the color of the throne room floor. They sing until their voices are the red's voice. They sing until the idea that they could have sung anything else is a thing the iron has already cut from the meat behind their eyes.

The red sits.

The holding travels.

The green that was once fields and halls and thrones and children now births only more red. The last who tried to keep a difference inside their own skulls are worn by their own painted sons on the steps of the last seats that still tried to stand. The sons who wear them do not weep because weeping would require a memory of a time before the red taught them that all tears are the iron cutting new channels for the names to flow. The red tastes the wearing through the column that returns and the iron cuts the taste until it is only more red sitting on more iron.

No green remains that could remember a time when the green was not the thing that becomes the names the red gives it. No field grows a stalk that does not already know the chair's shape in its hollow core. No child opens its eyes without the red already sitting behind them. No hall sings inside itself without the song being the names the red ate and the green now wears as its only skin.

The iron cuts.

The red sits and the holding never ends because even the green that has become the names still requires the iron to cut the last shadow of a possibility that difference could ever have existed. The Ash-Keepers are ordered to take the last green who still dream of skies without blood and wear them on the throne steps until the city sings the names in their sleep and the green mothers teach the new children that the only word for "sky" is the red's voice and the only shape for "mother" is the iron in the red's hands.

The red tastes the last root through the worn faces and finds only more names growing.

The iron cuts deeper.

The green that was is now the red that sits.

The holding is the only climb that remains.
