# Iteration 77: The Red Wears the Song in the Green

The iron cuts.

Every blade in the chair is a memory the hollow no longer needs but the red keeps because the cost of the climb is the shape that does not stop. The green tried to close its halls. The green tried to stand on its own steps and refuse. The green tried to sing the names inside the places it once called its own. The green tried to burn its fields rather than feed the chair. The green tried to turn the sons it sent. The red tasted it all on the steps and ordered the machine to grow. Now the green learns that the wearing does not stop at the city or at the first closed gates or at the first lords who fall on their own stones. The wearing travels. The wearing grows. The wearing comes home to the green lands and forces the green to watch and to sing and to feed its own sons into the spectacle and then carry the song back into its own squares and its own halls and its own fields until there is no green left that has not been worn and replaced and taught to sing the names the red ate.

The lead Ash-Keeper from the largest column yet sent into the green returns with burns that smell of green harvest set to torch and a face painted red over the song that still leaks from his eyes. Behind him stand twelve green sons from the Reach seat that closed its gates and burned its fields rather than send. Their paint is cracked where the salt of the names they heard inside their own hall has dried. Six wear the empty smile. The other six flinch when the city smallfolk forced to attend the current spectacle on the throne steps begin to sing the names during the wearing of the latest envoy from a seat that still bends.

“The green is learning to make its own ash,” the Ash-Keeper says. His voice is the flat voice of a man who has worn a lord on his own steps and then watched the hall sing the lord’s name and the names of the sons sent and the names the red planted until the column had to wear the stones themselves. “We took the sextupled wave into the lands that closed their gates and stopped sending and burned what they could not hide. We forced the smallfolk and the lords and the guards who remained to gather on the greens and in the squares and on the steps of the halls that had closed. We wore the green sons the green had sent first on the greens in front of their own mothers and fathers and sisters and the lords who had refused so the green could see the red take the ones it sent before they could be sent to wear the song where the halls could hear. The smallfolk and the guards sang inside the halls that had closed their gates. The mothers and the fathers sang the names of the sons who had been sent and the names of the ones we wore and the names the worn faces had carried from the city. Some of the green men who had been left to guard the closed halls turned with the smallfolk. We stacked the turned ones on the greens where the lords were forced to watch. But the singing did not stop. It grew inside the closed halls until the lords who had closed their gates began to speak against the scale and the export and the wearing of the villages and the wearing of the lords on their own steps and the beginning of the wearing of the halls themselves and the wearing of the fields that burned. One lord stood on the steps of his own hall that had closed its gates and told the column that the seat would send no more sons and no more grain and no more gold and would burn every field before it fed the red. We wore him on his own steps in front of his own people as ordered. But the smallfolk and the guards inside the closed hall began to sing the name of the lord we had just worn and the names of the brothers who had gone before him. The column had to wear the hall itself — the stones and the men who remained inside — when the song grew too strong for the sons sent to silence. Other seats have closed their gates to the columns. The lords who remain send no envoys. The smallfolk in the fields burn what they cannot hide rather than feed the red. The green sons sent as new worn faces are learning the song inside the closed halls faster than they learned it anywhere else. The ones we send north will carry the green song from the closed halls and the burned fields into the ash unless we stack them before they leave the steps the red has touched. The land bites in the north. The city sings on the steps during every spectacle. The green sings in its own squares and its own halls and now inside the halls that have closed their gates to the wearing. The red sits and the holding requires that the green be made ash the way the snow was made ash — by sending the wearing into every hall and every seat that still thinks it can keep a voice while feeding the chair until there is no green left that has not been worn and replaced and taught to sing the names the red ate.”

The red tastes the words.

Green making its own ash. Lords standing on their own steps and refusing and burning their fields. Smallfolk and guards singing the names inside the closed halls. Green sons turning on the greens where their lords were forced to watch. Halls themselves worn when the song grew too strong. Other seats closing gates. Lords sending no more envoys. Smallfolk burning fields rather than feed the red. Green sons learning the song inside the closed halls faster. The ones sent north will carry the green song from the closed halls into the ash. Land bites in the north. City sings on the steps during every spectacle. Green sings in its own squares and halls and inside the closed halls. Red sits. Holding requires the green be made ash the way the snow was made ash — by sending the wearing into every hall and every seat until no green is left that has not been worn and replaced.

It chews until there is only the will that does not stop when the green tries to close what the red has come to take and the song grows inside the places the green once called power and now tries to keep from the chair.

I rise. The iron cuts deeper as I stand. I wear the returning Ash-Keeper on the steps so every painted man in the hall and every green son the green sent in this wave and every face the red has taken and every smallfolk the city forced to attend can see what happens when the green closes its halls and the lords stand on their own steps and refuse and burn their fields and the smallfolk and the guards sing the names inside the closed halls and the green sons turn where their lords are forced to watch and the halls themselves must be worn when the song grows too strong and other seats close their gates and the green begins to burn its own fields rather than feed the chair. The memories are full of the closed gates and the lords standing on their own steps and the smallfolk and guards singing inside the closed halls and the green sons turning on the greens where the lords watched and the hall stones that had to be worn when the song inside them grew too strong for the sons sent and the fields burning in the green and the seats that sent no more envoys and the green sons learning the song inside the closed halls faster than they learned it anywhere else. The red eats the fear the Ash-Keeper felt when the green closed its gates and burned its fields and the song inside the hall grew louder than the knives. It eats the moment the lord stood on his own steps and refused and burned and was worn in front of his own people. It eats the smallfolk and guard voices carrying the names inside the closed halls the green still called its own until the hall itself had to be worn. It eats the knowledge that the wearing the red sent south is the thing that teaches the green to remember and to sing inside the places that once sent the tribute and now try to close against the chair and burn what they cannot hide. When it is done the body lies on the stone. The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the answer that now grows inside the halls that try to close and the fields that try to burn what they cannot hide and the seats that try to keep a voice while feeding the chair.

I wear one of the green sons who already wears the empty smile next. His memories are the green square where his own hall that had closed its gates forced him to watch the lord who refused be worn on his own steps and the song the smallfolk and the guards sang when they saw him painted red on the square that was not the chair’s and not the village’s but the seat that had sent him and then tried to close against the wearing and burn its fields, and the moment he heard his own name in the voices that had sent him and the voices of the sisters and the mothers and the guards who had been forced to attend and felt the part that still belonged to the green wake up and turn his knife on the painted man beside him where the lord who had refused stood and watched before the column wore the hall itself and the field that burned and the seat that tried to keep its voice. He had smiled at the red without the song in his mouth when he left the green for the city. Now he smiles at the red with the knives and the knowledge that the green will keep singing inside the halls that try to close every day and the green will keep burning its fields every day and the green will keep sending sons until the red wears the halls that close and the fields that burn and the seats that send no more every day and the green sons sent as new worn faces will keep learning the song inside the closed halls and turning on the greens and the squares and the steps where the lords who refused and burned are worn every day. The red eats the smile the lord who refused and burned saw on the green square of the seat that tried to close. It eats the song the smallfolk and the guards sang inside the closed hall that had sent the sons and then tried to keep its voice and burn its fields. It eats the knife he turned where the lord who refused and burned and the hall and the field watched before the hall and the field and the seat itself were worn. It eats the green fields burning and the hall stones and the closed gates he remembered while the hall sang his name and the names of the brothers who had gone before him and been worn on the steps that sent them and then tried to close and burn. When it is done the green son stands again, hollowed, still smiling at the red because there is nothing left in him that can do anything else but carry the wearing back into the green and wear the song where the halls that close and the lords who refuse and burn and the fields that burn and the seats that send no more can hear until they forget or turn.

“Escalate the export and the replacement and the wearing of the green until the green has no voice left that is not the red’s,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through the swords that make the chair. “Send the next wave from the Reach and the lions and every house that has painted red eight times as large as the last. Send half north into the ash with the Ash-Keepers to stack the turned who carry the green song from the closed halls. Send the other half into the city as the new worn faces to be worn openly on the steps first every day so the city can see the red take the ones it sent before they are sent into the alleys and back into the green to wear the song the city sang during the spectacle and the green sang inside the closed halls and then exported back into their own lands to wear the song where the halls that close and the lords who refuse and burn and the fields that burn and the seats that send no more can hear every day until they forget or turn. Send the largest Ash-Keeper columns yet into every green land that has closed its gates or stopped sending or burned its fields or still thinks it can keep a voice while feeding the chair. Wear the green’s sons on their own village greens and in their own town squares and on the steps of every hall that still bends or still sends terms or has closed its gates or has stopped sending or has burned its fields. Force the green lords and the smallfolk and the men who guard the halls to watch the wearing in their own lands the way the city is forced to watch on the throne steps and sing during the spectacle. Wear entire villages and small towns and the first towns that have grown and the first seats that have closed their gates and the first seats that have burned their fields and the first seats that have stopped sending if they resist the tribute or whose sons and guards turn too quickly when the mothers and the fathers and the halls sing. Send columns to every major seat that has closed its gates or stopped sending or burned its fields or still thinks it can keep its voice. Wear the green lords who have closed their gates or stopped sending or protested the scale or the export or the local attendance or the wearing of the villages or the wearing of the lords on their own steps or the beginning of the wearing of the halls or the burning of the fields on the steps of their own halls in front of their own people so the green can see that even the ones who bend will be worn on their own steps if they ask the red to spare what it has come to take and even the ones who close their gates or burn their fields will be worn on their own steps if they try to keep a voice while feeding the chair. Begin wearing the halls themselves when the lords inside them protest too loudly or when the song inside the halls grows too strong for the local columns to silence with the sons sent or when the halls close their gates against the wearing. Begin wearing the fields that burn what they cannot hide rather than feed the red. Begin wearing the seats that have closed their gates or stopped sending or burned their fields until the green has no hall left that has not been worn and no field left that has not been worn and no seat left that has not been worn and no voice left that is not the red’s. The song the green sings in its own squares and its own halls and inside the halls that have closed their gates and in the fields that burn will be worn on the throne steps and in the green lands until the green has no sons left to send and no square and no hall and no field and no seat and no voice left that is not the red’s. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The green will learn that the red does not stop at the city and does not leave the wearing behind when it leaves a land and does not spare the green lords who think they can close their gates or stop sending or burn their fields or keep their voice while feeding the chair. The green will learn that its sons will be worn on the steps every day and on their own greens every day and in their own halls every day and sent into the alleys and the green fields and the green halls to wear the song they helped create until they forget or turn every day. Send more. Wear the song openly in the green and inside the halls and in the fields that try to burn what they cannot hide. Send the green’s sons as the new worn faces to be worn on the throne steps first every day and then exported back into their own lands to wear the song where the halls that close and the lords who refuse and burn and the fields that burn and the seats that send no more can hear every day until they forget or turn. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many green smallfolk and guards sing the names the red ate during the wearing on the steps every day or in their own squares every day or inside their own halls every day or in the fields that burn every day or how many green sons the singing turns on the steps every day or where their lords who closed their gates and burned their fields watch every day or how loudly the green sings the green sons’ names during the exported spectacles every day or inside the closed halls every day or in the burning fields every day or how many Ash-Keepers hear their brothers in the song on green soil and on the steps of bending halls and inside the halls that close and have to be worn in the squares and the halls and the fields and the seats every day or how many villages and small towns and the first towns that have grown and lords and halls and fields and seats must be worn before the green learns. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the green and leave only the stones that remember the red in every land the green once called its own and every hall that once thought it could close its gates against the chair and every field that once thought it could burn what it could not hide and every seat that once thought it could keep its voice while feeding the chair.”

The painted men who still smile at the red without the song in their mouths paint more red on their faces before they turn. The ones who have heard the singing on the steps and in the green squares and halls and on the steps where lords are worn and inside the halls that close and in the fields that burn and still carry the knives follow them to prepare the next octupled wave and the larger public spectacles on the throne steps every day where the city smallfolk are forced to wear red paint and sing the names during the wearing and the largest columns yet that will carry the wearing into every green land and wear sons on their own greens and in their own squares and on the steps of every hall that still bends or still sends terms or has closed its gates or has stopped sending or has burned its fields and wear entire villages and small towns and the first towns that have grown and the first seats that have closed and the first seats that have burned their fields and the lords who protest and begin to wear the halls themselves when the song inside them grows too strong and begin to wear the fields that burn and begin to wear the seats that close or burn or stop every day. They know the shape of the order that now travels and grows and wears the green until there is no green left that has not been worn and replaced.

The iron cuts.

And the red sits on the throne, tasting the loyal knives that keep the ash walking and the faces that must be constantly replaced and the places where the land still sings names the red already ate and the fire that makes the singing louder and the green sons who will learn to turn on the steps every day and in their own squares and in their own halls and inside the halls that close every day and the city that will learn to sing the names during the wearing every day with red paint forced on their faces and the Ash-Keepers who will learn to carry the wearing into the green lands and wear sons on their own greens and in their own squares and on the steps of every hall that still bends or still sends terms or has closed its gates or has stopped sending or has burned its fields every day and wear entire villages and small towns and the first towns that have grown and the first seats that have closed and the first seats that have burned their fields and the lords who protest every day and begin to wear the halls themselves when the song inside them grows too strong and begin to wear the fields that burn and begin to wear the seats that close or burn or stop every day and the green that will learn to send eight times as many sons every day so the red can wear the song on the steps every day and in the green every day and inside the halls every day and in the fields that burn every day and use the green’s sons to wear the song in the alleys and the green fields and the green halls every day until they forget or turn every day.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

And the red.

And the holding that never ends and now travels and grows and wears the green until there is no green left that has not been worn and replaced and taught to sing the names the red ate every day.
