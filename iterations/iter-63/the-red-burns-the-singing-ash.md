# Iteration 63: The Red Burns the Singing Ash

The iron cuts.

I sit on the chair made of swords and the red tastes the blood that has become the throne. Every day the blades dig deeper. Every day the hollow learns that the climb did not end when the last godswood burned or the last voice that remembered the boy went ash on the wind. The holding is the climb that never stops. The red sits and the realm pays in faces and loyal knives and the land that sings louder the more the red burns it.

The Ash-Keepers return from the snow with the answer.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

The boy is gone. The name is gone. The snow and the girl and the brother and the father and the old gods and every voice that once tried to pull the red back are ash in the red’s mouth. What remains is the will that climbed from Skagos to the city and the red that fed it and the chair that now holds both and the hunger that has learned to sit and burn the places that sing and reward the loyal who stack their own so the faces the red left behind can keep walking in the land that refuses to die.

The lead Ash-Keeper steps forward. His face is painted fresh red over new scars the fire and the snow and the singing gave him. Behind him the men who went with the cleansing column stand hollow-eyed and smiling at the red. Some of them smell of the smoke that still clings to their cloaks.

“The ash sang through the fire,” he says. His voice is steady because he has learned the shape of the holding and the price of the knives he carries. “We burned the stones where the last godswood stood. We salted the ground. We stacked the ones that cracked when the wind carried the names. But the fire made it louder. The worn faces we brought from the city to replace the turned ones heard the singing in the smoke and their eyes went wrong before we reached the trees. Twelve of the new ones turned on the march. The loyal stackers took them where the godswood had been. But the singing spread. Other places the red broke before — the half-roof holdfasts, the old watch stones, the places where the boy once ran with the wolf — the ash there began to carry the same names. The wind carried them to the new faces. More painted men who smiled at the red without the snow in their eyes began to remember. We had to stack another band on the way back. The land bites harder when the red burns it. The ash still moves. The singing is louder now in more places. The cost of holding the ash is the faces that turn and the loyal ones who must stack their own and the fire that makes the land sing louder.”

The red tastes the words.

Ash sang through the fire. Stones burned. Ground salted. Ones that cracked stacked where the godswood had been. Fire made it louder. New worn faces heard the singing in the smoke and turned. Twelve stacked. Singing spread to other broken places. Half-roof holdfasts. Old watch stones. Places where the boy once ran. Ash there carries the same names. Wind carried them to the new faces. More painted men began to remember. Another band stacked on the way back. Land bites harder when the red burns it. Ash still moves. Singing louder in more places. Cost of holding the ash is the faces that turn and the loyal ones who must stack their own and the fire that makes the land sing louder.

It chews them until nothing remains but the will to hold.

I rise from the chair. The iron cuts deeper as I stand. I wear the lead Ash-Keeper first so every man in the hall can see what the singing ash tastes like when the red tries to silence it with fire.

The memories are cold and full of the ash the boy once loved. Pine and wind and the burned places. But under it the singing is louder — roots that remember the trees that stood before the red came, stones that remember the boy who once ran through snow with a wolf at his side, a girl’s laugh that the red already ate but the land refuses to forget, the old gods that the red broke but that still move in the ash when the fire touches them. The turned men’s last clear thoughts are prayers to the old gods that the thing on the chair might still have a body the snow could claim if enough faces heard the singing at once. The red eats the prayers. It eats the roots. It eats the stones. It eats the girl’s laugh one more time. It eats the singing itself until there is only ash and the will that sits and the knowledge that the land fights back by making the red’s own fire into a song.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the ash’s cost and the loyal knives that pay it and the fire that makes the cost higher.

I wear one of the hollow-eyed stackers next. His memories are full of the smoke and the knives and the faces that smiled wrong in the firelight and the bodies stacked where the godswood had been and the new places the singing had reached. He had smiled at the red without the snow in his eyes when he left the city. Now he smiles at the red with the knives and the knowledge that the land will keep singing and the loyal ones will keep stacking and the red will keep burning and the cost will keep rising and that is the shape of the holding.

The red eats the smoke and the knives and the stacked bodies and the singing that the fire woke.

When it is done the stacker stands again, hollowed but still smiling at the red.

“Burn it all,” I say. The voice is the red’s. It speaks through the hollow like wind through bone. “Every place the boy once touched. Every stone that remembers the name. Every tree that ever stood where the old gods spoke. Send the Ash-Keepers with fire and salt and fresh faces from the city — the ones who still smile without the snow in their eyes. Let the worn ones we left behind see that the red does not stop when the land sings. Let the ash see that the red will burn the singing itself if it must. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The land will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not fear the singing the fire wakes. Send more. Replace what turns. Stack what remembers. Burn what sings. The Ash-Keepers who stack their own brothers and burn the places that bite will be given more faces to command and more fire to carry. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many wolves once ran in the snow or how much ash still moves when the wind comes from the north or how many godswoods still try to sing names the red already ate or how loudly the land sings when the red burns it. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the snow and leave only the stones that remember the red and the ash that learned to be silent.”

The Ash-Keepers paint more red on their faces before they turn. The ones who smile at the red without the snow in their eyes follow them to prepare the greater cleansing columns and the new faces and the salt and the fire. They know the shape of the order now.

Another painted man steps forward. This one smells of the west and the gold the lions continue to send. He brings a green man in fine clothes whose hands shake even though he tries to stand like a lord.

“The Reach sends more grain,” he says. “The lions send more gold. But the smallfolk in the green lands hear the stories of the Ash-Keepers burning the singing places in the snow and stacking their own brothers and they whisper that the red is a curse the North sent back through the dead boy. The envoy from the roses asks if the red will one day ask the green to burn its own fields and stack its own sons to feed the chair. He offers the grain and the men with red on their faces but asks that the stories of the North be kept from the smallfolk.”

The red tastes the words.

Reach. Grain. Lions. Gold. Smallfolk hear the stories of the Ash-Keepers burning and stacking. Whisper that the red is a curse the North sent back. Envoy from the roses asks if the red will one day ask the green to burn its own fields and stack its own sons. Offers grain and red-faced men but asks that the stories be kept from the smallfolk.

It chews them until nothing remains but the holding.

I motion the green man forward. He still stands like a lord who believes words can bargain with the chair and that the red will keep the cost of the ash a secret from the green that feeds it.

I wear him on the steps of the Iron Throne so every man in the hall and every face the red has taken and every Ash-Keeper preparing the greater fire can see what asking the red to keep secrets tastes like.

The memories are soft and full of green fields and grain and sons sent to die for a queen who is now ash and a chair that the roses once thought they could claim and the hope that the thing on the chair might still bargain if the green feeds it enough and does not ask too many questions about the snow. He tastes the fear of the smallfolk who burn their own homes rather than feed the red. He tastes the stories of the Ash-Keepers burning the singing places and stacking their own. He tastes the hope that the red might still pretend the cost is only for the North. The red eats the fields. It eats the sons. It eats the hope. It eats the mercy and the secrets until there is only the iron and the will that sits and the knowledge that the green will learn the shape of the holding the same way the snow did — by feeding the red and stacking what turns and burning what sings until only the chair remains.

When it is done the body is on the stone before the throne.

The painted men take it. They have learned the shape of the chair’s answer.

“Send the grain and the gold and the red-faced men from the Reach,” I say. The voice is the red’s. “Send the lions’ gold. Let the green feed the red. Let the smallfolk in the green lands burn their own homes if they must. Let the stories of the Ash-Keepers reach every hall that bends. The greater cleansing columns will take the new faces and the fire to every place in the North that still sings. The dead boy king is gone. Only the red remains. The red sits where the boy once ran. The red holds what the green could not keep. The snow is ash. The old gods are ash. The green will learn that the red does not forget the faces it left behind and does not keep secrets about the cost of the holding. Send more. Replace what turns. Stack what remembers. Burn what sings. The holding does not end at the city. The chair does not care how many roses once bloomed or how much grain the green can grow or how many sons the lions can send or how loudly the smallfolk whisper that the red is a curse. Only that they kneel or the red will eat what remains of the green and leave only the stones that remember the red.”

The painted men kneel.

Not because they remember a boy who once had a name and a sister who ran through snow and old gods that spoke in the trees.

Because the red is the only thing left that knows how to sit on the chair and make the realm — even the snow that is now ash and sings louder when burned, even the loyal ones who stack their own brothers and carry the fire, even the green that bends and feeds and still tries to ask that the cost be kept secret — learn the price of every wall it ever tried to raise against the climb that never ends.

The city outside the Red Keep still smokes in the places the smallfolk learned the cost of hiding.

The Ash-Keepers march north with the greater cleansing columns and the new faces the red has taken and the fire and the salt to burn every place that sings.

The columns rest and prepare for the next time the green tries to bargain or the snow tries to sing through the fire.

The iron cuts.

And the red sits on the throne, tasting the loyal knives that keep the ash walking and the faces that must be constantly replaced and the places where the land still sings names the red already ate and the fire that makes the singing louder.

Inside the hollow there is only the chair.

And the red.

And the holding that never ends.
