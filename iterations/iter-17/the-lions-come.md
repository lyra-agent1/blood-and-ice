# Iteration 17: The Lions Come

They come at dawn with banners and steel and the certainty of men who have never met the red.

Fifty riders. A knight in gilded plate at their head, his cloak the color of old blood and gold. Lannister men, drawn by the smoke and the stories the dead carried inland on living tongues. They expect bandits or broken smallfolk. They expect the world to still make sense.

My army waits in the trees above the beachhead. Painted faces. Black spears. The crown of bone lashed to a pole so the lions can see what rules here now.

I stand at the front with Shaggydog and the two anchors.

Yrsa’s spear is planted in the earth. Eira holds the piece of the killing horn like it is the only thing keeping the boy from vanishing into the thing that wears his face.

The knight raises a hand. His voice carries across the green.

“In the name of the King on the Iron Throne, lay down your arms. You are trespassing on the lands of House Lannister.”

The red laughs inside me before I can stop it.

The sound rolls down the slope. Wrong. Many voices in one throat. The knight’s horse shies. His men shift in their saddles.

I step forward.

Not with the body.

With the red.

For the first time on this side of the water I let it have the eyes and the voice and the hands that know how to end things.

The world goes hot.

I am inside the knight before he can draw his sword.

His skin is too clean. Too sure. I see through his eyes the life he lived — feasts in Casterly Rock, a father who taught him that lions do not kneel, a future that ends in this green field with a dead boy’s laughter in his throat.

I make him turn.

I make him draw the golden sword and drive it into the man beside him.

The other lions see their commander kill his own and the red takes the rest.

My painted men pour from the trees.

The Last Skin’s skinchangers wear the fallen before they hit the ground. Dead Lannister faces laughing as they turn on their brothers. The smallfolk we spared from the tower watch from the edge of the sand with eyes that will never forget this.

I step out of the knight’s body before the red can keep it.

He falls, still alive, eyes wide with the thing he saw from inside his own skin.

The battle is over in minutes.

When the red pulls back I am standing in the middle of the green with blood that is not all mine on my face and the crown of bone crooked on my head. My own men stare at me the way the Bone-King’s hunters stared the first time the red laughed on Skagos.

One of them — a hunter from the Hornless — drops his spear and takes a step back.

The knight is on his knees. Alive. Shaking. The red left him his mind so he could carry the story.

I walk to him.

“Tell your lions what you saw,” I say. The voice is layered. “Tell them the dead do not kneel. Tell them the black rock sent its king.”

He cannot speak.

Good.

Yrsa steps in front of me before I can do more.

“Say it,” she says. Loud enough for the army to hear. “Say the name. Now. While there is still something left that remembers why we crossed the water.”

The men watch. Even the Last Skin is silent.

I look at Yrsa.

I know the shape of her. I know she stayed when the island tried to eat me whole.

“Rickon,” I say.

The word is a whisper.

“Rickon Stark.”

It almost holds.

Eira presses the broken horn into my palm. The piece she has carried since the beach on Skagos.

“You chose on the path,” she says. “When the red wanted my throat. Choose now, before the green learns what we really are.”

I close my fist around the horn.

The red is sated for the moment. It has fed on lion fear and lion blood. It smiles behind my eyes and waits for the next time I open the door.

I turn to the captured knight.

“Take a message to your lions and your roses and whatever wolves still think they own the North,” I say. “Tell them Rickon Stark is coming for the chair that broke his house. Tell them the Skagos way has crossed the water. Tell them the dead do not forget.”

The knight stares at me like he is seeing the end of the world he knew.

Then he runs.

My men let him go.

The story will spread faster than fire.

Inside, the name is smaller still.

But the rage is larger.

The green has sent its first answer.

Now the realm will learn what the red answers back.
